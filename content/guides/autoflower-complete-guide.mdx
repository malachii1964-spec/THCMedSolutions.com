Autoflowering cannabis strains flower based on age, not light cycle.
While photoperiod strains need 12 hours of darkness to trigger
flowering, autoflowers start flowering automatically 3–4 weeks after
germination, regardless of how much light they receive. This makes
them simpler to grow, faster to harvest, and more forgiving of light
schedule mistakes — but they also have unique requirements that
photoperiod experience doesn't always prepare you for.

## How autoflowers work

### The genetics

Autoflowering cannabis contains genetics from Cannabis ruderalis, a
wild subspecies that evolved in northern latitudes (Russia, Central
Asia) where summers are short. Ruderalis adapted by flowering based
on age rather than day length — it couldn't wait for 12-hour nights
that might never come before frost.

Modern autoflowers are hybrids: ruderalis genetics (for the
auto-flowering trait) crossed with indica or sativa strains (for
potency, flavor, and yield). The best modern autos produce results
that are nearly indistinguishable from photoperiod strains in
quality, though yields are typically 20–40% less per plant.

### The timeline

| Week | Stage | What's happening |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| 1 | Seedling | Seed sprouts, cotyledons emerge |
| 2 | Early veg | First true leaves, roots establishing |
| 3–4 | Late veg / pre-flower | Rapid growth; first pistils appear (flowering starts) |
| 5–6 | Early flower | Bud sites form, stretch finishes |
| 7–9 | Mid flower | Buds fatten, trichomes develop |
| 10–12 | Late flower / harvest | Trichomes mature, harvest window opens |

**Total: 8–12 weeks seed to harvest.** Most modern autos finish in
9–10 weeks. Compare to photoperiod strains at 14–20+ weeks (4–8
weeks veg + 8–12 weeks flower). The speed is the primary advantage.

## Light schedule

### The recommendation: 20/4

Run lights 20 hours on, 4 hours off for the entire grow — from
seed to harvest.

**Why 20/4 instead of 24/0:**
- The 4 hours of darkness lets the plant recover and process sugars
  produced during the light period
- Electricity savings (17% less power than 24/0)
- Equipment runs cooler with 4 hours off daily
- DLI (daily light integral) at 20 hours of quality light is
  sufficient for maximum photosynthesis

**Why not 18/6 or 12/12:**
- 18/6 works fine and saves more power, but you're leaving ~10%
  potential growth on the table by reducing light hours
- 12/12 works (autos will flower regardless) but significantly
  reduces yield — the plant gets 40% less light energy per day
  than at 20/4

### DLI targets

| Stage | Target DLI (mol/m²/day) | Approximate PPFD at 20h |
|-------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Seedling | 15–25 | 210–350 |
| Veg | 30–40 | 420–560 |
| Flower | 40–50 | 560–700 |

Use our [DLI calculator](/tools) to dial in your light height and
dimmer setting.

## Nutrient feeding

### Start light, stay moderate

Autoflowers are generally lighter feeders than photoperiod strains.
Their smaller size and shorter life cycle mean less total nutrient
consumption, and they're more sensitive to overfeeding — especially
in the first 2–3 weeks.

| Week | Feed strength (% of label) | EC target |
|------|---------------------------|-----------|
| 1–2 | 0% (plain water or very light) | 0.2–0.4 |
| 3 | 25–50% | 0.6–0.8 |
| 4–5 | 50–75% | 0.8–1.2 |
| 6–8 | 75–100% | 1.2–1.6 |
| 9–10 | 50–75% (reducing) | 0.8–1.2 |
| 11+ | 25% or plain water | 0.2–0.6 |

### When to switch to bloom nutrients

Autoflowers don't have a clear veg-to-flower transition triggered
by a light change. Instead, watch for pre-flowers (small pistils
appearing at the nodes, usually around week 3–4). When you see
pistils, begin transitioning to bloom nutrients over the next 5–7
days. Don't switch abruptly — the plant is still stretching and
growing when pre-flowers first appear.

### Cal-mag

Autos in coco need cal-mag from the start, just like photoperiod
plants. In soil, cal-mag is usually unnecessary unless using RO
water or LEDs. See our
[cal-mag guide](/guides/cal-mag-complete-guide).

## Container size

**3–5 gallons is the sweet spot** for autoflowers.

- **1–2 gallons:** too small. Root-bound autos yield significantly
  less — the root restriction caps the plant's potential during its
  short life
- **3 gallons:** good for smaller autos (Lowryder, Easy Bud) or
  tight spaces
- **5 gallons:** optimal for most modern autos (Mephisto, Night Owl,
  FastBuds varieties). Enough root space for full potential without
  wasted medium
- **7+ gallons:** diminishing returns. The plant usually doesn't
  fill the root space before it's done

### Don't transplant autoflowers

This is the single biggest difference from photoperiod growing.
**Plant autoflower seeds directly in their final container.**

Transplanting stresses the plant. Photoperiod strains recover
because they have unlimited veg time — they just pause growth for a
few days and continue. Autoflowers are on a fixed clock. Every day
of transplant stress is a day of lost growth that can't be recovered
by extending veg. A 2-day transplant stall in a 10-week lifecycle
is 3% of the plant's entire life.

If you must start in a smaller container (seedling plug or solo cup),
transplant before week 2 — before the auto-flower signal triggers.
After pre-flowers appear, transplanting is off the table.

## Training autoflowers

### What works

- **LST (Low Stress Training):** the best training method for autos.
  Bend and tie down branches to create an even canopy. Start in week
  2–3 when the plant is flexible. LST doesn't stress the plant
  enough to trigger a growth pause
- **Leaf tucking:** tuck large fan leaves under branches instead of
  removing them. Gets light to lower bud sites without the stress of
  defoliation

### What works with caution

- **Light defoliation:** removing a few large fan leaves that block
  light to bud sites is fine — but do it gradually. Remove 2–3
  leaves at a time, wait 2 days, assess, and remove more if needed.
  Never strip an autoflower like you would a photoperiod plant
- **Topping:** controversial. Topping an autoflower works IF you do
  it early (before the 4th node, during week 2–3) AND the plant is
  growing vigorously. The recovery time from topping costs growth,
  and a slow-growing auto may not recover before flowering starts.
  If in doubt, don't top — LST alone produces excellent results

### What to avoid

- **Mainlining / manifolding:** too many stress events, too much
  recovery time. By the time the manifold structure is established,
  the auto is already flowering
- **Heavy defoliation (schwazzing):** aggressive defoliation that
  photoperiod plants recover from in 3–5 days can stunt an auto
  permanently
- **Supercropping in flower:** breaking and bending stems is
  high-stress. In a photoperiod plant, the 5-day recovery is
  insignificant. In an auto, it's directly lost bud development time

## Genetics matter more with autos

Autoflower genetics have improved dramatically in recent years, but
quality varies more than in the photoperiod market. Premium breeders
produce autos that rival photoperiod quality. Budget seeds from
unknown breeders are much more likely to produce runts, herms, or
low-potency plants.

## Autoflower advantages

1. **Speed:** 8–12 weeks seed to harvest. You can run 3–4 cycles
   per year indoors
2. **Simplicity:** no light schedule change, no worrying about light
   leaks causing herms
3. **Stealth:** smaller plants fit in tight spaces (closets, small
   tents, balconies)
4. **Perpetual harvest:** because light schedule doesn't matter, you
   can run autos at different stages in the same tent. Start a new
   seed every 3 weeks and harvest every 3 weeks
5. **Outdoor flexibility:** autos can be planted anytime during the
   growing season (May–August in WNY) regardless of day length

## Autoflower limitations

1. **Lower yield per plant:** a typical auto yields 1–4 oz. A
   well-grown photoperiod plant yields 3–8+ oz indoors
2. **No veg extension:** if something goes wrong early (overwatering,
   nutrient burn), you can't extend veg to let the plant recover
3. **No cloning:** clones from an autoflower flower immediately at
   the same age as the mother. You can't maintain a mother plant or
   run clones
4. **Less training time:** the fixed timeline limits how much you can
   shape the plant before it flowers
5. **Genetics lottery:** autoflower seeds are less uniform than
   photoperiod seeds. Two seeds from the same pack may produce plants
   of significantly different sizes

## Common mistakes

1. **Transplanting** — plant in the final container from the start.
   Transplant stress in autos is unrecoverable lost time
2. **Overfeeding early** — autos are small in weeks 1–3 and don't
   need much. Feed at 25–50% strength until you see vigorous growth
3. **Running 12/12 light** — autos flower regardless, so 12/12 just
   cuts your light energy in half. Use 20/4 or 18/6
4. **Heavy training too late** — any training done after pre-flowers
   appear directly reduces bud development time. Get training done
   by week 3
5. **Comparing yields to photoperiod** — a single auto yielding 2 oz
   in 10 weeks isn't worse than a photoperiod yielding 4 oz in 16
   weeks. The auto is more efficient per-day-of-light. Run the
   numbers on grams per watt per day, not total grams per plant
6. **Choosing cheap genetics** — this is the one place not to budget.
   Premium auto seeds cost $5–10 more per seed but the difference in
   reliability, potency, and yield is enormous
