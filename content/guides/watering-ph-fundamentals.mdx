Nobody kills their first plant with bad genetics or a cheap
tent. They drown it. Watering is the one daily skill in
growing — get it right and everything else gets easier.

## The golden rule: water by weight, not by schedule

Plants don't drink on a calendar. Lift the pot:

- **Feather light** → water today
- **Noticeably heavy** → wait
- Learn the difference by lifting right after watering and
  again two days later. Your arms become the moisture meter

Signs you're getting it wrong: droopy leaves **with wet soil**
= overwatered (roots suffocating). Droopy **with bone-dry
soil** = underwatered. Same droop, opposite fixes — always
check the soil before you pour. For a deeper dive on
diagnosing watering problems, see the
[overwatering/underwatering guide](/guides/overwatering-underwatering).

## How much, when you do water

Water slowly until **10–20% runs out the bottom** (in amended
organic soil, aim lower — just a trickle — to avoid washing
out nutrition). Then stop. Small sips every day keep the top
wet and the bottom dry — roots chase water, so deep,
less-frequent watering builds deep roots.

**Wet-dry cycling is the engine of growth.** Roots need
oxygen between drinks. In fabric pots this rhythm is almost
automatic; in plastic, be patient.

### Watering by growth stage

| Stage | Frequency | Volume |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Seedling | Every 2–3 days | 30–60 ml, ring around stem |
| Early veg | Every 2 days | Until light runoff |
| Late veg | Daily or every other day | 10–20% runoff |
| Flower | Daily in coco; every 2 days soil | 10–20% runoff |

These are starting points — pot size, medium, temperature,
humidity, and plant size all shift the schedule. The lift test
always overrides the table.

## pH: the invisible gatekeeper

Nutrients only dissolve into a plant-usable form inside a
narrow pH window. Outside it, food is *in* the soil but the
plant physically can't take it — the dreaded **lockout** that
looks like a deficiency and gets worse the more you feed.

| Medium | Target pH |
| --- | --- |
| Soil | 6.0–6.8 |
| Coco / hydro | 5.5–6.1 |

The routine (30 seconds):

1. Fill your watering jug, add any nutrients first
2. Stick in the pH pen
3. Adjust with a few drops of pH-Down (usually) or pH-Up
4. Stir, re-check, pour

**Buffered exception:** quality amended living soils resist
pH swings — many living-soil growers never adjust. Tap water
at 7.0–7.5 into good soil is usually fine. Coco and
bottled-nutrient growers get no such pass: pH every time.

For a complete pH walkthrough, see
[pH for beginners](/guides/ph-for-beginners). For lockout
recovery, see
[nutrient lockout recovery](/guides/cannabis-nutrient-lockout-recovery).

## Water quality quick hits

- **Tap water:** usually fine. Let it sit a few hours (or
  overnight) so chlorine gases off — chloramine doesn't gas
  off, but at tap levels it's rarely a real problem
- **Softened water:** avoid — the sodium accumulates
- **Very hard water (300+ ppm):** consider RO or mixing with
  distilled; then add Cal-Mag back
- **Cold water shocks roots.** Room temperature, always

## Reading runoff

The water that drains from the bottom of your pot tells you
what's happening in the root zone:

- **Runoff pH much lower than input** — salt buildup is
  acidifying the medium. Flush with 2–3x pot volume of pH'd
  water
- **Runoff pH much higher than input** — the medium is
  buffering up (limestone, degrading organics). Normal in
  fresh soil; concerning in week 6+
- **Runoff EC/PPM climbing** — nutrients are accumulating.
  Give plain water for the next watering

You don't need to check runoff every watering — once a week
is plenty for soil. Coco and hydro growers benefit from more
frequent monitoring.

## The 10-second daily check

Lift the pot. Look at the leaves. Glance at the hygrometer.
That's the whole job — three data points a day and you'll
catch every problem a week before it gets serious.

If something looks wrong, start with pH and watering before
adding nutrients. See the
[nutrient deficiency chart](/guides/nutrient-deficiency-chart)
— most "deficiency" symptoms are actually pH lockout or
watering issues in disguise.
