Cannabis is naturally dioecious — plants are either male or female.
But under stress, female plants can produce male pollen structures
(hermaphroditism) as a survival mechanism. The plant's logic: "I'm
stressed and might die, so I'll pollinate myself to ensure seeds
survive." The grower's problem: those seeds ruin the seedless
(sinsemilla) flower you've spent months growing.

Understanding what triggers hermaphroditism — and knowing the
difference between full hermaphrodites and late-flower nanners —
determines whether you pull the plant or save the harvest.

## Types of hermaphroditism

### Full hermaphrodite (intersex)

The plant develops both male pollen sacs and female flowers,
usually visible during early flower (weeks 1–3). Full hermaphrodites
produce clusters of pollen sacs alongside or mixed with pistils,
often at the nodes.

**What to look for:**
- **Pollen sacs:** smooth, round, ball-shaped structures hanging
  from the nodes on short stems (like tiny bunches of grapes).
  Compare to female pre-flowers: pistils are tear-drop shaped with
  white hairs emerging
- **Mixed sex organs:** both pistils AND pollen sacs on the same
  node
- **Timing:** usually visible by the end of week 2 of flower

**Cause:** genetics (some strains are prone), or severe early-flower
stress.

**Action:** remove the plant from the grow space immediately. A full
hermaphrodite will pollinate itself and every other plant in the
room. The pollen is viable and abundant. There is no saving it — a
true hermaphrodite will continue producing pollen sacs.

### Nanners (late-flower hermaphroditism)

"Nanners" (short for bananas) are exposed anthers — male pollen
structures that emerge from inside female calyxes during mid-to-late
flower. They look like small yellow or lime-green bananas poking out
of the bud.

**What to look for:**
- **Small yellow or green banana-shaped structures** protruding from
  inside the bud
- Usually appear in the densest part of the cola
- Sometimes hidden — you may find them only during inspection or
  trimming
- **Timing:** typically weeks 5–8, most common in the last 2 weeks

**Cause:** stress (light leaks, heat, extended flowering time) or
genetics.

**Action:** depends on timing and severity (see below).

### Nanners vs. pollen sacs

| Feature | Pollen sacs | Nanners |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Shape | Round, ball-like, in clusters | Elongated, banana-shaped, individual |
| Location | Nodes, separate from buds | Inside the bud, emerging from calyxes |
| Timing | Early flower (weeks 1–3) | Mid-to-late flower (weeks 5–8+) |
| Pollen output | High — each sac contains thousands of grains | Lower — some are sterile, some viable |
| Severity | The plant is a true hermaphrodite | Stress response; often localized |
| Prognosis | Remove the plant | Often salvageable (see below) |

## What causes hermaphroditism

### 1. Light stress (the #1 cause)

**Light leaks during the dark period** are the most common trigger
for hermaphroditism. Cannabis needs uninterrupted darkness during
the 12-hour night period to maintain female flowering. Even brief
light exposure (a grow room door opening, an LED indicator inside
the tent, a street light through a window) can disrupt the
photoperiod enough to trigger male flowers.

**Prevention:**
- **Seal your tent completely.** Turn off all lights, close the
  tent, and wait 5 minutes for your eyes to adjust. If you see ANY
  light inside the tent, fix the source
- **Cover indicator lights** on fans, power strips, and controllers
  with electrical tape
- **Never open the tent during the dark period.** If you must, use
  a green headlamp (green light doesn't trigger photoperiod
  response, though this isn't 100% reliable)

### 2. Heat stress

Sustained canopy temperatures above 85°F during flower increase herm
risk, especially in genetically sensitive strains. The combination
of heat and light intensity is particularly dangerous.

**Prevention:**
- Keep flower canopy temps between 72–80°F
- Adequate ventilation and exhaust
- Dim the light slightly if temps are consistently high
- See our
  [temperature/VPD guide](/guides/temperature-humidity-vpd) for
  environmental management

### 3. Genetics

Some strains are genetically predisposed to hermaphroditism.
Historically, Thai and Haze genetics carry higher herm risk.
Unstable crosses, bag seeds, and feminized seeds from stressed
mother plants are more likely to herm than stable genetics from
reputable breeders.

**Prevention:**
- Buy seeds from reputable breeders with stable genetics
- Avoid growing bag seeds from unknown origins (the parent may have
  been hermaphrodite, passing the trait to offspring)
- If a strain herms under mild conditions, don't grow it again

### 4. Physical stress during flower

Root damage, aggressive defoliation, broken branches, or
transplanting during flower can trigger hermaphroditism. The plant
interprets severe physical stress as a survival threat.

**Prevention:**
- Complete all major training (topping, supercropping, manifolding)
  before the flip to 12/12
- Defoliate gently and in stages during flower, not all at once
- Don't transplant during flower
- Handle plants carefully — avoid jostling, dropping, or crushing

### 5. Extended flowering

Running a plant significantly past its optimal harvest window
increases nanner risk. The plant's last-resort survival mechanism:
self-pollinate to produce seeds before death. This is why
late-harvest nanners are so common — the plant is trying to
reproduce before its lifecycle ends.

**Prevention:** harvest on time. Check trichomes and harvest when
they reach the desired maturity level. Don't push an extra 2 weeks
"for more amber" if nanners start appearing.

## What to do when you find hermaphrodite traits

### Full hermaphrodite (pollen sacs, early flower)

**Remove the plant.** Do it gently — jostling releases pollen.
Place a plastic bag over the plant before cutting it down to contain
loose pollen. Remove it from the grow space and discard.

If other plants in the room are in early flower, they may not have
been pollinated yet (pollen sacs need to open first). Continue
growing and inspect remaining plants daily.

### Nanners (late flower)

**Evaluate the situation:**

**A few nanners, close to harvest (week 7–8+):**
- Carefully pluck the nanners with tweezers, checking for more
- Harvest within the next 3–7 days — the clock is ticking
- Some seeds may develop in the buds near the nanners, but if
  harvest is imminent, the seeds won't mature enough to be a major
  issue
- The harvest is still usable and often high quality despite the
  nanners

**Many nanners, far from harvest (week 5–6):**
- More difficult. You have 2–4 weeks of flower remaining
- Pluck visible nanners daily (labor-intensive)
- Increase airflow to disperse pollen (so it doesn't concentrate)
- Accept that some seeds will develop
- Consider harvesting early — partially mature buds are better than
  fully seeded buds

**Nanners throughout multiple buds:**
- The stress that caused them will continue producing more
- Harvesting now (even early) may be the best option
- Check trichomes — if at least 50% are milky, the harvest will
  still be usable

### If pollination occurred

If pollen sacs opened or nanners released pollen before you caught
them, seeds will develop in the buds. This doesn't ruin the harvest:
- The buds are still consumable — just pick out the seeds
- Potency may be slightly reduced (the plant diverts energy to seed
  production instead of trichome development)
- The seeds from a hermaphrodite pollination carry the herm trait
  and should NOT be planted — they'll likely hermaphrodite too

## Common mistakes

1. **Confusing swollen calyxes with pollen sacs** — in late flower,
   unpollinated calyxes swell and look slightly bulbous. They're
   teardrop-shaped with pistils. Pollen sacs are smooth, round, and
   have no pistils. When in doubt, wait a day — calyxes develop
   pistils, sacs don't
2. **Not light-testing the tent** — most hermaphroditism is caused
   by light leaks that the grower doesn't know about. Test in complete
   darkness with adapted eyes
3. **Keeping a known hermaphrodite "to see what happens"** — one
   open pollen sac can pollinate an entire room. Remove it immediately
4. **Growing unstable genetics in a perfect environment and blaming
   yourself** — some strains herm regardless of conditions. It's not
   always your fault
5. **Planting seeds from hermaphrodite pollination** — these seeds
   carry the hermaphrodite trait. They're not "free feminized seeds"
   — they're herm seeds. Discard them
