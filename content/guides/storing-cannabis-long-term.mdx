A successful harvest often produces more cannabis than you can
consume in weeks. Without proper storage, that surplus degrades —
terpenes evaporate, THC converts to CBN, moisture imbalance leads
to mold or crumbling dust, and your carefully grown flower loses
the qualities that made it worth growing.

Good storage is simple: eliminate the four enemies (light, heat,
oxygen, moisture imbalance) and the flower maintains quality for
months.

## The four enemies of stored cannabis

### 1. Light

UV radiation degrades THC into CBN. A University of London study
found that light is the single greatest factor in cannabinoid
degradation over time. Clear jars on a shelf in a lit room lose
potency faster than any other storage condition.

**Solution:** opaque or dark-colored containers, stored in a dark
location (closet, drawer, cabinet).

### 2. Heat

Heat accelerates every degradation reaction — terpene evaporation,
THC-to-CBN conversion, and microbial growth. Temperatures above 77°F
(25°C) significantly accelerate quality loss.

**Solution:** store at 60–70°F (15–21°C). Room temperature in a
climate-controlled home is usually fine. Avoid attics, garages, or
anywhere that gets warm.

### 3. Oxygen

Oxygen oxidizes cannabinoids and terpenes. Each time you open a jar,
you replace the air inside with fresh oxygen. Frequent opening
accelerates degradation.

**Solution:** fill containers as full as practical (less headspace =
less oxygen), minimize opening frequency, and consider vacuum sealing
for very long-term storage.

### 4. Moisture imbalance

**Too wet (above 65% RH):** mold growth. Aspergillus and other
molds thrive above 65% RH. Moldy cannabis is not salvageable — it
must be discarded.

**Too dry (below 55% RH):** trichomes become brittle and break off.
Terpenes evaporate. The flower crumbles to dust when handled, and
the smoke is harsh and flavorless.

**Solution:** maintain 58–62% relative humidity inside the container.
Humidity control packs make this automatic.

## Storage containers

### Mason jars (best for most growers)

Wide-mouth, airtight mason jars are the gold standard for home
cannabis storage:

- **Airtight seal** — the two-piece lid (flat disc + threaded band)
  creates a reliable air-tight closure
- **Non-reactive glass** — doesn't leach chemicals or absorb odors
- **Available in useful sizes** — pint (7–10g), quart (14–28g),
  half-gallon (28–56g)

**Limitations:** clear glass lets light through. Either use UV-
blocking jars or store clear jars in a dark location.

**UV-blocking jars:** violet glass (Miron, Infinity Jars) or amber
glass jars block UV radiation while still being glass. More
expensive ($10–20 per jar) but ideal for longer storage.

### Vacuum-sealed bags

For storage beyond 6 months, vacuum sealing removes oxygen from the
equation entirely:

- **Food-grade vacuum sealer** + bags ($30–50 for the machine)
- Compress and seal. The vacuum removes 99%+ of the oxygen
- Add a humidity pack inside the bag before sealing

**Limitations:** vacuum pressure can crush buds, reducing bag appeal
(the buds flatten). This matters less for personal use than for sale.
You can partially vacuum (remove most air but not fully compress) to
reduce flattening.

### Stash jars and containers

Commercial cannabis storage containers (Herb Guard, TightVac, CVault):

- **CVault** — stainless steel containers with built-in Boveda pack
  holders. Premium option ($15–30 per container). Lightproof,
  airtight, purpose-built
- **TightVac** — vacuum-sealed plastic containers. Press the button,
  push down the lid, and a partial vacuum forms. Not true vacuum,
  but reduces oxygen exposure
- **Herb Guard** — glass jars with UV coating. Similar to Miron at
  lower cost

### What NOT to use

- **Plastic bags** (sandwich bags, Ziploc) — not airtight, static
  pulls trichomes off the buds, plastic can leach chemicals over
  time
- **Metal tins** (unless coated) — can impart metallic taste over
  long storage
- **Clear containers on shelves** — light exposure degrades THC
- **Silicone containers** — fine for short-term but the silicone
  surface grabs trichomes, reducing potency over time

## Humidity control packs

### Boveda packs

Boveda is the industry standard for humidity control in cannabis
storage. These two-way humidity packs both add and remove moisture to
maintain a target RH:

- **Boveda 62%** — maintains 62% RH. Preferred for flower that will
  be smoked (slightly moister, smoother smoke)
- **Boveda 58%** — maintains 58% RH. Preferred for flower that will
  be vaporized (slightly drier burns more evenly in a vaporizer) or
  for long-term storage (lower moisture = lower mold risk)

**Sizing:**
- 8-gram packs for jars up to 1 ounce
- 67-gram packs for larger containers (up to 1 pound)

**Replacement:** Boveda packs last 2–4 months in a sealed jar.
When they feel rigid (they start soft), they're spent. Replace them.

### Integra Boost

A Boveda competitor with the same two-way humidity control. Available
in 55%, 62%, and other RH targets. Includes a humidity indicator card
that changes color when the pack needs replacing.

### The Boveda debate

Some growers believe Boveda packs strip terpenes from the flower
(the pack absorbs and releases moisture, and terpenes are volatile
in moist air). Boveda's own testing shows no measurable terpene
reduction, but the perception persists in some communities.

**The practical answer:** Boveda packs prevent mold and maintain
consistency. Any theoretical terpene loss from the pack is
negligible compared to the terpene loss from improperly stored
cannabis without humidity control.

## Storage duration and quality

How long does properly stored cannabis last?

| Storage duration | Quality (proper storage) | Quality (poor storage) |
|-----------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| 0–3 months | Peak quality — full terpene and cannabinoid profile | Still good if not exposed to heat/light |
| 3–6 months | Excellent — minimal detectable degradation | Noticeable terpene loss, dry buds |
| 6–12 months | Good — some terpene reduction, THC stable | Significant quality loss, harsh smoke |
| 12–24 months | Acceptable — noticeable terpene loss, THC starting to convert to CBN | Poor quality — hay smell, low potency |
| 24+ months | Diminished — CBD and CBN dominant, terpenes largely gone | Barely smokeable |

**The rule:** even with perfect storage, consume within 12 months for
peak quality. Cannabis doesn't "go bad" like food, but it slowly
loses what makes it great.

## Freezing: the long-game option

For storage beyond 12 months, freezing preserves cannabinoids and
terpenes better than any room-temperature method:

### How to freeze cannabis

1. Cure normally (2+ weeks in jars at 58–62% RH)
2. Place cured buds in a vacuum-sealed bag with a Boveda pack
3. Remove air with a vacuum sealer
4. Place in the freezer (standard freezer temperature is fine)

### Important rules for frozen storage

- **Do not handle frozen buds.** Frozen trichomes are brittle and
  shatter on contact. Let the sealed bag come to room temperature
  (1–2 hours) before opening
- **Do not repeatedly freeze and thaw.** Each freeze/thaw cycle
  degrades trichomes. Freeze in portion-sized bags so you only thaw
  what you need
- **Vacuum seal is critical.** Without vacuum sealing, freezer air
  (which is very dry) desiccates the buds, and temperature
  fluctuations cause condensation that leads to mold on thawing

### Freezing for extraction

If you're saving material for concentrates (bubble hash, rosin),
freezing is ideal — it's essentially the "fresh-frozen" technique
used for live hash. Freeze the material immediately after harvest
(skip drying and curing) and process it later.

## Common mistakes

1. **Storing in plastic bags** — static pulls trichomes off, plastic
   isn't airtight, and long-term chemical leaching is possible. Use
   glass or stainless steel
2. **Opening jars frequently** — each opening introduces fresh
   oxygen. If you're smoking daily, keep a small working jar and a
   sealed storage jar. Refill the working jar weekly from storage
3. **Storing in a warm location** — the top shelf of a closet near
   the ceiling is warmer than you think. Store low and cool
4. **No humidity control** — without Boveda or similar, the humidity
   inside the jar depends on how well you dried and when you sealed.
   Over months, this drifts. Humidity packs remove the uncertainty
5. **Storing uncured cannabis** — long-term storage assumes properly
   cured flower. Storing cannabis that hasn't been cured (still above
   65% moisture) will develop mold in a sealed container
