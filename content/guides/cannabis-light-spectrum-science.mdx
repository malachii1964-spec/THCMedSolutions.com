Every LED manufacturer claims their spectrum is "optimized for
cannabis." Most are selling you a color temperature and calling
it science. The reality is more interesting — and more useful.
Different wavelengths of light trigger different plant responses:
stem elongation, leaf expansion, branching, flowering speed,
trichome density, and terpene production are all influenced by
the light spectrum reaching the canopy.

Understanding the photobiology doesn't mean you need to buy
expensive multi-channel LEDs. It means you understand WHY
certain lighting choices work, and you can make smarter
decisions with whatever light you have.

## How plants see light

### The photosynthesis action spectrum

Plants don't use all wavelengths equally for photosynthesis.
The photosynthetic action spectrum — how efficiently each
wavelength drives photosynthesis — peaks at two regions:

**Blue peak (~430–450 nm):** absorbed primarily by chlorophyll a
and chlorophyll b. Drives photosynthesis efficiently but also
triggers morphological responses (compact growth, thick leaves).

**Red peak (~640–660 nm):** the most photosynthetically efficient
wavelength range. Red photons drive more photosynthesis per unit
of energy than any other color. This is why most grow LEDs are
heavy on red diodes.

**Green (500–565 nm):** contrary to the common claim that
"plants reflect green light," green photons DO drive
photosynthesis — just less efficiently than red or blue at the
leaf surface. However, green light penetrates deeper into the
canopy and through leaf layers. In dense cannabis canopies,
green light reaches lower bud sites that red and blue can't.
Modern research shows green may contribute 10–20% of total
canopy photosynthesis in dense plantings.

### Beyond photosynthesis: photomorphogenesis

Light doesn't just feed the plant. It shapes it. Different
wavelengths activate different photoreceptor proteins that
control how the plant grows:

| Photoreceptor | Wavelength | Effect |
|--------------|-----------|--------|
| Phytochrome (Pr/Pfr) | Red (660nm) / Far-red (730nm) | Flowering, stem elongation, shade response |
| Cryptochrome | Blue (400–500nm) | Compact growth, stomatal opening, circadian rhythm |
| Phototropin | Blue (400–500nm) | Phototropism (growing toward light), chloroplast movement |
| UVR8 | UV-B (280–315nm) | Stress defense, trichome production, flavonoid synthesis |

The most important of these for cannabis growers is the
phytochrome system.

## The phytochrome system and flowering

### How phytochrome controls flowering

Cannabis is a short-day plant — it flowers when nights are long
enough. But it's not actually measuring darkness. It's measuring
the ratio of two forms of phytochrome:

**Pr (phytochrome red):** the inactive form. Absorbs red light
(660 nm) and converts to Pfr.

**Pfr (phytochrome far-red):** the active form. Absorbs far-red
light (730 nm) and converts back to Pr. Pfr also slowly reverts
to Pr in darkness (thermal reversion).

**The trigger:** when Pfr drops below a threshold ratio during
the dark period (because it slowly converts back to Pr without
light input), the plant receives the signal to flower. This is
why even a brief flash of light during the dark period can
disrupt flowering — it reconverts Pr back to Pfr, resetting the
clock.

### Far-red and the Emerson effect

**The Emerson enhancement effect:** when red (660 nm) and
far-red (730 nm) light are delivered simultaneously, the rate
of photosynthesis exceeds what either wavelength achieves alone.
The two photosystems (PSI and PSII) work more efficiently when
both are activated together.

**Practical application:** LEDs with supplemental far-red diodes
(720–740 nm) can increase photosynthetic efficiency by 10–15%
compared to the same PPFD without far-red. This is sometimes
marketed as the "extended PAR" range (400–750 nm instead of the
traditional 400–700 nm).

### Far-red and the end-of-day signal

A burst of far-red light at the end of the light period rapidly
converts all Pfr to Pr, effectively starting the "night clock"
immediately. Some commercial growers use a 15-minute far-red
pulse after lights-off to:

- Accelerate the perceived night length (potentially allowing
  a 13/11 schedule instead of 12/12 without revegging)
- Promote stem elongation during the stretch phase
- Reduce total electricity cost (one fewer hour of full-power
  lighting)

This technique is well-documented in ornamental horticulture
and increasingly used in commercial cannabis, but research
specific to cannabis is still limited. Home growers can
experiment with far-red supplemental bars ($30–50) run on a
separate timer for 15 minutes after main lights turn off.

## Blue light and morphology

### What blue light does

Blue light (400–500 nm) activates cryptochrome and phototropin
receptors, triggering responses that shape how the plant grows:

**Compact growth:** high blue content (>20% of total PPFD)
produces shorter internodes, thicker stems, and more compact
plants. This is why plants grown under metal halide (MH) lamps
(heavy blue) are squatter than those under HPS (heavy red).

**Thicker leaves:** blue light promotes palisade cell development,
producing thicker leaves with more photosynthetic capacity per
unit area. This increases light absorption per leaf.

**Stomatal opening:** blue light triggers stomatal opening more
effectively than red. Open stomata increase CO₂ uptake and
transpiration — both beneficial during vegetative growth.

**Stronger stems:** blue-rich light during veg produces thicker,
stronger stems that support heavy buds during flower.

### Optimal blue ratios

| Stage | Blue % of total | Why |
|-------|----------------|-----|
| Seedling/clone | 20–30% | Prevent stretching, build compact structure |
| Vegetative | 15–25% | Strong stems and tight nodes |
| Early flower (stretch) | 10–15% | Allow some stretch for bud site development |
| Mid-late flower | 10–15% | Less critical — red drives photosynthesis |

Most modern full-spectrum white LEDs (3000–4000K) contain
15–25% blue, which covers all stages adequately. You don't
need to change spectrum between stages unless you're chasing
the last 5% of optimization.

## Red light and flowering

### What red light does

Red light (620–700 nm) is the primary driver of photosynthesis
and the dominant wavelength in flowering-stage lighting:

**Maximum photosynthetic efficiency:** red photons produce more
photosynthesis per photon than any other wavelength. A light
spectrum heavy on red (660 nm) produces the most growth per
watt.

**Stem elongation:** high red-to-blue ratio promotes internode
elongation — useful during the flower stretch, counterproductive
during veg if you want compact plants.

**Flower development:** red light (with appropriate dark
periods) drives flowering hormone cascades. More red during
flower supports bud development.

### The "blurple" vs. white debate

Early LEDs used narrow-band red and blue diodes (the purple
"blurple" lights). The theory was sound — give the plant only
the wavelengths it uses most efficiently. But the practice had
problems:

**Blurple disadvantages:**
- No green light = can't see plant health under the light
  (everything looks purple)
- Missing the Emerson enhancement effect (no far-red)
- Lower overall efficiency than modern white+red diode
  combinations
- Poor canopy penetration (no green to reach lower sites)

**Modern full-spectrum (white + red):**
- White LEDs (phosphor-converted) emit a broad spectrum
  including blue, green, and red
- Supplemental red diodes (660 nm) boost the red peak
- Some boards add far-red (730 nm) for the Emerson effect
- Better canopy penetration, accurate visual assessment, and
  equal or higher efficiency

Full-spectrum white+red boards have won. Blurple is outdated
technology. If you're buying a new light, buy a modern
full-spectrum quantum board.

## UV light and trichome production

### The UV-trichome connection

This is the claim that most excites cannabis growers: UV light
(particularly UV-B, 280–315 nm) may increase trichome density
and THC production.

**The mechanism:** UV-B radiation damages DNA. Plants defend
themselves by producing UV-absorbing compounds — flavonoids
and, in cannabis, cannabinoids. Trichomes serve partly as a
UV sunscreen for the developing seeds/buds. More UV stress →
more trichome production as a defense response.

**The evidence:**
- Cannabis grown at high altitude (more UV exposure) produces
  higher THC per unit weight — this is observational but
  consistent across studies
- Controlled UV-B supplementation studies (limited in cannabis
  specifically) show 10–30% increases in trichome density in
  some cultivars
- The effect appears cultivar-dependent — some strains respond
  strongly, others minimally
- UV-A (315–400 nm) is less stressful but also less effective
  at triggering the defense response

### Using UV in your grow

**UV-B supplemental bars:**
- Run for 2–4 hours during the middle of the light period
  (not the full 12 hours)
- Start at 30 minutes/day and increase gradually — too much
  UV-B causes bleaching and tissue damage
- Begin UV supplementation in week 3–4 of flower (when
  trichome production is ramping up)
- Wear UV-protective eyewear when working under UV lights —
  UV-B damages human eyes and skin

**UV-A from standard LEDs:**
- Most white LEDs emit some UV-A (380–400 nm) naturally
- This low level likely contributes marginally to trichome
  production but won't cause the dramatic increases seen
  with dedicated UV-B supplementation

**Cost-benefit for home growers:** a UV-B bar costs $40–80
and provides a modest, strain-dependent increase in trichome
density. It's a worthwhile experiment for advanced growers but
not essential for quality flower. Environmental optimization
(VPD, feeding, training) provides far more return on
investment.

## Practical spectrum recommendations

### Seedling/clone stage

**Target:** broad spectrum with moderate blue (3500–5000K
equivalent), 200–400 PPFD.

Seedlings need enough blue to prevent stretching but not so
much intensity that they stress. Most white LEDs dimmed to 30%
provide an appropriate spectrum and intensity.

### Vegetative stage

**Target:** broad spectrum, moderate-to-high blue (3500–4000K
equivalent), 500–700 PPFD.

A full-spectrum quantum board at moderate intensity provides
ideal veg conditions. The blue content prevents excessive
stretching and builds strong stems.

### Flowering stage

**Target:** broad spectrum with enhanced red (3000–3500K
equivalent or full-spectrum + supplemental 660nm reds),
800–1000+ PPFD.

This is where red diode supplementation pays off. Lights with
dedicated 660 nm red diodes alongside full-spectrum white
produce more flower weight per watt than white-only boards.

### One light for all stages

Most home growers use one light for all stages (dimmed for
seedlings, full power for flower). A modern full-spectrum
quantum board at 3000–3500K with supplemental 660 nm reds
is the best single-light compromise. It has enough blue for
veg and enough red for flower.

## The green light myth

"Don't use green light — plants reflect it." This is one of the
most persistent myths in cannabis growing.

**The reality:**
- Individual leaves reflect about 10–15% of green light and
  transmit another 10–15% through the leaf
- But that transmitted light hits the next leaf down, where
  another portion is absorbed
- In a multi-layer canopy (typical cannabis), green light
  drives substantial photosynthesis in lower leaves
- McCree's action spectrum (measured on individual leaves)
  showed green as less efficient — but whole-canopy
  measurements tell a different story
- Green is the only wavelength that penetrates deeply enough
  to feed lower bud sites

**Practical impact:** lights with zero green (pure blurple)
actually produce LESS total canopy photosynthesis than broad-
spectrum lights with green included, because the lower canopy
gets almost no usable light. Full-spectrum white (which
includes green) wins.

## Common mistakes

1. **Buying "blurple" LEDs** — narrow-band red+blue diodes are
   outdated technology. Full-spectrum white+red quantum boards
   are more efficient, produce better yields, and let you see
   your plants' actual color
2. **Chasing spectrum over intensity** — a 300W light with
   "perfect spectrum" produces less flower than a 480W light
   with a "good enough" spectrum. Intensity (PPFD/DLI) matters
   more than spectral optimization for most growers
3. **Running UV-B all day** — UV-B is a stress signal. 2–4
   hours during mid-day is protective. 12 hours of UV-B
   damages tissue and degrades cannabinoids. Less is more
4. **Assuming green is wasted** — green drives photosynthesis
   in lower canopy layers. A light with no green means
   starving your lower bud sites
5. **Changing spectrum between veg and flower** — if you have
   a multi-channel LED, small adjustments are fine. But the
   difference between a "veg" and "flower" spectrum on a
   standard full-spectrum board is minor compared to the
   difference in intensity and photoperiod. Don't overcomplicate
   it
