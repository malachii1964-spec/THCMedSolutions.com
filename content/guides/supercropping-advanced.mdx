Low-stress training bends branches gently into position. Topping
removes the growth tip entirely. Supercropping sits between them —
you pinch a branch until the internal structure gives way, then bend
it 90° so the top folds over. It looks like you've broken the plant.
You haven't. Within a week, the bent section forms a woody knuckle
that's thicker and stronger than the original stem, with a wider
nutrient pathway that feeds the branch more efficiently.

Supercropping is the fastest way to level a canopy when one branch
has outgrown the rest, and it's the only training technique that
actively strengthens the branch in the process.

## What happens inside the stem

A cannabis stem has a rigid outer layer (the epidermis and cortex)
surrounding a softer inner core of vascular tissue (xylem and phloem)
and pith. When you supercrop, you're crushing the inner core while
keeping the outer skin intact — like bending a garden hose without
cutting it.

**The plant's response:**

1. **Immediate:** the inner tissue tears. Nutrient and water flow
   through the bent section is temporarily reduced. The branch above
   the bend wilts slightly
2. **Hours 6–24:** the plant begins repair. Cells divide rapidly at
   the damage site. Auxin (growth hormone) accumulates at the bend
3. **Days 2–5:** scar tissue forms. The bent section calluses over
   and begins to swell
4. **Days 5–10:** a woody knuckle forms — hard, fibrous tissue that's
   significantly wider than the original stem. The xylem and phloem
   through this knuckle are enlarged, creating a wider pipeline for
   water and nutrients
5. **Days 10+:** the branch above the bend resumes normal growth.
   The tip has already turned upward toward the light. The enlarged
   nutrient pathway feeds the branch more effectively than before

This is why supercropping is sometimes called a "high-stress
improvement" rather than just "high-stress training." The branch
genuinely gets better at transporting nutrients after healing.

## When to supercrop

### Good timing

- **Late veg (weeks 4–6):** the stems are mature enough to bend
  without snapping (green and slightly woody, not purely herbaceous)
- **During the stretch (flower weeks 1–2):** a branch outrunning
  the canopy can be supercropped to bring it level with the rest.
  This is the most common tactical use — one branch is 4 inches
  above the canopy and you need it level NOW
- **When one branch dominates:** a branch that's receiving more
  light or has a genetic advantage and is overtaking the rest

### Bad timing

- **Seedlings:** the stems are too thin and herbaceous. They'll snap
  instead of folding
- **After week 3 of flower:** buds are forming. The stress disrupts
  bud development, and the recovery period eats into the finite
  flowering window
- **When the plant is already stressed:** heat stress, nutrient
  lockout, or pest damage + supercropping = compounded trauma.
  Fix the existing problem first

## The technique

### What you need

- Your fingers (no tools required)
- Soft plant tape or garden wire (for securing the bent branch)
- Optional: electrical tape (for emergency repair if you split the
  stem)

### Step by step

1. **Choose the spot.** Find the section of branch you want to bend
   — typically 2–4 inches below the tip, in the newer (greener) part
   of the stem. Don't supercrop the main trunk or old, woody stems.
   They'll snap instead of fold

2. **Soften the stem.** Gently roll the stem between your thumb and
   forefinger at the bend point, squeezing lightly. You're crushing
   the inner tissue without breaking the outer skin. Roll for 5–10
   seconds until the stem feels pliable — you'll feel the inner
   structure give way

3. **Bend.** Once the stem feels soft at the pinch point, bend the
   branch 90° so the top folds over. The branch tip should now point
   horizontally (or slightly downward). You should feel the inner
   tissue yield — if it feels like the outer skin is about to tear,
   you're bending too fast. Slow, steady pressure

4. **Secure.** Use soft plant tape or a garden clip to hold the bent
   section at the desired angle. Without support, the branch may
   spring back up as it heals (though the knuckle usually locks it
   in position within a few days)

5. **Monitor.** Check the branch after 6–12 hours. The tip should
   already be turning upward toward the light. If the branch above
   the bend has completely wilted and shows no recovery after 24
   hours, the outer skin may have split — apply electrical tape
   around the damaged area

### The feel

The most important skill in supercropping is knowing how much
pressure to apply. Too little and the stem doesn't fold — it springs
back. Too much and you snap the branch clean through.

**The target:** you want to feel the inner tissue crush while the
outer skin stays intact. It feels like cracking a knuckle — there's a
subtle internal give, a slight crunching sensation, and then the stem
becomes pliable at that point.

**Practice on a lower branch first.** If you accidentally snap it,
you've lost a small branch, not a main cola.

## Tactical uses

### Leveling the canopy

The most common use. One branch has outgrown the canopy by 3–6
inches. Bending it down with LST takes time and the branch fights
back. Supercropping brings it level instantly.

1. Identify the branch that's too tall
2. Supercrop it 3–4 inches below the tip, bending the top section
   to canopy height
3. The tip turns upward within 24 hours and resumes growing at the
   same height as the rest of the canopy
4. The knuckle that forms prevents the branch from ever growing back
   to its original dominant height

### Building stronger branches

Some strains produce thin, flexible branches that can't support heavy
buds in flower. Strategic supercropping during veg creates knuckles
that act as structural reinforcement.

1. Supercrop each main branch once during veg, about halfway up
2. The knuckle that forms is wider and stronger than the original stem
3. In flower, the branch is less likely to need support stakes

### Emergency height control

The plant is approaching the light during the stretch and you can't
raise the light any higher. Supercropping the tallest branches buys
you 4–6 inches of headroom immediately.

## Supercropping vs. other height control methods

| Method | Speed | Stress level | Recovery time | Effect on branch strength |
|--------|-------|-------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Supercropping | Instant | High | 5–10 days | Increases (knuckle) |
| LST (bend + tie) | Hours–days | Low | None | No change |
| Topping | Instant | High | 7–14 days | Branch is removed |
| Defoliation | Instant | Moderate | 3–5 days | No change |

## Damage control: when it goes wrong

### The stem split

You bent too fast and the outer skin tore. The branch droops and
wilts dramatically.

**Fix:**
1. Immediately align the torn edges as closely as possible
2. Wrap the damaged area tightly with electrical tape — at least 2
   full wraps, overlapping above and below the tear
3. Support the branch with a stake or tie so it doesn't bend at the
   damaged point
4. Leave the tape on for 2+ weeks. The plant will heal underneath
5. Most splits heal completely. The resulting knuckle is usually
   even larger than a normal supercrop

### The clean snap

The branch broke clean through. The top is hanging by a thread or
completely detached.

**If still attached (even by a thin strip of bark):**
1. Align the broken ends
2. Wrap tightly with electrical tape
3. Stake the branch so there's no movement at the break point
4. There's a good chance it heals — cannabis is remarkably resilient

**If completely detached:**
1. The branch above the break is lost. Remove it cleanly
2. The node below the break will produce new growth from its
   auxiliary buds
3. Not ideal, but not catastrophic — the plant redistributes energy
   to remaining branches

## Common mistakes

1. **Supercropping woody stems** — old, brown, thick stems crack
   instead of folding. Only supercrop green or lightly woody tissue
2. **Not softening first** — bending without rolling/pinching first
   = snapping. Always soften the tissue at the bend point
3. **Too many supercrop sites at once** — supercropping is high
   stress. Doing 4+ branches on the same day overwhelms the plant's
   repair capacity. Space it out — 2 branches per day maximum
4. **Supercropping during late flower** — the recovery cost isn't
   worth it after week 3 of flower. By then, bud development is
   the priority
5. **Forgetting to secure the branch** — an unsupported supercropped
   branch can spring back up as it heals, negating the height
   correction
