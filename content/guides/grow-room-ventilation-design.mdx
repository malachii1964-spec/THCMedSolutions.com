Ventilation does four things in a grow room: exchanges CO2,
removes heat, controls humidity, and scrubs odor. Get it wrong
and everything else suffers — even a perfect light, perfect
nutrients, and perfect genetics can't overcome stale, hot, humid
air. Most growers buy an inline fan based on tent size and call
it done, but understanding the airflow engineering helps you
avoid the two most common problems: undersized fans that can't
keep up with heat, and oversized fans that waste electricity and
create too much negative pressure.

## The CFM formula

**CFM (Cubic Feet per Minute)** is the volume of air your fan
moves. The baseline calculation:

```
Base CFM = room volume (cubic feet) × air exchanges per minute
```

Cannabis needs the entire room air replaced every 1–3 minutes:

| Room volume | Exchanges/min | Base CFM |
|-------------|---------------|----------|
| 4×4×6.5 (104 cu ft) | 1 | 104 CFM |
| 4×8×6.5 (208 cu ft) | 1 | 208 CFM |
| 5×5×6.5 (163 cu ft) | 1 | 163 CFM |
| 10×10×8 (800 cu ft) | 1 | 800 CFM |

### Derating for real-world conditions

Base CFM assumes zero resistance. Real installations have
resistance from carbon filters, ducting, bends, and altitude.
Apply these multipliers:

| Factor | Multiplier |
|--------|-----------|
| Carbon filter | ×1.25 |
| Each 90° duct bend | ×1.05 |
| Each extra 10 ft of duct length beyond 10 ft | ×1.05 |
| HPS lighting (vs. LED) | ×1.25 (more heat to remove) |
| Hot climate (ambient >80°F) | ×1.15 |
| High altitude (>5000 ft) | ×1.10 |

**Example:** 4×4 tent (104 CFM base) with a carbon filter (×1.25)
and two 90° bends (×1.05 × 1.05):

```
104 × 1.25 × 1.05 × 1.05 = 143 CFM needed
```

A 4-inch inline fan rated at 205 CFM (like the AC Infinity
Cloudline T4) handles this comfortably with headroom for speed
reduction.

### Fan sizing by tent size

| Tent size | Volume | Recommended fan | Rated CFM |
|-----------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| 2×2 | 26 cu ft | 4" inline | 190–205 CFM |
| 2×4 | 52 cu ft | 4" inline | 190–205 CFM |
| 3×3 | 59 cu ft | 4" inline | 190–205 CFM |
| 4×4 | 104 cu ft | 4" inline (adequate) or 6" (comfortable) | 205 / 400 CFM |
| 4×8 | 208 cu ft | 6" inline | 390–420 CFM |
| 5×5 | 163 cu ft | 6" inline | 390–420 CFM |
| 5×10 | 325 cu ft | 8" inline | 700–800 CFM |
| 10×10 | 800 cu ft | 8" inline or dual 6" | 800+ CFM |

**When in doubt, go one size up.** You can always turn a fan down
(most have speed controllers), but you can't make a small fan
move more air than its maximum rating.

## Negative pressure

### What it is

Negative pressure means the exhaust fan pulls air out faster than
it enters. This creates a slight vacuum inside the tent or room:

- The tent walls pull inward slightly (the "suck-in" you see on
  flexible tent walls)
- ALL air exits through the carbon filter (the only designed
  exhaust path)
- No unfiltered air leaks OUT — it only leaks IN through gaps
  and intakes

### Why it matters

Negative pressure ensures that **all odor passes through the
carbon filter** before leaving the tent. Without it, smell leaks
through every gap, zipper, and port — the carbon filter becomes
pointless.

### How to achieve it

The exhaust fan must move more air than enters through intake
ports:

- **Passive intake** (no intake fan): simply leave one or two
  intake ports open at the bottom of the tent. The exhaust fan's
  suction pulls fresh air in through these ports. The ports should
  be smaller than the exhaust to maintain negative pressure
- **Active intake** (with intake fan): use a smaller fan or run
  the intake fan at a lower speed than the exhaust. A common
  ratio: exhaust at 100%, intake at 60–70%

**Too much negative pressure:** tent walls collapse inward,
restricting the growing space and potentially crushing plants.
If walls are pulling in dramatically, open another intake port
or reduce exhaust speed slightly.

**Signs of correct negative pressure:**
- Tent walls pull in very slightly when zipped up
- No cannabis odor outside the tent when the exhaust is running
- Fresh air enters through intake ports (you can feel airflow
  with your hand)

## Duct planning

### Duct diameter

Match duct diameter to fan diameter. A 6-inch fan needs 6-inch
ducting. Reducing the duct size (6" fan into 4" duct) creates a
bottleneck that drastically reduces effective CFM.

### Duct length

Shorter is better. Every foot of duct adds friction resistance.
Flexible aluminum ducting (the standard for tent grows) has
significantly more resistance than rigid ducting because the
corrugated inner surface creates turbulence.

**Practical limit:** keep total duct length under 25 feet. If you
need more, upgrade to the next fan size.

### Bends

Each 90° bend reduces effective CFM by about 5%. Two tight 90°
bends in a 4-foot run can cost you 10% of your airflow.

**Best practice:**
- Use gradual curves instead of sharp 90° turns
- Stretch flexible duct fully straight (don't leave it compressed
  — the accordion folds add resistance)
- One straight exhaust run to the vent point is ideal

### Insulated ducting

If ducting runs through a cold attic or uninsulated space,
insulated ducting prevents condensation. Warm, humid exhaust air
hitting cold duct walls creates water droplets that drip back into
the tent or pool in low points. Insulated ducting maintains
temperature and prevents this.

## Carbon filter

### How it works

Activated carbon adsorbs odor molecules as air passes through the
filter bed. The air exits odor-free. Carbon filters are the
standard for indoor cannabis odor control — they're effective
enough that a properly installed filter eliminates virtually all
detectable cannabis odor.

### Sizing

The carbon filter's CFM rating must match or exceed the exhaust
fan's CFM at operating speed:

| Fan | Minimum carbon filter rating |
|-----|------------------------------|
| 4" at full speed (~200 CFM) | 200+ CFM |
| 6" at full speed (~400 CFM) | 400+ CFM |
| 8" at full speed (~800 CFM) | 800+ CFM |

**Under-sizing the filter** forces air through faster than the
carbon can adsorb odor — smell leaks through.

### Installation

Two options:

**Inside the tent (most common):** hang the filter inside the
tent connected directly to the inline fan. Air path: tent air →
through carbon filter → through fan → out the ducting → outside.
This keeps the filter in the growing environment where the odor
is highest.

**Outside the tent:** mount the filter at the end of the exhaust
ducting, outside the tent. Works but is less common and less
effective (odor molecules may deposit on duct walls before reaching
the filter).

### Lifespan

Carbon filters last 12–24 months depending on humidity and use.
Replace when you start smelling cannabis odor from the exhaust.
Humidity above 70% degrades carbon faster — keep humidity in the
50s or below for maximum filter life.

**Prefilter:** most carbon filters come with a sock-style
prefilter that catches dust and pollen before they clog the carbon
bed. Wash or replace the prefilter every 2–3 months to maintain
airflow.

## Sealed room vs. vented room

### Vented (exhaust + intake)

The standard for tent grows. Exhaust fan pulls tent air out, fresh
room or outdoor air enters through intake ports.

**Pros:**
- Simple, cheap, effective
- Naturally supplies fresh CO2 (ambient air is ~420 ppm CO2)
- Controls temperature passively (pulling cooler room air in)

**Cons:**
- Relies on room/outdoor air quality and temperature
- Can't supplement CO2 effectively (it just gets exhausted)
- Humidity dependent on outside air humidity

### Sealed (no air exchange with outside)

For dedicated grow rooms (not tents). No intake or exhaust to
the outside — all air is recirculated within the sealed space.

**Pros:**
- Total environmental control (temp, humidity, CO2 are all managed
  independently)
- CO2 supplementation is efficient (1200–1500 ppm)
- No pest entry from outside air
- No odor leaks (no exhaust path to outside)

**Cons:**
- Requires AC (mini-split or portable) for cooling
- Requires dehumidifier
- Requires CO2 tank or generator
- Significantly more expensive
- More complex to design and maintain

**When to go sealed:** when you need CO2 supplementation (pushing
DLI above 40 mol/m²/d with high-powered lights) or when outside
air quality is a problem (extreme heat, extreme humidity, pest
pressure).

For most home growers, vented is simpler, cheaper, and adequate.

## Oscillating fans

Inline exhaust handles air exchange. Oscillating clip fans handle
air circulation WITHIN the growing space — they're different jobs:

- **One fan per 4×4 area** at canopy height
- Point across the canopy, not directly at plants (constant direct
  wind causes wind burn — stem thickening and curled leaves)
- Oscillating is better than fixed — it distributes air more
  evenly
- Run 24/7 including during lights off

Oscillating fans:
- Strengthen stems (mechanical stress promotes thicker stems)
- Prevent humidity pockets inside the canopy
- Distribute CO2 evenly
- Deter flying pests (fungus gnats, whiteflies)

## Common mistakes

1. **Buying a fan based only on tent size without derating** — the
   rated CFM is max theoretical. Carbon filters, bends, and duct
   length reduce it by 25–35%. Size for the derated output
2. **Compressing flexible ducting** — a 10-foot run of compressed
   flex duct acts like a 20-foot run of straight duct. Stretch it
   fully or use rigid ducting for long runs
3. **Running the exhaust at 100% constantly** — most grows don't
   need maximum extraction all the time. Use a speed controller to
   match exhaust to conditions — save electricity and fan life
4. **No oscillating fan at canopy level** — the exhaust fan
   exchanges air, but stagnant pockets still form inside dense
   canopies. You need internal circulation
5. **Turning off ventilation during lights-off** — humidity spikes
   during the dark period are the prime mold window. Keep exhaust
   running 24/7
6. **Exhausting into the same room** — if the exhaust dumps into
   the same room the tent intakes from, you're recirculating hot,
   humid air. Exhaust to a different room, a window, or ducted
   outside
