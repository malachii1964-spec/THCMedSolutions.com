Cannabis produces trichomes partly as a UV shield. In nature,
plants at higher elevations and lower latitudes — where UV
radiation is stronger — tend to produce more resin. This
observation has driven growers to add supplemental UVB light
during flowering, aiming to trigger the plant's defensive
resin response and increase trichome density.

The science supports the concept. The execution is where most
growers get it wrong.

## The UV spectrum

Ultraviolet light falls below the visible spectrum:

| Type | Wavelength | Source | Effect on cannabis |
|------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| UVA | 320–400 nm | Sunlight, some LEDs, T5 bulbs | Mild stress, minor trichome stimulus |
| UVB | 280–320 nm | Sunlight, reptile bulbs, specialty fixtures | Significant trichome production trigger |
| UVC | 100–280 nm | Germicidal lamps | Damages/kills plant tissue — never use |

**UVB is the target.** UVA has some effect but is less
potent as a trichome trigger. UVC is destructive — it's used
to sterilize surfaces, not grow plants.

## How UVB triggers trichome production

### The defense mechanism

Trichomes contain UV-absorbing compounds — primarily
flavonoids and cannabinoids (THC itself absorbs UV). When the
plant senses UVB radiation:

1. Photoreceptors in leaf tissue detect UV wavelengths
2. The plant upregulates genes for secondary metabolite
   production (flavonoids, terpenoids, cannabinoids)
3. Trichome density increases, especially on sun-facing
   surfaces
4. Cannabinoid concentration within trichomes may increase

The plant is essentially building a layer of biological
sunscreen. More UV stress = more sunscreen production = more
trichomes and resin.

### What the research shows

- **Lydon et al. (1987):** cannabis grown under supplemental
  UVB produced significantly more THC than plants grown
  without UV exposure. One of the earliest controlled
  studies confirming the UV-THC connection
- **Magagnini et al. (2018):** UV exposure during flowering
  increased THC concentration without significantly affecting
  CBD. The ratio shifted toward THC
- **Altitude studies:** cannabis cultivated at high elevations
  (stronger natural UVB) consistently shows higher
  cannabinoid content than genetically identical plants grown
  at lower elevations
- **Practical grower reports:** experienced growers adding
  UVB during the last 3–4 weeks of flowering report visually
  denser trichome coverage and increased resin stickiness.
  Quantitative measurements vary, but 10–30% increases in
  trichome density are commonly reported

### What UVB doesn't do

- **It doesn't increase yield.** UVB stress can slightly
  reduce total biomass (the plant diverts energy from growth
  to defense). The goal is quality — more resin per gram, not
  more grams
- **It doesn't replace good genetics.** A low-trichome strain
  under UVB won't match a high-trichome strain without it.
  UVB enhances what genetics provide
- **It doesn't work in veg.** Trichome production is a
  flowering response. UVB during veg causes stress without
  meaningful trichome benefit

## UVB fixtures for cannabis

### Reptile UVB bulbs (most common)

Reptile bulbs designed for bearded dragons and other desert
reptiles are the most accessible UVB source for home growers:

**T5 HO reptile bulbs (recommended):**
- Arcadia D3+ 12% UVB or Arcadia D3 6% UVB
- Zoo Med ReptiSun 10.0 T5 HO
- Output is calibrated for reptile terrariums — close-range,
  moderate UVB

**Compact fluorescent reptile bulbs:**
- Lower output, smaller coverage area
- Suitable for 1–2 plant micro grows
- Not practical for full tent coverage

### Specialty horticultural UV fixtures

Purpose-built for plant cultivation:

- **Agromax Pure UV:** T5 HO bulb designed for horticultural
  UV supplementation. Higher UVB output than reptile bulbs
- **Solacure Flower Power:** broadband UV bulb specifically
  designed for flowering cannabis
- **California Lightworks UV bar:** designed to mount
  alongside LED arrays

These cost more but are engineered for the purpose — better
spectral output for trichome production and designed for the
mounting distances used in grow tents.

### LED UVB (emerging)

UV LEDs exist but are expensive and have limited lifespan at
the UVB wavelengths. Some high-end grow lights (Gavita,
Fluence) include UV diodes in their arrays, but the UVB
output is typically minimal — more marketing than functional
trichome stimulus.

As of 2026, dedicated fluorescent UVB tubes remain more
cost-effective for meaningful UVB supplementation than UV
LEDs.

## How to add UVB to your grow

### Mounting position

UVB intensity drops dramatically with distance (inverse
square law). The bulb must be close enough to deliver
meaningful UVB but far enough to avoid burning tissue:

| Fixture type | Recommended distance from canopy |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| T5 HO reptile (10.0/12%) | 12–18 inches |
| T5 HO horticultural (Agromax) | 18–24 inches |
| Compact fluorescent reptile | 8–12 inches |

**Mount the UVB fixture ABOVE the main grow light if
possible** — or at canopy level on the tent frame, angled
downward. UVB doesn't need to light the plant the way PAR
light does — it needs to reach the upper canopy where
trichomes form.

### Exposure schedule

**Do not run UVB 12 hours straight.** UVB is a stressor.
Continuous exposure causes UV bleaching (white/pale patches
on sugar leaves), leaf curl, and photodamage that reduces
quality rather than improving it.

**Recommended schedule:**

| Phase | UVB hours per day | Duration |
|-------|------------------|----------|
| Week 1–2 of flower | 0 (off) | Let stretch complete undisturbed |
| Week 3 of flower | 2 hours (mid-day in light cycle) | Acclimation period |
| Week 4 of flower | 3–4 hours (centered in light cycle) | Building exposure |
| Week 5–harvest | 4–6 hours (centered in light cycle) | Maximum benefit period |

**Center the UV exposure in the middle of the light
period** — this mimics the natural solar pattern where UVB
peaks at solar noon. Running UV at the start or end of the
light cycle when the plant is transitioning between light
and dark phases adds unnecessary stress.

### The acclimation principle

Plants adapt to UV exposure over time, producing protective
compounds. Starting with full UVB immediately overwhelms the
plant's defense response and causes damage. Ramp up gradually:
- Week 1: 1–2 hours
- Week 2: 2–3 hours
- Week 3+: 3–6 hours

This gives the plant time to upregulate its UV defense
pathways (the exact pathways that produce the trichomes
you want).

## Safety

### UVB and human health

**UVB radiation causes sunburn, eye damage, and increases
skin cancer risk.** This applies to you, not just the plants:

- **Never look directly at a UVB source.** Even brief
  exposure can cause photokeratitis (UV eye burn — feels
  like sand in your eyes 6–12 hours later)
- **Wear UV-blocking safety glasses** when working in the
  tent with UVB running. Standard sunglasses may not block
  enough UV — get rated safety glasses (ANSI Z87.1 with
  UV400 protection)
- **Minimize skin exposure.** Long sleeves or turn the UVB
  off while you work in the tent. A few minutes of incidental
  exposure won't cause a sunburn, but extended work sessions
  under UVB can
- **Put the UVB bulb on a separate timer** so you can shut
  it off independently from the main grow light when working
  in the tent

### UVB and plastics

UVB degrades many plastics over time — including the
reflective lining (Mylar) in some grow tents and the housing
of some electrical components. Inspect your tent lining and
any plastic components near the UVB fixture periodically for
brittleness or discoloration.

## Measuring UVB output

A standard PAR meter (Apogee, Photobio) does NOT measure
UVB — it measures photosynthetically active radiation
(400–700nm). To measure UVB specifically:

- **Solarmeter 6.2 UVB meter** (~$200): the standard tool
  for measuring UVB in reptile keeping and horticulture.
  Reads in µW/cm². Aim for 30–80 µW/cm² at canopy level
  during supplementation
- **UV index cards** (cheap but imprecise): give a rough
  sense of whether UVB is present but not a quantitative
  measurement

If you're using a reptile bulb at the recommended distance
from a reputable manufacturer, the output is within safe
ranges. The measurement tools are for optimizing, not for
basic safety.

## Common mistakes

1. **Running UVB all day** — more is not better. 4–6 hours
   centered in the light cycle is sufficient. Continuous
   exposure causes UV bleaching and leaf damage that reduce
   quality
2. **Starting UVB too early** — UVB during veg or the first
   two weeks of flower adds stress without trichome benefit.
   Start at week 3 of flower when trichome production begins
3. **Mounting too close** — UVB intensity is extreme at
   short distances. A 10.0 reptile bulb at 6 inches will
   burn leaf tissue. Follow the manufacturer's distance
   recommendations and observe plants daily for bleaching
4. **Expecting miracles** — UVB is a finishing technique
   that enhances trichome production by 10–30% in well-run
   grows. It doesn't fix nutrient problems, bad genetics,
   or poor environmental control. It's the last 10%, not
   the first 90%
5. **Ignoring safety** — UVB causes real human harm. Wear
   eye protection, minimize skin exposure, and put the
   UVB on a separate switch so you can kill it while
   working in the tent
