Sleep is the most common reason people use cannabis medicinally.
And anecdotally, it works — millions of people report falling asleep
faster and sleeping more deeply with cannabis. But the science is
nuanced, and "just smoke an indica" isn't as straightforward as the
internet makes it sound.

## What the research shows

### Short-term sleep effects

Cannabis reliably produces several short-term effects on sleep:

- **Faster sleep onset:** THC reduces the time it takes to fall
  asleep. Multiple studies confirm this, particularly at moderate
  doses
- **Increased deep sleep (stages 3–4):** THC appears to increase
  time spent in slow-wave sleep, the most physically restorative
  sleep stage
- **Reduced REM sleep:** THC suppresses REM (dream) sleep. This is
  why heavy users report not dreaming, and why stopping cannabis
  causes vivid dream rebound
- **Fewer nighttime awakenings:** cannabis users tend to wake up
  less during the night

### The REM question

The reduction in REM sleep is the most debated aspect. REM sleep
is associated with memory consolidation, emotional processing, and
cognitive function. Long-term REM suppression may have consequences
that aren't fully understood yet.

However: for people whose sleep is disrupted by anxiety, pain, or
PTSD-related nightmares, reduced REM (and reduced dreaming) may be
therapeutically beneficial — fewer nightmares, less anxiety-driven
waking.

### Tolerance and long-term use

With regular use, tolerance develops to cannabis's sleep effects:
- **2–4 weeks** of nightly use typically reduces the sleep-onset
  benefit
- **Higher doses** become necessary for the same effect
- **Stopping** after regular use causes rebound insomnia (1–3 weeks
  of worse sleep than baseline) as the endocannabinoid system
  recalibrates

This doesn't mean cannabis can't be used for sleep — it means
intermittent use (3–4 nights per week, not nightly) may maintain
effectiveness longer than daily use.

## Which cannabinoids help with sleep

### THC

- **Primary sedative cannabinoid** at moderate doses
- **Low doses (2.5–5 mg):** mild relaxation, may help with sleep
  onset
- **Moderate doses (5–15 mg):** reliably sedating for most people
- **High doses (15+ mg):** can cause anxiety or racing thoughts in
  some users, which is counterproductive for sleep
- **The curve:** THC's sleep benefit follows an inverted U — too
  little doesn't help, too much can hurt. The sweet spot varies
  per individual

### CBD

- **Not directly sedating** at typical doses — CBD doesn't make you
  drowsy the way THC does
- **Anxiolytic:** CBD reduces anxiety, which indirectly improves
  sleep for people whose insomnia is anxiety-driven
- **Doses for sleep:** 25–50 mg CBD is the range most commonly
  studied for anxiety and sleep improvement
- **Best used with THC:** a 1:1 or 2:1 CBD:THC ratio may produce
  better sleep outcomes than THC alone, with fewer side effects

### CBN

- **The "sleep cannabinoid"** — marketed heavily as sedating
- **The evidence is thin:** there's very little rigorous research
  supporting CBN as a standalone sleep aid. Much of the sleepiness
  associated with "old weed" (which is high in CBN from THC
  degradation) may be due to other compounds or placebo
- **May work synergistically with THC:** some studies suggest CBN
  enhances THC's sedative effects, but the evidence is preliminary
- **Don't pay a premium for CBN products** until the research
  catches up to the marketing

### Myrcene (terpene)

Myrcene is the most commonly cited terpene for sedation. It's found
in high concentrations in many "indica" strains and in hops (hence
the folk remedy of hops tea for sleep).

- **Evidence:** some animal studies support myrcene's sedative and
  muscle-relaxant properties
- **In practice:** strains high in myrcene (Granddaddy Purple, OG
  Kush, Northern Lights) are consistently reported as sedating
- **The entourage effect:** myrcene likely contributes to the overall
  sedative profile of a strain alongside THC and other terpenes

## Which strains help with sleep

### What to look for

Forget the "indica vs. sativa" label — it's unreliable for
predicting effects. Instead, look for:

1. **High THC (20%+)** with moderate total cannabinoid content
2. **High myrcene** in the terpene profile
3. **Presence of linalool** (another sedative terpene, also found
   in lavender)
4. **Dense, resinous buds** — typically correlate with higher
   terpene content

### Consistently sedating strains

From our [strain library](/strains):

- **Granddaddy Purple** — the classic sleep strain. High myrcene,
  strong body effect, rarely causes anxiety
- **Northern Lights** — sedating without excessive cerebral activity.
  Smooth, calming descent into sleep
- **Hindu Kush** — pure indica landrace. Deep body relaxation with
  minimal mental stimulation
- **Bubba Kush** — heavy, narcotic body effect. One of the most
  consistently sedating strains available
- **Purple Punch** — sweet, grape-flavored, powerfully sedating.
  High myrcene and linalool
- **9 Pound Hammer** — named for its knock-out effect. Not subtle

### Strains to avoid for sleep

- **Sativa-dominant strains** (Durban Poison, Jack Herer, Super
  Silver Haze) — energizing, cerebral, can increase mental activity
  when you need to quiet the mind
- **High-THC without myrcene** — some potent strains (Sour Diesel,
  Green Crack) are stimulating despite high THC because their
  terpene profile is dominated by limonene and pinene (energizing
  terpenes)
- **Pure CBD strains** (below 5% THC) — anxiolytic but not sedating
  enough for most insomnia

## Timing and method

### When to consume

- **30–60 minutes before desired sleep** for smoked/vaped cannabis
  (onset: 5–15 minutes, peak: 30–60 minutes)
- **1–2 hours before** for edibles (onset: 30–90 minutes, peak:
  2–3 hours)
- **Don't consume too early** — the sedative peak passes, and you
  may be awake and alert again by bedtime

### Method matters

| Method | Onset | Duration | Best for |
|--------|-------|----------|----------|
| Smoking/vaping | 5–15 min | 2–3 hours | Falling asleep (fast onset) |
| Edibles | 30–90 min | 5–8 hours | Staying asleep (sustained release) |
| Tincture (sublingual) | 15–30 min | 3–5 hours | Balance of both |
| Topical | N/A | N/A | Not effective for sleep (doesn't enter bloodstream significantly) |

**For staying asleep all night:** edibles are often more effective
than smoking because the sustained release of THC keeps blood levels
above the sedation threshold for 5–8 hours. Smoked cannabis peaks
and wears off, and some users wake up 3–4 hours later.

## Dose recommendations

### Start low

| Experience level | Smoked/vaped | Edible |
|-----------------|-------------|--------|
| New user | 1–2 hits, wait 15 min | 2.5–5 mg THC |
| Occasional user | 2–4 hits | 5–10 mg THC |
| Regular user | Normal session | 10–25 mg THC |

### Finding your dose

Cannabis for sleep is one area where more is definitively not
better. High doses can cause:
- Racing thoughts and anxiety (the opposite of sleep)
- Next-morning grogginess
- Tolerance development (requiring higher doses over time)

**The optimal sleep dose is the lowest dose that reliably produces
drowsiness within 30–60 minutes.** If you're increasing your dose
frequently, consider taking tolerance breaks.

## Common mistakes

1. **Using stimulating strains** — not all cannabis is sedating.
   Check the terpene profile, not just the indica/sativa label
2. **Consuming too late** — smoking right before closing your eyes
   means peak effects hit as you're trying to fall asleep. Consume
   30–60 minutes before bed to let the sedation build naturally
3. **Using edibles without testing the dose first** — an unexpectedly
   strong edible causes anxiety, not sleep. Test your dose on a
   weekend night first
4. **Nightly use without breaks** — tolerance develops. Take 2–3
   nights off per week if possible to maintain effectiveness
5. **Ignoring sleep hygiene** — cannabis doesn't fix a bright room,
   phone scrolling in bed, or caffeine at 6 PM. Combine cannabis
   with good sleep practices for best results
