Cannabis is legal to grow in New York State and an increasing number
of jurisdictions. But legal doesn't mean public. You may have
landlords, HOAs, nosy neighbors, or simply a preference for privacy.
And even in fully legal states, an obvious grow operation invites
theft — cannabis plants are valuable, and a house known to contain
them is a target.

This guide covers practical discretion for home growers — not
paranoid security theater, but sensible steps to keep your hobby
private.

## Smell control

Smell is the single biggest exposure risk. A flowering cannabis plant
in week 6 can fill an entire house with a distinctive aroma that
carries outdoors. Your neighbors will notice.

### Carbon filtration (mandatory)

A properly sized carbon filter on your exhaust fan eliminates 95%+
of odor. This is non-negotiable for discrete indoor growing:

- Run the carbon filter from day one — not just when you notice
  smell. By the time you notice, others have too
- Size the filter to match your fan's CFM rating
- Replace the filter when you start smelling cannabis outside the
  tent (typically every 12–18 months)

See our
[ventilation guide](/guides/ventilation-odor-control) for complete
filter setup.

### Negative pressure (mandatory)

Your tent must maintain negative pressure — the walls should suck
inward slightly, not puff outward. Negative pressure ensures ALL
air exits through the carbon filter. Positive pressure pushes
unfiltered air out through zipper gaps and vent seams, carrying
smell into the room.

### Supplementary odor control

- **ONA gel** outside the tent (never inside — it affects terpene
  development) neutralizes residual odor in the room
- **Seal the room** the tent is in — tape over door gaps if it leads
  to common areas
- **Minimize tent openings** during peak flower (weeks 5–8). Every
  time you unzip the tent, a burst of unfiltered, highly aromatic air
  escapes

### Harvest and drying

The highest-smell period is harvest day and the first 3–4 days of
drying. If your drying space isn't filtered:
- Dry inside the grow tent (with the carbon filter still running)
- Or set up a secondary carbon filter in the drying space

## Light leaks (outward)

Indoor grow lights are intense. At night, a 400W LED inside a thin
tent in a room with a window is visible from outside as an
unmistakable glow.

### Prevention

- **Close curtains or blinds** on any window in the room containing
  the tent
- **Check for light leaks** at night from outside — walk around the
  exterior of your house and look for glow from the grow room
- **Use a light schedule that aligns with normal household lighting**
  — lights on from 6 AM to midnight looks like normal home lighting
  from the outside. Lights on from midnight to 6 PM raises questions
- **Seal tent zippers and ports** — light leaking from the tent into
  the room is less of a security issue, but a light leak into the
  tent during the dark period can cause hermaphroditism

## Noise

Grow equipment makes noise. An inline fan runs 24/7. Air pumps buzz.
Oscillating fans hum. Individually, they're quiet. Together, they
create a constant drone that's audible through walls.

### Noise reduction

- **AC Infinity fans** are the quietest inline fans available —
  significantly quieter than budget fans
- **Insulated ducting** reduces fan noise transmitted through the
  duct
- **Isolation mount** the fan (hang it from bungee cords instead of
  rigid mounting) to prevent vibration transfer to the structure
- **Place air pumps (hydro) on foam or rubber** to dampen vibration
- **Speed controllers** — running a fan at 50% is dramatically
  quieter than 100%
- **Close the door** to the grow room. A closed interior door reduces
  noise by 15–20 dB

### In apartments

Noise is a bigger concern in multi-unit buildings. Your exhaust fan
may be audible through shared walls. In addition to the above:
- Run the fan on the lowest effective speed
- Add acoustic ducting (foam-lined duct specifically for noise
  reduction)
- Don't place the tent against a shared wall — vibrations transfer

## Power consumption

A grow tent adds 400–800W of continuous power consumption to your
electricity bill. This shows up as a sudden increase in kWh usage
starting the month you begin growing.

### How much does it cost?

| Setup | Wattage | Monthly cost (at $0.15/kWh) |
|-------|---------|---------------------------|
| Small tent (2×2, 100W LED) | ~150W | ~$16/month |
| Medium tent (4×4, 300W LED) | ~450W | ~$49/month |
| Large tent (4×4, 480W LED + dehumidifier) | ~700W | ~$76/month |
| Two tents (veg + flower) | ~900W | ~$98/month |

### Minimizing the footprint

- **LED instead of HPS** — LEDs use 30–50% less electricity for the
  same light output. A 300W LED replaces a 600W HPS
- **Dim the light when possible** — seedlings and early veg need
  25–50% intensity. Running at 50% saves half the light's power draw
- **Timer efficiency** — veg at 18/6 uses 25% less electricity than
  24/0 with negligible growth difference

### Does the power company care?

In legal states, no. Utility companies see thousands of accounts with
higher-than-average consumption (space heaters, hot tubs, EV
chargers, home servers). A single grow tent doesn't trigger anomaly
detection. Multiple large tents or a commercial-scale operation is
different, but that's beyond home growing.

## Digital footprint

### What to consider

- **Purchases:** growing equipment purchased online creates a record.
  If discretion matters, buy from brick-and-mortar garden stores or
  hydroponics shops with cash
- **Social media:** don't post photos of your grow with location
  information. Metadata in phone photos includes GPS coordinates.
  If you share grow photos online, strip metadata first (most social
  platforms do this automatically, but not all)
- **Delivery packages:** a steady stream of boxes from hydroponic
  retailers is noticeable. Have packages delivered to a P.O. box or
  alternate address if you prefer discretion

### What not to worry about

- **Buying seeds online** is legal in most jurisdictions (seeds are
  sold as "novelty" or "souvenir" items where growing is restricted)
- **Searching for growing information** is not suspicious in any
  legal context. You're reading a growing guide right now
- **Buying equipment** at garden stores is normal. Most of this
  equipment is used for legal gardening (tomatoes, peppers, herbs)

## Physical security

### Theft prevention

Cannabis plants are valuable. A single harvest can represent $1,000+
in retail-value flower. If someone knows you're growing, your home
becomes a target.

- **Don't tell people you grow.** This is the single most effective
  security measure. Every person who knows is a potential leak
- **Don't discuss quantities or harvest timing** with anyone who
  doesn't need to know
- **Store harvested cannabis in a safe or locked container** — a
  mason jar in a kitchen cabinet is not secure if someone enters your
  home
- **Standard home security** (locked doors, window locks, exterior
  lighting, security cameras) applies to growers as much as anyone

### Apartment considerations

Many leases prohibit cannabis growing even in legal states. Before
setting up a grow in a rented space:
- Read your lease for cultivation restrictions
- Understand the risk: a lease violation can result in eviction even
  if growing is legal
- If your lease is silent on the topic, consider the landlord's
  likely position before committing to a permanent setup

## Common mistakes

1. **Skipping the carbon filter "until flower"** — by the time
   flower smell is noticeable, your neighbors have been smelling veg
   terpenes for weeks. Install from day one
2. **Telling friends** — every person who knows increases your
   exposure. Grow quietly, share the results generously, but keep
   the operation private
3. **Visible equipment** — a tent in the living room visible through
   a window, or a stack of hydroponics boxes by the recycling bin.
   Set up in an interior room without exterior visibility
4. **Social media with identifying info** — a grow photo with your
   face, your pet, or a visible address is a security risk. Keep
   your grow and your identity separate online
5. **No lock on the grow room** — if anyone has access to your home
   (guests, maintenance, landlord), a lock on the grow room door
   prevents casual discovery
