Cannabis leaves are constantly broadcasting the plant's health
status. Color, texture, shape, and position all change in
specific ways in response to specific problems. Learning to
read these signals turns you from a grower who reacts to
problems after damage is done into one who catches them early
enough to prevent yield loss.

This isn't about memorizing every possible deficiency. It's
about learning the 10 most common leaf signals — the ones
you'll actually see in a home grow — and knowing what to do
when you see them.

## How to read a leaf

### Location matters

**Where on the plant** the symptom appears narrows the
diagnosis immediately:

| Location | Nutrients affected | Why |
|----------|-------------------|-----|
| Lower/older leaves | Mobile nutrients: N, P, K, Mg | Mobile nutrients can be relocated from old leaves to new growth — the plant sacrifices old leaves first |
| Upper/new leaves | Immobile nutrients: Ca, Fe, Mn, Zn, S | Immobile nutrients can't be moved — new growth shows deficiency first because it has no reserve |
| All leaves uniformly | Environmental stress (light, heat, pH) | Affects the whole plant simultaneously |
| Leaf tips only | Nutrient burn, salt buildup | Tips are the most distal point — stress shows there first |
| Leaf edges | Potassium deficiency, heat stress | Edge symptoms are characteristic of specific stresses |

### Speed matters

**How fast** the symptom appeared:

- **Overnight/sudden:** environmental — light burn, heat stress,
  overwatering, chemical burn from a foliar spray
- **Over 3–7 days:** nutrient issue — deficiency or toxicity
  developing as reserves deplete or accumulate
- **Gradual over weeks:** pH drift, root deterioration, slow
  nutrient depletion, age-related (natural lower-leaf yellowing
  in late flower)

## The 10 most common leaf signals

### 1. Uniform lower-leaf yellowing

**What it looks like:** bottom leaves turn evenly yellow (pale
green → yellow → drop off). The yellowing starts at the oldest
leaves and progresses upward.

**Diagnosis: nitrogen deficiency.** Nitrogen is mobile — the
plant pulls it from old leaves to feed new growth. This is the
single most common nutrient deficiency in cannabis.

**When it's normal:** during late flower (weeks 6–8), some
lower-leaf yellowing is expected as the plant redirects
resources to buds. This is natural senescence, not a problem.

**When it's a problem:** during veg or early flower, uniform
lower yellowing means the plant needs more nitrogen.

**Fix:** increase nitrogen feeding. If already feeding, check
pH (nitrogen availability drops below pH 5.5 and above 7.0).

### 2. Yellow leaves with green veins (interveinal chlorosis)

**What it looks like:** the tissue between the veins turns
yellow while the veins themselves stay green, creating a
striped or net-like pattern.

**On lower leaves: magnesium deficiency.** Magnesium is mobile.
The plant pulls it from old leaves, leaving the characteristic
interveinal pattern.

**Fix:** Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate) at 1 tsp per gallon in
the next watering. Or add cal-mag supplement. Check pH — Mg
is locked out below 5.8.

**On upper/new leaves: iron deficiency.** Iron is immobile.
New growth shows the yellow-with-green-veins pattern.

**Fix:** check pH first (iron locks out above 6.5 in soil, 6.0
in hydro). If pH is correct, apply chelated iron foliar spray
for fastest correction.

### 3. Brown spots on leaves

**What it looks like:** brown or rust-colored spots scattered
across leaf surfaces, often starting on lower leaves.

**Diagnosis depends on pattern:**

- **Random brown spots, lower leaves first:** calcium deficiency.
  Calcium is immobile — but the spots appear on lower leaves
  because those leaves are older and have experienced more
  transpiration cycles concentrating the deficiency effect.
  Fix: cal-mag supplement. Check pH (Ca locks out below 6.0)
- **Spots with yellow halos:** potassium deficiency (lower
  leaves) or a fungal infection (any leaf, especially in high
  humidity). If spots are geometric and spread concentrically,
  suspect fungus. If irregular and scattered, suspect K
  deficiency
- **Spots at leaf tips and edges first:** nutrient burn from
  too-concentrated feeding. The brown tips are "burnt"
  tissue. Fix: flush with plain water, reduce feeding strength

### 4. Leaf tips burning (crispy brown tips)

**What it looks like:** the very tips of leaves turn brown and
crispy, sometimes with a yellow band behind the brown.

**Diagnosis: nutrient burn.** The plant is receiving more
dissolved minerals than it can process. The excess accumulates
at the leaf tips (the furthest point from the roots) and
burns the tissue.

**Severity:**
- Tips only: mild burn. Back off feeding by 10–20%
- Tips + first ¼ inch: moderate burn. Flush with plain pH'd
  water, then resume feeding at 50–75% strength
- Tips + edges + significant brown: severe burn. Full flush
  needed. Major reduction in feeding strength

**Note:** tiny brown tips (1–2mm) are actually ideal — they
indicate you're feeding at the maximum the plant can handle.
Many experienced growers aim for "just barely burnt tips" as
the sweet spot.

### 5. Leaves curling upward ("taco" or "canoe")

**What it looks like:** leaf edges fold upward, making the
leaf look like a taco or a canoe viewed from above.

**Diagnosis: heat stress or light stress.** The leaf is folding
to reduce its exposed surface area — a survival response to
excessive heat or light energy.

**Fix:**
- Check temperature at canopy level. If above 82°F, improve
  ventilation or raise the light
- If temperature is fine, check light intensity. The light may
  be too close or too intense. Raise it 4–6 inches
- Check VPD — low humidity combined with high temperature
  compounds the stress

### 6. Leaves curling downward ("clawing")

**What it looks like:** leaf tips and edges curl downward,
giving the leaves a claw-like appearance.

**Diagnosis depends on leaf color:**

- **Dark green + clawing:** nitrogen toxicity. Too much
  nitrogen causes dark, waxy leaves that claw downward.
  Fix: flush and reduce nitrogen in feed
- **Normal green + clawing:** overwatering. The roots are
  oxygen-starved and the plant can't maintain turgor properly.
  Fix: let the medium dry out. Water less frequently
- **Clawing on new growth only:** possible root zone
  temperature issue or pH fluctuation affecting specific
  nutrient uptake

### 7. Wilting (drooping leaves)

**What it looks like:** all leaves droop simultaneously. The
plant looks sad and limp.

**Diagnosis:**

- **Soil is wet + wilting:** overwatering. Roots are
  suffocating. DO NOT water more. Let the medium dry out
- **Soil is dry + wilting:** underwatering. Water immediately.
  The plant will recover within 1–4 hours
- **Soil moisture is fine + wilting:** root damage (root rot,
  extreme pH, salt buildup). Check roots — healthy roots are
  white. Brown, slimy roots indicate rot

**The weight test:** lift the pot. Heavy = wet = don't water.
Light = dry = water. This is more reliable than looking at
the soil surface.

### 8. Purple or red stems and petioles

**What it looks like:** stems and leaf stalks turn purple or
reddish instead of green.

**Diagnosis:**
- **Plant is healthy, growing well, temps are normal:** genetics.
  Many strains have naturally purple stems. Not a problem
- **Plant is slow, cool temperatures (below 68°F):** phosphorus
  lockout. Cold roots can't absorb phosphorus efficiently.
  Fix: warm the root zone (heat mat, raise pot off cold floor)
- **Purple stems + slow growth + no cold stress:** check pH.
  Phosphorus is locked out above pH 7.0 in soil

### 9. Yellow or brown edges (leaf margin damage)

**What it looks like:** leaf edges turn yellow, then brown and
crispy, progressing inward from the margin.

**On lower leaves first: potassium deficiency.** K is mobile
and shows deficiency on older leaves. The leaf edges burn
because potassium regulates water pressure in cells, and edge
cells lose turgor first.

**Fix:** increase potassium in the feed. Most bloom nutrients
are high in K. Check pH — K is less available above 7.0.

**On all leaves simultaneously: heat stress.** Combined with
low humidity, heat causes rapid transpiration that damages
leaf margins.

### 10. Pale new growth (light green or yellow new leaves)

**What it looks like:** new growth at the top of the plant
comes in noticeably lighter than the rest of the plant.

**Diagnosis:**
- **Light yellow new growth, older leaves normal:** sulfur
  deficiency (rare) or iron deficiency. Check pH first — both
  lock out at high pH. Foliar iron spray for fastest correction
- **Pale lime-green new growth, slight stretch:** nitrogen
  deficiency beginning. The plant is redirecting all available
  N to the newest growth but there isn't enough. Increase N
- **White/bleached new growth:** light burn. The newest growth
  closest to the light is being bleached by excessive PPFD.
  Raise the light immediately

## The diagnostic process

When you see a leaf problem:

1. **Where is it?** Top (immobile nutrient) or bottom
   (mobile nutrient) or everywhere (environment)?
2. **How fast did it appear?** Sudden (environment) or gradual
   (nutrient)?
3. **What color is it?** Yellow (N, Mg, Fe, S), brown spots
   (Ca, K, burn), dark green (N toxicity), purple (P, genetics)
4. **Check pH.** Most "deficiencies" are actually pH lockout.
   The nutrient is in the soil but the pH prevents absorption.
   Fix pH before adding more nutrients
5. **Check environment.** Temperature, humidity, light distance,
   and watering frequency cause most symptoms that look like
   nutrient problems but aren't

**The 80/20 rule:** 80% of leaf problems in home grows are
caused by pH issues, overwatering, or light stress. Check
those three before reaching for supplements.

## Common mistakes

1. **Treating symptoms without checking pH** — adding cal-mag
   when calcium is locked out by low pH just adds more minerals
   the plant can't absorb. Fix pH first, then see if the
   symptom resolves before supplementing
2. **Reacting to every yellow leaf** — some lower-leaf
   yellowing during flower is normal. Don't chase every yellow
   leaf with nitrogen — excess N during flower reduces bud
   quality
3. **Diagnosing from a single leaf** — one weird leaf isn't a
   diagnosis. A pattern across multiple leaves IS. Look at the
   whole plant, not one leaf
4. **Overreacting** — the most common response to a leaf
   symptom is to add something. The better first response is
   usually to check something (pH, temperature, watering
   frequency). Adding nutrients to a locked-out plant makes
   the problem worse
