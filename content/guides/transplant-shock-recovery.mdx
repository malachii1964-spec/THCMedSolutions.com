Every time you move a cannabis plant from one container to another,
the roots get disturbed. The plant responds by pausing growth — 
sometimes for a day, sometimes for a week. That pause is transplant
shock. It's not a disease. It's the plant redirecting energy from
above-ground growth to root repair and exploration.

Some shock is unavoidable. The goal isn't zero stress — it's
minimizing the stress and shortening the recovery window so the
plant gets back to growing as fast as possible.

## Why transplant at all?

Starting a seedling in a small pot and moving it to a larger one
(called "potting up") has real benefits:

- **Faster root establishment.** A seedling in a 1-gallon pot fills
  the root zone quickly. Full root zones drink faster, which means
  faster wet/dry cycles, which stimulates root growth. A seedling
  in a 5-gallon pot sits in wet soil for days because the tiny root
  system can't absorb the water — that invites root rot and slow
  growth
- **Water control.** Small pots dry out predictably. You can water
  a seedling in a solo cup every 1–2 days and dial in the wet/dry
  cycle. A 5-gallon pot with a seedling is a guessing game
- **Space efficiency.** Seedlings in small containers take up less
  space under a small light while you veg larger plants under the
  main light

### The common progression

| Stage | Container | Duration |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Germination | Rapid Rooter, Jiffy pellet, or solo cup | Days 1–7 |
| Seedling | Solo cup or 1-gallon pot | Days 7–21 |
| Early veg | 1–3 gallon pot | Days 21–35 |
| Final pot | 3–7 gallon pot (flower in this) | Day 35+ through harvest |

### The alternative: start in the final pot

Some growers skip transplanting entirely by starting the seed in the
final 5-gallon pot. This works — it eliminates transplant shock
completely. The trade-off is slower early growth (the seedling sits in
wet soil longer) and less control over the root development timeline.
For beginners who are nervous about transplanting, this is a valid
approach.

**Autoflowers** should almost always start in their final pot.
Autoflowers have a fixed vegetative period — they can't afford the
1–5 day growth pause from transplant shock.

## Preventing transplant shock

### Water before transplanting

Water the plant in its current container 1–2 hours before
transplanting. A moist root ball holds together when you remove it
from the pot. A dry root ball crumbles, exposing and damaging fine
root hairs. An overly wet root ball is heavy and falls apart.

### Prepare the new container first

1. Fill the new pot 2/3 full with your medium (soil, coco, etc.)
2. Dig a hole in the center the size of the old container
3. Pre-moisten the medium lightly — not soaked, just damp
4. The medium in the new pot should be at room temperature, not cold

### The transfer

1. **Invert the old pot** and gently squeeze the sides to loosen the
   root ball
2. **Support the stem** between two fingers at soil level — the stem,
   not the leaves
3. **Slide the root ball out** in one piece. If it doesn't come
   easily, squeeze the pot sides again. Don't pull on the stem
4. **Place the root ball** in the prepared hole. The top of the old
   soil level should sit about 0.5–1 inch below the rim of the new
   pot
5. **Fill around the root ball** with new medium, gently pressing
   to eliminate air pockets. Don't pack it hard
6. **Water the new pot** until you see a small amount of runoff.
   This settles the medium around the root ball and eliminates
   remaining air pockets

### Timing matters

- **Time of day:** transplant at the start of the light cycle or
  during lights-off. The plant has the full day/night cycle to begin
  recovery before facing intense light
- **Plant readiness:** transplant when roots are visible at the
  bottom drainage holes, or when the plant drinks its water within
  24 hours (signs the root zone is full)
- **Don't transplant during stress.** If the plant is already dealing
  with a nutrient problem, pest issue, or environmental stress, fix
  that first. Transplanting an already-stressed plant compounds the
  problem

### Mycorrhizae: the transplant insurance

Mycorrhizal fungi form a symbiotic relationship with cannabis roots,
dramatically increasing the root system's effective surface area.
Adding mycorrhizae during transplanting speeds root establishment in
the new container.

**How to use it:**
- Sprinkle granular mycorrhizae directly on the root ball before
  placing it in the new pot
- Or mix it into the top layer of the new medium where the roots
  will grow into it
- Products: Great White, Mykos, Dynomyco

## Recognizing transplant shock

After transplanting, watch for these signs over the next 1–5 days:

### Mild shock (normal, expected)

- **Slight wilting** — leaves droop slightly for 12–24 hours. The
  root system hasn't established contact with the new medium yet
- **Growth pause** — no visible growth for 1–3 days. The plant is
  growing roots, not leaves
- **Slightly pale color** — nutrient uptake slows while roots adjust

**This is fine.** Don't do anything. The plant recovers on its own
in 2–4 days.

### Moderate shock (concerning but manageable)

- **Significant wilting** — leaves droop heavily, especially the
  lower ones
- **Yellowing lower leaves** — the plant sacrifices old leaves to
  conserve energy for root recovery
- **Growth pause exceeding 5 days** — the plant is struggling to
  re-establish root function

**Action:** reduce light intensity by 25% for 2–3 days. Ensure the
medium is moist (not wet, not dry). Don't add nutrients for the first
watering after transplant — plain water only.

### Severe shock (needs intervention)

- **Complete wilt** — the entire plant collapses, every leaf pointing
  straight down
- **Stem weakness** — the stem goes soft at the soil line
- **No recovery after 48 hours** of proper conditions

**Action:** 
1. Move to a shaded area or dim the light to 25% intensity
2. Create a humidity dome (clear plastic bag or cup over the plant,
   not touching the leaves) to reduce transpiration demand
3. Mist the leaves lightly — the plant can absorb some water through
   the leaves while the roots recover
4. Do NOT water heavily — the roots can't absorb it, and sitting in
   wet medium invites rot
5. Wait 3–5 days. Most plants recover. If the stem collapses at the
   soil line, the plant may not survive

## Recovery timeline

| Shock severity | Growth pause | Full recovery |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Mild | 1–2 days | 3–4 days |
| Moderate | 3–5 days | 5–10 days |
| Severe | 5–10 days | 10–14 days (if it survives) |

## Transplanting into flower

**Don't.** Transplanting during flower causes a growth pause that the
plant can't afford — every day of flower is a day of bud development
that can't be recovered. The plant should be in its final container
at least 1 week before the flip to 12/12, ideally 2 weeks.

If a plant becomes severely rootbound during flower (roots circling
the bottom of the pot, water running straight through), your options
are limited:
- **Top-dress with fresh soil** — add a layer of fresh, nutrient-rich
  soil on top
- **Root prune by scoring** — if using a hard pot, score the outer
  root ball with a knife to encourage new growth. This is emergency-
  level intervention

## Fabric pots: built-in transplant prevention

Fabric pots air-prune roots — when root tips reach the fabric wall,
they dry out and stop growing, prompting the plant to grow new roots
from the interior. This prevents root circling (roots wrapping around
the bottom of the pot) and means the plant is less likely to become
severely rootbound.

Many growers using fabric pots skip the intermediate transplant
step and go directly from a solo cup to the final 5-gallon fabric
pot. The air pruning keeps roots healthy even if the root system
hasn't filled the pot yet.

## Common mistakes

1. **Pulling the plant out by the stem** — this tears fine root
   hairs. Squeeze the pot, invert, and let gravity help
2. **Transplanting into dry medium** — the root ball sits in dry
   soil, losing moisture rapidly. Pre-moisten the new pot
3. **Burying the stem too deep** — a little burying (up to the
   cotyledons for stretched seedlings) is fine. Burying mature
   stem sections invites rot
4. **Feeding immediately after transplant** — the roots are damaged
   and can't efficiently absorb nutrients. Use plain water for the
   first watering. Resume feeding after you see new growth (a sign
   the roots have recovered)
5. **Transplanting too often** — each transplant costs 2–5 days of
   growth. Two transplants (solo cup → 1 gal → final pot) is
   standard. Three is the maximum. More is counterproductive
