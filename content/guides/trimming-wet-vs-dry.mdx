Trimming is the difference between homegrown that looks homegrown
and homegrown that looks dispensary. A well-trimmed bud reveals
trichome coverage, tightens the bud's shape, and removes the harsh-
smoking fan leaves and sugar leaves that dilute the flavor. A
poorly trimmed bud looks like it was torn from the plant and
stuffed in a jar.

The first decision: do you trim wet (immediately after cutting) or
dry (after drying for 7–14 days)?

## Wet trim vs. dry trim

### Wet trimming

**When:** immediately after cutting the plant down, before drying.

**How it works:**
1. Cut the plant at the base
2. Remove individual branches
3. Trim all fan leaves and sugar leaves while the plant is still
   fresh and pliable
4. Hang the trimmed buds to dry

**Advantages:**
- **Faster and easier.** Wet leaves are turgid (stiff and full of
  water), sticking straight out from the bud, making them easy to
  see and cut. Dry leaves curl into the bud and are harder to reach
- **Cleaner cuts.** Scissors move through fresh tissue cleanly
- **Faster drying.** Removing leaves exposes the bud surface,
  reducing drying time by 2–4 days
- **Less space needed.** Trimmed buds take up significantly less
  hanging space than untrimmed whole plants
- **Better in humid environments.** Exposed bud surfaces dry faster,
  reducing mold risk during drying

**Disadvantages:**
- **Potentially faster drying than ideal.** If your drying
  environment is already dry (below 50% RH), wet-trimmed buds can
  dry too fast, resulting in harsh smoke and reduced flavor
- **Stickier work.** Wet trichomes are sticky and coat your scissors,
  gloves, and hands. You'll need to clean scissors frequently with
  isopropyl alcohol

### Dry trimming

**When:** after drying for 7–14 days, when the branches snap
(not bend) and the bud surface feels dry to the touch.

**How it works:**
1. Cut the plant and hang whole branches (or the entire plant) with
   fan leaves still attached
2. Dry for 7–14 days until branches snap
3. Cut buds from branches and trim sugar leaves

**Advantages:**
- **Slower, more controlled drying.** The attached leaves act as a
  moisture buffer, slowing moisture release from the bud interior.
  This produces smoother, more flavorful flower
- **Better terpene preservation.** Slower drying retains volatile
  terpenes that evaporate quickly when buds are exposed
- **Preferred by connoisseurs.** Most premium flower is dry-trimmed
  for flavor quality

**Disadvantages:**
- **More difficult and time-consuming.** Dry leaves curl into the
  bud, are brittle, and break apart instead of cutting cleanly.
  Trimming is slower and less precise
- **More space required.** Whole plants with leaves need significantly
  more hanging space
- **Higher mold risk.** Leaves retain moisture around the bud during
  drying. In humid environments (above 65% RH), dry trimming
  increases Botrytis risk during the drying phase
- **More handling.** The buds are dry and trichomes are brittle —
  more trichomes break off from handling during dry trimming

### Which to choose

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Humid drying environment (>60% RH) | Wet trim (reduces mold risk) |
| Dry drying environment (<50% RH) | Dry trim (leaves slow the drying) |
| Limited hanging space | Wet trim (smaller footprint) |
| Maximum flavor priority | Dry trim (slower = more terps) |
| Large harvest, limited time | Wet trim (faster processing) |
| First-time grower | Wet trim (easier, less can go wrong) |
| Experienced, dialed-in drying room | Dry trim |

**The compromise: partial wet trim.** Remove large fan leaves
immediately (wet), leave the small sugar leaves, and trim those
after drying. This gives you the drying benefit of some leaf
coverage without the difficulty of fully dry trimming.

## Trimming technique

### Tools

- **Trimming scissors:** spring-loaded, curved-tip scissors designed
  for cannabis trimming. The curve lets you follow the bud's contour.
  Brands: Chikamasa, Fiskars Softouch, Gonicc
- **Straight scissors or shears:** for cutting branches and removing
  large fan leaves
- **Gloves:** nitrile or latex. Trichomes coat your skin and are
  difficult to remove. Gloves also keep oils from your hands off the
  buds
- **Isopropyl alcohol (90%+):** for cleaning scissors when they gum
  up (every 5–10 minutes during wet trimming)
- **Trim tray:** a tray with a screen to catch trichomes (kief) that
  fall during trimming. Worth saving

### The technique

**For each bud:**

1. **Remove the stem** below the bud to the first branch junction.
   Leave a short "handle" of stem to hold the bud by
2. **Remove fan leaves** — these are the large leaves with long
   petioles (stems). Cut them flush where the petiole meets the bud.
   No ragged edges
3. **Shape the sugar leaves** — sugar leaves are the small,
   trichome-covered leaves embedded in the bud. Three approaches:
   - **Close trim:** cut sugar leaves flush with the bud surface.
     Produces the tightest, most manicured look (dispensary style).
     Removes some trichomes with the leaves
   - **Medium trim:** trim sugar leaves to about half their length.
     Leaves a light frosting of sugar leaf tips covering the bud.
     Good balance of appearance and trichome preservation
   - **Loose trim:** leave most sugar leaves intact, only removing
     the largest or those without visible trichomes. Lower bag
     appeal but maximum trichome retention
4. **Shape the bud** — rotate it in your fingers and trim any
   protruding leaf material to create a uniform shape. The goal is
   a smooth, rounded surface with no "crow's feet" (leaf stems
   sticking out)
5. **Don't squeeze** the bud while trimming — handle it by the stem
   and touch the bud surface as little as possible. Every touch
   knocks off trichomes

### Trim speed

- **Beginner:** 1–2 ounces per hour
- **Experienced:** 3–4 ounces per hour
- **Fast (production):** 5+ ounces per hour (sacrificing some
  precision)

Trimming is meditative but exhausting on the hands. Take breaks.
Switch between left and right hands if possible. Stretch your
fingers and wrists.

## What to do with trim

Don't throw away your trim — it's covered in trichomes. Uses:

- **Bubble hash:** agitate trim in ice water to separate trichomes,
  filter through bags. See our
  [hash guide](/guides/bubble-hash-dry-sift)
- **Dry sift:** shake/tumble trim over a fine screen to collect kief
- **Butter/oil infusion:** simmer trim in butter or coconut oil for
  edibles. See our
  [cooking guide](/guides/cannabis-cooking-advanced)
- **Tincture:** soak trim in high-proof alcohol for a liquid extract
- **Rosin press:** press sugar leaves between heated plates for
  solventless concentrate (lower yield than bud rosin but still
  worthwhile). See our [rosin guide](/guides/pressing-rosin-at-home)

Freeze trim immediately after trimming if you're not processing it
right away. Frozen trichomes are more brittle and separate more
easily for hash and dry sift.

## Machine trimming

Electric trim machines (bowl trimmers, tumble trimmers) cut trimming
time dramatically but reduce quality:

- **Loss of trichomes:** the mechanical action knocks off significant
  trichomes
- **Inconsistent cut:** machines can't follow the contour of each
  bud like hand scissors
- **Damaged bud structure:** aggressive machines can shred bud
  surfaces

Machine trimming is acceptable for large harvests where hand
trimming is impractical, but premium flower is always hand-trimmed.
If you use a machine, follow up with a light hand trim to clean up
the rough spots.

## Common mistakes

1. **Over-trimming** — cutting into the bud itself, removing calyxes
   along with sugar leaves. Trim to the bud surface, not into it
2. **Not cleaning scissors** — gunked-up scissors tear leaves instead
   of cutting them, leaving ragged edges. Clean with ISO every 5–10
   minutes
3. **Trimming too fast** — speed comes at the cost of precision.
   Slow down, especially for your best colas
4. **Handling buds excessively** — every touch knocks off trichomes.
   Hold by the stem, not the bud. Don't roll buds between your
   fingers
5. **Throwing away trim** — trim with visible trichomes is valuable.
   Freeze it for later processing into hash, edibles, or tincture
