Bottled nutrients are convenient. You measure, you pour, and the plant
gets what it needs in a form it can absorb immediately. But there's
another approach: build a soil so rich and biologically active that the
plant feeds itself, pulling nutrients from organic amendments as the
soil biology breaks them down.

This is the foundation of organic growing — and it starts with
understanding what each amendment contributes, how they interact, and
how to combine them into a complete soil recipe.

## How organic amendments work

Organic amendments don't feed the plant directly. They feed the soil
biology (bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes), which breaks them down
into plant-available forms. This is a slower, gentler delivery system
than synthetic nutrients — less risk of burn, more consistent feeding,
and a soil that improves over time instead of depleting.

**The cycle:**
1. You add an amendment to the soil (kelp meal, bone meal, etc.)
2. Soil microbes colonize the amendment and begin breaking it down
3. Bacterial and fungal activity converts the raw material into
   plant-available nutrients (nitrate, phosphate, potassium ions)
4. The plant's roots absorb these nutrients via the rhizosphere
   (the thin zone of soil immediately around the roots)
5. As the plant exudes sugars through its roots, it feeds the microbes,
   which break down more amendment — a self-reinforcing loop

**Timeline:** most amendments take 2–4 weeks to become fully available
after mixing into soil. This is why amended soil is often prepared
weeks before planting ("cooking" the soil). See our
[living soil guide](/guides/living-soil-no-till) for a full build.

## The amendments: what each one does

### Worm castings (vermicompost)

**What it is:** worm poop. Earthworms (typically red wigglers) digest
organic matter and excrete castings that are rich in plant-available
nutrients, beneficial microbes, and humic acids.

**What it provides:**
- Balanced NPK (roughly 1-0-0 to 2-1-1, depending on the worm's diet)
- Massive microbial diversity — one gram of castings contains billions
  of beneficial bacteria
- Humic and fulvic acids that improve nutrient uptake

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 15–30% by volume as a base ingredient
- Top-dress with ¼ inch layer every 2–4 weeks as an ongoing feed
- Brew into compost tea for a liquid microbial inoculant

**Can you overdo it?** Almost impossible. Worm castings are mild enough
to use straight without burning plants. This is the single safest and
most universally useful amendment.

### Kelp meal

**What it is:** dried, ground seaweed (typically Ascophyllum nodosum).

**What it provides:**
- Potassium (K) — the primary macronutrient contribution
- Over 60 trace minerals and micronutrients
- Natural growth hormones (cytokinins, auxins) that promote root growth,
  stress resistance, and overall vigor
- Alginic acid, which improves soil structure

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Top-dress with 1 tablespoon per gallon of pot size every 3–4 weeks
- Kelp extract (liquid) can be watered in for faster availability

**Best for:** potassium supplementation, micronutrient insurance, stress
resistance. Kelp is the "multivitamin" of soil amendments.

### Bat guano

**What it is:** bat droppings, aged and processed. Available in two
types with very different nutrient profiles.

**High-nitrogen guano (Mexican/Indonesian):**
- NPK roughly 10-3-1
- Dark brown, earthy smell
- Best for vegetative growth
- Use: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil, mixed in or top-dressed

**High-phosphorus guano (Jamaican/cave-aged):**
- NPK roughly 1-10-0
- Lighter color, chalky texture
- Best for flowering (drives bud development)
- Use: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon, mixed in pre-flower or top-dressed
  at the flip

**Caution:** guano is potent. Overuse causes nutrient burn. Start at
the low end and observe. It's also slightly alkaline — in already-high-
pH soils, it can push pH further up.

### Bone meal

**What it is:** finely ground animal bones, steamed or raw.

**What it provides:**
- Phosphorus (P) — typically 3-15-0 or similar
- Calcium — significant amounts, making it partially a cal-mag source
- Slow-release — bone meal breaks down over 2–4 months

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 2–3 tablespoons per gallon during soil preparation
- Not effective as a top-dress mid-grow (too slow to activate from the
  surface)
- Must be mixed into the soil where microbial activity can access it

**Best for:** long-term phosphorus provision throughout the grow. A
single soil mix with bone meal provides P from start to finish without
re-amendment.

**Note:** bone meal works best in slightly acidic soil (pH 6.0–6.5).
In alkaline soil, the phosphorus binds with calcium and becomes less
available.

### Blood meal

**What it is:** dried, powdered animal blood from slaughterhouse
processing.

**What it provides:**
- Nitrogen (N) — typically 12-0-0 or similar. One of the highest-
  nitrogen organic amendments
- Fast-acting compared to other organic sources (available within 1–2
  weeks)

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 1–2 tablespoons per gallon
- Can be top-dressed sparingly as a nitrogen boost during veg
- Use less than you think — blood meal is concentrated and can burn
  if over-applied

**Best for:** quick nitrogen supplementation in veg. If lower leaves
are yellowing and you need an organic N boost, blood meal is the
fastest option.

**Caution:** blood meal can attract animals (dogs, raccoons) in
outdoor grows. It can also lower soil pH slightly, which may be
beneficial in alkaline soils.

### Dolomite lime

**What it is:** ground limestone (calcium magnesium carbonate).

**What it provides:**
- Calcium and magnesium in a slow-release form
- pH buffering — raises and stabilizes soil pH

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 1–2 tablespoons per gallon during soil prep
- Acts as a pH buffer, preventing acidic drift over time
- Not a quick fix — takes 2–4 weeks to fully react with the soil

**Best for:** preventing pH drift in peat-based soils (which acidify
over time) and providing a steady baseline of calcium and magnesium.
Essential in organic soil mixes where you're not adding synthetic
cal-mag.

### Gypsum (calcium sulfate)

**What it is:** a mineral compound (CaSO₄·2H₂O).

**What it provides:**
- Calcium without raising pH (unlike dolomite lime)
- Sulfur, which supports terpene production and overall plant health
- Improves soil structure by reducing compaction

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 1–2 tablespoons per gallon
- Can be top-dressed during flower for a calcium and sulfur boost

**Best for:** calcium supplementation when pH is already in the correct
range and you don't want to raise it further. Also useful in heavy
clay soils to improve drainage.

### Neem meal / neem cake

**What it is:** the pressed residue after neem oil extraction from
neem tree seeds.

**What it provides:**
- Mild NPK (roughly 6-1-2)
- Natural pest deterrent — neem contains azadirachtin, which disrupts
  pest reproduction. Mixing neem meal into soil discourages fungus
  gnats, root aphids, and other soil-dwelling pests
- Soil conditioning

**How to use:**
- Mix into soil at 1–2 tablespoons per gallon
- Top-dress with 1 tablespoon per gallon every 3–4 weeks

**Best for:** dual-purpose nutrient and pest control in organic builds.
The pest deterrent effect makes it especially valuable in outdoor grows
and grows in basements where fungus gnats are common.

### Perlite (not a nutrient, but essential)

**What it is:** expanded volcanic glass — those white, lightweight
pebbles you see in commercial soil mixes.

**What it provides:**
- Zero nutrients — perlite is inert
- Drainage and aeration — prevents soil compaction and ensures roots
  get oxygen

**How to use:**
- Mix into any soil at 20–30% by volume
- More perlite = faster drainage = more frequent watering
- Non-negotiable in any container soil mix. Without adequate perlite,
  organic soil compacts and waterlogging becomes inevitable

## Building a basic amended soil

A simple, effective organic soil recipe:

| Component | Ratio | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Base soil (peat or coco) | 40% | Structure |
| Perlite | 25% | Drainage and aeration |
| Worm castings | 25% | Nutrients, microbes, humic acids |
| Compost (quality garden compost) | 10% | Additional nutrients and biology |

**Plus amendments per gallon of mix:**
- Kelp meal: 1 tbsp
- Bone meal: 2 tbsp
- Blood meal: 1 tbsp (or neem meal for dual-purpose)
- Dolomite lime: 1 tbsp (if using peat base; skip for coco)
- Gypsum: ½ tbsp

**The cook:** mix all ingredients, moisten evenly (the soil should
feel like a wrung-out sponge), and let sit in a covered container for
2–4 weeks. The microbial activity during this period breaks down the
amendments into plant-available forms and balances the pH. Stir the
mix once a week.

After cooking, the soil is ready to plant in directly. Many growers
don't feed any additional nutrients for the first 4–6 weeks — the
amended soil provides everything.

## The amendment calendar

For a full grow cycle in amended soil:

| Timing | Amendment | Purpose |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Pre-plant (2–4 weeks before) | Full soil mix with all amendments | Complete nutrient base |
| Week 3–4 of veg | Top-dress worm castings + kelp meal | Boost N and K for vigorous growth |
| Week 1 of flower (the flip) | Top-dress bat guano (high-P) + kelp | Transition to P-heavy for bud development |
| Week 4–5 of flower | Top-dress worm castings + kelp | Mid-flower nutrient refresh |
| Week 7+ of flower | Nothing — let the soil finish what it started | Plant draws from reserves; natural fade |

## Common mistakes

1. **Over-amending** — more is not better. Excessive blood meal or
   guano causes nitrogen toxicity. Start conservative; the soil biology
   makes nutrients available gradually
2. **Not cooking the soil** — raw amendments can burn seedlings. The
   2–4 week cook lets microbial activity balance the nutrient load
3. **Skipping perlite** — amended organic soil is dense. Without
   adequate perlite, it compacts, holds too much water, and suffocates
   roots
4. **Mixing incompatible amendments** — bone meal (alkaline) and sulfur
   (acidifying) in the same mix can cause pH chaos. Use dolomite for
   gentle pH buffering; don't try to force pH with concentrated
   amendments
5. **Forgetting the biology** — amendments don't work without soil
   microbes. If you're starting with sterile or depleted soil, add
   microbial inoculant (Recharge, Great White, or homemade compost tea)
   to kickstart the biology
