Few topics in cannabis growing trigger more debate than flushing. Forums are
full of growers who swear pre-harvest flushing gives smoother, cleaner smoke
— and an equal number who call it pointless bro-science. Let's look at what
the actual research says, when flushing is useful, and when it's a waste of
water and time.

## What is flushing?

Flushing means feeding a plant plain pH'd water (no nutrients) for some
period before harvest. The claim is that this forces the plant to "use up"
stored nutrients in the leaves and buds, producing a cleaner final product
that burns to white ash and tastes smoother.

**Two kinds of flushing:**

1. **Pre-harvest flush** — plain water for 1–14 days before chop (the
   controversial one)
2. **Corrective flush** — running heavy plain water through the medium to
   wash out excess salts when you've overfed (this one is universally
   accepted as useful)

This guide focuses on pre-harvest flushing, since corrective flushing is
just a standard rescue technique that nobody argues about.

## The RX Green Technologies study (2020)

The most cited actual research on pre-harvest flushing comes from
RX Green Technologies, published in cooperation with researchers who
conducted a controlled trial with Cherry Diesel grown in coco coir.

**Setup:** identical clones, identical environment, four groups flushed at
0, 7, 10, and 14 days before harvest with plain water.

**Results:**

| Metric | 0 days (no flush) | 7 days | 10 days | 14 days |
|--------|-------------------|--------|---------|---------|
| Yield | No significant difference across all groups |
| THC | No significant difference |
| Terpenes | No significant difference |
| Mineral content in buds | No significant difference |
| Blind taste panel | **Preferred the 0-day (unflushed)** |

The unflushed group actually won the blind taste test — the opposite of
what flushing advocates predict.

**Important caveats:**

- One study, one strain, one medium (coco coir), one facility
- Sample sizes were small
- The study doesn't prove flushing *never* matters — it proves it
  didn't matter under these specific conditions

## Why the bro-science persists

Flushing "works" in anecdotal experience because of two confounding
factors:

### 1. Confirmation bias + poor curing

Bad-tasting cannabis is almost always a curing problem, not a flushing
problem. A well-cured flush and a well-cured non-flush taste identical.
A poorly cured flush and a poorly cured non-flush both taste harsh. But
if someone only flushes AND only cures well in the same grow, they
attribute the smoothness to flushing.

### 2. Overfeeding to begin with

Many growers run nutrients too hot, especially in late flower. When they
"flush," what's actually happening is they're finally stopping the
overfeeding. The buds taste better because the plant isn't being salt-
blasted in its final weeks — not because stored minerals are being
"pulled out" of the flower tissue.

**The real lesson:** if your buds taste harsh, fix your feed strength and
your cure. Those are the actual levers.

## What the plant biology says

The core claim of flushing is that withholding nutrients forces the plant
to mobilize and consume stored minerals in the buds. But plant biology
doesn't support this mechanism:

- **Minerals in flower tissue are structural** — they don't get
  reabsorbed just because the roots stop getting fed
- **The yellowing during flush is nitrogen deficiency**, not "using up
  stored nutrients in a helpful way" — it's the plant cannibalizing fan
  leaves because it's starving
- **Leaf-to-bud mineral transport** is minimal in late flower — the buds
  are a sink for sugars (photosynthate), not a source of minerals

Deliberately starving a plant in its final days doesn't improve the
product. It just weakens the plant when it should be fattening buds.

## When flushing IS useful

Despite the pre-harvest flush being unsupported by evidence, flushing
has real, uncontroversial uses:

### Corrective flush (salt buildup)

If you measure high runoff EC/PPM (much higher than your input), excess
salts are accumulating in the medium. Running 2–3x the pot volume of
plain pH'd water through the medium washes those salts out. This is
standard practice in coco and hydro, and it works.

**Signs you need a corrective flush:**

- Burnt leaf tips that keep getting worse
- Runoff EC is 50%+ higher than input EC
- Nutrient lockout symptoms despite feeding correctly
- White salt crust forming on the soil surface

### Medium between cycles

Flushing the medium between grows (especially coco) removes old nutrient
residue and gives the next plant a clean start.

### Hydro reservoir changes

Draining and refilling the reservoir with fresh solution is just good
hydro hygiene.

## What to do instead of pre-harvest flushing

1. **Feed at appropriate strength** — start at 50% manufacturer
   recommendation and increase only if the plant asks for more
2. **Taper feed in the last 2 weeks** — reduce to 50–75% strength in
   the last 10–14 days; this is gentler than a hard cutoff
3. **Focus on the cure** — a proper 10–14 day dry followed by 2–4 weeks
   of jar curing is what actually makes smooth cannabis. See our
   [drying and curing guide](/guides/drying-and-curing)
4. **Monitor runoff** — if you keep input and output EC within 20% of
   each other, salt buildup never becomes a problem
5. **Use quality nutrients** — cheap salts with lots of filler leave more
   residue; brands like Jack's, Athena, or Canna produce cleaner runoff

## The verdict

**Pre-harvest flushing is not supported by the available evidence.** The
one controlled study found no benefit — and actually found unflushed buds
tasted better in a blind test.

**Corrective flushing to remove salt buildup is genuinely useful** and
should be in every grower's toolkit.

**The cure is what makes smooth cannabis.** If your buds taste harsh,
look at your drying environment (60°F / 60% RH / 10–14 days), your
curing process (burp jars daily for 2 weeks, then weekly for 2–4 more),
and your late-flower feed strength — not at whether you ran plain water
for two weeks.

Don't starve your plants when they're trying to fatten buds. Feed them
well, dry them slowly, cure them patiently, and the smoke will speak for
itself.
