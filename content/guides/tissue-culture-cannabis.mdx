Tissue culture (TC) is the process of growing plant cells, tissues,
or organs on sterile nutrient media under controlled conditions.
It's how commercial nurseries produce millions of identical plants,
how breeders preserve genetics indefinitely, and how researchers
create virus-free stock. For cannabis growers, tissue culture
offers three capabilities that traditional cloning can't match:

1. **True genetic preservation** — a single cutting stored in TC
   can be maintained indefinitely, frozen in time, without a
   living mother plant consuming space, light, and nutrients
2. **Pathogen elimination** — TC techniques can produce clean
   stock from infected plants (virus, HpLVd, fusarium)
3. **Massive multiplication** — one explant can produce hundreds
   of clones in weeks through repeated subculturing

The trade-off: tissue culture has a steep learning curve, requires
sterile technique, and involves chemistry most growers haven't
encountered. This guide gives you the complete process honestly —
including where it's overkill.

## Is tissue culture right for you?

**TC makes sense if:**
- You have genetics worth preserving long-term (rare cuts, proven
  elite clones, your own breeding work)
- You want to eliminate a persistent pathogen (especially Hop
  Latent Viroid, HpLVd)
- You need to produce large numbers of clones quickly
- You're interested in the science and willing to invest time in
  learning sterile technique

**TC is overkill if:**
- You grow 1–4 plants and swap strains frequently
- You're happy with traditional cloning success rates
- You don't have a clean indoor workspace for sterile work

## Equipment and workspace

### The sterile workspace

Contamination is the #1 failure mode in TC. Mold and bacteria in
the air land on your media and outcompete the plant tissue within
days. You need a workspace that minimizes airborne contamination:

**Ideal:** a laminar flow hood ($300–1500) — a HEPA-filtered
workstation that blows sterile air across your workspace. This is
what labs use and it reduces contamination rates from 50%+ to
under 5%.

**Budget alternative:** a Still Air Box (SAB) — a large clear
plastic tub (66-quart minimum) turned upside down with two
arm-holes cut in the sides, sealed with rubber gloves. The
enclosed volume limits air movement and contamination. SABs are
free to build and bring contamination rates to 10–20% with good
technique.

**Not adequate:** working on an open countertop. Even in a clean
room, airborne contaminants will ruin most cultures.

## The four stages of tissue culture

### Stage 1: Initiation (establishing the culture)

**Goal:** take a piece of the mother plant, sterilize it, and
place it on growth medium to establish a living, contamination-
free culture.

**Selecting the explant (tissue sample):**
- **Best source:** actively growing shoot tips (the top 1–2 cm of
  a growing branch). These have the highest concentration of
  meristematic (dividing) cells
- **Also works:** nodal segments (a section of stem containing a
  node with an axillary bud)
- **Avoid:** mature tissue, woody stems, leaves without growth
  points (these have low regeneration potential)

**Surface sterilization protocol:**
1. Take cuttings from the healthiest, most vigorous growth on the
   mother plant
2. Rinse under running tap water for 2 minutes to remove loose
   debris
3. Soak in 70% isopropyl alcohol for 30 seconds (kills surface
   bacteria)
4. Soak in 10% bleach solution (+ 1 drop dish soap as a
   surfactant) for 10–15 minutes
5. Rinse 3× in sterile distilled water (previously autoclaved)
6. Transfer to initiation medium in your sterile workspace

**Initiation medium recipe (1 liter):**
- 4.4 g Murashige & Skoog (MS) powder with vitamins
- 30 g sucrose (regular table sugar)
- 7 g plant agar
- pH adjusted to 5.7–5.8 (using KOH or NaOH)
- Add 1.0 mg/L BAP (6-Benzylaminopurine) — a cytokinin that
  promotes shoot growth
- Autoclave at 121°C / 15 PSI for 20 minutes, then pour into
  sterile vessels

**What to expect:**
- Days 1–7: the explant sits on the medium. No visible growth.
  Watch for contamination (fuzzy mold growth or cloudy bacterial
  colonies on the medium surface)
- Days 7–21: if contamination-free, green tissue begins to swell.
  Small shoots or callus (undifferentiated cell mass) may appear
  at the cut surface
- Day 21–28: a small rosette of shoots should be forming. This
  culture is now established

**Contamination rate:** expect 30–60% contamination in your first
attempts with a SAB. Don't be discouraged — start multiple
explants simultaneously to ensure some survive.

### Stage 2: Multiplication (making more)

**Goal:** take the established culture and multiply it into many
identical shoots.

Once your initiation culture has produced a rosette of 3–5 small
shoots (typically 3–6 weeks after initiation):

1. Remove the culture vessel from the shelf
2. In your sterile workspace, carefully separate individual shoots
   or shoot clusters
3. Transfer each shoot to fresh multiplication medium
4. Each shoot produces a new rosette of 3–5 shoots in 3–4 weeks
5. Repeat: each subculture cycle multiplies your total by 3–5×

**Multiplication medium:**
- Same as initiation medium but with higher BAP (1.5–2.0 mg/L)
  to maximize shoot production
- Some protocols add 0.1 mg/L NAA (naphthaleneacetic acid — an
  auxin) to balance the cytokinin and prevent hyperhydricity
  (vitrification — watery, translucent tissue that won't survive
  outside culture)

**Multiplication rate:** 3–5× per 4-week cycle. Starting from one
explant:
- Week 0: 1 explant
- Week 4: 3–5 shoots
- Week 8: 9–25 shoots
- Week 12: 27–125 shoots
- Week 16: 81–625 shoots

**Subculture interval:** transfer to fresh medium every 3–4 weeks.
Cultures left too long on the same medium deplete nutrients and
accumulate metabolic waste products, which leads to declining
vigor.

### Stage 3: Rooting

**Goal:** induce the multiplied shoots to produce roots, preparing
them for transfer to soil.

Shoots produced during multiplication typically don't have roots —
the high cytokinin (BAP) in the multiplication medium suppresses
root formation. To root them, transfer to rooting medium:

**Rooting medium:**
- MS powder at half-strength (2.2 g/L)
- 20 g/L sucrose (reduced from 30)
- 7 g/L agar
- pH 5.7–5.8
- 0.5–1.0 mg/L IBA (indole-3-butyric acid — an auxin that
  promotes root initiation)
- No BAP (cytokinin suppression allows root development)

**Timeline:** roots appear in 7–21 days. Plantlets with 2–3 roots
at least 1 cm long are ready for acclimation.

**Alternative:** skip the rooting stage in culture and root
directly during acclimation (Stage 4). Many growers find this
faster and simpler — remove unrooted shoots from multiplication
medium, dip the base in rooting hormone (Clonex gel), and plant
directly into seedling mix under a humidity dome. Success rate is
lower (60–80% vs. 90%+ with in-culture rooting) but saves 2–3
weeks.

### Stage 4: Acclimation (hardening off)

**Goal:** transition the plantlet from the sterile, high-humidity
culture environment to normal growing conditions.

This is the most critical stage — TC plantlets are adapted to
100% humidity, zero air movement, and sterile conditions. Moving
them directly into a grow tent kills most of them. The transition
must be gradual:

**Day 1–3:**
- Remove rooted plantlet from the culture vessel
- Gently wash agar from the roots under room-temperature water
- Plant in a small container (solo cup) with pre-moistened
  seedling mix (peat + perlite, 70/30)
- Cover with a humidity dome (clear plastic cup or cut water
  bottle) to maintain ~90% humidity
- Place under low light (100–200 PPFD)

**Day 4–7:**
- Begin ventilating the dome — open one side for 1 hour on day 4,
  2 hours on day 5, gradually increase
- Mist the inside of the dome if humidity drops below 70%

**Day 7–14:**
- Remove the dome for increasing periods (4 hours, 8 hours, then
  full days)
- Increase light gradually to 300+ PPFD
- Begin light feeding at 25% strength

**Day 14–21:**
- Dome fully removed
- Plant is growing in ambient humidity (50–65%)
- Feed at 50% strength
- The plantlet is now a normal vegetative plant and can be treated
  as any other clone

**Acclimation success rate:** 85–95% with careful gradual
transition. Rushing this step is the most common failure point for
home TC growers.

## Hop Latent Viroid (HpLVd) cleanup

One of the highest-value applications of TC for cannabis is
cleaning HpLVd-infected genetics. HpLVd is a viroid (smaller than
a virus) that reduces THC by 30–50%, decreases yield, and makes
plants look "dudded" — stunted, small buds, abnormal trichome
development.

**Meristem tip culture** can produce clean plants from infected
mothers:
1. The meristem (the very tip of a growing shoot, consisting of
   just 1–3 cell layers, ~0.3–0.5mm) is excised under
   magnification
2. Because viroids spread through vascular tissue but haven't yet
   reached the undifferentiated meristem tip, explants from this
   zone are often viroid-free
3. The meristem is placed on initiation medium and grown out
4. Resulting plantlets are tested (RT-qPCR) to confirm they're
   clean

**This is very difficult at home.** Excising a 0.3mm meristem
requires a stereomicroscope and micromanipulation skills. Most
home growers who need HpLVd cleanup should send tissue to a
commercial TC lab (Dark Heart Nursery, Conception Nurseries, or
similar) — typical cost is $50–100 per variety.

## Long-term storage

**Slow-growth storage:** cultures can be maintained on low-nutrient
medium at reduced temperature (59–64°F / 15–18°C) and low light.
This slows growth so cultures need subculturing only every 3–6
months instead of monthly. Useful for preserving a genetics
library without constant maintenance.

**Cryopreservation:** shoot tips can be stored in liquid nitrogen
(−196°C) essentially indefinitely. This requires specialized
cryoprotectants and equipment beyond home capability, but
commercial labs offer it as a service.

## Common mistakes

1. **Inadequate sterilization** — contamination is the #1 failure.
   Autoclave media at 121°C for a full 20 minutes. Surface-
   sterilize explants thoroughly. Work in a SAB or flow hood.
   Spray your gloves with 70% IPA between every manipulation
2. **Too much BAP** — excess cytokinin causes hyperhydricity
   (vitrified tissue): shoots look glassy, translucent, and watery.
   They can't survive outside culture. Reduce BAP if you see this
3. **Skipping the acclimation** — TC plantlets die from sudden
   environmental change. The 2–3 week gradual transition from dome
   to open air is essential
4. **Using kitchen-grade sterile technique** — "clean" is not
   "sterile." Your kitchen counter, however clean, has millions of
   bacterial and fungal spores. Work inside a SAB or flow hood,
   not on an open surface
5. **Giving up after first-attempt contamination** — 50%+ loss to
   contamination is normal when learning. Start 10 explants to get
   5 clean cultures. Your technique improves rapidly with practice
6. **Over-transferring to multiplication** — shoots subcultured too
   many times (20+ cycles) can develop somaclonal variation
   (genetic mutations from repeated cell division in culture).
   Refresh from a new explant every 6–12 months to maintain genetic
   fidelity
