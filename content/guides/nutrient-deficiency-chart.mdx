Leaves are the plant's dashboard. When something is wrong
nutritionally, the plant tells you — through color changes,
spots, curling, and where on the plant the symptoms appear.

The single most important diagnostic question:
**where did it start — old growth at the bottom, or new growth
at the top?** That one answer eliminates half the possibilities.

## The mobile vs. immobile rule

This is the master key to nutrient diagnosis.

**Mobile nutrients** (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium,
magnesium) can be moved by the plant from old leaves to where
they're needed. When a mobile nutrient runs low, the plant
pulls it from the bottom first. Symptoms appear on old
growth and move upward.

**Immobile nutrients** (calcium, iron, manganese, boron,
zinc, copper, sulfur) cannot be relocated once deposited in a
leaf. When an immobile nutrient runs low, only the newest
growth is affected. Symptoms appear at the top of the plant.

This rule alone tells you whether you're dealing with N/P/K/Mg
or Ca/Fe/Mn and friends — before you read a single chart.

## Before you diagnose: check pH first

**Most "deficiencies" are actually pH lockout.** The nutrients
are present in the medium but chemically unavailable because
the pH is outside the absorption window.

The correct ranges:

| Medium | pH range |
|--------|----------|
| Soil | 6.0–6.8 |
| Coco coir | 5.5–6.3 |
| Hydro (DWC/NFT) | 5.5–6.1 |
| Living soil / no-till | 6.2–6.8 |

**How to check:** test runoff pH. Water with pH'd water
(target the middle of your range), collect the runoff,
measure it. If runoff is more than 0.5 below your input,
the root zone is acidifying — likely salt buildup. If it's
more than 0.5 above, something is buffering up (limestone
in the mix, hard water, or degrading organics).

If pH is out of range, correct it first and wait 3–4 days
before treating any specific deficiency. Adding more
nutrients to a locked-out plant makes it worse.

For a complete pH guide, see [pH for beginners](/guides/ph-for-beginners).
For lockout recovery, see [nutrient lockout recovery](/guides/cannabis-nutrient-lockout-recovery).

## The deficiency chart

### Mobile nutrients (symptoms start at the bottom)

**Nitrogen (N) — the most common deficiency**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Even yellowing of entire leaves | Bottom leaves first, moves up | Starts as light green, progresses to full yellow |
| Leaves drop off after yellowing | Bottom | Plant is cannibalizing stored N |
| Small, pale new growth | Top (severe cases) | The plant has exhausted its reserves |

**The fix:** increase nitrogen in your feed. In veg, this
means upping your base nutrient. In flower, some yellowing
of bottom leaves is normal (the "fade") — don't chase it
unless it's climbing rapidly past the lower third.

**Don't confuse with:** overwatering (drooping but green),
light deprivation (even yellowing but all levels at once),
or the natural fade in weeks 7–8 of flower.

---

**Phosphorus (P)**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Dark, dull leaves with blue-green tint | Bottom/mid | First sign before obvious damage |
| Purple stems and petioles | Throughout | Not always P — some strains are naturally purple |
| Red, bronze, or rust-brown patches | Bottom leaves | The classic P deficiency pattern |
| Slow bud development | Flowers | In bloom, P is critical for flower formation |

**The fix:** increase bloom nutrients. Check that pH allows
P absorption (it locks out below 5.5 and above 7.0). Cold
root zones (below 65°F / 18°C) dramatically reduce P
uptake — warm your reservoir or medium first.

**Don't confuse with:** cold temperatures (which cause
purple stems via anthocyanins, not P deficiency), or
magnesium deficiency (which shows as interveinal yellowing,
not dark dullness).

---

**Potassium (K)**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Yellow-brown scorched leaf **edges** | Bottom-mid | The "burnt edge" look — very distinctive |
| Brown, crispy leaf margins | Progresses upward | Edges die while center stays green |
| Weak stems, slow growth | Whole plant | K is critical for water regulation and transport |

**The fix:** increase K in your feed — bloom nutrients are
K-heavy. Flush first if you suspect salt buildup (burnt tips
plus burnt edges usually means lockout, not deficiency).

**Don't confuse with:** nutrient burn (which starts at the
very tips, not the edges), heat stress (which causes upward
leaf curl before edge damage), or wind burn (which is
localized to fan-facing leaves).

---

**Magnesium (Mg)**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Yellow **between veins**, veins stay green | Bottom-mid leaves | The "interveinal chlorosis" pattern |
| Rust-colored spots in the yellow areas | Bottom leaves (advanced) | Spots develop as yellowing worsens |
| Leaves curl upward at edges | Bottom-mid | Less common but diagnostic |

**The fix:** Cal-Mag supplement, or Epsom salt (magnesium
sulfate) at 1 teaspoon per gallon. Very common in coco coir,
which has a natural calcium/magnesium affinity that pulls
these from the nutrient solution.

**Don't confuse with:** iron deficiency (which shows on NEW
growth, not old), or pH lockout (which can mimic multiple
deficiencies simultaneously — check pH first).

### Immobile nutrients (symptoms start at the top)

**Calcium (Ca)**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Rusty brown **spots** on new growth | Upper leaves | Random spots, not edge-following |
| Twisted, distorted new leaves | Growing tips | Ca is needed for cell wall formation |
| Hollow stems when split open | Stems | Advanced calcium deficiency |
| Blossom end issues | Buds | Rare but possible |

**The fix:** Cal-Mag supplement (standard dose: 2–5 mL/gal).
This is the #1 deficiency under modern LED lights — cooler
canopies mean less transpiration, which means less calcium
transport from roots to leaves. If you grow in coco under
LED, run Cal-Mag as a **default**, not a treatment.

**Don't confuse with:** pest damage (which has irregular
patterns and may include webbing or insects), or pH-caused
nutrient burn (which scorches tips, not random spots).

---

**Iron (Fe)**

| What you see | Where | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| New leaves emerge yellow-white | Top/newest growth | Veins eventually stay green but leaf tissue is pale |
| Interveinal chlorosis (like Mg, but on NEW growth) | Growing tips | This is the key distinction from magnesium |

**The fix:** almost always a pH problem, not a true iron
shortage. Iron locks out above pH 6.5 in soil and above
6.0 in hydro/coco. Fix pH and the symptoms resolve within
a week. Only supplement iron directly if pH is confirmed
correct.

---

**Manganese (Mn), Zinc (Zn), and other micros**

These are rarer and harder to diagnose individually:

| Nutrient | Key symptom | Where |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Manganese | Tan/gray spots between veins | New growth |
| Zinc | Extremely tight internode spacing, small new leaves | Growing tips |
| Boron | Thick, brittle stems; hollow core | Stems/new growth |
| Copper | Wilting new leaves, dark blue-green | New growth |
| Sulfur | Uniform yellowing of new leaves (like N, but top-first) | New growth |

**The fix for all micros:** check pH first (almost always the
cause). If pH is correct, a quality base nutrient at proper
strength provides adequate micros. Micro deficiencies in a
well-fed plant at correct pH are genuinely rare.

## Overfeeding vs. underfeeding

| Sign | Overfed | Underfed |
|------|---------|----------|
| Leaf tips | Burnt brown, curling down | Pale, not burnt |
| Color | Very dark green (N toxicity) | Light green to yellow |
| New growth | Clawing downward (N toxicity) | Small, pale, slow |
| Leaf texture | Shiny, waxy | Dull, thin |

**Burnt brown leaf tips** are the universal sign of
overfeeding. If you see them, reduce feed strength to 50%
and give plain pH'd water for the next 1–2 waterings before
resuming at reduced strength.

For a nitrogen toxicity deep-dive, see [nitrogen toxicity](/guides/nitrogen-toxicity-excess).

## The three fixes that solve 90% of cases

**1. Fix pH.** Check runoff. If it's out of range, flush
with 2–3x pot volume of pH'd water and resume feeding at
your target pH. This alone resolves most "deficiency"
symptoms because the nutrients were present but locked out.

**2. Add Cal-Mag.** Under LED in coco or light mixes,
calcium and magnesium are the two most common genuine
deficiencies. A Cal-Mag supplement at 2–5 mL/gal solves
them both.

**3. Reduce feed strength.** If you see burnt tips, dark
green leaves, or clawing — you're overfeeding, not
underfeeding. Cut to 50%, flush, and rebuild.

## How to tell deficiency from other problems

- **Spots that wipe off** → pests or mildew, not nutrition.
  See [pest identification](/guides/pest-identification-guide)
- **Symmetrical patterns across the whole plant** → nutrition
  or pH
- **One branch only** → physical damage or localized root
  issue
- **Overnight droop with no color change** → watering or
  root zone, not feed. See [overwatering/underwatering](/guides/overwatering-underwatering)
- **Uniform browning and drying of ONLY the top canopy** →
  [light burn](/guides/light-burn-light-stress), not nutrition

## The recovery timeline

Fix one variable at a time and give it 3–4 days.

**Damaged leaves do not heal.** Judge recovery by new
growth only. If new leaves come in green and healthy, your
fix worked — even if the old leaves still look terrible.
Never remove damaged leaves to "clean up" during diagnosis;
they're still photosynthesizing, and removing them adds
stress on top of the deficiency stress.
