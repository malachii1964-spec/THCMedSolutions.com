Bud rot — caused by the fungus Botrytis cinerea — is the most
devastating late-flower threat to cannabis. It starts inside the
densest parts of your biggest colas, invisible from the outside, and
by the time gray fuzz appears on the surface, the interior of the
bud is already dead mush. A single bud rot outbreak in the last
weeks of flower can destroy 20–50% of a harvest overnight.

Every cannabis grower will encounter Botrytis eventually. The
question is whether you catch it early enough to save the harvest.

## What bud rot is

Botrytis cinerea is a necrotrophic fungus — it kills plant tissue
and feeds on the dead cells. It's ubiquitous in the environment
(present in virtually all soil and air) and attacks hundreds of
plant species, not just cannabis. It enters the bud through
microscopic wounds, dead leaves trapped inside the bud, or directly
through the calyxes in high-humidity conditions.

### Why buds are vulnerable

Cannabis buds are the perfect Botrytis habitat:
- **Dense structure** traps moisture inside the bud
- **Limited airflow** at the bud's interior prevents drying
- **Dead matter** (dead pistils, trapped fan leaves) provides an
  initial food source for the fungus
- **High humidity** in the microclimate inside and around the bud
  sustains fungal growth

The biggest, densest colas are the most vulnerable because they
trap the most moisture internally. Ironically, the buds you're most
proud of are the ones most likely to rot.

## How to identify bud rot

### Stage 1: Internal infection (invisible)

The fungus is growing inside the bud. From the outside, the bud
looks normal. There are no visual symptoms yet.

**Early warning signs (subtle — look closely):**
- A single leaf or sugar leaf emerging from the bud turns yellow
  or brown while surrounding vegetation is healthy
- A small section of the bud looks slightly different in color —
  faded, dull, or off-white compared to the rest
- When you gently squeeze the bud, one section feels softer or
  more compressible than the rest (the interior is decomposing)

### Stage 2: Visible exterior symptoms

- **Brown, dead calyxes** on the surface of the bud — a patch of
  dark brown in an otherwise green/purple bud
- **Gray fuzz** — the characteristic sign. Botrytis produces gray,
  fuzzy mycelium on the bud surface once the infection is advanced
- **Mushy texture** — the affected section of the bud feels wet and
  spongy, even when the rest of the bud is firm
- **Dark spore dust** — if you touch the gray fuzz, dark brown or
  gray spore dust releases. This is the Botrytis reproduction stage

### Stage 3: Spread

Once visible, Botrytis spreads rapidly:
- Spores release into the air and land on nearby buds
- Adjacent branches and colas become infected
- The entire plant can be compromised within days in warm, humid
  conditions

### Bud rot vs. other problems

| Feature | Bud rot (Botrytis) | Powdery mildew | Caterpillar damage |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Location | Inside the densest part of the bud | Leaf surface | Inside the bud (frass visible) |
| Color | Gray-brown, dark | White, powdery | Brown spots + dark frass (poop) |
| Texture | Mushy, wet | Dry, dusty | Tunneled, holey |
| Smell | Musty, rotten | None distinctive | None distinctive |
| Affected tissue | Dead, brown calyxes | Living leaves (parasite) | Chewed/eaten buds |

## Prevention

Bud rot prevention is about controlling the environment. Botrytis
spores are always present — you can't eliminate them. You can only
make conditions inhospitable for germination and growth.

### 1. Control humidity

**The single most important factor.** Botrytis germinates above
60% RH and thrives above 70%.

- **Flower target:** 40–50% RH. Below 50% makes Botrytis
  germination unlikely
- **Use a dehumidifier** if your ambient humidity is above 50%
  during flower
- **Exhaust fan** running adequately to exchange humid tent air for
  drier room air
- **Monitor at canopy level** — the humidity inside a dense canopy
  is higher than what your sensor reads at tent level

### 2. Maximize airflow

Stagnant air around buds creates a humid microclimate. Moving air
prevents this:

- **Oscillating fans** pointed at the canopy, not directly at buds
  (direct airflow dries the surface but doesn't address interior
  moisture)
- **Open the canopy** — defoliation during weeks 3 and 6 of flower
  improves airflow between and around colas
- **Don't let colas touch each other** — touching buds create
  pockets of dead air between them

### 3. Defoliate strategically

Fan leaves trap humidity around buds. Remove leaves that are:
- Blocking airflow to the interior of dense colas
- Touching or overlapping with bud sites
- Yellowing and dying (dead leaves inside buds are a Botrytis
  entry point)

See our [defoliation guide](/guides/defoliation-guide) for timing
and technique.

### 4. Choose resistant genetics

Some strains are more Botrytis-resistant than others:
- **Open, airy bud structure** resists rot better than ultra-dense
  buds (ironic — the densest, most desirable buds are the most
  vulnerable)
- **Sativa-dominant strains** tend to have looser bud structure and
  are less rot-prone
- **Strains bred for outdoor growing** (Frisian Dew, Durban Poison,
  Early Skunk) often have built-in Botrytis resistance

### 5. Inspect daily in late flower

From week 6 onward, inspect every cola daily:
- Look at the base of the cola where it meets the stem — this is
  where Botrytis often starts
- Check for any single brown leaf emerging from a bud
- Gently squeeze the densest colas — any soft spot needs
  investigation
- Smell the buds — a musty or rotten smell in one area indicates
  active infection

## What to do when you find bud rot

### Immediate action

1. **Do not shake or jostle the infected bud.** Spores release with
   movement. Handle gently
2. **Cut the affected bud/branch off the plant** — cut 2–3 inches
   below the visible rot to ensure you remove all infected tissue
3. **Bag and remove** the infected material from the grow space
   immediately. Don't compost it — bag it and trash it
4. **Sterilize your scissors** with isopropyl alcohol before touching
   any other part of the plant
5. **Inspect adjacent colas** — if one bud is infected, the buds
   touching it are likely infected too

### Environmental response

After removing infected material:
- **Drop humidity to 40%** or below if possible
- **Increase airflow** — add another fan, increase exhaust
- **Increase defoliation** around the remaining dense colas
- **Harvest early** if the infection is widespread. Partially
  developed buds are better than rotted buds. Check trichomes — if
  they're mostly milky, the harvest will still be usable

### Can you smoke bud-rot-affected cannabis?

**No.** Botrytis-affected cannabis contains fungal spores and
potentially mycotoxins. Smoking or consuming it can cause
respiratory issues, especially for immunocompromised individuals.
Remove all visibly affected material and carefully inspect remaining
buds before consumption.

**The trim:** when cutting around bud rot, be conservative. If any
part of the bud smells musty, is discolored, or feels soft, discard
it. Save only buds that look, smell, and feel completely healthy.

## Outdoor and greenhouse considerations

Bud rot is more common outdoors and in greenhouses because humidity
is harder to control:

- **Rain** directly on buds is a Botrytis invitation. Outdoor
  growers in rainy climates (including Western New York) should
  consider covering plants with a canopy during rain or growing in
  a greenhouse
- **Morning dew** settles on buds overnight. If buds don't dry
  before midday, rot risk increases. Shake plants gently in the
  morning to knock off dew
- **Fall humidity** — WNY's September-October humidity (lake effect)
  is the highest-risk period for outdoor cannabis. Harvest before
  prolonged wet weather if possible

## Common mistakes

1. **Ignoring humidity in late flower** — "it was fine all through
   veg" doesn't matter. Buds create their own microclimate. What
   was fine at 60% RH in veg becomes dangerous at 60% RH when
   surrounded by dense flower
2. **Not inspecting daily** — bud rot moves fast. A bud that looked
   fine yesterday can be 50% rotted today. Daily inspection during
   weeks 6–9 is not optional
3. **Trying to save rotted buds** — cutting around the rot and
   keeping the adjacent tissue is risky. Botrytis mycelium extends
   beyond the visible gray fuzz. Be ruthless in your removal
4. **Growing ultra-dense strains in humid conditions** — GMO,
   Wedding Cake, and other ultra-dense strains are beautiful but
   extremely rot-prone in uncontrolled environments. Choose
   strains suited to your conditions
5. **Not improving conditions after the first sign** — finding and
   removing one rotted bud without changing the environment means
   more rotten buds are coming. Drop humidity, increase airflow,
   and consider harvesting early
