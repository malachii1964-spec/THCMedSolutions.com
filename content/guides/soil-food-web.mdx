Most cannabis nutrient regimens treat soil like an inert sponge —
pour salts in, plant absorbs them. It works, but it's the crude
approach. In healthy soil, plants don't absorb nutrients directly
from fertilizer. They trade sugars with microorganisms in exchange
for minerals delivered in exactly the forms and quantities the
plant needs, when it needs them. This is the soil food web — a
biological economy where the plant is the employer, microbes are
the workers, and root exudates are the currency.

Understanding and building this system is the difference between a
grower who feeds a plant and a grower who builds a soil.

## What the soil food web is

The soil food web is the community of organisms living in soil and
the feeding relationships between them. It's not a metaphor — it's
a literal web of who eats whom:

### The trophic levels

**Level 1 — Primary producers:** plants. They capture sunlight and
pump 20–40% of their photosynthetic output into the soil as root
exudates (sugars, amino acids, organic acids). This isn't waste —
it's targeted feeding of specific microbe populations.

**Level 2 — Decomposers:** bacteria and fungi. They break down
organic matter into plant-available nutrients and form direct
symbiotic relationships with roots.

**Level 3 — Bacterial and fungal feeders:** protozoa (amoebae,
flagellates, ciliates) and fungal-feeding nematodes. They eat
bacteria and fungi, releasing nutrients in plant-available forms
(primarily ammonium and other mineralized nutrients).

**Level 4 — Higher predators:** predatory nematodes, micro-
arthropods (mites, springtails). They eat Level 3 organisms,
further cycling nutrients.

**Level 5 — Top predators:** centipedes, ground beetles, predatory
mites. They regulate populations below them.

### Why this matters for cannabis

At each trophic level, organisms eat others and excrete nutrients
in plant-available forms. This is called **nutrient cycling** or
**mineralization.** The critical insight: protozoa and nematodes
eating bacteria release nitrogen as ammonium (NH4+) near the root
zone — exactly where and when the plant needs it. This is more
efficient than dumping synthetic nitrogen into the entire medium.

**Plants actively recruit the organisms they need.** Different
root exudate compositions attract different bacterial and fungal
populations. A plant needing more phosphorus shifts its exudate
profile to recruit phosphorus-solubilizing bacteria. A plant
needing more nitrogen pumps out sugars that feed nitrogen-fixing
bacteria. The plant is not a passive recipient — it's the
conductor of the microbial orchestra.

## The key players

### Bacteria

**Population:** 100 million to 1 billion per gram of healthy soil.

**What they do:**
- **Decompose organic matter** — breaking down dead plant
  material, compost, and amendments into simpler compounds
- **Fix atmospheric nitrogen** — species like Azotobacter and
  Rhizobium convert N2 gas to plant-available forms
- **Solubilize phosphorus** — Bacillus and Pseudomonas species
  dissolve rock phosphate into forms roots can absorb
- **Produce plant growth hormones** — some bacteria produce auxins
  and cytokinins that directly stimulate root growth
- **Suppress pathogens** — Bacillus subtilis and B. amyloliquefaciens
  produce antibiotics that kill Pythium, Fusarium, and other root
  pathogens (this is why Hydroguard works — it's B. amyloliquefaciens)

**Bacteria-dominated soil** (high bacteria-to-fungi ratio) favors
fast-growing annuals — which is what cannabis is. Early veg cannabis
benefits from a bacteria-dominated soil.

### Fungi

**Two critical types for cannabis:**

**Saprophytic fungi** — decomposers that break down tough organic
matter (wood chips, straw, lignin-rich material) that bacteria
can't process efficiently. They create humus — the stable, dark,
spongy organic matter that gives healthy soil its structure.

**Mycorrhizal fungi** — form a direct symbiosis with plant roots.
Mycorrhizal hyphae (threads) extend the root system's effective
reach by 100–1000x, accessing water and nutrients (especially
phosphorus) far beyond where roots alone can reach.

There are two types of mycorrhizae:
- **Endomycorrhizae (arbuscular):** penetrate root cells, forming
  arbuscules (tree-like exchange structures). **Cannabis forms
  endomycorrhizal associations.** This is the type you want
- **Ectomycorrhizae:** surround root cells without penetrating.
  Found in trees. Not relevant for cannabis

**The mycorrhizal trade:** the fungus delivers phosphorus, zinc,
copper, and water to the plant. The plant delivers 10–20% of its
photosynthetic sugars to the fungus. Both organisms grow better
together than apart.

**Critical note:** synthetic phosphorus fertilizers at high
concentrations **suppress mycorrhizal colonization.** If
phosphorus is freely available, the plant has no incentive to feed
the fungus. This is why living soil growers use slow-release
phosphorus sources (rock phosphate, bone meal) instead of
water-soluble fertilizers.

### Protozoa

Protozoa are single-celled organisms (amoebae, flagellates,
ciliates) that eat bacteria. A single protozoan eats 10,000
bacteria per day. When they eat bacteria, they release excess
nitrogen as ammonium (NH4+) directly in the root zone.

**This is the primary nitrogen delivery mechanism in healthy soil.**
Protozoa don't just cycle nitrogen — they deliver it precisely
where roots can absorb it, in a slow, steady supply rather than
the feast-or-famine of synthetic feeding.

### Nematodes

Not all nematodes are harmful. Beneficial nematodes include:
- **Bacterial-feeding nematodes** — like protozoa, they eat
  bacteria and release nitrogen as ammonium
- **Fungal-feeding nematodes** — eat saprophytic fungi, cycling
  nutrients from decomposing organic matter
- **Predatory nematodes** — eat harmful root-knot nematodes and
  other pests

Harmful (root-knot) nematodes are a problem primarily in outdoor
and greenhouse growing. In container growing with quality soil,
they're rare.

## Building the soil food web for cannabis

### Starting from scratch

If you're transitioning from synthetic nutrients to living soil:

**Base soil recipe (per cubic foot):**
- 1/3 quality compost (thermal or vermicompost)
- 1/3 peat moss or coco coir (structure)
- 1/3 perlite or pumice (drainage and aeration)

**Mineral amendments (per cubic foot):**
- 1/2 cup glacial rock dust (broad-spectrum minerals)
- 1/2 cup basalt rock dust (silica, iron, magnesium)
- 1/4 cup gypsum (calcium, sulfur — doesn't alter pH)
- 1/4 cup oyster shell flour (calcium, pH buffer)

**Slow-release nutrients (per cubic foot):**
- 1/2 cup neem seed meal (nitrogen, pest deterrent)
- 1/2 cup kelp meal (potassium, micronutrients, growth hormones)
- 1/4 cup fish bone meal (phosphorus, calcium)
- 1/4 cup crustacean meal (chitin — triggers plant immune
  response, feeds beneficial fungi)

**Biological inoculants:**
- Mycorrhizal inoculant (endomycorrhizae) — apply directly to
  roots at transplant
- Compost tea or microbial inoculant (see below)
- Quality vermicompost (worm castings) — the richest source of
  diverse soil biology available to home growers

This soil will feed a cannabis plant through its entire lifecycle
without bottled nutrients if the container is large enough (15–30
gallons minimum for full-cycle growing in living soil).

### Compost tea

Actively aerated compost tea (AACT) is the fastest way to
multiply and introduce beneficial microorganisms:

**Simple recipe (5-gallon batch):**
1. Fill a 5-gallon bucket with dechlorinated water (let tap water
   sit uncovered 24 hours, or use a dechlorination filter)
2. Add 1–2 cups quality vermicompost in a mesh bag (paint strainer
   bag works)
3. Add 1 tablespoon unsulfured blackstrap molasses (bacterial food)
4. Aerate vigorously with an aquarium pump and air stone for 24–36
   hours
5. Apply immediately — compost tea has a 4-hour shelf life after
   aeration stops (the organisms die without oxygen)

**Apply as:**
- Root drench: pour directly onto soil
- Foliar spray: mist leaves (in veg only — avoid spraying buds)

**Frequency:** every 2–4 weeks during veg. Monthly in flower.
Reduce or stop in late flower to avoid moisture on buds.

### Maintaining the web

Once established, the soil food web is self-sustaining if you
don't kill it. The things that damage soil biology:

**Salt-based synthetic nutrients** — high salt concentrations
create osmotic stress that kills bacteria and fungi. Transitioning
from synthetic to living soil requires a flushing period to clear
residual salts.

**Hydrogen peroxide** — kills all soil biology indiscriminately.
Never use H2O2 on living soil.

**Chlorinated water** — chlorine kills bacteria. Dechlorinate
water before using on living soil (let sit 24 hours or use a
carbon filter). Chloramine (used in some municipal systems) does
not off-gas — a carbon filter is required.

**Tilling or heavily disturbing the soil** — breaks up fungal
networks. Living soil systems use no-till practices for this
reason. See our
[living soil guide](/guides/living-soil-no-till).

**Leaving soil bare** — exposed soil dries out, UV light kills
surface organisms, and you lose the microbial-rich top layer.
Mulch (straw, cover crop) protects the surface.

## The fungal-to-bacterial ratio

Different plants prefer different fungal-to-bacterial (F:B) ratios
in their soil:

| Plant type | Preferred F:B ratio |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Annual vegetables | 0.3:1 to 1:1 (bacteria-dominated) |
| Cannabis (veg) | 0.5:1 to 1:1 (slightly bacteria-dominated) |
| Cannabis (flower) | 1:1 to 2:1 (balanced to slightly fungal) |
| Perennial shrubs | 2:1 to 5:1 (fungal-dominated) |
| Old-growth forest | 100:1+ (heavily fungal) |

Cannabis is an annual that performs best in bacteria-dominated to
balanced soil. As the plant shifts to flowering, a higher fungal
presence improves phosphorus delivery (mycorrhizae) and overall
plant health.

**How to shift the ratio:**
- **More bacteria:** fresh green material (fresh compost, molasses,
  fish hydrolysate)
- **More fungi:** woody material (wood chips, straw mulch), humic
  acids, mycorrhizal inoculants

## Cover cropping in cannabis

Cover crops planted in living soil containers maintain and enhance
the soil food web between and during grows:

**During grows (living mulch):**
- White clover — fixes nitrogen, stays low, doesn't compete
  significantly with cannabis
- Alfalfa — deep roots, nitrogen fixation, can be chopped and
  dropped as mulch
- Crimson clover — same benefits, dies after flowering

**Between grows:**
- Plant a cover crop mix immediately after harvest. Let it grow
  for 2–4 weeks, then chop and drop (cut at soil level, leave
  roots intact, let green material decompose on the soil surface)
- This feeds the soil biology, prevents bare-soil degradation, and
  fixes nitrogen for the next cycle

## Korean Natural Farming (KNF) inputs

KNF is a biological farming system from South Korea that's gained
significant traction in cannabis growing. The core inputs are
homemade microbial cultures:

**IMO (Indigenous Microorganisms):**
Collected from local forest floor litter. A box of cooked rice is
placed in a shaded forest spot for 3–5 days, allowing local fungi
and bacteria to colonize it. The colonized rice is mixed with
equal-weight brown sugar and fermented. The resulting culture
contains a diverse microbial community adapted to your local
climate. Applied as a soil drench.

**FPJ (Fermented Plant Juice):**
Fresh, vigorously growing plant tips (comfrey, nettles, clover)
are mixed with equal-weight brown sugar and fermented for 7–10
days. The result is a concentrated liquid containing plant growth
hormones, amino acids, and minerals. Applied as foliar spray or
soil drench at 1:500–1:1000 dilution.

**LAB (Lactic Acid Bacteria):**
Collected by exposing rice wash water to air for 3–5 days, then
mixing with milk and fermenting for 7 days. LAB break down organic
matter, suppress pathogens, and improve soil structure. Applied as
soil drench at 1:1000 dilution.

KNF inputs are free to produce, locally adapted, and
extraordinarily effective. The learning curve is in the
preparation — the recipes are simple but timing and conditions
matter.

## Biology vs. chemistry: why this matters

**Synthetic nutrient feeding:**
- Fast results (nutrients are immediately available)
- Precise control (you measure exactly what goes in)
- Requires consistent input (stop feeding, plant starves)
- Degrades soil biology over time
- Runoff contains unused salts (environmental concern)
- Each cycle requires fresh nutrients (ongoing cost)

**Biological soil feeding:**
- Slower ramp-up (biology takes weeks to establish)
- Less precise control (the biology decides delivery rate)
- Self-sustaining once established (soil improves with each cycle)
- No salt buildup, no runoff concerns
- Each cycle the soil gets better, not worse
- Lower ongoing cost (amendments are cheaper than bottled nutrients)

**The honest tradeoff:** biology-based growing has a higher
knowledge floor. You need to understand what you're building and
why. The payoff is a system that improves over time instead of
requiring constant input — and flower that many growers find
smoother, more complex, and better-tasting than salt-grown
cannabis.

Neither approach is wrong. Many excellent growers use synthetic
nutrients. But if you're interested in building something that
gets better each cycle and produces genuinely exceptional flower,
the soil food web is the path.

## Common mistakes

1. **Adding mycorrhizae to sterile medium, then feeding synthetic
   P** — the mycorrhizae colonize the roots but get no benefit
   from the symbiosis because phosphorus is freely available. The
   plant stops feeding them, and they die. If you're using
   mycorrhizae, use slow-release phosphorus
2. **Making compost tea without adequate aeration** — unaerated
   compost tea goes anaerobic and grows harmful bacteria. Brew
   with a real air pump (not just stirring), and use within 4
   hours of turning off the pump
3. **Using chlorinated water on living soil** — chlorine is a
   biocide. It kills the organisms you're trying to cultivate.
   Always dechlorinate
4. **Expecting instant results** — biology takes time. A freshly
   built living soil needs 2–4 weeks to develop populations before
   planting. "Cook" the soil (keep it moist and covered) before
   use
5. **Tilling living soil between cycles** — breaks fungal networks
   that took months to establish. Chop and drop cover crops at the
   surface; leave the root systems intact underground
6. **Ignoring the soil between grows** — bare, dry soil loses its
   biology. Keep living soil moist and covered (mulch or cover
   crop) even when no cannabis is growing in it
