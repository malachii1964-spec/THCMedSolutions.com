You can't see roots during a grow (unless you're running
hydro), so they're easy to ignore. But every above-ground
symptom — slow growth, yellowing, wilting, deficiencies that
don't respond to feeding — has a root zone cause more often
than a canopy cause. The root system is the plant's mouth,
lungs, and immune system. When it fails, everything fails.

This guide is about building and maintaining root health
proactively — not waiting until the plant is sick to think
about what's underground.

## What healthy roots look like

In every growing medium, healthy cannabis roots share the
same characteristics:

- **White or cream-colored:** bright white roots are actively
  growing. Cream/tan is normal for mature roots. Brown, gray,
  or slimy roots are sick
- **Abundant fine root hairs:** the thin, fuzzy-looking roots
  that extend from the main root branches. These are the
  primary absorption surface. Healthy root systems are fuzzy
  with root hairs
- **Firm texture:** healthy roots feel firm when touched (in
  hydro). Mushy, slimy roots indicate rot
- **Fresh smell:** healthy roots smell like soil or have no
  smell. Foul or sour smell indicates anaerobic conditions
  and rot

## The root zone ecosystem

### Oxygen is everything

Cannabis roots are aerobic — they require oxygen to function.
Root cells use oxygen for cellular respiration, which powers
nutrient uptake. Without adequate oxygen:

- Nutrient absorption stops (even if nutrients are present in
  the solution)
- Root cells die and begin decomposing
- Anaerobic pathogens (Pythium, Fusarium) colonize the dead
  tissue
- The plant wilts despite wet soil — it's drowning

**How to maintain root zone oxygen:**

- **Soil/coco:** use a well-aerated medium with 25–30% perlite
  or pumice. Allow wet-dry cycles — the dry phase pulls air
  into the medium as water drains out
- **Hydro (DWC):** run an air pump with adequate air stones.
  Dissolved oxygen should be above 6 mg/L. Cold water holds
  more dissolved oxygen than warm water (another reason to
  keep reservoir temps below 68°F)
- **Don't overwater:** the #1 cause of root zone oxygen
  depletion in soil and coco. Saturated medium has no air
  pockets. Water when the top inch is dry (soil) or when
  the pot feels light

### Beneficial microbes

The root zone isn't sterile (and shouldn't be). A healthy
rhizosphere (the zone around the roots) contains:

**Mycorrhizal fungi:** form a symbiotic network with the roots.
The fungi extend the plant's effective root surface area by
100–1000× and deliver phosphorus, zinc, and water in exchange
for plant sugars. Add mycorrhizal inoculant at transplant —
apply directly to roots.

**Trichoderma:** beneficial fungi that colonize the root surface
and outcompete pathogenic fungi (Pythium, Fusarium). Effective
as a preventive biological control. Products like RealGrowers
Recharge, Great White, or Rootshield contain Trichoderma.

**Bacillus bacteria:** nitrogen-fixing and phosphorus-
solubilizing bacteria that make nutrients more available. Found
in compost, worm castings, and microbial inoculant products.

**Important:** chloraminated water kills these beneficial
organisms. If you're running an organic or living soil program,
treat your water for chloramine before watering. See our
[water quality guide](/guides/cannabis-water-quality).

### Root zone temperature

| Temperature | Effect on roots |
|-------------|----------------|
| Below 55°F | Root activity near zero. No nutrient uptake |
| 55–60°F | Slow uptake, phosphorus lockout common |
| 60–68°F | Optimal for most systems |
| 68–72°F | Acceptable. Monitor for pathogens in hydro |
| 72–75°F | Danger zone in hydro (low dissolved oxygen) |
| Above 75°F | Pythium and root rot thrive |

**Soil growers:** soil temperature lags behind air temperature.
A tent at 75°F with good airflow keeps soil at 65–70°F — fine.
In winter, a cold floor (concrete basement in WNY) can chill
root zones below optimal. Elevate pots off the floor with a
rack or foam insulation board.

**Hydro growers:** water temperature IS root temperature.
Active cooling (aquarium chiller, frozen water bottles) is
necessary when ambient temperatures push reservoir temps above
68°F.

## Container choice and root health

### Fabric pots (Smart Pots, Root Pouches)

**Root pruning advantage:** when roots reach the fabric wall,
they're exposed to air and "air-prune" — the root tip
desiccates and the plant grows new lateral roots. This creates
a dense, fibrous root system instead of the circular root-
binding pattern of plastic pots.

**Oxygen advantage:** the fabric is breathable. Air penetrates
from all sides, not just the top surface. This increases root
zone oxygen throughout the medium.

**Watering difference:** fabric pots dry faster than plastic
because water evaporates through the sides. In hot environments,
you may need to water more frequently. In humid environments,
the faster drying is beneficial (prevents overwatering).

### Plastic pots

Standard nursery pots. Roots circle the bottom and sides,
eventually becoming root-bound. Adequate for single-cycle
grows where the plant doesn't outgrow the container.

**When plastic is fine:** short grows, autoflowers, or when
the container is large enough that root binding isn't a concern
during the grow cycle.

### Air pots

Perforated plastic containers with holes along the sides. Air
pruning happens at each hole, similar to fabric pots but with
more structural rigidity.

**Advantage:** best air pruning of any container type.
**Disadvantage:** expensive, dry out even faster than fabric,
and the holes can clog with soil.

### No container (in-ground)

Unlimited root space. Roots spread as far as they need.
Outdoor plants in good soil develop massive root systems that
access deep water and nutrients. This is why outdoor plants
can grow so much larger than container plants — the root
system isn't artificially constrained.

## Root problems and solutions

### Root bound (circling roots)

**Signs:** roots visible at drainage holes, spiraling in a
circle. Growth slows, the plant drinks water abnormally fast
(all roots, no medium), and leaves may yellow from effective
nutrient starvation (root surface can't expand to meet demand).

**Fix:** transplant to a larger container. Score the root ball
with a clean knife (3–4 vertical cuts, 1/4 inch deep) to sever
circling roots and encourage outward growth into the new medium.

**Prevention:** use fabric or air pots, transplant before
roots fill the current container, or start in the final
container.

### Root rot (Pythium / Fusarium)

**Signs:** brown, slimy roots with foul smell. Wilting despite
wet medium. Slow growth, general decline that doesn't respond
to feeding.

**Fix:** see our detailed
[root rot guide](/guides/root-rot-pythium).

### Salt damage to roots

**Signs:** burnt root tips (brown, crispy), stunted root
growth, nutrient lockout symptoms above ground despite
adequate feeding.

**Cause:** salt accumulation from over-feeding or insufficient
runoff. Salts reach concentrations that osmotically damage
root cells.

**Fix:** flush with 3× pot volume of plain pH'd water. Resume
feeding at 50% strength. See our
[flush guide](/guides/cannabis-flush-technique).

### pH-related root dysfunction

Roots absorb nutrients within a pH-dependent window. Outside
that window, the nutrients are present in the medium but
chemically unavailable to the roots:

| Element | Available range (soil) |
|---------|----------------------|
| Nitrogen | 6.0–7.5 |
| Phosphorus | 6.0–7.0 |
| Potassium | 6.0–7.5 |
| Calcium | 6.0–7.5 |
| Magnesium | 6.0–7.5 |
| Iron | 5.5–6.5 |
| Manganese | 5.5–6.5 |

**The sweet spot: pH 6.2–6.5 in soil** keeps all essential
nutrients in their available range.

## Common mistakes

1. **Overwatering** — the #1 root killer. When the medium is
   constantly saturated, roots can't breathe. The wet-dry
   cycle is essential: water thoroughly, then wait until the
   medium partially dries before watering again. Lift the pot
   — heavy means wet, light means ready
2. **Cold root zones** — especially in winter. Cold concrete
   floors, uninsulated garages, and cold water from the tap
   all chill the root zone below optimal. Elevate pots and
   let water warm to room temperature before watering
3. **Ignoring chloramine** — municipal water with chloramine
   kills the beneficial microbes that protect roots and make
   nutrients available. Treat your water before watering,
   especially in organic or living soil systems
4. **Not using mycorrhizae** — a $10 bag of mycorrhizal
   inoculant applied once at transplant dramatically improves
   root function for the entire grow. It's one of the
   highest-ROI inputs in cannabis growing
5. **Starting in too-large a container** — a seedling in a
   5-gallon pot can't dry the medium fast enough. The outer
   soil stays wet, creating an anaerobic zone. Start in small
   containers and transplant up as the root system grows
