Companion planting is the practice of growing specific plants alongside
your cannabis to create a healthier, more resilient garden. It's been used
in agriculture for thousands of years, and it works just as well for
cannabis — especially outdoors, but some companions are useful indoors too.

## Why companion plant?

Monoculture (growing one species alone) is an invitation for pests and
disease. A diverse garden confuses pests, attracts beneficial insects,
improves soil biology, and can even improve terpene production by
creating mild biotic stress (the plant's defense response produces more
secondary metabolites — including terpenes).

**Four jobs companion plants do:**

1. **Pest repellent** — aromatic plants confuse or repel problem insects
2. **Beneficial attractor** — flowers that bring in predatory wasps,
   ladybugs, and lacewings
3. **Soil builder** — nitrogen-fixing legumes and deep-rooted plants
   that mine minerals
4. **Camouflage and windbreak** — visual cover and environmental
   protection

## The companion plant roster

### Tier 1 — High-value allies (plant these first)

#### Basil

The #1 cannabis companion. Basil repels aphids, spider mites, thrips,
whiteflies, and mosquitoes. The volatile oils (linalool, eugenol) create
a scent barrier that confuses pest navigation. Plant basil in the same
containers or within 12 inches of your cannabis.

**Bonus:** some growers report that basil growing near cannabis
encourages terpene production. The mechanism isn't proven, but the pest
protection alone is worth it.

#### Marigolds (*Tagetes* species)

Marigolds are the classic companion plant. They repel root-knot nematodes
(their roots release alpha-terthienyl, which is toxic to nematodes),
deter whiteflies and aphids above ground, and attract hoverflies whose
larvae eat aphids. Use French marigolds (*Tagetes patula*) — they're
more effective than African marigolds for pest control.

**Plant them as a border** around your cannabis garden. Their bright
flowers also attract pollinators, which helps any fruiting companions
you're growing nearby.

#### White Clover

A living mulch that fixes nitrogen from the air (via Rhizobium bacteria
in root nodules), suppresses weeds, retains moisture, and feeds
earthworms. Plant Dutch white clover as a ground cover in the spaces
between your cannabis plants.

**Indoor use:** clover works in large containers (5+ gallon) and raised
beds. In living soil grows, it's practically mandatory.

### Tier 2 — Strong supporters

#### Lavender

Repels fleas, moths, and mosquitoes. Attracts bees, butterflies, and
beneficial wasps. The high linalool content creates a complementary
terpene environment. Plant on the garden perimeter.

#### Chamomile

Accumulates calcium, potassium, and sulfur — when the plant dies back
or is composted, these minerals return to the soil. Chamomile also
attracts hoverflies and parasitic wasps. Makes a good companion tea
(literally: brew and water in for a gentle mineral feed).

#### Dill

Attracts ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps — the three most
effective aphid predators. Let some dill bolt and flower; the umbrella
flower heads are landing pads for beneficials.

**Caution:** dill can grow tall and compete for light. Keep it on the
perimeter, not next to short cannabis plants.

#### Sunflowers

Tall sunflowers serve as a windbreak, attract pollinators, and provide
visual cover for outdoor gardens. Their taproots break up compacted soil.
Plant them on the north side of your garden so they don't shade the
cannabis.

### Tier 3 — Useful specialists

#### Peppermint / Spearmint

Strong pest repellent (aphids, ants, flea beetles, mice). **Always plant
mint in containers** — it's aggressively invasive and will take over
any bed it's planted in. Set potted mint between cannabis plants.

#### Yarrow

Deep taproot mines potassium and phosphorus from subsoil. Attracts
ladybugs and parasitic wasps. Tolerates poor soil. Good for the
perimeter of outdoor grows.

#### Coriander / Cilantro

Repels aphids, potato beetles, and spider mites. Let it bolt to attract
hoverflies. Fast-growing — you can succession-plant it throughout the
season.

#### Alfalfa

The king of nitrogen fixers. Alfalfa also accumulates iron, magnesium,
phosphorus, and potassium. Chop-and-drop alfalfa makes an excellent
mulch. Best used in large outdoor grows or as a pre-season soil builder.

## What NOT to plant near cannabis

Some plants compete too aggressively or attract pests:

| Plant | Problem |
|-------|---------|
| Fennel | Allelopathic — inhibits growth of nearby plants |
| Corn | Attracts earworms that also attack cannabis buds |
| Walnut trees (nearby) | Produces juglone, toxic to many plants |
| Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage) | Attract cabbage moths and caterpillars |

## Indoor companion planting

You can companion plant indoors, with adjustments:

- **Basil in the same pot or adjacent pots** — the most practical
  indoor companion
- **Clover as living mulch** in large containers — suppresses gnats
  and builds soil biology
- **Potted mint on the floor** — the scent rises and deters pests
- **Marigolds at tent entrances** — visual barriers won't work indoors,
  but their scent still helps

Keep companion plants smaller than your cannabis canopy so they don't
compete for light.

## Layout for an outdoor garden

Here's a proven companion layout for a 4-plant outdoor grow:

```
       [Sunflowers — windbreak, north side]
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  │  Dill  │  CANNABIS  │  Lavender       │
  │        │            │                 │
  │  Basil │  CANNABIS  │  Chamomile      │
  │        │            │                 │
  │  Mint* │  CANNABIS  │  Marigolds      │
  │        │            │                 │
  │  Basil │  CANNABIS  │  Marigolds      │
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
       [White clover — ground cover throughout]

  * Mint in a container, sunk into the ground
```

## Integration with living soil

Companion planting is the natural partner for living soil / no-till
grows. The companions feed the soil food web, the soil food web feeds the
cannabis:

1. **Clover** fixes nitrogen → bacteria → plant-available N
2. **Chop-and-drop mulch** from companions feeds fungal hyphae
3. **Diverse root exudates** from multiple species support diverse
   soil microbes
4. **Pest pressure stays low** because the ecosystem is balanced

If you're building a living soil bed, see our
[living soil and no-till guide](/guides/living-soil-no-till) for the
full approach.

## The bottom line

Companion planting isn't magic — it's ecology. A diverse garden has
fewer pest problems, better soil, and more resilient plants than a
monoculture. Start with basil, marigolds, and clover. Those three alone
will make a visible difference in your garden's health. Add more
companions as your space allows, and your cannabis will thank you with
healthier growth and fewer pest battles.
