You grew great weed. You trimmed it. Now you have a pile of sugar leaf
trim, small buds, and larf that's too frosty to throw away but not
pretty enough to jar. This is hash material — and with the right
technique, the hash you make from it can be more potent and flavorful
than the flower it came from.

This guide covers the two primary solventless extraction methods that
any home grower can do safely: **bubble hash** (ice water extraction)
and **dry sift** (screen sieving). No solvents, no explosions, no
specialized lab equipment.

## Why solventless?

Solvent-based extractions (BHO, PHO, ethanol) produce excellent
concentrates, but they require specialized equipment, proper ventilation,
and carry real safety risks. Butane is explosive. Ethanol is flammable.
Home solvent extraction has caused fires and injuries.

Solventless methods use only ice, water, agitation, and screens. The
worst thing that can happen is you make bad hash. That's a quality
problem, not a safety problem.

## Bubble hash: the ice water method

### How it works

Cannabis trichomes — the resin glands that contain cannabinoids and
terpenes — are attached to the plant material by a thin stalk. When you
agitate frozen plant material in ice water, the cold makes the trichome
stalks brittle. They snap off, and the trichome heads (which are denser
than water) sink through a series of progressively finer mesh bags,
separating by size.

Different trichome sizes collect in different bags. The 70–120 micron
range typically contains the highest concentration of intact, full-melt
trichome heads — this is what people call "full melt" or "6-star" hash.

### Equipment

**Essential:**
- Bubble bag set (5–8 bags, 25µ to 220µ) — $30–80 for a decent set
- Two 5-gallon buckets (food grade)
- 10–15 lbs of ice (more is better)
- Mixing spoon or paint mixer drill attachment
- Spray bottle with cold water
- 25µ pressing screen
- Parchment paper

**Helpful:**
- Freeze dryer (for commercial-quality drying — expensive, $2000+)
- Washing machine-style hash washer (for large batches)
- Cold room or basement (ambient temp below 50°F helps)

### The process

**Step 1: Freeze your material**

Fresh-frozen is ideal — chop the plant, trim the fan leaves, and freeze
the sugar leaf and small buds immediately in ziplock bags. Freezing
within 2 hours of harvest preserves the terpene profile that drying
would partially evaporate.

If using dried trim, freeze it overnight before washing. Cold = brittle
trichome stalks = cleaner separation.

**Step 2: Set up the bags**

Stack the bags in one bucket, finest (25µ) at the bottom, coarsest
(220µ) on top. The work bag (220µ) holds your plant material; everything
below it collects trichomes by size.

Standard bag order (top to bottom):
1. 220µ — work bag (holds plant material, passes everything smaller)
2. 190µ — catches plant debris
3. 160µ — large trichome heads + some debris
4. 120µ — good trichomes (second grade)
5. 90µ — premium trichomes (this is often the money bag)
6. 73µ — premium trichomes (money bag)
7. 45µ — small trichome heads (good, but often not full-melt)
8. 25µ — the finest; catches broken trichomes, mostly for edibles

**Step 3: Wash**

1. Fill the work bag with frozen material — don't overfill; leave room
   for ice and water to circulate
2. Add ice — roughly 50/50 material to ice by volume
3. Add cold water until everything is submerged (ice should float on top)
4. **Wait 15 minutes** — let everything reach freezing temperature before
   agitating
5. Stir gently for 15–20 minutes. For the first wash, be gentle — heavy
   agitation breaks plant material into smaller pieces that contaminate
   the hash. A paint mixer on a drill at low speed works, or hand-stir
   with a wooden spoon
6. Let settle for 10 minutes after agitation
7. Lift the work bag out slowly, letting it drain into the bucket
8. Pull each bag out one at a time, draining it, and collect the hash
   from the bottom of each bag onto the pressing screen

**Step 4: Repeat**

You can wash the same material 3–6 times. Each wash yields less, but
the first wash often has more plant contamination, and washes 2–3 can
actually produce the cleanest hash. Many growers discard wash 1 or
blend it with later washes.

**Step 5: Collect and dry**

This is where most home hashmakers fail. Wet bubble hash is 50%+ water.
If you don't dry it thoroughly and quickly, it will mold from the inside.

**Microplaning method (without a freeze dryer):**
1. Collect the wet hash from each bag onto a 25µ pressing screen
2. Press out excess water gently with a spoon (don't smash — you're
   draining, not compressing)
3. Place the patty on parchment paper and freeze solid (2+ hours)
4. Grate the frozen patty through a fine microplane onto parchment paper
   — you want a light, fluffy layer of tiny hash pieces
5. Spread the grated hash in a thin layer on parchment
6. Place in a cool, dark, low-humidity room (ideally below 40% RH)
   on a pizza box or cardboard (absorbs moisture from below)
7. Let dry for 5–7 days, breaking up any clumps daily
8. Hash is dry when it crumbles to powder between your fingers

**Freeze dryer method (commercial quality):**
1. Spread wet hash on freeze dryer trays lined with parchment
2. Run a full cycle (24–36 hours)
3. Hash comes out bone-dry, terpene-rich, ready to press or jar

### Grading your hash

| Grade | Description | Use |
|-------|------------|-----|
| 6-star (full melt) | Pure trichome heads, bubbles completely when dabbed, leaves zero residue | Dab directly, press into rosin |
| 5-star | Mostly trichome heads, slight residue when dabbed | Dab or press into rosin |
| 3–4 star | Mix of trichome heads and stalks, some plant material | Press into rosin, top bowls |
| 1–2 star (cooking grade) | Significant plant contamination | Edibles, infusions |

The 73µ and 90µ bags typically produce the highest-grade hash. The 45µ
and 120µ bags produce good but usually lower-grade material. The 25µ
bag and 160µ+ bags are usually cooking grade.

## Dry sift: the screen method

### How it works

Dry sift is simpler than bubble hash: you rub dried, frozen cannabis
over a fine mesh screen, and the trichomes fall through while the plant
material stays on top. It's faster and requires less equipment, but
produces lower purity unless you do multiple passes with progressively
finer screens (static tech cleaning).

### Equipment

- Dry sift screens (120µ, 90µ, 70µ, and optionally 45µ) — kief boxes
  work for small amounts; dedicated screens for larger batches
- Frozen dried trim or bud
- Parchment paper
- Credit card or scraper for collection
- Optional: static cling sheet for cleaning (DVD case, silk cloth)

### The process

**Step 1: Freeze everything**

Freeze your dried material and your screens for at least 4 hours. Work
in a cold room if possible. Warm trichomes are sticky and won't separate
cleanly; frozen trichomes are brittle and fall through screens easily.

**Step 2: First pass (120µ)**

Gently card the frozen material back and forth across the 120µ screen.
Don't press hard — let gravity and gentle motion do the work. Pressing
forces plant material through the screen and contaminates your sift.

Collect the material that falls through on parchment paper below.

**Step 3: Clean-up passes**

Take the collected sift and pass it through the 90µ screen. What falls
through is higher quality — smaller trichome heads with less
contamination. Pass again through 70µ for even higher purity.

Each pass reduces yield but increases quality. For top-shelf, 3 passes
is standard.

**Step 4: Static tech (optional, for max purity)**

This advanced technique uses static electricity to separate trichome
heads from debris:

1. Spread your sift thinly on a 45µ screen
2. Place a DVD case, silk cloth, or piece of parchment 4–6 inches above
   the sift
3. Rub the DVD case/cloth to build a static charge
4. Hover it over the sift — the lighter trichome heads jump up and stick
   to the charged surface, while heavier plant debris stays on the screen
5. Collect the trichomes from the charged surface

This can turn 3-star sift into 5-star material. It's tedious but
produces exceptional quality from mediocre starting material.

## Bubble hash vs. dry sift

| Factor | Bubble hash | Dry sift |
|--------|------------|----------|
| Purity (without extra work) | Higher | Lower |
| Speed | 3–4 hours + drying | 30–60 minutes |
| Equipment cost | $50–100 | $20–60 |
| Best starting material | Fresh frozen | Dried trim |
| Yield | Lower (more selective) | Higher (less selective) |
| Skill ceiling | Medium | High (static tech) |
| Terpene preservation | Excellent (if fresh frozen) | Good |
| Risk | Mold if not dried properly | Over-pressing contaminates |

**For most home growers:** start with dry sift (it's faster, simpler,
and forgiving). Graduate to bubble hash when you have enough trim to
justify the setup and want top-shelf quality for pressing into rosin.

## Storing your hash

Proper storage preserves potency and terpenes:

- **Short term (weeks):** parchment paper in a sealed jar, stored in
  a cool dark place
- **Medium term (months):** vacuum-sealed, in the freezer
- **Long term (6+ months):** vacuum-sealed, in the freezer, with
  minimal air exposure when accessing

Heat, light, air, and moisture are the enemies. Hash stored properly
in the freezer maintains quality for over a year.

## What to do with your hash

- **Dab it** — full melt (5–6 star) can be dabbed directly at low
  temperature (400–500°F) for maximum flavor
- **Press it into rosin** — any grade of hash can be pressed into
  hash rosin, which is typically higher quality than flower rosin.
  See our [rosin pressing guide](/guides/pressing-rosin-at-home)
- **Top a bowl** — sprinkle over flower for enhanced potency
- **Make edibles** — cooking-grade hash is perfect for infusing into
  butter or oil (decarb first at 240°F for 40 minutes)
- **Roll it into temple balls** — a traditional hash form where you
  hand-roll warm hash into smooth, dense spheres that age and cure
  over time
