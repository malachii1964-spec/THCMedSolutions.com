Foliar feeding — spraying a nutrient solution directly onto the
leaves — is a supplemental feeding method, not a replacement for root
feeding. The leaves can absorb water and dissolved minerals through
their stomata (the tiny pores on the underside of leaves) and
through the cuticle (the waxy outer layer).

It's faster than root uptake for certain nutrients. A calcium spray
addresses a cal-mag deficiency in 24–48 hours; the same calcium
through the roots might take 5–7 days to reach the affected leaves.
But foliar feeding has strict rules — wrong concentration, wrong
timing, or wrong stage can burn leaves and damage buds.

## When foliar feeding is useful

### Correcting deficiencies fast

The primary use case. When you see a nutrient deficiency developing
(yellowing, spotting, discoloration), a foliar spray delivers the
missing nutrient directly to the affected leaves while the root-zone
correction catches up.

**Common deficiency sprays:**

| Deficiency | Foliar solution | Rate |
|------------|----------------|------|
| Calcium | Calcium chloride or cal-mag | 1–2 mL/L |
| Magnesium | Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate) | 1 g/L (1/4 tsp per quart) |
| Iron | Iron chelate (EDTA or DTPA) | Per label, usually very dilute |
| Nitrogen | Urea (46-0-0) | 1–2 g/L |
| Potassium | Potassium sulfate | 1 g/L |

### Micronutrient supplementation

Some micronutrients (iron, manganese, zinc) are absorbed more
efficiently through leaves than roots, especially when root-zone pH
is slightly outside the optimal range for that nutrient.

### IPM (pest management sprays)

Not technically "feeding," but many IPM sprays (neem oil, insecticidal
soap, spinosad) are applied foliarly and can be combined with light
nutrient sprays.

### Seedling and clone support

Young seedlings and fresh clones have limited root systems. A light
foliar mist of very dilute nutrients (1/4 strength) can support the
plant while the roots develop.

## How foliar feeding works

### Stomatal absorption

Stomata are the pores on the underside of leaves that open and close
to regulate gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ and water vapor out). When
stomata are open, they can also absorb dissolved nutrients from
droplets on the leaf surface.

**Stomata are open:**
- During the light period (CO₂ absorption for photosynthesis)
- In moderate temperatures (65–80°F)
- In moderate humidity (40–70%)

**Stomata are closed:**
- During darkness
- In extreme heat (above 85°F) — the plant closes stomata to
  conserve water
- In very low humidity — same conservation response

### Cuticular absorption

The leaf cuticle (waxy outer layer) can absorb nutrients, but slowly.
Surfactants (wetting agents) improve cuticular absorption by reducing
the surface tension of the spray droplets, allowing better contact
with the waxy surface.

## How to foliar feed

### Equipment

- **Pump sprayer** ($10–15) — the manual pump-up garden sprayer is
  the standard. Get one with an adjustable nozzle for fine mist
- **Never use the same sprayer for pesticides and nutrients** without
  thorough cleaning. Residual pesticide in a nutrient spray burns
  leaves

### Preparation

1. **Mix the nutrient solution at 25–50% of the root-feeding
   strength.** Leaf tissue is more sensitive than root tissue —
   full-strength nutrients on leaves will burn
2. **pH the solution to 5.8–6.2.** Slightly acidic promotes stomatal
   absorption
3. **Add a surfactant** (optional but recommended) — a drop of
   unscented liquid soap (Castile or Dr. Bronner's) per liter, or
   a commercial wetting agent like Coco-Wet. This breaks the surface
   tension so droplets spread evenly instead of beading up
4. **Water temperature:** room temperature or slightly warm (65–75°F).
   Cold spray on warm leaves causes shock

### Application

1. **Spray the undersides of the leaves.** This is where the stomata
   are concentrated. Spraying only the top surface wastes most of
   the solution
2. **Fine mist, not dripping wet.** You want even coverage, not
   puddles. If the leaves are dripping, you've applied too much
3. **Spray from below, angling upward.** This naturally targets the
   undersides
4. **Cover all leaves.** Move the sprayer around the plant to reach
   the interior leaves, not just the outer canopy

### Timing

**Best time to spray:**

- **Early in the light cycle** — stomata are open, the plant is
  actively photosynthesizing
- **Dim the lights or spray just after lights-on** before the lights
  reach full intensity. Water droplets on leaves under intense light
  can act as lenses, concentrating light and causing localized burns
- **After dimming or before full intensity:** spray, then wait 30–60
  minutes for the leaves to dry before returning to full light

**Do NOT spray:**

- **During lights-off** — stomata are closed; the nutrients sit on
  the leaf surface and can promote fungal growth
- **Under full-intensity light** — burn risk from droplet lensing
- **In high humidity (>70%)** — the spray won't dry, creating mold
  conditions

## When to stop foliar feeding

### Stop when buds form (week 2–3 of flower)

This is the hard rule. Once buds begin developing, foliar spraying
risks:

- **Bud rot** — moisture trapped in dense bud sites promotes
  Botrytis (gray mold)
- **Residue on buds** — nutrient residue on the flower affects taste
  and safety. You don't want to smoke dissolved salts
- **Mildew** — wet leaf surfaces in the humid microclimate around
  buds invite powdery mildew

**Exception:** IPM sprays (like BT for caterpillars) may be
necessary during early flower. Apply early in the light cycle, use
the absolute minimum volume, and ensure complete drying before
lights-off.

After week 3 of flower, stop all foliar spraying. If a nutrient
deficiency develops in mid to late flower, correct it through the
root zone only.

## What to spray (and not spray)

### Good candidates for foliar feeding

- **Cal-mag solutions** (for calcium/magnesium correction)
- **Epsom salt** (for magnesium deficiency)
- **Kelp extract** (growth hormones, micronutrients, stress relief)
- **Fulvic acid** (chelator — improves nutrient availability on the
  leaf surface)
- **Silica** (potassium silicate at half strength — strengthens
  leaves)
- **Compost tea** (beneficial microbes, organic nutrients)
- **IPM treatments** (neem, spinosad, insecticidal soap, BT)

### Don't spray

- **Full-strength base nutrients** — way too concentrated for leaves.
  Burns guaranteed
- **Phosphorus-heavy solutions** — phosphorus is poorly absorbed
  through leaves and tends to leave residue
- **Anything with oil base during lights-on** — oil-based sprays
  (like neem) should be applied during the last hour before lights-
  off or immediately after lights-on while intensity is low
- **Harsh chemicals** — bleach, hydrogen peroxide at high
  concentrations, or synthetic pesticides not labeled for foliar use

## Common mistakes

1. **Spraying at full nutrient strength** — leaf tissue is more
   sensitive than roots. Always dilute to 25–50% of root feed
   strength
2. **Spraying buds** — moisture on buds = mold risk + residue.
   Stop all foliar spraying by week 3 of flower
3. **Spraying under intense light** — water droplets focus light
   and burn leaf tissue. Dim the light or spray during the low-
   intensity period
4. **Only spraying the tops of leaves** — stomata are on the
   undersides. Spray from below
5. **Using foliar feeding as primary nutrition** — leaves can't
   absorb enough nutrients to replace root feeding. Foliar is a
   supplement, not a substitute
