Every experienced grower keeps a journal. Not because they enjoy
documentation, but because cannabis grows have too many
variables to hold in memory. You can't compare your current
plant to your last plant if you don't remember what you fed
the last one, what temperature you ran, or when you flipped
to flower. A grow journal turns each grow from a standalone
experiment into a cumulative data set that gets more valuable
every cycle.

## What to record

### The essentials (record these or the journal is useless)

**Daily (takes 2 minutes):**
- Date and day number (from seed/transplant and from flip)
- Watering: amount, pH going in, pH runoff (if measuring),
  nutrient mix and strength (EC/PPM)
- Temperature: high and low for the day
- Humidity: high and low for the day
- Any visual observations: new growth, color changes, pest
  signs, deficiency symptoms

**Weekly:**
- Plant height and spread (measure from soil to top of canopy)
- Node count (during veg)
- Photos (same angle each week for comparison)
- Equipment status: filter changes, reservoir top-ups, any
  adjustments

**Per event:**
- Training actions: when you topped, LST adjustments,
  defoliation sessions (how much removed)
- Transplanting: date, container size, medium
- Light changes: height adjustments, intensity changes,
  schedule changes
- Nutrient changes: new formula, strength adjustments
- Problems and fixes: what you saw, what you diagnosed, what
  you did, whether it worked

### The optional but valuable extras

- VPD (calculated from temperature and humidity)
- Leaf surface temperature (if you have an IR thermometer)
- Runoff EC/PPM (tells you what the plant didn't absorb)
- Weight of harvested buds (wet and dry, per plant)
- Dry weight per branch (if you want to compare training
  effectiveness by position)
- Electricity cost per cycle
- Taste/smell/effect notes after curing

## When to record

Record at the same time each day — consistency makes the data
comparable. Most growers check the tent once or twice a day:

**Morning check (before or just after lights on):**
- Read the min/max from your thermometer/hygrometer
- Check soil moisture (lift the pot or check moisture meter)
- Visual scan for problems
- Record everything

**Evening check (optional):**
- Water/feed if needed
- Any training or maintenance
- Record what you did

**The 2-minute rule:** if recording takes more than 2 minutes,
your format is too complicated and you'll stop doing it. Keep
it fast, keep it consistent, keep it going every day.

## Format options

### Paper notebook

**Advantages:** simple, no battery, sits next to the tent,
no technology to learn.

**Disadvantages:** can't search, hard to compare across grows,
photos stored separately, water damage risk in a grow room.

**Format:** one page per day, or a table with columns for
date, day, temp, humidity, pH, EC, water amount, notes. Leave
room for weekly measurements and photos (print or reference
photo filenames).

### Spreadsheet (Google Sheets / Excel)

**Advantages:** sortable, searchable, chartable. You can graph
temperature trends, pH drift, or feeding schedules over time.
Best for data-driven growers.

**Recommended columns:**

| Date | Day # | Stage | Temp H | Temp L | RH H | RH L | pH In | EC In | Water (L) | Runoff pH | Runoff EC | Height | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|--------|--------|------|------|-------|-------|-----------|----------|----------|--------|-------|

**Tips:**
- Create a template sheet and duplicate it for each grow
- Use conditional formatting (red for out-of-range values)
- Create charts for temperature and humidity over time
- Add a summary tab with harvest data for cross-grow comparison

### Phone apps

Several cannabis-specific grow journal apps exist (Grow with
Jane, Bud, etc.). They combine photo timelines, feeding logs,
and environmental data.

**Advantages:** photos integrated with entries, reminders,
stage tracking.

**Disadvantages:** data locked in the app (hard to export),
some require subscriptions, apps may disappear.

### Photos as documentation

Whatever format you use, take photos:
- **Same angle, same distance each time** (mark a spot on the
  floor to stand on)
- **One full-plant shot and one close-up** per week minimum
- **Photograph problems immediately** — today's yellow leaf is
  next week's diagnosis mystery without a photo
- **Label photos** with date and day number (or use a
  whiteboard in the frame with the date written on it)

## How to use your journal

### During the grow: troubleshooting

When a problem appears, the journal answers "what changed?"

**Example:** leaf tips are curling down and yellowing on day 35.

Without a journal: "I think I fed it too much... or maybe the
pH was off... or maybe it's the temperature..."

With a journal: "pH was 6.2 on day 33, 6.0 on day 34 — normal.
EC was 1.4, same as last week. BUT temperature hit 92°F
yesterday (new heat wave). The timing matches heat stress, not
nutrient burn."

The journal narrows your diagnosis from "everything" to
"the thing that changed."

### Between grows: optimization

After harvest, review the full journal and answer:
- **What went wrong?** Identify the 2–3 problems that cost you
  the most yield or quality. Note what you'll do differently
- **What worked well?** Identify the practices worth repeating
  exactly
- **What was the yield?** Compare to previous grows. If yield
  dropped, the journal tells you why (different strain? training
  issue? environmental problem?)
- **When did the plant look best?** The week with the healthiest
  growth tells you your optimal conditions. Try to replicate
  those conditions for more of the next cycle

### The comparison power

After 3+ grows with journal data, you can compare:
- Strain performance in YOUR environment (not the breeder's
  claims — YOUR data with YOUR water, lights, and climate)
- Training method effectiveness (did mainlining out-yield LST
  in your space?)
- Nutrient line performance (did switching brands improve
  anything measurable?)
- Seasonal variation (summer grows vs. winter grows — how does
  outside weather affect your indoor environment?)

This is the real value of the journal. Individual entries are
mundane. The cumulative dataset across 5, 10, 20 grows is
irreplaceable.

## Harvest summary (complete after every grow)

Create a dedicated harvest summary page/entry at the end of
each grow:

| Field | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Strain | Northern Lights (ILGM) |
| From | Feminized seed |
| Medium | Fox Farm Ocean Forest |
| Container | 5-gal fabric pot |
| Nutrients | General Hydroponics Flora trio |
| Light | Spider Farmer SF-4000 (480W) |
| Veg days | 42 |
| Flower days | 63 |
| Total days | 105 (seed to harvest) |
| Wet weight | 340g |
| Dry weight | 85g (6.0 oz) |
| Yield/watt | 0.18 g/W |
| Problems encountered | Spider mites week 5 veg (treated with neem), cal-mag deficiency week 4 flower (added cal-mag) |
| Overall quality | 8/10 — dense, frosty, smooth smoke after 3-week cure |
| What I'd do differently | Flip 1 week earlier (stretched into the light), start cal-mag week 1 of flower instead of waiting |

This summary is what you'll reference when starting the next
grow with the same or similar strain.

## Common mistakes

1. **Starting enthusiastic, stopping by week 3** — the journal
   only works if it covers the entire grow. The most valuable
   data comes from late flower and harvest, which is exactly
   when most people stop recording. Keep it short (2 minutes)
   so you don't burn out
2. **Recording too much** — 20 fields per entry sounds thorough
   but becomes a chore within a week. Record the essentials
   daily and the extras weekly. A short entry every day beats
   a detailed entry twice a month
3. **Not recording the baseline** — your first journal entry
   should capture the starting conditions: tap water PPM, room
   ambient temperature range, light model and height, medium
   type. Without the baseline, individual entries lack context
4. **No photos** — text descriptions of plant problems are
   almost useless for diagnosis. A photo captures color,
   pattern, and location in ways words can't. Take photos
5. **Never reviewing the journal** — the journal's value is in
   the review, not the recording. If you record for 4 months
   and never look at it again, it was wasted effort. Spend 30
   minutes after harvest reviewing the whole log and writing
   your summary
