Supercropping is the act of gently crushing the inner pith
of a cannabis stem between your fingers, creating a controlled
bend point — a hinge that lets you fold a vertical branch
horizontal without removing any plant material. Done correctly,
the plant repairs the damage within days by building a thick
callus (a "knuckle") that's actually stronger than the original
stem and improves nutrient transport.

It's the highest-impact single training move you can make,
and it takes 10 seconds per branch.

## Why supercropping works

### The stress response

When you crush the stem's inner pith:

1. The outer skin (epidermis) stays intact — it bends, it
   doesn't break
2. The inner xylem and phloem (water and nutrient transport
   tissue) partially tears
3. The plant detects the damage and floods the site with
   growth hormones (auxin, ethylene)
4. A callus of new vascular tissue forms around the damage
   site over 3–5 days
5. The result: a thick knuckle that's physically wider than the
   surrounding stem, with MORE transport capacity than before

### The practical benefits

**Height control:** a vertical branch becomes horizontal in
one move. The branch tip redirects upward within 24 hours
(phototropism), but the bend point stays low. Net effect: the
branch grows horizontally before curving up, reducing total
height by the length of the horizontal section.

**Canopy leveling:** supercrop the tallest branches down to
the height of the shortest ones. Instant flat canopy without
removing growth.

**Nutrient redistribution:** the knuckle acts as a slight
flow restriction (temporarily) that sends more energy to
lower branches. Side shoots below the supercrop site grow
more vigorously.

**No lost growth:** unlike topping, you don't remove any part
of the plant. The supercropped branch continues growing — it
just grows horizontally before curving up. Zero lost
photosynthetic surface.

## How to supercrop

### Finding the right spot

- Choose a point on the stem that's still green and flexible
  (not woody/brown/lignified)
- The ideal location is between two nodes, in the middle of
  an internode
- Choose a spot that, when bent horizontally, will bring the
  tip to the height of your other branch tips

### The technique

1. **Grip the stem between your thumb and forefinger** at the
   chosen point. Hold the stem below the bend point with your
   other hand to stabilize
2. **Gently roll and squeeze** the stem between your fingers.
   You're crushing the inner pith, not the outer skin. Apply
   pressure gradually — you should feel the inner tissue give
   way with a slight crackling sensation
3. **Once the inner pith is crushed,** the stem becomes
   flexible at that point like a hinge
4. **Fold the branch over** at the crushed point to roughly
   90 degrees. The outer skin should stretch and fold, not
   tear
5. **Secure the branch** in the bent position with a soft
   tie, clip, or tape. This keeps it horizontal while the
   plant repairs the damage

### What "right" feels like

- A gentle crackling/crunching sensation as the inner pith
  compresses
- The stem becomes flexible at the squeezed point
- The outer skin stretches but doesn't split
- You can fold the branch over without it snapping off

### What "wrong" feels like

- A sudden sharp snap — you went too hard. See "Handling a
  full snap" below
- The outer skin tears open — you crushed too aggressively.
  This is recoverable but slower to heal
- No give at all — the stem is too woody (lignified). Move
  higher to a younger section of stem

## When to supercrop

### Best times

**Late veg (weeks 4–6):** stems are firm enough to hold a
bend but young enough to be flexible. This is the primary
supercropping window.

**First 2 weeks of flower (stretch phase):** supercropping
during the stretch controls height spikes. Any branch that
outgrows the canopy during the stretch can be supercropped
flat.

### Avoid supercropping

**Seedlings and early veg (weeks 1–3):** stems are too thin
and delicate. They snap instead of hinging.

**After week 3 of flower:** stems are lignifying (turning
woody). Supercropping lignified stems results in snapping, not
hinging. The plant also needs all its energy for bud
development, not callus repair.

**When the plant is already stressed:** supercropping is a
high-stress technique. Don't combine it with other stresses
(transplanting, nutrient change, heat wave) in the same week.

## Handling a full snap

Sometimes you squeeze too hard and the branch snaps — the
outer skin breaks and the branch is hanging by a thread (or
completely detached).

**This is fixable.** Cannabis is extremely resilient:

### If the branch is still attached (even barely)

1. Gently straighten the branch to roughly its original
   position
2. Wrap the break point tightly with plant tape (Parafilm,
   grafting tape) or electrical tape
3. Support the branch with a stake or tie to hold it upright
   while it heals
4. The plant will callus over the break within 5–7 days
5. Remove the tape after 2 weeks once the knuckle has formed

### If the branch broke clean off

1. If the break is fresh (within minutes), you can treat it
   like a clone — dip in rooting hormone and root it
2. If you don't want to root it, the plant will redirect
   growth to remaining branches

**The 90% rule:** a supercropped branch that snaps and is
taped back heals 90% of the time. Don't throw away the branch
— tape it and give it a week.

## Supercropping vs. other training

| Technique | Growth removed? | Recovery time | Height control | Stress level |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Supercropping | No | 3–5 days | Excellent | High |
| Topping | Yes (apex removed) | 5–7 days | Good | High |
| LST | No | None | Good | Low |
| Mainlining | Yes (extensive) | 5–7 days per top | Excellent | Very high |

### When to supercrop instead of top

- **During the flower stretch:** you should never top during
  flower, but you CAN supercrop during weeks 1–2 of flower
- **When you don't want to slow growth:** topping causes a
  3–5 day growth pause. Supercropping causes a 1–2 day pause
  at most
- **For emergency height management:** if a branch is suddenly
  too close to the light, supercropping is the fastest fix

### When topping is better

- **During early veg** when you want to create multiple main
  colas from one apex
- **For manifolding/mainlining** where symmetrical branching
  requires topping

### Combining supercropping with other training

The strongest canopy management uses multiple techniques:

1. **Top during early veg** to create multiple main branches
2. **LST the branches outward** to create a flat, wide canopy
3. **Supercrop any branch that outgrows the canopy** during
   late veg and the flower stretch
4. **Use a SCROG net** to hold the supercropped branches in
   position

This combination produces the flattest, most productive canopy
possible. See our

## The knuckle

The callus that forms at a supercrop site is often impressive —
a bulge 2–3x the diameter of the surrounding stem. Some
growers worry about it, but the knuckle is beneficial:

- More vascular tissue = more nutrient transport capacity
- Stronger than the original stem at that point
- A well-healed knuckle can support heavy colas without
  support stakes
- The plant essentially builds a bigger pipe at the demand
  point

## Common mistakes

1. **Supercropping woody stems** — if the stem is brown and
   hard, it will snap, not hinge. Always supercrop green,
   flexible sections. Move your grip higher on the branch to
   find younger tissue
2. **Not securing the bent branch** — a supercropped branch
   left unsecured will curve back upward within 24 hours
   (phototropism). Tie it or tape it in position for 5–7 days
   until the knuckle forms and holds the shape
3. **Combining with other high-stress events** — supercropping
   during a transplant week, defoliation, or nutrient change
   compounds stress. Space high-stress events at least 5 days
   apart
4. **Supercropping after week 3 of flower** — stems are too
   rigid and the plant needs its energy for buds. If you need
   height control after week 3, tie branches down instead of
   bending them
5. **Panicking at a snap** — a broken branch taped back in
   place heals almost every time. Don't cut it off — tape it,
   support it, and check in a week
