Every nutrient mix, every pH adjustment, every feed starts with water.
And most growers pour from the tap without knowing what's in it. That
works — until it doesn't. Mystery calcium issues, persistent pH drift,
unexplained leaf burn, or nutrient lockout that won't resolve even after
a flush — these often trace back to water quality.

This guide covers what's in your water, what matters, what doesn't, and
when you need to treat or filter it.

## What's in your tap water

Municipal tap water contains dissolved minerals (calcium, magnesium,
sodium, chloride, and others) plus disinfectants (chlorine or
chloramine) added during treatment. The exact composition varies by
city, season, and source.

### The numbers that matter

| Measurement | What it tells you | Good range for cannabis |
|------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| PPM / TDS (total dissolved solids) | Total mineral content | Below 200 PPM is good; below 100 is ideal |
| EC (electrical conductivity) | Same as PPM but in mS/cm | Below 0.4 EC |
| pH | Acidity / alkalinity | 6.5–7.5 from the tap (you'll adjust before feeding) |
| Hardness (GH) | Calcium + magnesium concentration | Soft to moderately hard (50–150 PPM) |
| Chlorine | Disinfectant | Any level (easy to remove) |
| Chloramine | Persistent disinfectant | Any level (harder to remove — see below) |
| Sodium | Salt content | Below 50 PPM |

### How to find your water data

1. **Your city's annual water quality report** — every US municipality
   publishes one (search "[your city] water quality report" or "CCR").
   It lists PPM, hardness, pH, and disinfectant type
2. **A TDS meter** — $10–15 on Amazon. Stick it in a glass of tap water.
   Gives you PPM instantly. Every grower should own one
3. **A full water test** — Ward Labs ($30) tests for individual minerals,
   heavy metals, and more. Worth doing once if you're on well water or
   have persistent nutrient issues

## Chlorine vs. chloramine

These are the two disinfectants used in municipal water. Which one you
have determines how you treat your water.

### Chlorine

**What it is:** chlorine gas dissolved in water. The "pool smell."

**Effect on plants:** chlorine at municipal levels (0.5–2 PPM) is mildly
harmful to beneficial soil microbes but doesn't damage plant tissue
directly. If you're growing in living soil or with beneficial microbes,
you should remove it. In sterile coco or hydro, it's mostly irrelevant.

**How to remove it:** let your water sit in an open container for 24
hours. Chlorine is a gas — it evaporates. An air stone (aquarium
bubbler) speeds this to 1–2 hours. That's it. No filter needed.

### Chloramine

**What it is:** chlorine bonded to ammonia (NH₂Cl). More stable than
chlorine, doesn't off-gas, and persists longer in the distribution
system. That's why cities switched to it — it's more effective at
preventing bacterial growth in pipes.

**Effect on plants:** similar to chlorine, but the ammonia component
can add nitrogen to your solution in small amounts. More importantly,
chloramine kills beneficial microbes in living soil and doesn't
evaporate on its own.

**How to remove it:**
- **Activated carbon filter** — a basic carbon filter (like a garden
  hose filter or under-sink unit) removes chloramine effectively. Flow
  rate matters — slow is better for complete removal
- **Campden tablets** (sodium metabisulfite) — one tablet treats 20
  gallons. Dissolves chloramine bond instantly. $5 for 50 tablets.
  This is the cheapest and most practical solution for most growers
- **RO system** — removes chloramine along with everything else
- **Vitamin C (ascorbic acid)** — neutralizes chloramine. 1g per 100
  gallons. More common in aquarium use but works

**Letting water sit does NOT remove chloramine.** This is the most
common misconception. Chloramine is stable — it doesn't off-gas like
free chlorine. If your city uses chloramine (check your CCR), you need
an active removal method.

## Hard water vs. soft water

Water hardness measures calcium and magnesium content. It directly
affects your feeding because hard water already contains significant
Ca and Mg before you add nutrients.

### The hardness scale

| PPM (as CaCO₃) | Classification | Cannabis implications |
|----------------|---------------|---------------------|
| 0–60 | Soft | Add cal-mag with every feed |
| 60–120 | Moderately hard | May need supplemental cal-mag in coco; usually fine in soil |
| 120–180 | Hard | Reduce or eliminate cal-mag supplementation; watch for buildup |
| 180–300 | Very hard | Consider RO or blending; excess Ca/Mg can compete with K uptake |
| 300+ | Extremely hard | RO system recommended; buildup will cause problems |

### Why hardness matters

**In coco:** coco coir has a natural affinity for calcium and magnesium
(it holds them on its cation exchange sites). In soft water, you need
to add cal-mag because coco steals what little Ca/Mg your water
provides. In hard water (180+ PPM), the excess calcium can saturate the
coco's exchange sites, compete with potassium uptake, and cause
potassium deficiency symptoms even when K is present in the feed.

**In soil:** soil buffers hardness better than coco. Moderate hardness
(60–150 PPM) is actually beneficial — the water provides baseline
calcium and magnesium for free. Very hard water (250+ PPM) causes
mineral buildup over time, visible as white crusty deposits on the
soil surface and pot edges.

**In hydro:** hardness matters most. The reservoir is the root zone.
Hard water raises baseline EC before you add a single milliliter of
nutrients, leaving less room in your target EC range for actual food.
Most serious hydro growers use RO water.

## PPM: the starting line

Your tap water PPM tells you how much "stuff" is already dissolved
before you add nutrients. This matters because your nutrient line
assumes a specific starting point.

**Below 100 PPM:** excellent starting water. You can use it directly.
Your nutrient dosing matches the manufacturer's recommendations closely.

**100–200 PPM:** acceptable. The minerals in your water contribute to
the total EC, which means you may need to reduce nutrient dosing by
10–20% compared to label recommendations.

**200–300 PPM:** marginal. You're starting with significant mineral
load. If you're having persistent nutrient issues (lockout, buildup,
unexplained deficiencies), water quality is a likely contributor.
Consider an RO system.

**Above 300 PPM:** problematic for most setups. An RO system or
blending RO with tap (50/50) will produce noticeably better results.

## When you need an RO system

An RO (reverse osmosis) system removes 95–99% of dissolved minerals,
giving you near-zero PPM water. It's a blank canvas — you add back
exactly what the plant needs and nothing else.

### You need RO if:

- Your tap water is above 300 PPM
- You're running hydro/DWC (precise nutrient control matters)
- You have persistent nutrient issues you can't diagnose
- Your tap water has high sodium (above 50 PPM) — sodium is toxic to
  cannabis and can't be removed by carbon filtration
- You're growing in coco with very hard water (frequent lockout)

### You don't need RO if:

- Your tap water is below 150 PPM
- You're growing in soil (soil buffers mineral variation)
- Your current grows are healthy with tap water
- You're on a budget and the water is workable

### RO system basics

A basic under-sink RO unit ($100–200) produces 50–100 gallons per day.
For a home grow, this is more than enough. A 4-stage system (sediment
filter → carbon block → RO membrane → carbon post-filter) handles
everything.

**The catch:** RO water has zero calcium and magnesium. You must add
cal-mag back (1–2 mL/L) before feeding, or you'll get immediate
deficiency. Many nutrient lines now offer "hard water" and "soft water"
formulas — use the soft water version with RO.

**Waste water:** RO systems produce waste water (typically 3–4 gallons
of waste per 1 gallon of pure water). Use the waste water for non-
cannabis plants, cleaning, or other household uses.

## Well water

If you're on a well, your water quality depends entirely on your
local geology. Wells can be excellent (clean, mineral-balanced) or
terrible (high iron, sulfur, heavy metals, extreme hardness).

### Get a full test

A Ward Labs test ($30) or equivalent tells you exactly what's in your
well water. Test for:
- PPM/TDS
- pH
- Hardness (Ca and Mg separately)
- Iron (high iron causes nutrient lockout and stains)
- Sulfur (hydrogen sulfide — the rotten egg smell)
- Sodium
- Heavy metals (lead, arsenic — not for the plant, for you)

### Common well water issues

**High iron (above 0.3 PPM):** iron precipitates out of solution when
exposed to air, forming rust-colored deposits. It can clog drip
emitters, stain equipment, and compete with manganese uptake. An iron
filter or sediment filter helps.

**High sulfur:** the rotten egg smell (hydrogen sulfide). At low levels
it's harmless to plants. At high levels it can inhibit root function.
Aeration (bubbling air through the water) removes dissolved H₂S.

**Extreme hardness (400+ PPM):** common in limestone regions. An RO
system is the most practical solution. Alternatively, blend well water
50/50 with RO or rainwater.

## Rainwater and collected water

Rainwater is naturally soft (near-zero PPM) and slightly acidic
(pH 5.5–6.0 from dissolved CO₂). It's excellent for cannabis — many
outdoor growers use it exclusively.

**Collection concerns:**
- Don't collect from a roof with chemical treatments (zinc strips, algae
  inhibitors, treated wood)
- First-flush diverters reduce contamination from roof debris
- Store in a dark, sealed container to prevent algae growth
- Use within a week, or add an aquarium air stone to keep it oxygenated
- Add cal-mag (rainwater has zero Ca/Mg)

## The practical water setup

### For most home growers

1. Get a TDS meter ($10) and test your tap water
2. If PPM is below 200, you're good. Use tap water, let it sit 24 hours
   if your city uses chlorine, or use a Campden tablet for chloramine
3. Add cal-mag if growing in coco (regardless of water hardness — start
   at 1 mL/L and adjust based on plant response)
4. pH after adding nutrients, not before
5. Done. Don't overthink it

### For growers with water problems

1. Test your water fully (Ward Labs or equivalent)
2. If PPM is above 300, sodium is above 50, or hardness is above 250:
   get an RO system and add cal-mag back
3. If PPM is 200–300: try blending 50/50 with RO or distilled water
   from the store (temporary solution while you decide on RO)
