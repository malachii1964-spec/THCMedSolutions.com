A well-designed grow room is a pleasure to work in. A poorly
designed one fights you at every step — equipment in the wrong place,
power cords across walking paths, no room to water without bumping
plants, and airflow that doesn't reach the canopy. Most growers set
up their first grow by placing equipment wherever it fits, then
spend months working around the mistakes.

This guide helps you plan the space before buying a single piece of
equipment.

## Choosing the location

### Basements (best for most)

**Advantages:**
- Naturally cool — reduces cooling costs, especially in summer
- Structurally solid — can support heavy equipment
- Below grade — sound and light are naturally contained
- Separate from living spaces — less disruption

**Considerations:**
- **Humidity** — basements tend toward high humidity (50–70%+). Budget
  for a dehumidifier, especially during flower
- **Temperature** — winter basements in WNY can drop below 60°F.
  You may need supplemental heat during lights-off
- **Water access** — a nearby sink or utility tub makes watering and
  mixing drastically easier. Carrying water from upstairs gets old
  fast
- **Drainage** — a floor drain or sump pit is ideal. Spills happen
- **Radon** — check radon levels if your basement hasn't been tested.
  Exhaust ventilation helps mitigate radon naturally

### Spare bedrooms

**Advantages:**
- Climate controlled (HVAC)
- Convenient access
- Electrical outlets already present

**Considerations:**
- **Flooring** — carpet and water don't mix. Use a waterproof tarp
  or drip tray system. Hard floors are better
- **Smell** — closer to living spaces means odor control is critical.
  Carbon filter is mandatory
- **Noise** — fan noise is more noticeable in a bedroom than a
  basement
- **Light** — the room likely has windows. Block light both directions
  (light out during day, light in during dark period)

### Closets

**Advantages:**
- Small, contained, easy to hide
- Works for 1–2 plants

**Considerations:**
- **Airflow** — closets have no ventilation by default. You must
  create an intake and exhaust path (see below)
- **Size** — a standard closet (24" × 60") fits a 2×4 tent snugly
- **Heat** — enclosed spaces heat up fast with lights running. A
  300W LED in a closet with no exhaust can hit 100°F+

### Garages

**Advantages:**
- Usually spacious
- Separate from living areas
- Often has concrete floors (water-friendly)

**Considerations:**
- **Temperature extremes** — uninsulated garages in WNY range from
  20°F in winter to 90°F+ in summer. Climate control costs can
  exceed the grow itself
- **Pests** — garages have more pest entry points than interior rooms
- **Security** — easier to spot from outside

## Space planning

### Minimum space requirements

| Setup | Minimum space | Fits |
|-------|--------------|------|
| Micro grow (1 plant) | 2' × 2' | Small tent, closet corner |
| Standard (2–4 plants) | 4' × 4' | Standard grow tent |
| Expanded (4–6 plants) | 4' × 8' | Two-tent or large tent |
| Perpetual (veg + flower) | 2' × 4' + 4' × 4' | Two separate tents |

**Working space around the tent:** allow at least 18–24 inches on
the access side for opening the tent, watering, and working inside.
A 4×4 tent in a room needs at least a 6' × 6' floor area to be
comfortable.

### The work triangle

Design your layout around the three things you do most:
1. **Water source** (sink, reservoir)
2. **Tent access** (the side you open)
3. **Equipment controls** (fan controllers, timers, power strips)

These three points should form a tight triangle. If you're crossing
the room to get water, then crossing back to open the tent, then
reaching behind the tent for the power strip — your layout is
fighting you.

## Airflow design

### The path

Every grow room needs an airflow path:
**Intake → through the canopy → exhaust out of the room**

For a tent-based setup:
1. **Passive intake:** bottom ports on the tent are left open (or
   fitted with intake filters). Fresh air enters from the room
2. **Through the canopy:** oscillating fans inside the tent circulate
   air around the plants
3. **Active exhaust:** an inline fan pulls air out through the top
   of the tent, through a carbon filter, and vents it outside the
   room (or out a window)

### Exhaust routing options

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Into the room | Simple setup | Heat and humidity stay in the room |
| Through a window | Exhausts everything outside | Light leak risk, weather exposure |
| Into the attic | Effective, hidden | Moisture in the attic can cause mold |
| Through an existing vent | Uses existing ductwork | May need permission, duct sizing |

For most home grows: exhaust into the room and keep the room well
ventilated with an open window or room fan. If the room gets too
hot or humid, route the exhaust out a window using a window vent
plate.

### Negative pressure

The tent must have negative pressure — the walls should pull inward
slightly. This means all air exits through the carbon filter, and no
unfiltered air (with smell) escapes through gaps. To achieve this:
- The exhaust fan must move more air than enters through the passive
  intakes
- Open fewer intake ports if the walls aren't pulling in

See our
[ventilation guide](/guides/ventilation-odor-control) for sizing fans
and filters.

## Electrical planning

### Power requirements

| Equipment | Wattage | Circuit |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| LED grow light (4×4) | 300–480W | Dedicated 15A circuit |
| Inline exhaust fan | 30–100W | Shared circuit OK |
| Oscillating fans (2) | 40–80W | Shared circuit OK |
| Dehumidifier | 200–500W | Dedicated circuit recommended |
| Seedling heat mat | 20–50W | Shared circuit OK |
| Air pump (if hydro) | 5–15W | Shared circuit OK |
| **Total** | **600–1200W** | |

### Circuit capacity

A standard 15-amp, 120V circuit provides 1800 watts. **Never load
a circuit above 80% (1440W).** If your total equipment draw exceeds
1440W, you need a second circuit.

**Warning signs of an overloaded circuit:**
- Breaker trips when the dehumidifier kicks on
- Lights dim momentarily when equipment starts
- Warm outlets or power strips

### Electrical safety

- **Use a GFCI outlet or GFCI breaker** — water and electricity are
  both present. A GFCI shuts off power if it detects ground fault
  (water contact). This is a life safety item, not optional
- **No daisy-chaining power strips** — plug power strips directly
  into wall outlets
- **Keep electrical connections above floor level** — water runs
  downhill. Mount power strips on the wall, not on the floor
- **Timer quality matters** — digital timers are more reliable than
  mechanical. A stuck timer can give your plants 24 hours of light
  or 24 hours of dark — either causes problems
- See our
  [electrical guide](/guides/grow-room-electrical) for complete
  wiring details

## Water management

### Mixing and storage

- **Dedicated mixing container** — a 5-gallon bucket with a lid for
  mixing nutrients. Keep it in the grow room so the water reaches
  room temperature before feeding
- **Measuring equipment** — pH pen, EC/PPM pen, measuring syringes
  or cups for nutrients
- **Storage for nutrient bottles** — shelving or a rack near the
  mixing area. Keep nutrients at room temperature, away from direct
  light

### Runoff handling

Every watering produces runoff. Plan for it:
- **Drip trays or saucers** under every pot (minimum)
- **Drain-to-waste trays** with a low-point drain fitting connected
  to a hose for easy removal
- **Wet/dry vacuum** — the fastest cleanup tool for spills and
  excess runoff
- **Waterproof flooring** — a tarp under the entire setup protects
  the floor beneath

## Environmental monitoring

### What to monitor

- **Temperature** — at canopy level, not tent ceiling
- **Humidity** — at canopy level
- **VPD** — calculated from temp and humidity (see our
  [VPD guide](/guides/temperature-humidity-vpd))
- **Light intensity** — a PAR/PPFD meter or app for dialing in light
  height

### Controller placement

Place your environmental controller sensor at canopy level, between
plants, shielded from direct light. The sensor at the top of the
tent reads 5–10°F warmer and 10–15% lower humidity than what the
plants actually experience.

## Layout examples

### 4×4 tent in a spare bedroom

```
┌─── Room (10' × 10') ───────────┐
│                                 │
│  ┌── Tent (4' × 4') ──┐       │
│  │  ┌─┐ ┌─┐ ┌─┐ ┌─┐  │  Mix  │
│  │  │1│ │2│ │3│ │4│  │  table │
│  │  └─┘ └─┘ └─┘ └─┘  │  + pH/ │
│  │   [oscillating fan] │  EC   │
│  │   [light overhead]  │       │
│  └─────────────────────┘       │
│        ↑ door side             │
│                                │
│  [dehumidifier] [power strip]  │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

- Tent along one wall, door facing the center of the room
- Mixing table on the same side as the tent door
- Power strip mounted on the wall behind the tent
- Dehumidifier outside the tent (draws from the room)

### Perpetual harvest (two tents)

```
┌─── Basement corner ────────────┐
│                                 │
│  ┌── Flower 4×4 ──┐ ┌ Veg 2×4┐│
│  │  4 plants in    │ │ Clones ││
│  │  flower (12/12) │ │ + veg  ││
│  │                 │ │ (18/6) ││
│  └─────────────────┘ └────────┘│
│        ↑ doors face center     │
│                                │
│  [sink]  [mix table] [shelving]│
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

- **Light separation is critical** — the flower tent must be sealed
  from the veg tent's light. If veg lights leak into the flower
  tent during its dark period, hermaphroditism results
- Run the tents on offset light schedules: flower 6 AM–6 PM,
  veg 6 PM–12 PM — this balances power draw and lets you work in
  each tent during its light period

## Common mistakes

1. **Not planning for water** — the most-used resource in the grow
   room. If the water source is far from the tent, you'll dread
   watering
2. **Forgetting about the dehumidifier** — flower-stage humidity
   control requires a dehumidifier in most climates. They're bulky,
   heavy, produce heat, and need draining. Plan space and power for
   one
3. **Insufficient electrical capacity** — tripping breakers mid-grow
   is dangerous (lights going off during flower triggers stress).
   Calculate total wattage before setup
4. **No access to the back of the tent** — equipment behind the tent
   (fans, duct connections) needs occasional maintenance. Leave
   access room
5. **Skipping the GFCI** — water near electricity is a constant in
   grow rooms. A GFCI protects your life. Install one before anything
   else
