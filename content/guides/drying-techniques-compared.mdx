Drying is where more cannabis quality is lost than any other stage.
A perfectly grown, trichome-covered bud can end up smelling like
hay and tasting harsh if the dry is too fast, too hot, or too
uncontrolled. Conversely, a mediocre grow dried slowly in perfect
conditions produces surprisingly smooth, flavorful flower.

The variables are simple — temperature, humidity, airflow, and time.
The execution is where growers diverge into different techniques,
each with trade-offs.

## The science of drying

### What's happening

When cannabis dries, three processes occur simultaneously:

1. **Water evaporation** — moisture moves from the interior of the
   bud to the surface and into the air. The goal is to reduce
   moisture content from ~80% at harvest to ~10–12% for curing
2. **Chlorophyll breakdown** — chlorophyll degrades into colorless
   compounds. This is what eliminates the "green" taste. It happens
   faster in the dark and at moderate temperatures
3. **Terpene preservation (or loss)** — terpenes are volatile. High
   temperatures, direct light, and fast airflow accelerate terpene
   evaporation. The slower and cooler the dry, the more terpenes
   survive

### The target environment

| Variable | Target | Why |
|----------|--------|-----|
| Temperature | 60–65°F (15–18°C) | Slows evaporation, preserves terpenes |
| Humidity | 55–62% RH | Controls evaporation rate — too low = too fast |
| Airflow | Gentle, indirect | Prevents case-hardening (dry outside, wet inside) |
| Light | Total darkness | Light degrades THC and accelerates chlorophyll breakdown unevenly |
| Duration | 10–14 days | Slow drying = best quality |

### What goes wrong at wrong temperatures

| Temp | Effect |
|------|--------|
| Below 55°F | Drying stalls. Mold risk increases dramatically |
| 60–65°F | Ideal. Slow, even drying. Maximum quality |
| 66–72°F | Acceptable. Faster drying (7–10 days). Slight terpene loss |
| Above 75°F | Too fast. Hay smell, harsh smoke, significant terpene loss |
| Above 85°F | Destroys terpenes and degrades THC. Do not dry above 85°F |

## Technique 1: Whole-plant hang dry (recommended)

### How it works

Harvest the entire plant (or individual branches). Remove large fan
leaves but leave sugar leaves intact. Hang the plant or branches
upside down from a line or rack in the drying space.

### Step by step

1. **Chop the plant at the base** (or cut individual branches at
   their base)
2. **Remove large fan leaves** — they contain minimal trichomes and
   hold excess moisture. Leave sugar leaves (the small, resin-coated
   leaves around the buds)
3. **Hang upside down** from clothesline, coat hangers, or a drying
   rack. Space branches so they don't touch each other
4. **Set the environment:** 60–65°F, 55–62% RH, dark room, gentle
   air circulation (fan pointed at the wall, not at the buds)
5. **Wait 10–14 days.** Check daily. Buds are dry enough to jar when
   the small stems snap (not bend) and the outside of the buds feels
   dry but not crispy

### Why whole-plant hang dry is best

- **Slowest drying method** — the stems and residual leaves act as
  a moisture buffer, releasing water gradually into the air. This
  slows the overall drying rate
- **Most terpene preservation** — the slow rate means fewer volatile
  terpenes evaporate during the dry
- **Best chlorophyll breakdown** — the extended time allows complete
  chlorophyll degradation, which is why properly hang-dried cannabis
  has no hay smell
- **Easiest** — hang it up and wait. No daily intervention needed

### When it doesn't work

- **High-humidity environments** — if your ambient humidity is 70%+
  and you can't control it, the drying space stays too wet. Mold
  risk is high. You need a dehumidifier or a different approach
- **No space** — whole plants take up room. A 4-plant harvest needs
  a closet-sized space to hang

## Technique 2: Branch hang dry

### How it works

Instead of hanging the whole plant, cut individual branches and hang
them. This is the same principle as whole-plant but more space-
efficient and slightly faster (less stem mass retaining moisture).

### When to use it

- **Limited space** — branches can be hung in a smaller area than
  whole plants
- **Uneven maturity** — some branches are ready before others. Cut
  and hang the ripe ones, leave the rest to mature
- **Standard approach** — this is the most common drying method for
  home growers. Many people call this "hang drying" without
  distinguishing from whole-plant

### Drying time

8–12 days. Slightly faster than whole-plant because individual
branches have less mass retaining moisture.

## Technique 3: Rack dry (individual buds)

### How it works

Individual buds are trimmed from branches and laid on drying racks
(mesh screens or stackable drying nets). The buds dry faster because
they're separated from the moisture-retaining stems.

### Step by step

1. **Trim buds from branches** (wet trim — see below)
2. **Spread buds on drying racks** in a single layer, not touching
3. **Set environment:** 60–65°F, 55–60% RH (slightly lower target
   than hang dry because buds dry faster without stems)
4. **Rotate buds daily** — the bottom surface in contact with the
   screen dries slower. Flip them to prevent flat spots and uneven
   drying
5. **Dry for 5–7 days.** Individual buds dry significantly faster
   than branch-hung cannabis

### Pros

- **Space-efficient** — stackable drying nets hold a large harvest
  in a small footprint
- **Good for wet-trimmed buds** — if you trim before drying, the
  buds need to be on a rack since they have no branch to hang from
- **Faster** — useful if you can't maintain a drying environment for
  10+ days

### Cons

- **Faster isn't better for quality.** The shorter drying time means
  less chlorophyll breakdown and more terpene loss
- **Daily attention required** — buds must be flipped to prevent flat
  spots
- **Higher risk of over-drying** — individual buds without stems can
  go from "perfect" to "crispy" in a day if conditions are too dry

## Wet trim vs. dry trim

This is the other major decision alongside drying method. The trim
decision and the drying method interact — they're not independent
choices.

### Wet trim (trim before drying)

**What it is:** trimming sugar leaves off the buds immediately after
harvest, while the plant is still fresh and moist.

**Pros:**
- **Easier trimming** — wet leaves stick out from the buds at
  natural angles, making them easy to identify and cut. Dry leaves
  curl into the bud, making them harder to access
- **Faster overall process** — trim once while wet, then dry, then
  jar. No second trimming step
- **Cleaner appearance** — wet-trimmed buds look manicured from the
  moment they go in the jar

**Cons:**
- **Faster drying** — removing the sugar leaves removes moisture
  buffers. The buds dry faster, which means less terpene preservation
  and less chlorophyll breakdown
- **Must rack dry** — wet-trimmed buds have no stem structure to
  hang from (unless you trim the buds and leave them attached to
  the branch — a partial wet trim)
- **Trichome loss** — handling wet buds knocks off trichomes. The
  resin sticks to your fingers and scissors

### Dry trim (trim after drying)

**What it is:** drying the buds with sugar leaves intact, then
trimming the dried buds before jarring.

**Pros:**
- **Slower drying** — the sugar leaves act as moisture shields,
  slowing evaporation and protecting the buds during the dry. This
  is the primary quality advantage
- **Better terpene preservation** — the slower dry preserves more
  volatile terpenes
- **Less trichome loss** — dry sugar leaves break away cleanly
  without the sticky resin pulling trichomes off the bud

**Cons:**
- **Harder to trim** — dried sugar leaves curl into the bud. You
  need sharper scissors and more patience
- **Two-step process** — dry, then trim, then jar. More total time
- **Slightly rougher appearance** — dry-trimmed buds often have
  small leaf remnants that wet-trimming would have caught

### The recommendation

**Dry trim + hang dry** is the highest-quality combination. The sugar
leaves slow the dry, the hanging provides even airflow, and the
extended timeline (10–14 days) allows complete chlorophyll breakdown
and maximum terpene preservation.

**Wet trim + rack dry** is the fastest, easiest combination but
produces lower-quality results. Use this if you're processing a large
harvest and speed matters more than peak quality, or if your
environment can't sustain a 10+ day dry.

## Monitoring the dry

### Daily checks

- **Bend the stems.** During the first few days, stems are flexible
  and bend without snapping. As drying progresses, smaller stems
  begin to snap. When the small stems (pencil-thin) snap cleanly
  instead of bending, the buds are approaching ready
- **Squeeze the buds gently.** They should feel dry on the outside
  but slightly spongy, not crispy. If the outer layer crunches,
  they've over-dried
- **Check humidity.** If ambient RH drops below 50%, the buds are
  drying too fast. Add a humidifier or slow the exhaust

### The jar test

When you think the buds are ready:
1. Place a few buds in a mason jar with a hygrometer
2. Seal the jar for 2–4 hours
3. Check the hygrometer reading:
   - **Above 70% RH:** too wet — remove from jar, continue drying
   - **65–70% RH:** almost ready — dry 1–2 more days
   - **60–65% RH:** perfect for curing — begin the cure
   - **Below 55% RH:** over-dried (see recovery below)

### Recovering over-dried buds

If you over-dried (buds are crispy, below 55% RH in the jar):
- Add a Boveda 62% humidity pack to the jar. It will slowly release
  moisture back into the buds over 24–48 hours
- Or place a fresh fan leaf in the jar for 2–4 hours (monitor closely
  — can overshoot)
- Over-dried buds won't fully recover their terpene profile, but the
  moisture rebalancing improves the smoke quality

## Common mistakes

1. **Drying too fast** — the most common error. If your buds smell
   like hay after 4 days, the dry was too fast. Lower the temperature
   and raise the humidity
2. **Fan blowing directly on buds** — direct airflow case-hardens the
   outside while the interior stays wet. Point fans at walls for
   indirect circulation
3. **Light exposure** — drying in a room with windows or lights on.
   Light degrades THC to CBN and accelerates uneven chlorophyll
   breakdown. Dry in darkness
4. **Over-drying** — leaving buds hanging too long in dry conditions.
   Check daily after day 7
5. **Crowding buds** — hanging branches too close together or stacking
   buds on racks traps moisture between them, creating mold pockets
