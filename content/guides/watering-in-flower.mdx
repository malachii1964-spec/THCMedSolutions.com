Watering in veg is forgiving — the plant is growing fast, roots are
expanding, and minor mistakes get absorbed. Flower is different. The root
system is mature, transpiration rates fluctuate with bud load, and
overwatering in late flower is one of the most common causes of bud rot.

This guide covers how watering changes across flower and how to read
what the plant is telling you.

## Why flower is different

In veg, the plant is building mass — stems, leaves, roots. Water demand
increases steadily as the plant grows. In flower, three things change:

1. **Root growth slows** — the plant redirects energy to bud production
2. **Transpiration fluctuates** — heavy bud loads increase demand, but
   dense canopies reduce airflow around the pot
3. **Nutrient ratios shift** — the plant needs more P and K, which
   changes EC and pH dynamics in the root zone

The result: watering schedules that worked perfectly in veg often need
adjustment around week 3 of flower.

## The dry-back method

The most reliable way to water in flower — any medium, any container.

### How it works

1. **Water to 10–20% runoff** (pour slowly until liquid exits the drain
   holes)
2. **Wait for the dry-back** — let the medium dry to a specific point
   before watering again
3. **Repeat** — the cycle is water → dry → water → dry

### How dry is "dry enough"?

| Medium | Water when... | How to check |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Soil/peat mix | Top 1–2 inches feel dry | Finger test, or lift the pot — dry pots feel noticeably lighter |
| Coco coir | Surface feels barely moist | Lift the pot — coco should never fully dry out |
| Fabric pots | Sides feel dry to the touch | Press the side of the pot — moist coco pushes back, dry doesn't |

**The lift test:** pick up (or nudge) the pot. After a few waterings,
you'll learn what "fully saturated" and "ready to water" feel like by
weight alone. This is the most reliable method regardless of medium.

### Dry-back timing through flower

| Phase | Typical dry-back period |
|-------|------------------------|
| Early flower (wk 1–3) | Every 2–3 days (similar to late veg) |
| Mid flower (wk 4–5) | Every 1.5–2.5 days (demand increases with bud weight) |
| Late flower (wk 6–8) | Every 2–4 days (transpiration can slow as plant finishes) |
| Final week | Reduce volume — some growers water at half volume with plain pH'd water |

These are guidelines, not rules. Your plant, medium, pot size,
temperature, and humidity all affect dry-back time. The lift test beats
any schedule.

## Runoff: what it tells you

### Why collect runoff

Runoff carries information about what's happening in the root zone. By
measuring the EC (conductivity) and pH of the runoff, you can catch
problems early.

### How to read runoff

| Measurement | Good range | What high means | What low means |
|------------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Runoff pH (soil) | 6.0–6.8 | Nutrient lockout developing (calcium, iron) | Possible over-acidification from excess fertilizer |
| Runoff pH (coco) | 5.5–6.3 | Salt buildup likely | Check input pH, probably too low |
| Runoff EC vs. input EC | Within 0.3–0.5 higher | Salts accumulating — increase runoff or reduce feed | Plant is eating everything — increase feed slightly |

**Rule of thumb:** if your runoff EC is more than 1.0 higher than your
input EC, the root zone has excess salts. Do a flush with plain pH'd
water at 2× pot volume, then resume feeding at a lower concentration.

### Runoff volume

Aim for 10–20% runoff every watering. This prevents salt buildup without
wasting nutrients. In coco, runoff is essential — coco holds cations
(Ca, Mg, K) on exchange sites, and without runoff, the buffering shifts
and nutrient availability changes.

In soil with dry amendments (organic), runoff is less important — you
don't want to wash out your top-dressed amendments. Water just until the
first drops appear, then stop.

## Overwatering in flower

### Why it's worse in flower

Overwatering in veg causes slow growth and sad, droopy leaves. In flower,
overwatering creates two serious problems:

1. **Bud rot** — excess humidity in the canopy + wet root zone =
   Botrytis heaven. Dense buds hold internal moisture; if the soil is
   also wet, the plant can't transpire and humidity around the buds
   climbs
2. **Root rot** — Pythium thrives in anaerobic, waterlogged media. In
   flower, the plant can't outgrow damaged roots like it can in veg

### Signs you're overwatering

- Leaves feel heavy and droop (not curling — flat drooping)
- The pot still feels heavy 3+ days after watering
- Lower leaves yellow and fall off faster than normal
- Fungus gnats appear (they breed in perpetually wet topsoil)
- The medium smells sour or swampy

### The fix

1. Stop watering until the pot feels light
2. Increase airflow — add a fan below the canopy, increase exhaust
3. If root rot is present (sour smell, brown roots), treat with
   hydrogen peroxide (3 ml of 3% H₂O₂ per liter of water) as a
   one-time drench
4. Going forward, water less volume more frequently rather than
   flooding the pot

## Underwatering in flower

Less common but equally harmful. A severely underwatered plant in flower
will:

- Droop dramatically (leaves go limp and point down)
- Produce smaller, airier buds
- Develop premature senescence (early yellowing and leaf death)
- In extreme cases, force early finish — the plant rushes to seed

**The fix:** water immediately and thoroughly. The plant will recover in
30–60 minutes if it hasn't been dry too long. Chronic underwatering
reduces yield more than almost any other factor.

## Watering and flushing

The "flush" debate is covered in our
[flushing guide](/guides/flushing-facts-and-myths), but the watering
angle is simple:

- **Corrective flush** (fixing salt buildup): 2× pot volume of plain
  pH'd water. This is a legitimate tool for fixing EC problems
- **Pre-harvest flush** (final 1–2 weeks of plain water): the science
  doesn't support improved flavor, but reducing nutrient input in the
  final week is common practice and doesn't hurt

Either way, don't flood the pot and leave it sitting in runoff. Drain
saucers within 15 minutes. Standing water is the fastest path to root
rot.

## Medium-specific notes

### Soil

Most forgiving medium. Soil buffers pH and holds nutrients. Water less
frequently, ensure good drainage (at least 25% perlite), and rely on the
lift test.

### Coco coir

Coco dries faster than soil and should never fully dry out — the
EC swings are harsh on roots. In flower, many coco growers water
daily or even twice daily with low-concentration fertigation. Always
water to runoff in coco.

### Fabric pots

Fabric pots air-prune roots (preventing circling) and dry faster than
plastic pots. They're excellent in flower — the increased oxygenation
promotes healthy root function. Downside: they dry fast, so you'll
water more frequently, especially in large tents with high PPFD.

## The simple protocol

If everything above feels overwhelming, use this:

1. Water when the pot feels light (lift test)
2. Water slowly until 10–15% runoff
3. Drain saucers within 15 minutes
4. Check runoff EC once a week — if it's climbing, increase runoff
   percentage next watering
5. In the last week, use plain pH'd water at half volume

That's it. Master the lift test and you'll avoid 90% of watering problems
in flower.
