A standard cannabis grow cycle is 4–5 months from seed to
harvest. That means one harvest per cycle, a gap while you
dry and cure, and another 4–5 months until the next. A
perpetual harvest system staggers multiple plants at different
growth stages so you're harvesting fresh flower every 4–6
weeks — continuous production instead of feast-and-famine.

The concept is simple: while one plant is finishing flower,
another is in mid-flower, another is in early flower, and
another is in veg. When you harvest the finished plant, you
flip the veg plant to flower, start a new seed or clone, and
the cycle continues.

## The minimum setup

### Two separate light zones

Perpetual growing requires separate spaces with different
light schedules:

**Veg zone (18/6):** where seedlings, clones, and vegetating
plants live under 18 hours of light.

**Flower zone (12/12):** where flowering plants produce buds
under 12 hours of light.

You cannot run perpetual in a single tent with one light
schedule. The light schedules are mutually exclusive.

### Common space configurations

| Setup | Veg space | Flower space | Harvest frequency |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 2-tent system | 2×2 tent (veg) | 4×4 tent (flower) | Every 4–5 weeks |
| Closet + tent | Small closet (veg) | 4×4 tent (flower) | Every 4–5 weeks |
| 2-room system | Small room (veg) | Large room (flower) | Every 3–4 weeks |
| Single tent, partitioned | Half for veg | Half for flower | Every 6–8 weeks |

**The veg space can be small.** A 2×2 tent or a closet shelf
with a small LED is sufficient — veg plants don't need much
space because they're young and compact. The flower space
should be your main tent.

### Light requirements

**Veg zone:** a 100–150W LED is sufficient for 2–4 young
plants in a 2×2 space. Seedlings and early veg plants don't
need the intensity of flowering plants.

**Flower zone:** your main grow light at full power (300–600W
depending on tent size). This is where the plant spends most
of its life and energy demand.

## Scheduling the rotation

### The 4-plant rotation (most common)

With an 8-week flowering strain:

| Week | Plant A | Plant B | Plant C | Plant D |
|------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Flower wk 1 | Flower wk 5 | Veg wk 4 | Seed/clone |
| 2 | Flower wk 2 | Flower wk 6 | Veg wk 5 | Veg wk 1 |
| 3 | Flower wk 3 | Flower wk 7 | Veg wk 6 | Veg wk 2 |
| 4 | Flower wk 4 | Flower wk 8 → harvest | Flip to flower | Veg wk 3 |
| 5 | Flower wk 5 | Dry/cure | Flower wk 1 | Veg wk 4 |
| 6 | Flower wk 6 | — | Flower wk 2 | Veg wk 5 |
| 7 | Flower wk 7 | New seed/clone | Flower wk 3 | Veg wk 6 |
| 8 | Flower wk 8 → harvest | Veg wk 1 | Flower wk 4 | Flip to flower |

**Result:** harvesting one plant every 4 weeks. Continuous
supply of fresh flower after the initial ramp-up.

### The 3-plant rotation (simpler)

For growers with less space. Stagger 3 plants with an 8–9
week flowering strain, harvesting every 3 weeks.

### Strain timing alignment

**This is the key challenge.** If Plant A is a 7-week strain
and Plant B is a 10-week strain, the rotation doesn't align
cleanly.

**Solutions:**
- **Run the same strain.** Every plant finishes at the same
  time (relative to its flip date), making scheduling
  predictable. This is the easiest approach
- **Choose strains with similar flower times.** An 8-week and
  a 9-week strain align closely enough. An 8-week and a
  12-week strain don't
- **Adjust veg time to compensate.** If Strain B flowers 2
  weeks longer, give it 2 weeks less veg time. The total
  cycle length stays aligned

## Clones vs. seeds in perpetual

### Clones (recommended for perpetual)

Taking clones from a mother plant (or from your current veg
plants) provides:
- Identical genetics = identical flower time = predictable
  scheduling
- No sexing needed (all female)
- Faster startup than seed (skip germination)
- Consistent quality and yield across harvests

**The mother plant approach:** keep one mother plant in the veg
zone permanently. Take 2–3 clones every time you harvest a
flowering plant. The clones root while the next plant finishes
flower, then move to veg, then flip to flower. Perpetual loop.

See our [cloning guide](/guides/cloning-and-propagation) and
[mother plant guide](/guides/mother-plant-maintenance).

### Seeds

Using seeds in a perpetual system is possible but less
predictable:
- Each seed is a unique individual (variable flower time,
  height, yield)
- Germination adds 1–2 weeks to the cycle
- Non-feminized seeds require sexing (males waste space)

If using seeds, use feminized seeds from a single breeder's
line to minimize variation.

## Space management

### Veg zone utilization

The veg zone runs continuously. At any time, it contains:
- 1–2 plants in active veg (preparing to flip)
- 0–2 fresh clones or seedlings (next generation)
- Optionally: a mother plant (permanent resident)

**The veg zone bottleneck:** if your flowering space holds 2
plants and your flower time is 8 weeks, you need a new plant
ready to flip every 4 weeks. Your veg zone must produce one
trained, ready-to-flip plant every 4 weeks.

### Flower zone utilization

The flower zone should always be full:
- As one plant is harvested, the next should be ready to move
  in (ideally the same day or within a few days)
- Dead time in the flower zone (empty space with lights
  running) is wasted electricity
- If your flower zone holds 2 plants, stagger so they
  alternate harvest dates

### The transition day

When you harvest a plant from the flower zone:

1. Harvest the finished plant
2. Clean the area (remove dead leaves, wipe surfaces)
3. Move the veg plant into the flower zone
4. Change the light schedule to 12/12 (or the veg plant
   joins an existing 12/12 schedule if other plants are
   already flowering)
5. Start a new clone/seed in the veg zone
6. Dry the harvest in a dedicated drying space (NOT in the
   flower tent — the light schedule conflicts)

### Drying logistics

In perpetual growing, the flower tent is always occupied —
you can't dry in it. You need a separate drying space:
- A small closet with a fan and carbon filter
- A cardboard box with controlled airflow
- A drying rack in a dark, cool room
- A dedicated drying tent (2×2 is sufficient for 1–2 plants)

See our [drying guide](/guides/drying-room-setup).

## Nutrient management in perpetual

### Separate feeding schedules

Each plant in the rotation is at a different stage, requiring
different nutrient ratios:
- Veg plants: higher nitrogen
- Early flower: transition feeding
- Mid-flower: heavy bloom (P/K dominant)
- Late flower: reduced feeding or plain water

**Organization is essential.** Label each plant with its start
date and current week. Mix nutrients per plant, not per batch
(unless multiple plants are at the same stage).

### Reservoir management (hydro)

If running hydro with a shared reservoir, all plants must be
at the same stage (shared nutrient solution). This limits
perpetual hydro to SOG-style setups where all plants flip
simultaneously but are replaced in waves.

Individual reservoirs per plant allow true perpetual scheduling
but increase equipment and management complexity.

## Common mistakes

1. **No separate veg space** — trying to run perpetual in one
   tent with one light schedule doesn't work. You need 18/6
   for veg and 12/12 for flower simultaneously. Two separate
   spaces are the minimum
2. **Strains with wildly different flower times** — an 8-week
   indica and a 12-week sativa in the same rotation creates
   scheduling chaos. Use the same strain or strains within
   1 week of each other
3. **No drying space** — the flower tent is always occupied.
   Trying to dry in the flower tent means either stopping the
   rotation or drying under 12/12 light (which degrades
   cannabinoids and terpenes). Plan a separate drying area
4. **Not having plants ready to flip** — the entire system
   depends on having a veg plant ready when the flower slot
   opens. If your clone failed or your seed didn't germinate,
   the flower space sits empty. Always have a backup plant in
   veg
5. **Overcomplicating the schedule** — start with 3–4 plants
   of the same strain. Master the rotation before adding
   strain variety or increasing plant count
