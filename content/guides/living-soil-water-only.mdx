Water-only living soil is exactly what it sounds like: you build
a soil mix with enough nutrition and biological activity to feed
a cannabis plant through its entire lifecycle using only water.
No bottled nutrients, no pH adjustment (the biology buffers it),
no feeding schedules. You water when the pot's light, and the
soil does the rest.

It's the simplest way to grow cannabis once you've built the mix
— but building the mix correctly is where the work lives. Get the
recipe right and the first grow is almost hands-off. Get it wrong
and you'll see deficiencies by week 4.

## Why water-only works

Traditional growing treats soil as an inert sponge — you add
nutrients in liquid form, the plant absorbs them directly. Water-
only living soil works differently:

1. **Slow-release amendments** (bone meal, kelp meal, rock dust)
   break down over weeks, providing a steady supply of nutrients
   — not a feast-or-famine cycle
2. **Microbial populations** (bacteria, fungi, protozoa) process
   organic amendments into plant-available forms. The biology IS
   the feeding system
3. **Mycorrhizal fungi** extend the root system's reach 100–1000×,
   accessing phosphorus and micronutrients the roots alone can't
   reach
4. **The soil buffers pH naturally** — carbonates in oyster shell
   and mineral amendments maintain pH in the 6.2–6.8 range without
   intervention

The key insight: you're not feeding the plant. You're building a
living ecosystem that feeds the plant.

biology behind this system.

## The recipe

This recipe builds enough soil for approximately 30 gallons —
enough for two 15-gallon pots or three 10-gallon pots. Scale
proportionally.

### Base mix (equal parts by volume)

| Component | Volume | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Sphagnum peat moss | 10 gallons | Water retention, structure |
| Aeration (perlite OR pumice) | 10 gallons | Drainage, air space |
| Quality compost or worm castings | 10 gallons | Biology, nutrition, structure |

**Peat vs. coco:** peat is preferred for living soil because it's
naturally acidic (pH 4.5–5.5), which is buffered up to optimal
range by the mineral amendments. Coco starts at neutral pH and
can go too alkaline with carbonate amendments. If using coco,
reduce oyster shell by half.

**Compost quality matters:** the compost is where most of your
starting biology comes from. Quality vermicompost (worm castings)
from a reputable source is ideal — it's the richest source of
beneficial microbes available to home growers. Avoid cheap
"compost" from garden centers that may be partially decomposed
or have poor microbial diversity.

### Mineral amendments (per cubic foot of base mix)

| Amendment | Amount | Provides |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Glacial rock dust | 1/2 cup | Broad-spectrum minerals (Ca, Fe, Mg, Si + trace) |
| Basalt rock dust | 1/2 cup | Silica, iron, magnesium |
| Gypsum (calcium sulfate) | 1/4 cup | Calcium + sulfur (doesn't alter pH) |
| Oyster shell flour | 1/4 cup | Calcium + pH buffer |

### Organic nutrient amendments (per cubic foot of base mix)

| Amendment | Amount | Primary nutrients |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Neem seed meal | 1/2 cup | Nitrogen + pest deterrent |
| Kelp meal | 1/2 cup | Potassium, micronutrients, growth hormones |
| Fish bone meal | 1/4 cup | Phosphorus, calcium |
| Crustacean meal (crab/shrimp) | 1/4 cup | Nitrogen, calcium, chitin (immune trigger) |
| Alfalfa meal | 1/4 cup | Nitrogen, triacontanol (growth stimulant) |

### Optional boosters

| Amendment | Amount | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Malted barley (ground) | 1/4 cup | Enzymes that break down organic matter |
| Humic acid granules | 2 tbsp | Improve nutrient availability |
| Azomite | 1/4 cup | 70+ trace minerals |
| Insect frass | 1/4 cup | Chitin + nitrogen + beneficial bacteria |

### Biological inoculants

| Product | Application | Purpose |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Mycorrhizal inoculant (endomycorrhizae) | Dust on roots at transplant | Phosphorus delivery, root extension |
| Recharge or Great White | Mix into soil at 1 tsp/gal | Diverse microbial population |
| Worm castings (additional) | Top-dress 1/4" layer monthly | Ongoing biology replenishment |

## Building and cooking the soil

### Mixing

1. Lay out a tarp on a flat surface (garage, patio)
2. Dump the base mix components and blend thoroughly
3. Add mineral amendments and mix until evenly distributed
4. Add organic amendments and mix again
5. Add biological inoculants and blend gently

### The cook (mandatory)

**Do not plant directly into freshly mixed soil.** Raw organic
amendments can burn roots as they decompose (the process generates
heat and produces ammonia). The soil needs to "cook" — a 2–4 week
microbial decomposition period:

1. Moisten the mixed soil to about 50% saturation (squeeze test:
   a handful of soil should form a ball that crumbles easily —
   not dripping wet, not dusty dry)
2. Place in a large container (storage tote, trash can, or the
   final growing pot)
3. Cover loosely (not airtight — the biology needs oxygen)
4. Keep in a warm location (65–80°F)
5. Turn or stir every 3–5 days to introduce oxygen
6. After 2–4 weeks, the soil will smell earthy and pleasant (not
   ammonia-like). It's ready to plant

**If it smells like ammonia:** the nitrogen amendments are still
breaking down. Continue cooking — it needs more time.

**If it smells sour or rotten:** the soil has gone anaerobic.
Turn it more frequently and ensure it's not too wet.

## Pot size

Water-only soil needs enough volume to contain a full cycle's
worth of nutrition. If the pot is too small, the plant will
exhaust the nutrients before harvest:

| Pot size | Suitability |
|----------|-------------|
| 5 gallons | Short autoflowers only. Will likely need supplementation in late flower |
| 7 gallons | Small autoflowers or short-veg photoperiods. Tight but workable |
| 10 gallons | The minimum for a full photoperiod cycle without supplementation |
| 15 gallons | Comfortable for most photoperiod grows. Recommended starting size |
| 20–30 gallons | Optimal for large plants or long veg periods. Virtually never runs out |

**Recommendation:** 15-gallon fabric pots for most growers. The
extra soil volume provides insurance against deficiencies and makes
the system more forgiving.

## Growing in water-only soil

### Watering

This is the entire ongoing maintenance:
- Water when the pot feels light (lift it)
- Use dechlorinated water (let tap water sit for 24 hours, or use
  a carbon filter). Chlorine kills the beneficial microbes you
  spent weeks building
- Do NOT pH adjust the water (unless your tap water is extreme —
  below 5.0 or above 8.0). The soil biology buffers pH
  automatically through carbonate chemistry
- Water thoroughly until 10–20% runoff
- Do not feed nutrients. The soil is the food

### Mulching

Cover the soil surface with 1–2 inches of mulch:
- **Straw** — cheap, effective, decomposes slowly
- **Dried leaves** — free if you have trees
- **Cover crop (living mulch)** — white clover is ideal

Mulch prevents the surface from drying out, protects surface
biology from UV light, and feeds the soil as it decomposes.

### Top-dressing (if needed)

If the plant shows signs of deficiency in late flower (yellowing
beyond the normal fade, very pale new growth), top-dress with:
- **Worm castings:** 1/4 inch layer on the soil surface, water in
- **Kelp meal:** 1–2 tablespoons per 10 gallons, scratched into
  the surface
- **Craft blend** (Dr. Earth, Down To Earth, Build-A-Soil craft
  blend): follow package directions

Top-dressing takes 5–10 days to become available (the biology must
process it), so it's a correction for gradual deficiencies, not an
emergency fix. If you need fast nitrogen, a compost tea with fish
hydrolysate can deliver results in 2–3 days.

### Compost tea

Actively aerated compost tea (AACT) is a supplemental biology
boost, not a replacement for the soil recipe:
1. 2 cups worm castings in a mesh bag
2. 1 tbsp unsulfured blackstrap molasses
3. 5 gallons dechlorinated water
4. Aerate with an aquarium pump for 24–36 hours
5. Apply immediately as a root drench

Use once every 2–4 weeks during veg. Monthly in flower. See our
[soil food web guide](/guides/soil-food-web) for the full protocol.

## Recycling the soil

The best part of living soil: **you never throw it away.** After
harvest, the soil is re-amended and reused. Each cycle, the soil
gets better — microbial populations mature, soil structure
improves, and the nutrient cycling becomes more efficient.

### Post-harvest re-amend

1. Remove the root ball from the pot — or if using no-till, chop
   the stalk at soil level and leave the roots to decompose
2. Remove any large root pieces
3. Add fresh amendments at **half the original rate** (the soil
   still contains residual nutrients)
4. Add a fresh layer of worm castings (1–2 cups per 10 gallons)
5. Moisten, cover, and let cook for 1–2 weeks
6. Plant the next cycle

### No-till approach

The ultimate living soil method:
1. After harvest, chop the stalk at soil level
2. Top-dress with worm castings and amendment blend
3. Plant a cover crop (clover, alfalfa)
4. Let the cover crop grow for 2–4 weeks
5. Chop and drop the cover crop (cut at soil level, leave roots)
6. Plant the next cannabis directly into the same soil
7. Never disturb the root zone — the fungal networks persist cycle
   to cycle

No-till soil improves with every cycle. Growers running the same
soil for 5+ cycles report their best harvests from the most mature
soil.

## Troubleshooting

### "My plant is showing deficiency in veg"

Possible causes:
- **Soil didn't cook long enough** — amendments haven't broken
  down yet. Top-dress with worm castings and compost tea
- **Pot too small** — not enough nutrient reserve. Transplant to a
  larger pot with fresh amended soil
- **Water pH too extreme** — while living soil buffers, tap water
  below 5.0 or above 8.0 can overwhelm the buffer. Test your
  source water

### "My plant runs out of food in late flower"

Common with 10-gallon or smaller pots. Solutions:
- Top-dress with a flowering amendment (high-phosphorus: bone meal
  or bat guano)
- Apply compost tea with kelp and fish hydrolysate
- Next cycle, use a larger pot or increase the initial amendment
  rate by 25%

### "Fungus gnats"

Living soil's organic surface is attractive to fungus gnats. The
larvae feed on organic matter (and root hairs in severe
infestations). Management:
- **Yellow sticky traps** — catch adults
- **BTI (Mosquito Bits)** — Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis
  kills larvae. Sprinkle on surface and water in
- **Let the surface dry between waterings** — gnat larvae need
  moist conditions in the top 1 inch
- **Diatomaceous earth** — sprinkle on the surface as a physical
  barrier

## Common mistakes

1. **Skipping the cook** — planting in raw, unmixed amendments
   burns roots. The 2–4 week cook is not optional
2. **Using too-small pots** — 5-gallon pots don't hold enough
   nutrition for a full photoperiod cycle. Use 10+ gallons
3. **Adding bottled nutrients** — synthetic salt nutrients kill
   the biology you built. The whole point of living soil is that
   the biology feeds the plant. Adding liquid ferts shortcuts the
   biology and can cause salt buildup that damages the microbial
   population
4. **Using chlorinated water** — chlorine is a biocide. It
   kills the bacteria and fungi that make this system work.
   Always dechlorinate
5. **Tilling between cycles** — disturbing the soil destroys
   fungal networks that took months to establish. Use no-till
   practices: chop and drop, top-dress, and replant without
   turning the soil
6. **Expecting instant results** — living soil takes 2–3 cycles
   to reach peak performance as microbial populations mature.
   Your second and third grows in the same soil will outperform
   your first
