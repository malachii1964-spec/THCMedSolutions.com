The best time to deal with pests is before you have them. Once
spider mites or thrips are established in a flowering cannabis
grow, your options range from bad to worse: spray chemicals on
buds you'll smoke, lose yield to pest damage, or harvest early.
Every one of these outcomes is avoidable with prevention.

This guide isn't about identifying and killing pests (see our
[pest identification guide](/guides/pest-identification-guide)
for that). It's about building systems that keep them from
establishing in the first place.

## The three lines of defense

### Line 1: Keep them out (exclusion)

Most indoor pest problems start with pests hitchhiking IN on
clones, clothes, pets, soil, or outdoor air.

**Clone quarantine:**
- Inspect every clone under a loupe before bringing it into your
  grow space. Check leaf undersides for mites, thrips, and eggs
- Quarantine new clones in a separate space for 5–7 days before
  introducing to the main grow
- Consider a preventive dip: submerge the entire clone (leaves
  and all) in a solution of 2ml/L neem oil + 1ml/L insecticidal
  soap for 30 seconds, then let dry
- See our [clone guide](/guides/cannabis-from-clone-guide)

**Personal hygiene:**
- Don't enter the grow room after being outdoors (especially
  gardening) without changing clothes
- Don't let pets into the grow space — dog and cat fur carries
  mites
- Wash hands before handling plants
- If you visit another grower's space, change clothes and shower
  before entering yours

**Grow room sealing:**
- Screen intake vents with 340+ mesh (fine enough to block most
  insect species)
- Seal gaps around duct penetrations with foam or silicone
- Keep the tent zipped except when actively working inside

**Soil and medium:**
- Quality commercial soil is usually pest-free. Discount or
  outdoor soil may contain fungus gnat larvae, mites, or eggs
- If reusing soil, bake it (180°F for 30 minutes) or solarize it
  (sealed in a black bag in direct sun for 2–3 days) to kill eggs
  and larvae
- Store unused soil in sealed containers — open bags attract gnats

### Line 2: Make the environment hostile (environmental control)

Different pests thrive under different environmental conditions.
Optimizing your environment for the plant simultaneously makes it
less hospitable for most pests:

| Pest | Preferred conditions | Prevention |
|------|---------------------|------------|
| Spider mites | Hot (>80°F), dry (<40% RH) | Keep humidity 50–60% in veg, temps below 82°F |
| Thrips | Warm (75–85°F), moderate humidity | Difficult to control environmentally — use other lines |
| Fungus gnats | Wet soil surface, warm, organic matter | Let soil surface dry between waterings |
| Broad mites | Warm (70–80°F), high humidity | Hard to prevent environmentally — quarantine is key |
| Powdery mildew | Moderate temp (60–80°F), stagnant air, high humidity | Airflow + lower humidity in flower |
| Whiteflies | Warm (75–85°F), poor airflow | Good ventilation, sticky traps |

**Airflow is universally preventive.** Most pests and pathogens
prefer still air. An oscillating fan at canopy height deters
flying insects and disrupts the microenvironment that pests
prefer on leaf surfaces.

### Line 3: Maintain a biological defense (beneficials and preventive sprays)

Even with exclusion and environmental control, some pests will
eventually find their way in. A biological defense layer catches
them before they establish.

## Preventive spray schedule

A preventive spray program applies organic, plant-safe products
on a regular schedule BEFORE pests appear. This is the core of
cannabis IPM (Integrated Pest Management):

### Vegetative phase

| Week | Treatment | Target |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Every 7–10 days | Neem oil (cold-pressed, 2ml/L + drop of soap) | Mites, aphids, whiteflies, thrips |
| Every 14 days | BT (Bacillus thuringiensis) spray | Caterpillars (outdoor) |
| Every 14 days | Potassium bicarbonate spray | Powdery mildew prevention |

**Application:** spray all leaf surfaces (top AND bottom) in the
evening (lights off or dim). Neem under strong light can burn
leaves.

### Early flower (weeks 1–3)

| Week | Treatment | Target |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Week 1–2 | Final neem oil spray (STOP after week 2 of flower) | Residual pest deterrent |
| Week 1–3 | BT spray (outdoor grows) | Caterpillars |

**CRITICAL: stop spraying neem oil after week 2 of flower.** Neem
leaves residue on buds that affects taste and may contain compounds
you don't want to inhale. After week 2, the buds are developing
and no sprays should contact them.

### Mid-to-late flower (weeks 4+)

**No sprays on buds.** If you have a pest problem this late, your
options are:
- **Beneficial insects** (predatory mites, parasitic wasps) — they
  don't leave residue
- **Manual removal** — physically pick off pests with tweezers
  or a small vacuum
- **Harvest early** if the infestation is severe and the buds are
  near maturity
- **Accept minor damage** rather than spraying chemicals on flower
  you'll consume

## Beneficial insects

Beneficial insects are predators that eat pest species. They're
completely chemical-free, leave no residue, and self-maintain if
conditions are right:

### For spider mites

**Phytoseiulus persimilis** — the gold standard mite predator.
Each P. persimilis eats 5–7 spider mites per day. Release when
mite populations are low (prevention) or at the first sign of
mites.

- Release rate: 2–5 per square foot
- Conditions: needs >60% RH to thrive (low humidity = poor
  survival)
- Limitation: only eats spider mites. When mites are gone,
  P. persimilis dies or migrates

**Amblyseius californicus** — a generalist predatory mite that
survives on pollen when prey is absent. Better for prevention
because it persists in the environment without prey:

- Release rate: 1–2 per square foot
- Survives at lower humidity than P. persimilis
- Slower predation but maintains a resident population

### For thrips

**Amblyseius cucumeris** — predatory mite that feeds on thrips
larvae. Release in sachets (slow-release bags hung on plants) for
continuous protection.

- Release rate: 1 sachet per plant or 50 mites per plant
- Targets larvae only — combine with sticky traps for adults

**Orius insidiosus (minute pirate bug)** — feeds on adult thrips,
aphids, and mite eggs. Excellent generalist predator.

### For fungus gnats

**Hypoaspis miles (Stratiolaelaps scimitus)** — soil-dwelling
predatory mite that feeds on fungus gnat larvae in the medium.

- Release rate: 1 per 10 sq inches of soil surface
- Establish once and they self-maintain as long as the medium
  stays moist
- Also feeds on thrips pupae (bonus)

**BTI (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis) — Mosquito Bits:**
not technically an insect, but a biological larvicide. Sprinkle
on the soil surface and water in. The bacteria kill fungus gnat
larvae on contact. Reapply every 7–10 days.

## Yellow sticky traps

The simplest and cheapest pest monitoring tool:

- Hang yellow sticky traps at canopy height — 1 per 4×4 area
- **Check weekly.** The trap tells you what's flying in your
  space before populations become visible on plants
- **Fungus gnats:** small, dark flies with long legs. A few is
  normal in soil grows. A trap covered in them means a population
  problem
- **Thrips:** tiny, elongated, tan-colored insects. Even a few on
  a trap means they're in the canopy
- **Whiteflies:** tiny white moth-like insects. Cluster on the
  underside of leaves

Sticky traps are for monitoring, not control. They catch a
fraction of flying adults — the larvae and eggs are the real
population, and those are on the plants and in the medium.

## Clean-room habits

Simple habits that prevent the vast majority of pest introductions:

1. **Dedicate grow room clothes** — a pair of shoes, shorts, and
   a shirt that only go into the grow room. Change before entering
2. **No outdoor plants in the grow room** — houseplants, fresh
   flowers, and especially outdoor garden plants carry pests
3. **Clean between grows** — wipe down the tent interior with
   10% bleach or hydrogen peroxide between cycles. Wash pots.
   Inspect and clean equipment
4. **Don't reuse soil without treating it** — unless it's a
   living soil system, old soil harbors eggs and larvae. See our
   [living soil guide](/guides/living-soil-water-only)
5. **Inspect plants weekly** — a 2-minute visual check with a
   loupe catches problems before they spread. Look at leaf
   undersides at the top of the plant (spider mites start there)
   and lower leaves (thrips often start below)

## The prevention calendar

| Timing | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Before every grow | Clean tent, pots, equipment. Inspect soil/medium |
| Every new plant introduction | Quarantine 5–7 days, inspect with loupe, preventive dip |
| Every time you enter the grow room | Clean hands, dedicated clothes |
| Weekly (veg) | Visual inspection, check sticky traps, preventive neem spray |
| Biweekly (veg) | Beneficial insect assessment (release if population is low) |
| Weeks 1–2 of flower | Final neem spray, last chemical-based treatment |
| Weeks 3+ of flower | Visual inspection only, beneficials if needed, NO SPRAYS |
| Between grows | Full tent cleanup, equipment sterilization |

## Common mistakes

1. **Waiting for visible pests before taking action** — by the
   time you see spider mites with your naked eye, there are
   thousands. Prevention starts before you see anything
2. **Spraying buds in late flower** — neem, insecticidal soap,
   and especially pesticides leave residue you'll inhale. No
   sprays on buds after week 2 of flower
3. **Bringing outdoor clones straight into the grow room** — this
   is the #1 vector for spider mites. Always quarantine and
   inspect
4. **Ignoring fungus gnats because "they're harmless"** — adult
   gnats are mostly harmless. Larvae feed on root hairs and can
   stunt seedlings significantly. BTI in the medium kills larvae
5. **Only treating the visible pest** — if you see spider mites
   on one plant, check ALL plants. Treat the entire grow space,
   not just the affected plant
6. **Skipping the preventive schedule because "nothing's wrong"**
   — the schedule works because it's preventive. Stopping it
   because you don't see pests is like stopping sunscreen because
   you're not burned
