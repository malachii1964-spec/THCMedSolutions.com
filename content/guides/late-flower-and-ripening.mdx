You've made it through the stretch, the bulk-up phase, and the
mid-flower nutrient demands. Your plant is covered in frosty, swelling
buds. Now comes the hardest part of growing cannabis: not cutting it
down too early.

Late flower (roughly weeks 7–10 for most strains, 9–12+ for long-
flowering sativas) is where potency, density, bag appeal, and terpene
complexity reach their peak — but only if you let the plant finish on
its own schedule. This guide covers what's happening in the plant
during these final weeks and what you should (and shouldn't) be doing.

## What's happening in the plant

### Weeks 7–8: the swell

This is typically the last major growth phase. Calyxes are stacking,
buds are fattening visibly day by day, and trichome production is
ramping up. The buds are gaining the final 20–30% of their dry weight
during these two weeks.

**What to watch for:**
- Pistils are mostly white with some starting to recede and turn
  orange/amber. If 90%+ of pistils are still white, you have
  significant time left
- Trichomes are predominantly clear to milky under magnification
- Fan leaves may begin to yellow from the bottom up — this is normal
  and expected as the plant cannibalizes stored nutrients for bud
  production
- Buds feel dense when squeezed but still have give

### Weeks 8–9: the ripen

Growth slows. The plant shifts from building new calyxes to ripening
existing ones. Trichome heads swell with cannabinoid-rich resin. This
is the highest-value period — rushing through it is the most common
mistake in home growing.

**What to watch for:**
- Pistils are 50–70% orange/amber and have curled back into the buds
- Trichomes transitioning from clear → milky (cloudy). This is when
  THC concentration is building
- More fan leaves yellowing and dropping — the plant is pulling mobile
  nutrients from leaves to fuel bud ripening
- Bud aroma reaches peak intensity

### Weeks 9–10+: the finish line

The final ripening stage. Most of the calyx swelling is done. The plant
is now focused on resin production and terpene development.

**What to watch for:**
- Trichomes are mostly milky with 10–30% amber (the harvest window —
  see below)
- Pistils are 70–90% orange/amber and fully receded
- Some lower fan leaves have dropped completely
- Buds feel rock-hard and dense
- Foxtailing may appear on the top colas (strain-dependent — some
  strains naturally foxtail; light-stress foxtailing is different)

## Nutrient management in late flower

### The taper

Most nutrient lines have a "late bloom" or "ripening" phase that reduces
nitrogen and increases phosphorus and potassium. Follow this — but don't
overdo it.

**Weeks 7–8:**
- Continue your bloom nutrients at full strength (or the late-bloom
  formula if your line has one)
- This is NOT the time to reduce feed. The plant is still building mass
  and needs fuel
- Potassium is especially important now — it drives calyx swelling and
  sugar production

**Weeks 8–9:**
- Begin reducing nitrogen to near zero. Nitrogen promotes vegetative
  growth, which you don't want at this stage. Some yellowing of fan
  leaves is expected and beneficial — it means the plant is redirecting
  resources
- Maintain phosphorus and potassium at full strength
- Continue cal-mag if you're in coco

**Weeks 9–10 (the final week or two):**
- This is where the "to flush or not to flush" debate lives

### The flushing debate

**Traditional wisdom:** flush (water with plain pH'd water) for the last
1–2 weeks to "remove nutrients from the buds" and produce a cleaner,
smoother smoke.

**The science:** a 2020 study by RX Green Technologies found no
measurable difference in mineral content, potency, or smoke quality
between flushed and unflushed cannabis. The plant's vascular system
doesn't work like a pipe you can rinse out — nutrients bound in plant
tissue stay bound.

**The practical middle ground:** reduce nutrients to 25–50% strength
in the final week, then give plain water for the last 2–3 days. This
isn't because flushing "works" — it's because:
1. The plant genuinely needs less food when growth has stopped
2. You save nutrient solution (why feed a finished plant?)
3. A dry-back in the final 48 hours may slightly increase resin
   production as a mild stress response

See our [flushing guide](/guides/flushing-facts-myths) for the full
science breakdown.

## Reading trichomes: when to harvest

This is the single most important skill in late flower. Bud size,
pistil color, and breeder-listed flower times are rough guides.
Trichomes are the definitive answer.

### What you need

A jeweler's loupe (30x–60x) or a USB microscope ($15–30 on Amazon).
Phone cameras with macro mode work but are harder to hold steady. A
USB microscope with a stand is the easiest tool for beginners.

### What to look at

Check trichomes on the **buds** (not the sugar leaves — sugar leaf
trichomes mature faster and will mislead you). Check the middle of the
cola, not the very top (which gets more light stress) or the very
bottom (which lags behind).

### The trichome color guide

| Trichome color | What it means | Effect |
|---------------|--------------|--------|
| Clear (transparent) | Immature — cannabinoids still developing | Low potency, racy/anxious effect |
| Milky (cloudy white) | Peak THC — maximum cannabinoid concentration | Energetic, cerebral, peak potency |
| Amber (honey/orange) | THC degrading to CBN — sedative compound increasing | Heavier, sleepier, couch-lock effect |
| Mixed milky/amber | The harvest window | Depends on ratio (see below) |

### Dialing your harvest for effect

The ratio of milky to amber trichomes lets you customize the effect
of your harvest:

- **All milky, 0% amber:** maximum THC, most energetic effect. Some
  growers harvest here for sativa-leaning strains they want racy and
  heady
- **Mostly milky, 10–15% amber:** the sweet spot for most growers.
  Peak potency with the first hints of body effect. Best overall bag
  appeal and balanced high
- **50/50 milky/amber:** heavy body effect, strong sedation. Good for
  nighttime strains or medical use (pain, insomnia)
- **Mostly amber:** heavy CBN content, strongly sedative. Not
  recommended unless you specifically want a sleep aid — this is
  technically past peak potency

**The recommendation:** harvest when you see 10–20% amber trichomes on
the buds. This catches the plant at or very near peak potency while
adding enough body to balance the cerebral effect.

## Environmental tuning for late flower

### Temperature drops

Lowering nighttime temperatures in the final 2–3 weeks can:
- Trigger anthocyanin production in strains with purple/color genetics
  (cosmetic — bag appeal, not potency)
- Slow evaporation of volatile terpenes, potentially preserving more
  aromatics into the cured product
- Signal "autumn" to the plant, which some growers believe accelerates
  final ripening

**Target:** drop night temps to 60–65°F (from the typical 68–72°F). A
10°F day/night differential is ideal. Don't go below 55°F — that stalls
metabolic processes and can damage tissue.

### Humidity control

Late flower is the highest mold-risk period. Buds are dense, packed
with moisture, and surrounded by sugar leaves that trap humidity.

**Target RH:** 40–45% in the final 2–3 weeks. Lower than mid-flower
(which runs 45–55%) because:
- Dense buds create microclimates inside that are 10–15% higher RH
  than the room average
- Botrytis (bud rot) thrives above 60% RH inside the bud
- A drier environment also causes mild stress that may boost resin
  production

Run a dehumidifier if needed. Check the insides of your largest, most
dense colas every 2–3 days by gently pulling them apart and looking for
any gray, brown, or mushy tissue inside.

### Light in late flower

**Don't change your light schedule.** 12/12 until chop day. Some
growers drop to 11/13 in the final week — this is optional and the
evidence for its benefit is anecdotal at best.

**Don't increase light intensity.** Late-flower buds are vulnerable to
light bleaching and foxtailing. If you've been running your light at
full power, keep it there. If you haven't had issues, leave it alone.
This is not the time to experiment.

### The 48-hour dark period

Some growers put the plant in complete darkness for 48 hours before
harvest, claiming it spikes trichome production as a final stress
response. The evidence is entirely anecdotal — no controlled study
has confirmed or denied it.

If you want to try it: it won't hurt. Skip the last light cycle and
chop 48 hours later. At worst, you lost 2 days of light (which barely
matters when growth has stopped). At best, you get a marginal resin
bump.

## Common late-flower mistakes

### 1. Harvesting too early

The number one mistake. Growers get excited, the buds look done to the
naked eye, and they chop 2 weeks too soon. Those last 2 weeks are where
15–20% of the final dry weight and significant terpene complexity
develop. Use a loupe. Check trichomes. Don't trust breeder timelines
(they're best-case estimates under ideal conditions).

### 2. Panic-feeding at yellowing

Fan leaves yellowing and dropping in late flower is **normal and
desirable**. The plant is consuming its own stored nutrients. Adding
extra nitrogen at this point forces the plant back into vegetative
mode — you'll see new leaf growth on buds (foxtailing, single-blade
leaves, nanners). Let it fade.

### 3. Ignoring humidity

A single mold outbreak in week 9 can destroy 30–50% of a harvest in 48
hours. Bud rot starts inside the densest colas where you can't see it.
By the time you spot gray mold on the outside, the inside is gone.
Keep RH at 40–45%, maintain airflow, and physically inspect your
fattest buds every other day.

### 4. Moving to harvest without a plan

Before you chop, have your drying space ready, cleaned, and
climate-controlled. Have your trimming tools clean. Have jars or
containers ready for the cure. Scrambling to set up a drying room
after you've already cut the plant leads to rushed drying, which
ruins months of work.
