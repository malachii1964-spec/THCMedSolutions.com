Most edible guides stop at "make cannabutter and add it to brownies."
That works, but it treats cannabis cooking as a recipe instead of a
process. If your edibles are inconsistent — one batch puts you on the
moon, the next does nothing — the problem isn't the recipe. It's
imprecise decarboxylation, uncontrolled infusion, or missing dosing
math.

This guide covers the science behind each step so you can produce
edibles with predictable, consistent potency every time.

## Decarboxylation: the conversion that makes edibles work

### Why you have to decarb

Raw cannabis contains THCA, not THC. THCA is non-psychoactive —
eating raw flower does nothing. Heat removes a carboxyl group
(CO₂) from THCA, converting it to THC. This is decarboxylation.

When you smoke or vaporize cannabis, decarb happens instantly from
the flame or vaporizer temperature. In cooking, you need to
deliberately apply heat to the raw material before or during
infusion.

### The decarb curve

Decarboxylation is a time-temperature reaction. Too little heat
or time leaves THCA unconverted. Too much heat degrades THC into
CBN (sedating but less psychoactive) or destroys it entirely.

| Temperature | Time | THCA → THC conversion | Notes |
|------------|------|----------------------|-------|
| 200°F (93°C) | 90 min | ~70% | Gentle; preserves terpenes; slower |
| 220°F (105°C) | 45 min | ~85–90% | Most recommended home method |
| 240°F (116°C) | 30 min | ~90–95% | Faster but more terpene loss |
| 250°F (121°C) | 25 min | ~90–95% | Near the degradation threshold |
| 280°F+ (138°C+) | Any | Degradation begins | THC converts to CBN; avoid |

**The sweet spot: 220°F for 40–45 minutes** is the most forgiving
home method. It converts 85–90% of THCA to THC with minimal
degradation. If you want to preserve terpenes for flavor, go lower
and longer (200°F for 90 minutes).

### Decarb technique

1. **Break cannabis into pea-sized pieces** — don't grind to powder
   (it'll burn on the edges). Rough-broken pieces heat evenly
2. **Spread on a parchment-lined baking sheet** in a single layer
3. **Cover tightly with foil** — this traps volatile terpenes and
   prevents them from venting into your oven (and your house)
4. **Bake at 220°F for 40–45 minutes**
5. **Check:** the cannabis should be dry, crumbly, and light golden
   brown. If it's green, it's undercooked. If it's dark brown, it's
   overcooked

**Mason jar method (lower smell):**
1. Place broken cannabis in a mason jar, seal finger-tight (not
   fully — the jar needs to vent CO₂)
2. Place the jar on its side on a baking sheet
3. Bake at 220°F for 45 minutes
4. Remove, cool, then open — the terpenes are trapped in the jar

## Fat selection: why it matters

THC is fat-soluble, not water-soluble. You need a fat or oil to
extract it from the plant material. But not all fats extract equally.

### Fat absorption capacity

| Fat/oil | Saturated fat % | THC absorption | Best for |
|---------|----------------|---------------|----------|
| Coconut oil | 82% | Highest | Edibles, capsules, topicals |
| Clarified butter (ghee) | 60% | Very high | Baking, cooking |
| Butter | 51% | High | Baking (traditional) |
| Olive oil | 14% | Moderate | Savory dishes, tinctures |
| Vegetable/canola oil | 7–8% | Lower | General cooking |

**Saturated fats bind THC more effectively** than unsaturated fats.
Coconut oil has the highest saturated fat content of common cooking
fats, making it the most efficient extraction medium. It also has
a long shelf life and works in virtually any recipe that calls for
oil or butter.

### Why not butter?

Butter works, but it contains ~20% water and milk solids. The water
doesn't absorb THC, and the milk solids can burn during infusion.
Clarified butter (ghee) removes the water and milk solids, leaving
pure butterfat — a better extraction medium.

If you want butter specifically (for taste or recipe compatibility),
use unsalted butter and strain carefully after infusion to remove
all plant material and milk solid residue.

## Infusion: extracting THC into fat

### Stovetop method

1. **Combine decarbed cannabis and fat** in a saucepan. Use a ratio
   of 1 cup fat to 7–10 grams of decarbed cannabis (adjust based on
   desired potency — see dosing section)
2. **Add ½ cup water** — this prevents the fat from scorching and
   makes straining easier. The water separates from the fat after
   cooling
3. **Heat to 160–180°F (71–82°C)** — use a thermometer. Do not let
   it boil or simmer. THC degrades above 200°F in liquid, and the
   decarb already happened in the oven
4. **Maintain temperature for 2–3 hours**, stirring every 20 minutes
5. **Strain through cheesecloth** into a container. Squeeze the
   cheesecloth to extract all the oil (use gloves — it's hot)
6. **Refrigerate overnight.** If you added water, the fat solidifies
   on top and the water (with chlorophyll and plant matter) settles
   below. Remove the solid fat disc; discard the water

### Slow cooker method (easiest)

1. Combine decarbed cannabis, fat, and ½ cup water in a slow cooker
2. Set to LOW (160–180°F on most slow cookers)
3. Cook for 4–6 hours, stirring occasionally
4. Strain and refrigerate as above

### Infusion machine (most consistent)

Devices like the Magical Butter Machine or Ardent FX automate the
time-temperature control:
- Load cannabis and fat
- Select the oil/butter setting
- The machine maintains precise temperature for the set time
- Strain after the cycle completes

These cost $100–250 but remove the biggest variable (temperature
control) from the process. If you make edibles regularly, they pay
for themselves in consistency.

## Dosing math: the part most people skip

### Calculating potency

You need to know (or estimate) the THC percentage of your starting
material. If you grew it yourself without lab testing, estimate based
on strain data:

- **Mid-grade homegrown:** 15–18% THC
- **Good homegrown:** 18–22% THC
- **Exceptional homegrown:** 22–28% THC
- **Trim/sugar leaves:** 5–10% THC
- **Kief:** 40–60% THC

### The formula

**Total THC (mg) = grams of cannabis × THC% × 1000 × decarb
efficiency**

Example: 10 grams of 20% THC flower, decarbed at 85% efficiency:

10 × 0.20 × 1000 × 0.85 = **1700 mg THC total**

### Per-serving calculation

Divide total THC by the number of servings:

1700 mg ÷ 24 brownies = **~71 mg per brownie**

That's a strong dose. For most people, a comfortable dose is:

| Experience level | Dose per serving |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| First-time / low tolerance | 2.5–5 mg |
| Occasional user | 5–10 mg |
| Regular user | 10–25 mg |
| High tolerance | 25–50 mg |
| Very high tolerance | 50–100 mg |

### Adjusting your recipe

If 71 mg per brownie is too strong (it is for most people), you have
options:

1. **Use less cannabis** — 3 grams instead of 10 gives ~510 mg
   total, or ~21 mg per brownie (24 servings). Still strong
2. **Make more servings** — cut the brownies into 48 pieces instead
   of 24. Now it's ~35 mg each
3. **Dilute the infused fat** — mix your infused coconut oil 1:1 with
   regular coconut oil before baking. Halves the potency
4. **Use trim instead of flower** — 10g of trim at 8% THC gives
   680 mg total, or ~28 mg per brownie (24 servings). More
   manageable

### The biggest dosing mistake

Using 14 grams (half ounce) of flower in one batch of brownies
because "that's what the recipe said." At 20% THC, that's 2380 mg
in one pan. If you cut 12 brownies, each one is 198 mg — enough to
make an experienced user uncomfortably high for 8 hours.

**Always do the math before cooking.** Decide on your target per-
serving dose, then work backward to determine how much cannabis to
use.

## Lecithin: the emulsifier debate

Many edible recipes call for sunflower or soy lecithin as an
emulsifier. The claimed benefits:

- **Improves bioavailability** — lecithin helps THC absorb through
  intestinal walls, potentially making the same dose feel stronger
- **Improves texture** — lecithin creates a more uniform distribution
  of infused fat through the batter, reducing hot spots

The bioavailability claim has some supporting evidence from
pharmaceutical lipid research, though no cannabis-specific studies
confirm the effect. The texture claim is well-supported in food
science.

**If you use it:** add 1 teaspoon of sunflower lecithin per cup of
infused fat, mixing it in during the infusion process (last 30
minutes). Sunflower lecithin is preferred over soy for allergen
reasons.

## Shelf life and storage

| Product | Room temperature | Refrigerated | Frozen |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|--------|
| Infused coconut oil | 2–3 months | 6+ months | 1+ year |
| Infused butter | 1–2 weeks | 2–3 months | 6+ months |
| Infused olive oil | 1–2 months | 4–6 months | 1+ year |
| Baked goods | 3–5 days | 1–2 weeks | 2–3 months |
| Gummies/candies | 1–2 weeks | 1–2 months | 6+ months |

**Storage rules:**
- Keep infused fats in opaque, airtight containers (light degrades
  THC)
- Label everything with the date, total THC, and per-serving dose
- Store edibles where children and pets cannot access them — this is
  a legal and safety requirement in every legal state

## Common mistakes

1. **Skipping decarb** — eating raw cannabis does nothing. The oven
   step is not optional
2. **Grinding too fine** — powder burns during infusion and passes
   through cheesecloth, adding chlorophyll (green color, bitter
   taste). Break into pea-sized pieces, not dust
3. **Infusing too hot** — boiling or simmering the fat during
   infusion degrades THC. Keep it below 180°F. If you see bubbles,
   it's too hot
4. **Not doing dose math** — "a little cannabis in some butter"
   produces unpredictable edibles. Calculate total THC and per-
   serving dose every time
5. **Eating more because "it's not working"** — edibles take 60–120
   minutes for onset (sometimes longer on a full stomach). Eating a
   second dose at the 45-minute mark is how people end up uncomfortably
   high for 8 hours. Wait at least 2 hours before taking more
