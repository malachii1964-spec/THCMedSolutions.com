Aeroponics grows plants with roots suspended in air, fed by
a nutrient mist rather than submerged in water (DWC) or
planted in a medium (soil, coco). The roots have unlimited
oxygen access — they're literally hanging in air — and
nutrient delivery is direct and efficient. The result is the
fastest vegetative growth of any growing method.

The tradeoff: aeroponics is the least forgiving system to run.
A pump failure in DWC gives you hours before damage. A pump
failure in aeroponics gives you minutes — exposed roots
desiccate rapidly without continuous misting.

## How aeroponics works

### The basic principle

1. Plant stems are held in net pots or neoprene collars
   mounted in the lid of an enclosed chamber
2. Roots hang freely inside the dark chamber
3. Misters or sprayers deliver nutrient solution as a fine
   mist onto the roots at timed intervals (or continuously)
4. Excess solution drains to a reservoir at the bottom of
   the chamber and is recirculated through the misters

The roots never sit in water. They never contact a growing
medium. They receive nutrients, water, and oxygen
simultaneously — the mist provides nutrients and moisture
while the air gaps between misting provide oxygen.

### Low-pressure aeroponics (LPA)

**How it works:** a standard submersible pump pushes nutrient
solution through sprinkler heads or misting nozzles at normal
water pressure (15–40 PSI). The droplets are relatively large
(50–100+ microns).

**Pros:**
- Inexpensive — uses standard aquarium/pond pumps
- Simple plumbing (PVC or flexible tubing)
- Less clogging than high-pressure systems
- DIY-friendly

**Cons:**
- Large droplets don't coat roots as evenly
- Higher water usage than true high-pressure aero
- Growth advantage over DWC is moderate (not dramatic)

**Honestly:** low-pressure aeroponics is DWC with the water
level dropped and sprayers added. The roots in an LPA system
often grow long enough to sit in the reservoir solution
anyway, at which point you're running a hybrid DWC/aero
system. It works, but the "aeroponics" label oversells the
difference from DWC.

### High-pressure aeroponics (HPA)

**How it works:** an accumulator tank pressurized to 80–120
PSI feeds solution through specialized misting nozzles that
produce a true fog — droplets under 50 microns (ideally
5–50 microns). The fine mist coats every root surface with
a microscopic film of nutrients.

**Pros:**
- Maximum root zone oxygen (roots are essentially in humid
  air, not wet spray)
- Fastest growth rates documented in any growing method
- 95%+ water efficiency (almost nothing is wasted)
- NASA-level nutrient delivery — originally developed for
  space agriculture

**Cons:**
- **Expensive:** accumulator pumps, solenoid valves, and
  precision nozzles cost $300–800+ for a small system
- **Clogging:** the fine nozzles (often 0.1mm orifice) clog
  easily, especially with organic nutrients
- **Complexity:** pressure regulation, cycle timing,
  solenoid control, and nozzle maintenance add significant
  system management
- **Failure intolerance:** nozzle clog = root death in that
  zone within 15–30 minutes

### Which to choose

| Factor | LPA | HPA |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Cost (6-plant system) | $100–200 | $400–1,000+ |
| Growth speed vs. DWC | 10–20% faster | 30–50% faster |
| Maintenance | Moderate | High |
| DIY feasibility | Easy | Moderate to difficult |
| Failure consequence | Moderate (roots survive longer in larger droplets) | Severe (fine mist stops = fast desiccation) |
| Best for | Hobbyists wanting to try aero | Experienced hydro growers chasing maximum performance |

**For most home growers experimenting with aeroponics, LPA
is the practical choice.** HPA is for growers who've already
mastered DWC and want to push performance further.

## Building a basic LPA system

### Components

| Part | Spec | Cost |
|------|------|------|
| Tote/chamber | 27-gallon dark (opaque) storage tote | $15–25 |
| Net pots | 3" or 2" net pots mounted in lid | $5–10 |
| Submersible pump | 400–800 GPH with 1/2" output | $20–30 |
| Misting/sprinkler heads | 360° micro-sprayers, 4–8 per chamber | $10–15 |
| PVC manifold | 1/2" PVC pipe + tees + elbows | $10–15 |
| Timer | Cycle timer (1 min on / 3–5 min off) | $20–35 |
| Neoprene collars | To hold stems in net pots | $5–8 |
| Air pump + air stone | Backup oxygenation for reservoir | $15–20 |

**Total: $100–160**

### Assembly

1. **Cut holes in the tote lid** for net pots. Space plants
   8–12 inches apart. Use a hole saw matched to your net pot
   diameter
2. **Build the spray manifold** inside the tote: PVC tee
   fittings connecting a central pipe with branch lines.
   Mount micro-sprayers pointing upward (toward the roots)
   every 6–8 inches
3. **Connect pump to manifold.** Place the submersible pump
   in the bottom of the tote. Run tubing from the pump output
   to the PVC manifold input
4. **Set the cycle timer.** Start with 1 minute on, 4 minutes
   off. Adjust based on root moisture — roots should glisten
   but not drip continuously
5. **Add an air stone** in the reservoir as backup aeration.
   If roots grow into the reservoir (they will), the air stone
   ensures that pooled solution doesn't go anaerobic
6. **Light-proof everything.** The chamber must be completely
   dark inside. Light hitting the reservoir grows algae. Cover
   any gaps with tape or foam

### The root chamber environment

**Temperature:** keep reservoir and chamber at 65–68°F.
Aeroponic systems are MORE sensitive to temperature than DWC
because the fine mist environment is ideal for Pythium at
warm temperatures. A chiller or frozen water bottles may be
necessary in warm climates.

**Darkness:** absolute. No light leaks into the root chamber.
Algae growth in the mist environment is rapid and clogs
nozzles.

**Humidity inside chamber:** near 100% during misting, dropping
between cycles. This wet-dry cycle in the chamber drives root
hair development — the roots grow new absorption tissue in
response to the alternating moisture.

## Nutrient management

### Feed strength

Aeroponic delivery is more efficient than medium-based
growing — the roots absorb nutrients from the mist without
competing with a medium's cation exchange or buffering.

**Start at 50–60% of the strength you'd use in soil or coco.**
A feed chart that calls for 1200 PPM in coco should start at
600–700 PPM in aero. Increase only if the plant shows hunger.

### pH

- **Target: 5.5–6.0** (same as DWC)
- Check twice daily — aero systems have no medium buffer, so
  pH swings faster than in soil or coco
- An auto-dosing pH controller is almost mandatory for
  serious aeroponic growing

### Nutrient type

**Use mineral (salt-based) nutrients only.** Organic nutrients
contain particulates and microbes that clog misting nozzles.
Even in LPA with larger sprayer heads, organic nutrients
cause biofilm buildup in the manifold and pump.

Recommended nutrient lines for aero:
- General Hydroponics Flora series (3-part)
- Athena Pro (powder, very clean dissolve)
- Jack's 321 (simple, mineral, no clogging)

### Reservoir changes

Change the entire reservoir every 5–7 days. Aero systems
recirculate aggressively, and the solution degrades faster
than in DWC (more surface area exposed = faster evaporation,
faster pH drift, faster nutrient ratio shifts).

## Aeroponic cloning

One area where aeroponics unambiguously excels: cloning.
Aeroponic cloners (like the EZ-Clone or DIY bucket cloners)
root cuttings in 5–10 days with near 100% success rates —
faster and more reliable than rockwool, soil, or water
cloning.

**Why it works so well for clones:** the stem cutting hangs
in humid air with the cut end misted continuously. The
humidity prevents the cutting from desiccating (the biggest
clone killer), while the air exposure drives rapid root
initiation. The roots that form are perfectly adapted to
absorb nutrients the moment they emerge.

Even growers who grow in soil or coco often use an aeroponic
cloner because the rooting speed and reliability are
genuinely superior to other methods.

## When aeroponics makes sense

**Good candidates:**
- Growers who've mastered DWC and want faster growth
- Cloning operations (aeroponic cloners are worth it for
  everyone)
- Research or experimental grows where growth rate data
  matters
- Growers in hot climates where DWC reservoir temps are
  hard to manage (aero can use less water volume, making
  chilling easier)

**Bad candidates:**
- First-time growers (learn in soil first)
- Growers who travel frequently (system failures are fast
  and fatal)
- Growers using organic nutrients (incompatible with
  misting nozzles)
- Anyone without a backup plan for pump failure (UPS, dual
  pumps, or someone who can respond in < 30 minutes)

## Common mistakes

1. **No backup for pump failure** — in aeroponics, pump
   failure = root death within 30–60 minutes. Run a dual
   pump setup (second pump on a separate timer as failsafe),
   put the primary pump on a UPS (uninterruptible power
   supply), or accept the risk and be present
2. **Using organic nutrients** — organic particulates clog
   misting nozzles and grow biofilm in lines. Use clean
   mineral nutrients and flush the system between reservoir
   changes
3. **Ignoring reservoir temperature** — warm mist (above
   72°F) in a dark, humid chamber is a perfect Pythium
   incubator. Keep solution at 65–68°F. This is more
   critical in aero than in any other hydro method
4. **Running mist continuously** — continuous misting
   waterlogged roots even in aero. The dry cycle between
   misting events is essential — it's when roots absorb
   oxygen and develop root hairs. Start with 1 min on / 4
   min off and adjust
5. **Light leaks in the root chamber** — any light entering
   the chamber grows algae on roots and inside nozzles.
   Tape every seam, cover every gap, and check with the
   grow light on (light finds paths you don't expect)
