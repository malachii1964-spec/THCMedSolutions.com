Your cannabis plant can't talk, but it's constantly telling you how it
feels. Every leaf position, color change, growth pattern, and texture
shift is a signal. Learning to read these signals is the single most
valuable skill in cannabis cultivation — it's what separates growers who
react from growers who anticipate.

This guide covers the visual language of cannabis: what to look for,
what it means, and what to do.

## Leaf position: the posture check

The position of the fan leaves is the plant's real-time mood indicator.
Check it every time you visit the tent.

### Healthy ("praying")

Leaves angled upward at 45° toward the light, like hands in prayer.
This means: light intensity is in the sweet spot, the plant is actively
photosynthesizing, and water/nutrient levels are balanced.

**Action:** nothing. This is the goal. Take note of what your
environment settings look like when the plant looks like this.

### Drooping down (overwatered)

Leaves hang limply downward while still feeling thick and heavy. The
plant looks sad, like it's melting.

**What's happening:** roots are waterlogged. The soil has been wet for
too long, and the roots can't get oxygen. Without oxygen, nutrient
uptake stalls and the plant goes into survival mode.

**Action:** stop watering. Let the medium dry back until the pot feels
light. Increase airflow around the base of the plant. Don't water again
until the top 1–2 inches of soil are dry (or the pot is noticeably
lighter).

### Drooping down (underwatered)

Leaves droop AND feel thin, papery, almost crispy. The difference from
overwatering: underwatered leaves feel dry and light; overwatered leaves
feel heavy and waterlogged.

**Action:** water immediately, slowly and thoroughly, until you see
10–15% runoff. The plant will recover within 30–60 minutes. If it's
been severely underwatered, recovery may take 2–4 hours.

### Curling up ("taco leaves")

Leaf edges curl upward, making the leaf look like a taco shell when
viewed from the end.

**What's happening:** heat stress or light stress. The leaf is trying to
reduce its surface area exposed to the light/heat source.

**Action:** check temperature at canopy level. If above 82°F, raise the
light or increase exhaust. If the light is too close (check manufacturer
specs), raise it 4–6 inches. Also check VPD — low humidity plus high
heat amplifies this response.

### Curling down ("clawing")

Leaf tips curl downward like a claw. The leaf itself may look dark green
and waxy.

**What's happening:** usually nitrogen toxicity. The plant has absorbed
too much nitrogen, which causes dark, waxy leaves that claw at the tips.

**Action:** reduce nitrogen in your feed. If using a complete nutrient
line, dilute to 50–75% strength. If in soil, skip the next feed and
water with plain pH'd water.

## Leaf color: the nutrient dashboard

### Uniform medium green

Healthy. The plant is getting enough of everything. This is your
baseline — memorize what this shade of green looks like for your
strain, because deviations from it are your first signal of trouble.

### Light green / lime (new growth)

New leaves are always lighter than mature leaves. This is normal. If
only the newest growth is light green and the rest of the plant is
healthy, everything is fine.

**Worry when:** ALL leaves turn light green, including the mature ones.
That's a nitrogen deficiency — the plant is cannibalizing older leaves
to feed new growth.

### Yellowing from the bottom up

The oldest (lowest) leaves turn yellow first and may fall off. Upper
growth stays green.

**What it means:** mobile nutrient deficiency — nitrogen, magnesium, or
phosphorus. The plant moves these nutrients from old leaves to new growth
when supply is short.

- **Nitrogen:** general yellowing, lower leaves go pale green → yellow →
  brown and drop off. Most common
- **Magnesium:** interveinal chlorosis (yellow between the veins, veins
  stay green) on middle-aged leaves
- **Phosphorus:** dark leaves with purple petioles and stems, slow growth

**Action:** check pH first (lockout mimics deficiency). If pH is correct,
increase the relevant nutrient.

### Yellowing from the top down

New growth comes in yellow or very pale. Older leaves stay green.

**What it means:** immobile nutrient deficiency — iron, sulfur,
manganese, or zinc. The plant can't move these from old leaves, so
new growth suffers first.

- **Iron:** bright yellow new leaves with green veins (classic iron
  chlorosis)
- **Zinc:** small, twisted new leaves with shortened internodes
- **Manganese:** small yellow spots between veins on new leaves

**Action:** almost always a pH problem. Iron and manganese lock out
above pH 7.0. Correct pH first; the symptoms usually resolve in new
growth within a week.

### Brown spots

Random brown spots appearing on leaves, usually mid-canopy.

**What it means:** calcium deficiency, pH fluctuation, or foliar spray
burn.

- **Calcium:** irregular brown spots, often starting at leaf edges,
  progressing inward. Common in coco without cal-mag supplementation
- **Spray burn:** if you recently foliar sprayed, the spots are where
  droplets concentrated under the light

**Action:** for calcium, add cal-mag to your feed (1–2 ml/L). For
spray burn, don't foliar spray with lights on — always spray during
the dark period or just before lights off.

### Burnt (brown/crispy) leaf tips

The very tips of the leaves turn brown and crispy. Also called "tip
burn" or "nute burn."

**What it means:** nutrient excess — the plant has more dissolved salts
than it can process. This is the opposite of deficiency.

**Action:** reduce nutrient concentration by 20–30%. If runoff EC is
high, do a mild flush with half-strength nutrient solution. Tip burn
is cosmetic and won't worsen if you reduce the feed. The tips don't
heal, but new growth will come in clean.

## Stem signals

### Purple or red stems

Common, usually genetic. Many strains show anthocyanin pigmentation in
stems, especially with day/night temperature differentials.

**Worry when:** purple stems come with yellowing, slow growth, AND cold
root zone. That combination suggests phosphorus uptake issues from cold
temps. Warm the root zone to 68–72°F.

### Thick, woody stems

A sign of a healthy, well-trained plant. Mechanical stress (wind, LST,
supercropping) triggers the plant to thicken its stems for structural
support.

### Thin, stretchy stems

Not enough light. The plant is stretching to reach the light source.
Move the light closer or increase intensity.

## Growth speed signals

### Explosive growth (3–4 inches/day in veg)

Everything is dialed. The plant has enough light, nutrients, water,
and CO₂ to grow at its genetic maximum. In ideal conditions during
late veg, some strains can grow 3–4 inches per day.

### Stalled growth

The plant is alive but hasn't visibly grown in 5+ days. Check (in
order):
1. Root zone temperature (too cold = stalled growth)
2. pH (lockout)
3. Watering frequency (over or underwatering)
4. Light intensity (not enough)
5. Root bound (needs transplant)

### Uneven growth

One side of the plant grows faster than the other.

**What it means:** uneven light distribution. The side closer to or
more directly under the light grows faster. Use LST to even the canopy,
or rotate the pot 90° every 2–3 days.

## Root signals (fabric pots)

If you're growing in fabric pots, the roots are partially visible:

- **White root tips poking through the fabric:** healthy. The plant is
  actively exploring for nutrients. This is air pruning in action
- **Brown, mushy roots visible through the fabric:** root rot. Reduce
  watering, increase airflow, consider a hydrogen peroxide drench
- **No roots visible:** either the plant is very young, or the root
  system is weak. Check for overwatering or cold root zone

## The daily 60-second inspection

Make this a habit every time you visit the tent:

1. **Posture check** — are the leaves praying, drooping, or taco-ing?
2. **Color scan** — any new yellowing, spots, or tip burn since
   yesterday?
3. **Growth check** — has the plant grown? Is growth even?
4. **Smell check** — the plant should smell like cannabis. A sour or
   rotting smell means problems in the root zone or mold
5. **Pest check** — flip 2–3 leaves and check the undersides. Spider
   mites, thrips, and aphids hide there

This takes 60 seconds and catches 90% of problems before they become
serious. The growers who struggle are the ones who water and walk away
without looking.

## The big picture

Reading your plant is a learned skill. Your first grow, you'll miss
signals and overreact to normal variation. By your third grow, you'll
catch a nitrogen issue from across the room before the symptoms are
visible to a beginner.

The best way to build this skill: take a photo of your plant every day,
at the same angle, under the same light. A week's worth of photos
reveals patterns that a single glance misses. Changes that are invisible
day-to-day become obvious in a time-lapse.
