Western New York sits in USDA hardiness zone 6a–6b, with a growing
season roughly 150–170 days (late May through mid-October). The
Lake Erie microclimate adds unique variables: moderate summer
temperatures (rarely above 90°F), persistent humidity from the
lake, early fall dew and fog, and unpredictable frost dates that
can catch outdoor growers by surprise.

This calendar is specific to the Buffalo / Erie County / Niagara
region. Growers east of Rochester or south of Olean may find
slightly different dates — the lake effect weakens with distance.

## March: planning and seed starting

**Outdoors:** still frozen. Snow cover possible through late March.
Nothing goes outside.

**Indoors:**
- **Order seeds** if you haven't already. Shipping takes 1–3 weeks
  from most seed banks
- **Start photoperiod seeds indoors** under lights during the last
  week of March or first week of April. This gives plants 6–8 weeks
  of indoor veg before outdoor transplant
- **Autoflowers:** don't start yet — autos can go directly outdoors
  in May. Starting too early means transplanting or keeping them
  under grow lights longer than necessary

**Strain selection for WNY outdoors:**
- Choose strains that finish flowering by early October: 8–9 week
  flower times maximum
- Mold-resistant genetics are critical — WNY fall humidity is brutal
- Good WNY choices: Northern Lights, Durban Poison, Early Skunk,
  Frisian Dew, Critical+, AK-47, Amnesia Haze (early-finishing
  phenotypes)
- Avoid 12+ week sativa strains (Super Silver Haze, Haze crosses)
  — they won't finish before frost

## April: indoor veg continues

**Outdoors:** frost risk remains through late April. Soil
temperature is still below 50°F — too cold for cannabis roots.

**Indoors:**
- Seedlings started in March are now 3–4 weeks old
- Begin topping and LST training under lights
- Transplant from starter containers to 3–5 gallon pots (the plants
  will go outdoors in these, or be transplanted again into ground or
  larger containers)
- **Harden off** preparation: start opening a window near the plants
  for a few hours daily in late April. This acclimates them to
  outdoor air, temperature swings, and wind

**April weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 55°F / Low: 38°F
- Last frost: typically April 25–May 10 (variable)
- Outdoor soil temp: 40–50°F

## May: the outdoor transition

**Timing is everything this month.** Too early and a late frost
kills your plants. Too late and you lose valuable veg growth.

**Week 1–2:** monitor weather forecasts closely. WNY's last frost
date averages May 1–10 but can be as late as May 20 in cold years.
Don't put plants outside until nighttime temperatures are
consistently above 50°F.

**Hardening off (mandatory):**
Before transplanting outdoors permanently, harden off indoor-grown
plants over 7–10 days:
- Days 1–3: 2–3 hours of outdoor shade, then back inside
- Days 4–6: 4–6 hours of partial sun
- Days 7–10: full days outside, bring in at night if below 50°F
- Day 10+: leave outside permanently

Skipping hardening off causes sunburn (bleached leaves), wind
damage, and transplant shock.

**Week 3–4 (post-frost):**
- Transplant into final outdoor containers (10–25 gallon fabric pots)
  or directly into the ground
- If planting in the ground, amend the soil: WNY soil is often heavy
  clay. Mix in compost, perlite, and peat moss to improve drainage
- **Autoflowers:** plant seeds directly outdoors after May 15.
  Autos planted in late May will finish by early August

**May weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 67°F / Low: 48°F
- Daylight: 14.5–15 hours (increasing)
- Soil temp: 55–65°F

## June: explosive vegetative growth

**The best month for outdoor cannabis in WNY.** Long days (15+
hours of light), warm temperatures, and regular rain drive rapid
vegetative growth.

**Tasks:**
- **Training:** continue LST and topping. Outdoor plants can grow
  6–12 inches per week in June. Control the shape now before the
  plant gets too large to manage
- **Feeding:** begin regular feeding if in containers. Plants in
  amended ground soil may not need supplemental nutrients yet
- **Pest scouting:** check for aphids, caterpillars, and slugs
  weekly. Early detection prevents infestations
- **Companion planting:** basil, marigolds, and lavender planted
  around cannabis deter some pests and attract beneficial insects.
  See our [companion planting guide](/guides/companion-planting-cannabis)

**June weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 76°F / Low: 57°F
- Daylight: 15–15.3 hours (summer solstice June 20–21)
- Typical rainfall: 3–4 inches

## July: peak veg, preparing for flower

**Early July:** plants are at maximum vegetative growth rate. Final
major training should be completed by mid-July.

**Late July:** day length begins decreasing noticeably (dropping
below 15 hours). Some early-flowering strains may show pre-flowers
by late July. Sativa-dominant strains won't trigger until August.

**Tasks:**
- **Final defoliation** to open the canopy before flower
- **Support structures:** install stakes, tomato cages, or netting
  for heavy colas. Outdoor buds can get heavy enough to break
  branches
- **Pest management:** caterpillar (budworm) season begins. Begin
  BTK (Bacillus thuringiensis kurstaki) spray every 7–10 days as a
  preventive
- **Water management:** July can be hot and dry. Outdoor containers
  may need daily watering. Ground-planted cannabis is more drought-
  tolerant but still benefits from supplemental watering during dry
  spells

**July weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 81°F / Low: 63°F
- Daylight: 14.4–15.3 hours (decreasing after solstice)

## August: flower begins

**The transition month.** Day length drops below 14 hours by mid-
August, triggering flowering in photoperiod strains.

**Weeks 1–2:** pre-flowers visible on most strains. White pistils
appear at the nodes. The stretch begins — plants may grow another
30–50% taller.

**Weeks 3–4:** flower formation accelerates. Bud sites develop and
begin producing resin.

**Tasks:**
- **Switch to bloom nutrients** (high P and K) once pre-flowers are
  visible
- **Caterpillar patrol is critical.** Check inside developing buds
  for frass (dark pellets = caterpillar droppings). BTK spray every
  7 days
- **Stop topping and heavy training.** The plant is now flowering —
  stress can trigger hermaphroditism
- **Autoflowers planted in May** should be harvesting now or very
  soon

**August weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 79°F / Low: 61°F
- Daylight: 12.8–14.2 hours (rapidly decreasing)

## September: peak flower — the danger zone

**The most critical month for WNY outdoor growers.** Buds are
fattening, trichomes are developing, but the lake-effect humidity
creates ideal conditions for bud rot (Botrytis).

**Weeks 1–2:** buds are in peak development. Dense colas are now
vulnerable to moisture.

**Weeks 3–4:** trichomes are developing. Early-finishing strains
(8-week flower) may be approaching harvest. Check trichomes weekly.

**The WNY September challenge:**
- **Lake-effect humidity:** morning humidity regularly exceeds 80%
  as cool lake air meets warm land
- **Morning dew:** heavy dew settles on buds overnight. If buds
  don't dry by midday, rot risk skyrockets
- **Rain:** September rain directly on buds is a bud rot invitation

**Damage control:**
- **Shake plants gently every morning** to knock off dew
- **Cover plants** with a temporary canopy (clear plastic sheeting
  on a frame) during rain. Don't let rain sit on buds
- **Inspect buds daily** for bud rot — look for brown spots, gray
  fuzz, or soft mushy patches. Remove infected buds immediately
- **Defoliate** around dense colas to improve airflow
- See our [bud rot guide](/guides/bud-rot-botrytis)

**September weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 72°F / Low: 54°F
- Daylight: 11.4–12.8 hours
- Average humidity: 70–80% (mornings)

## October: harvest window

**For most WNY strains, October is harvest month.** Trichomes
should be reaching peak maturity (milky to amber) on 8–9 week
flower strains.

**Week 1–2:** peak harvest window for most strains. Check trichomes
every 2–3 days and harvest when you see 70–80% milky, 20–30% amber.

**Week 3–4:** late-finishing strains may still be developing.
Monitor frost forecasts closely.

**First frost:**
- WNY average first frost: October 10–25 (highly variable)
- A light frost (28–32°F for a few hours) won't kill a cannabis
  plant but will damage exposed buds
- A hard frost (below 28°F for several hours) can destroy an
  unharvested crop
- **If frost is forecast and the plant isn't ready:** harvest early.
  Partially mature buds are infinitely better than frozen buds

**The purple bonus:** cool October nights (40s–50s°F) trigger
anthocyanin production in many strains, producing beautiful purple
and blue colors in buds and leaves. This is cosmetic — it doesn't
affect potency, but it's one of the visual rewards of WNY outdoor
growing.

**October weather averages (Buffalo):**
- High: 59°F / Low: 43°F
- First frost: October 10–25 (average)
- Daylight: 10.2–11.4 hours

## November: drying and curing

All outdoor plants should be harvested by early November at the
latest. November is for drying and curing.

**Drying:**
- Hang harvested branches in a dark room at 60°F, 55–65% RH
- WNY basements in November are often ideal for drying — cool and
  moderately humid
- If your basement is too humid (above 65% RH), run a dehumidifier
- Drying takes 7–14 days. Stems should snap (not bend) when ready
- See our [drying guide](/guides/drying-room-setup)

**Curing:**
- Trim dried buds and place in glass jars at 60–65% RH
- Burp jars daily for the first 2 weeks, then every 2–3 days
- Cure for a minimum of 2 weeks, ideally 4–8 weeks
- See our [curing guide](/guides/curing-in-jars)

## Quick-reference timeline

| Month | Activity | Key risk |
|-------|----------|----------|
| March | Start seeds indoors | None (too early to plant outside) |
| April | Indoor veg + training | Don't plant outside yet |
| May (post-frost) | Transplant outdoors | Late frost, transplant shock |
| June | Explosive veg growth | Pests (aphids, slugs) |
| July | Final training, pre-flower | Caterpillars begin |
| August | Flower begins | Hermaphrodite risk from stress |
| September | Peak flower | Bud rot (humidity + dew) |
| October | Harvest | Frost, bud rot, caterpillars |
| November | Dry and cure | Mold during drying if too humid |

## Common mistakes

1. **Planting too early** — a frost in early May kills your plants.
   Wait until nights are consistently above 50°F
2. **Choosing 12-week strains** — they won't finish before frost.
   Stick to 8–9 week flower times for WNY outdoor
3. **Ignoring September humidity** — the lake-effect humidity in
   WNY September is the #1 harvest threat. Shake off dew daily,
   cover during rain, inspect for rot daily
4. **Not starting caterpillar prevention early enough** — BTK spray
   should start in July, not when you find the first caterpillar in
   September
5. **Trying to push past frost** — an extra week of maturation isn't
   worth the risk of losing the entire crop to a hard freeze. If
   trichomes are mostly milky, harvest before frost
