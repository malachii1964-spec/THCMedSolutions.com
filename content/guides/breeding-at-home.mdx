Every strain you've ever grown was created by someone crossing two
parent plants. Home breeding isn't complicated — cannabis pollinates
easily — but doing it intentionally, with a goal, and without
accidentally seeding your entire flower room requires discipline and
isolation.

This guide covers practical home breeding: how to collect pollen, make
a controlled cross, grow out seeds, and what to realistically expect
from your results.

## Why breed at home?

- **Create strains tailored to your grow** — cross a high-yielder with
  a terp bomb. Cross a mold-resistant outdoor strain with a frosty
  indoor pheno
- **Preserve genetics** — make seeds from a clone-only cut before you
  lose it. Reversing a female to produce feminized seeds gives you a
  seed bank of your favorite pheno
- **Exploration** — growing out a cross you made is one of the most
  rewarding experiences in cannabis cultivation. Every seed is unique,
  and some of them will surprise you
- **Cost** — seeds from premium breeders cost $10–15 each. A single
  pollinated branch produces hundreds of seeds

## The basics of cannabis reproduction

Cannabis is dioecious — separate male and female plants. Female plants
produce the buds we consume. Male plants produce pollen. When pollen
lands on a female flower's pistil (stigma), the flower is fertilized
and produces a seed instead of (or alongside) a seedless bud.

### What you need for a cross

1. **A male plant** (or a reversed female — see feminized seeds below)
   that provides pollen
2. **A female plant** in early-to-mid flower that receives the pollen
3. **Isolation** — a way to deliver pollen to specific branches without
   contaminating your seedless flower room

## Selecting your parents

### The male

Males are the forgotten half of breeding. Most growers kill males on
sight, but a good male contributes 50% of the offspring's genetics.

**What to look for in a male:**
- **Vigor** — fast, healthy vegetative growth. Vigor in males correlates
  with vigor in offspring
- **Structure** — sturdy stems, good branching, tight internodal spacing.
  These structural traits pass to the progeny
- **Smell** — rub the stems. Males produce terpenes too, and the stem
  rub gives you a rough preview of what terpenes the male will pass
  along. A male that smells like fuel or fruit is more interesting
  than one that smells like grass
- **Flower timing** — males that flower at a similar pace to the intended
  female mother produce offspring with more consistent flowering times
- **What you can't select for in males:** THC/CBD content, bud density,
  resin production — these only manifest in female flowers. This is why
  breeding is partly art: you're guessing about half the genetics based
  on proxy traits

### The female

Pick the female parent based on the traits you want to preserve.
She contributes the other 50% of the genetics, and unlike the male,
you can evaluate her flower directly.

**What to look for:**
- The traits you want to see in the offspring: potency, terpene profile,
  yield, bud structure, flowering time, pest resistance
- A healthy, vigorous plant (weak mothers produce weak seeds)
- Proven genetics that you've grown before and know well

## Collecting pollen

### From a male plant

1. **Identify male flowers** — males produce pollen sacs (small oval
   structures that cluster at the nodes) instead of pistils. They're
   visible 2–3 weeks into flower
2. **Isolate the male** — move the male away from all female plants.
   Pollen is airborne and travels far. Even in a different room, seal
   vents and doorways. The safest approach: grow the male in a separate
   tent or closet with its own exhaust (vented outside or through a
   filter)
3. **Harvest pollen sacs** — when sacs begin to open (you'll see
   yellow pollen grains inside), carefully clip the sacs into a clean,
   dry container. Tap them over parchment paper to release the pollen
4. **Dry the pollen** — spread the collected pollen on parchment paper
   in a low-humidity environment for 24–48 hours. Moisture is pollen's
   enemy — wet pollen clumps and loses viability
5. **Store in an airtight container** with a desiccant packet, in the
   freezer. Pollen stored this way remains viable for 6–12 months

### From a reversed female (feminized seeds)

To make feminized seeds (all-female offspring), you reverse a female
plant to produce pollen. This pollen has only female chromosomes (XX),
so every seed it produces will be female.

**Reversal methods:**
- **Colloidal silver (CS)** — spray a branch with 30–50 PPM colloidal
  silver daily, starting 2 weeks before the flip and continuing for
  2–3 weeks into flower. The silver inhibits ethylene production,
  causing the branch to produce pollen sacs instead of pistils
- **Silver thiosulfate (STS)** — more effective than CS. Mix silver
  nitrate and sodium thiosulfate (recipes widely available), spray
  once or twice during the reversal period. STS is the professional
  standard

**Important:** only spray the branches you want to reverse, not the
entire plant. The reversed branches produce pollen; the unsprayed
branches produce normal female flowers. You can pollinate the same
plant with its own reversed pollen (a "self" or S1) or pollinate a
different female.

**Safety:** don't consume any flower from a plant that has been sprayed
with CS or STS. The chemicals are absorbed by the plant tissue. Only
use the pollen; discard the sprayed plant material.

## Controlled pollination

You do NOT want pollen drifting through your flower room. Controlled
pollination delivers pollen to specific branches, leaving the rest
of the female plant seedless.

### The isolation method

1. **Select 1–2 lower branches** on the female plant for pollination.
   Lower branches produce fewer seeds individually but won't affect
   the quality of the main colas
2. **Wait until the female is 2–3 weeks into flower** — pistils
   should be fully developed and receptive (white, upright)
3. **Turn off all fans** in the grow space. Airflow = pollen spread
4. **Apply pollen to the selected branches** using a small paintbrush
   or Q-tip. Dab the brush in the collected pollen, then gently brush
   it onto the pistils. Be thorough — every pistil you touch has a
   chance of producing a seed
5. **Bag the pollinated branch** — cover it with a paper bag (not
   plastic — needs airflow) and tie it loosely at the base. Leave the
   bag on for 24–48 hours to prevent stray pollen from reaching other
   branches
6. **After 48 hours**, remove the bag. Mist the area around the
   pollinated branch with plain water to neutralize any remaining
   pollen (pollen dies on contact with water)
7. **Turn fans back on** and resume normal growing

### Timeline after pollination

| Days after pollination | What's happening |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| Day 1–7 | Pollen tubes grow down the pistil into the ovule |
| Day 7–14 | Fertilization occurs. Pistils on pollinated calyxes darken and recede faster than unseeded calyxes |
| Week 2–4 | Seed embryo develops inside the calyx. The calyx swells noticeably larger than surrounding unseeded calyxes |
| Week 4–6 | Seed coat hardens. Seeds are green/white and immature |
| Week 6–8 | Seeds mature — the coat darkens to brown/gray/tan with tiger striping. They feel hard when squeezed |

**Seeds need 4–6 weeks from pollination to mature.** This means you
need to pollinate early enough in flower that the seeds have time to
ripen before the rest of the plant is ready for harvest. Pollinating
in week 2–3 of flower gives seeds 5–7 weeks to develop — usually
enough.

## Harvesting seeds

### When seeds are ready

- Seeds are dark brown, gray, or tan with visible striping or mottling
- The seed coat is hard — you can't crush it between your fingers
- Seeds fall freely from their calyxes when the bud is broken apart
- Green or white seeds are immature and won't germinate reliably

### Collection

1. Harvest the pollinated branch separately from the rest of the plant
2. Dry it normally (7–10 days at 60°F/60% RH)
3. Once dry, break apart the buds over a clean surface
4. Seeds fall out as you break the calyx open. Collect them all
5. Separate seeds from plant matter by gently rolling the dried material
   on a tilted surface — round seeds roll, flat plant material stays

### Yield expectation

A single pollinated branch can produce 20–100+ seeds depending on how
thoroughly you applied pollen and how many calyxes were pollinated. A
fully pollinated small plant can produce 500+ seeds.

## Growing out your cross

### What to expect: F1 generation

The first generation from your cross (called F1) will show a mix of
traits from both parents. You'll see variation — some plants leaning
toward the mother, some toward the father, some showing novel
combinations.

**Practical expectations:**
- If both parents are stable, named strains: F1 offspring will be
  relatively uniform (hybrid vigor) with traits from both
- If either parent is an F1 itself (polyhybrid): the offspring will
  show wide variation. You may need to grow 10+ seeds to find a
  standout pheno
- Every seed is unique — this is the excitement (and frustration) of
  breeding. Not every cross produces winners. Many produce interesting
  but unremarkable plants

### Selecting from F1s

Grow 6–10 seeds minimum. Evaluate each plant for the traits you
selected in the parents. The best plant(s) can become:
- A mother for cloning (if the phenotype is worth keeping)
- A parent for further breeding (F2 generation, backcross, etc.)

### Stabilizing a line (advanced)

Creating a truly stable, consistent strain (where every seed produces
a similar plant) requires multiple generations of selective breeding —
typically 5–7 generations of inbreeding (F1 → F2 → F3...) with
rigorous selection at each stage. This is a multi-year project for
dedicated breeders. Home breeders typically stop at F1 and clone the
best pheno instead.

## Common mistakes

1. **Not isolating the male** — one pollen sac can seed an entire room.
   Males must be isolated from the moment they show sex. Check daily
   during early flower
2. **Pollinating too late** — if you pollinate in week 5–6 of flower,
   seeds won't have time to mature before the plant is ready to harvest.
   Pollinate in weeks 2–3
3. **Not documenting** — label everything. Which male pollinated which
   female, which branch, what date. Without records, your seeds are
   "mystery genetics" — interesting but unreproducible
4. **Expecting consistency from F1** — your first cross will produce
   varied offspring. That's normal. Consistency comes from generations
   of selection, not from one cross
5. **Using weak parents** — "I'll cross my two worst plants and hope
   for something good" doesn't work. Breeding amplifies traits, both
   good and bad. Start with the best parents you can find
