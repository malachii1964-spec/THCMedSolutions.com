A cannabis plant left to grow naturally produces a Christmas tree
shape: one tall main cola that shades every branch below it. Only the
top 6–12 inches of the plant receive full light. Everything below gets
partial light at best, producing small, airy "larf" buds that nobody
wants to smoke.

Canopy management is the discipline of flattening that Christmas tree
into a table — a level surface where every bud site sits at the same
height, the same distance from the light, receiving the same PPFD.
The result: instead of one top-heavy cola and a dozen underperformers,
you get 8–20 colas of near-equal size and density.

This is the single highest-ROI technique in indoor cannabis cultivation.
It doesn't require more light, more nutrients, or more space — just
intentional shaping during veg and early flower.

## The physics: why flat wins

### The inverse square law in practice

Light intensity follows the inverse square law: double the distance
from the source, quarter the intensity. In a grow tent, this means:

- A bud 12 inches from the light gets 100% of the available PPFD
- A bud 24 inches from the light gets ~25% of the available PPFD
- A bud 36 inches from the light gets ~11%

In a 36-inch tall plant with one dominant cola, the top cola gets 100%
PPFD. A branch at the halfway point gets ~44%. A branch at the base
gets 11%. The bottom branches aren't just shaded — they're in a
different light universe.

A flat canopy eliminates this disparity. If every bud site is at the
same height, every bud gets the same light. A 10% improvement in
canopy evenness translates to a measurable yield increase because
you're converting wasted light into bud development.

### Light penetration vs. canopy depth

Even with a flat canopy top, light doesn't penetrate more than 8–12
inches into a dense canopy. Below that depth, PPFD drops below the
threshold for meaningful bud development. This is why lollipopping
(removing growth below the canopy line) isn't just cosmetic — it
redirects the plant's energy from doomed lower buds to the productive
upper canopy.

## Building the canopy: veg phase

### Step 1: Topping for branching (weeks 3–5)

Top the plant once at node 4–5 to create two main branches. Each of
these can be topped again at their node 3–4 for four main branches.
For most indoor grows, 4–8 main branches is the sweet spot.

**How many tops?**

| Plant count | Tops per plant | Total canopy sites |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 plant in a 4×4 | 8–16 | 8–16 |
| 2 plants in a 4×4 | 4–8 each | 8–16 |
| 4 plants in a 4×4 | 2–4 each | 8–16 |

Notice the pattern: regardless of plant count, the target is roughly
the same number of total canopy sites filling the same space. More
plants with fewer tops is faster (less veg time); fewer plants with
more tops is slower but gives the individual plant more root mass
per cola.

### Step 2: LST to level (ongoing)

After topping, each branch wants to grow upward. Your job is to bend
them outward and downward so they spread across the available space
instead of stacking vertically.

**The technique:**
1. When a branch grows 4–6 inches above the canopy plane, bend it
   outward toward the pot rim
2. Secure it with soft plant wire, garden clips, or pipe cleaners
   hooked to the pot rim or stakes
3. The branch tip will turn upward within 24 hours (it always grows
   toward the light). New growth from nodes along the bent branch also
   grows upward — these become secondary canopy sites
4. Continue bending and securing until the canopy fills the available
   space

**The goal:** a flat "table" of growth tips all at the same height,
covering the entire grow space with minimal gaps.

### Step 3: The canopy check

Run a piece of string or a stick across the top of the canopy. If
any branch sits more than 2 inches above or below the average height,
it needs adjustment:

- **Too tall:** bend it down and secure it
- **Too short:** remove the tie so it can grow upward, or defoliate
  the fan leaves shading it to let more light reach it

Do this check every 2–3 days during veg. The plant grows fast, and
a branch that's level today can be 4 inches above the canopy tomorrow.

## Managing the canopy: flower phase

### The stretch (weeks 1–3 of flower)

When you flip to 12/12, most strains "stretch" — doubling or even
tripling in height over 2–3 weeks as they transition to flowering.
This is your last opportunity for canopy management before buds form.

**During the stretch:**
- Continue tucking branches under the trellis net (if using SCROG)
  to fill empty squares
- Bend overly tall branches back to canopy height with LST or
  supercropping
- If a branch is dramatically outpacing the others, supercrop it
  (pinch and bend 90°) to bring it level. The branch will recover
  and form a knuckle within a week
- Monitor daily — the stretch happens fast, and a branch can hit
  the light overnight if unchecked

### Defoliation for light penetration (week 3 and week 6)

Once buds begin forming, the canopy fills with fan leaves that shade
bud sites below. Strategic defoliation opens the canopy to light
without removing the plant's energy production capacity.

**The "Schwazze" technique (aggressive, controversial):**
Strip 80–90% of fan leaves at day 1 of flower and again at day 21.
Proponents claim 30%+ yield increase from massive light penetration.
Critics argue the stress reduces final quality. This is advanced and
not recommended until you've grown the strain before and know how it
responds.

**The moderate approach (recommended):**
- **Week 3 of flower:** remove fan leaves that are directly blocking
  bud sites. Don't strip the plant — target specific leaves that are
  casting shadows on specific buds. 20–30% leaf removal maximum
- **Week 6 of flower:** a second, lighter pass. By now, most fan
  leaves below the canopy are yellowing naturally and can be removed.
  Remove any remaining fan leaves that are blocking air flow to the
  interior of dense colas

**The rule:** if removing a leaf exposes a bud site to direct light,
remove it. If a leaf is in open space photosynthesizing without
blocking anything, leave it.

See our
[defoliation guide](/guides/defoliation-guide) for detailed technique.

### Lollipopping (pre-flower or week 1)

Remove all growth below the bottom 1/3 of the plant — every small
branch, every sucker, every bud site that will never reach the canopy
line. These sites will produce small, airy popcorn buds that aren't
worth the energy the plant invests in them.

**How:** cut or snap off everything below the "canopy line" — the
height where serious bud sites begin. The bottom of the plant should
be bare stems with no leaves or branches.

**When:** ideally 1–3 days before the flip to 12/12, or during the
first week of flower. Doing it earlier means the plant wastes less
energy on doomed growth.

## The SCROG framework

A SCROG (Screen of Green) is a physical trellis net that enforces
canopy evenness mechanically. It's the most reliable way to maintain
a flat canopy because the net physically prevents branches from
growing past it.

### Setup

1. Place the net 8–12 inches above the pot rims (adjustable is better)
2. As branches grow through the net squares, tuck them back under
   toward empty squares
3. Continue tucking during late veg and the first 2 weeks of flower
4. Once buds start forming (week 2–3 of flower), stop tucking and
   let them grow vertically through the net

### The result

Every net square has a branch tip. The net holds them at a uniform
height. When buds form, they grow vertically through the net at
equal heights. The canopy is mechanically even.

See our [SCROG guide](/guides/scrog-screen-of-green) for the complete
technique.

## Measuring canopy performance

### PAR uniformity

If you have a PAR meter or phone app, map the PPFD at canopy level
across your grow space. Measure at 9 points (3×3 grid) and compare:

- **Center vs. edges:** the center should be no more than 30% higher
  than the edges. If it is, you're wasting light on center buds that
  are already saturated while starving edge buds
- **Fix:** raise the light slightly (sacrifices peak center PPFD but
  improves uniformity) or add supplemental side lighting

### Canopy fill percentage

Look down at your canopy from above. What percentage of the grow space
is filled with green, actively growing bud sites?

| Fill % | Assessment | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Below 50% | Underfilled — you're wasting light and space | More training, more tops, or more plants next round |
| 50–70% | Adequate but room for improvement | Longer veg next round, more aggressive LST |
| 70–90% | Good — most light is being captured | Minor adjustments; you're in the zone |
| 90%+ | Optimal — every photon is hitting a bud site | Maintain airflow; dense canopies trap humidity |

### Bud size uniformity at harvest

After harvest, weigh your top 3 buds and your bottom 3 buds. The
ratio tells you how even your canopy was:

- **Top buds are 5× bigger than bottom buds:** your canopy had
  significant height variation — the top was getting all the light
- **Top buds are 2× bigger than bottom buds:** normal — some variation
  is inevitable
- **Top and bottom buds are similar size:** excellent canopy management.
  Light was distributed evenly

## Common mistakes

1. **Not topping enough** — a single-topped plant has two mains. That's
   not a canopy — that's two branches. Top 2–3 times for 4–8 mains
   before attempting to fill a space
2. **Vegging too short** — the canopy needs time to fill. If you flip
   before the canopy covers 60%+ of the space, you'll harvest with
   unused real estate. Veg until the canopy is nearly full, then flip
3. **Ignoring the stretch** — the stretch rearranges your carefully
   built canopy in 2 weeks. Plan for it: leave room for the height
   increase, and actively manage branches during weeks 1–3 of flower
4. **Over-defoliating** — removing too many fan leaves reduces the
   plant's photosynthetic capacity. It's a balance: enough removal to
   expose bud sites, not so much that the plant can't feed itself
5. **Ignoring humidity in dense canopies** — a 90%+ filled canopy is
   beautiful but creates humidity pockets that breed mold. Maintain
   strong airflow and keep RH at 40–50% during flower
