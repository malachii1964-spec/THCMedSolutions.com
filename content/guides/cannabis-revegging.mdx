You've harvested a plant and discovered it's the best flower
you've ever grown. But you didn't take clones before harvest.
The genetics are sitting in a pot with a stripped stump, and if
you throw it away, that phenotype is gone forever.

Revegging — putting a harvested cannabis plant back into
vegetative growth — is the recovery technique for exactly this
situation. It works, it's not difficult, and it saves genetics
that would otherwise be lost. But it's slow, ugly, and only
worth the effort when the alternative is losing something
genuinely irreplaceable.

## When to reveg

### Good reasons

- **You found a special phenotype and didn't clone it.** This
  is the primary use case. A seed-grown plant that produces
  exceptional flower, and you have no other way to preserve
  those genetics
- **You want a second harvest from the same root system.**
  Revegged plants produce a second (sometimes third) harvest.
  Yields are typically 70–80% of the first harvest but require
  no germination or early veg time
- **Monster cropping.** Taking clones from a plant in early
  flower (weeks 1–3), then revegging the clones. The resulting
  plants exhibit extreme branching and can produce impressive
  yields. This is an intentional training technique, not a
  rescue operation

### Bad reasons

- **"I want to save money on seeds."** Seeds cost $5–15 each.
  The 4–6 weeks of electricity and space used for revegging
  costs more. Take clones before harvest if you want to run
  the same strain again
- **"I'm lazy."** Revegging is harder and slower than starting
  a new seed or clone. It's a specialized technique, not a
  shortcut

## How to reveg

### Step 1: Harvest selectively

When you harvest, leave the plant partially intact:

- **Leave the bottom 1/3 of the plant.** Don't cut the entire
  plant at the soil line. Leave several branches with small
  buds and a few leaves in the lower canopy
- **Leave as many fan leaves as possible** on the remaining
  branches. The plant needs photosynthetic surface area to
  power the transition
- **Leave at least 3–5 small bud sites** on the remaining
  branches. These will be the initial growth points for new
  vegetative shoots

The more plant material you leave, the faster the reveg. A
stump with no leaves takes much longer to recover than one with
intact lower branches.

### Step 2: Switch the light schedule

Change from 12/12 back to 18/6 (or 20/4). The extended light
period signals the plant to revert to vegetative growth. This
is the only essential change — the rest is support.

### Step 3: Adjust feeding

- Switch from bloom nutrients back to vegetative nutrients
  (higher nitrogen)
- Feed at 50% strength for the first 2 weeks (the plant has
  minimal leaf area and can't process full-strength feeding)
- Increase to 75% strength once new growth appears
- Return to full-strength veg feeding once the plant is
  actively growing

### Step 4: Wait (the ugly phase)

The reveg transition takes 2–6 weeks. During this time, the
plant looks terrible:

- **Week 1–2:** nothing visible happens. The plant is
  redirecting hormones internally
- **Week 2–3:** weird, rounded single-blade leaves emerge from
  bud sites. These "reveg leaves" don't look like normal
  cannabis leaves — they're smooth-edged, rounded, and may be
  a single blade instead of the typical 5–7 finger structure
- **Week 3–4:** growth accelerates. New shoots emerge from
  every remaining bud site. The plant looks bushy and chaotic
- **Week 4–6:** normal-looking leaves (5–7 blades) begin
  appearing. The plant has fully reverted to vegetative growth

**Don't panic at the ugly growth.** The weird single-blade
leaves are normal during the hormonal transition. They'll be
replaced by normal foliage within a few weeks.

### Step 5: Shape and train

Once the plant is actively vegetating (normal leaves, vigorous
growth):

- Remove any remaining dried flower material
- Select 3–4 of the strongest new branches to keep
- Remove weak, redundant, or poorly positioned shoots
- Begin LST or training as you would a normal veg plant
- **Take clones** now that you have vigorous vegetative growth
  — this was likely the whole point of revegging

### Step 6: Flower again (optional)

If you're growing the revegged plant to harvest (not just
saving the genetics via clones):

- Veg until the plant is the size and shape you want
- Flip to 12/12 as normal
- Expect a bushier plant than the first round due to the
  multiple branching points
- Yield is typically 70–80% of the first harvest from the
  same plant

## Monster cropping (intentional reveg cloning)

Monster cropping is a specific technique that uses the reveg
process as a training method:

1. Take clones from a plant in weeks 1–3 of flower (while
   stems are still flexible and not fully lignified)
2. Root the clones under 18/6 or 24/0 light
3. The clones will reveg — producing the characteristic
   single-blade leaves and extreme branching
4. Once revegged, the clone has far more branching points than
   a standard clone or seed-grown plant
5. The result is a very bushy, multi-topped plant without any
   manual topping

**Why it works:** the flowering clone has already developed
multiple bud sites (potential branching points). When reverted
to veg, each of these sites produces a new branch. The result
is a naturally manifolded structure.

**Timing:** take the clone between days 7–21 of flower. Earlier
(week 1) reverts faster but has fewer bud sites. Later (week 3)
has more sites but takes longer to reveg and has a lower success
rate for rooting.

## Timeline

| Phase | Duration | What to expect |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Selective harvest | Day 0 | Leave lower 1/3 with leaves and small buds |
| Light switch to 18/6 | Day 0 | No visible change |
| Hormonal transition | Days 1–14 | Nothing visible — plant is internally adjusting |
| First reveg growth | Days 14–21 | Weird, rounded single-blade leaves emerge |
| Active reveg | Days 21–42 | Vigorous new growth, normal leaves appear |
| Ready for clones/training | Days 35–50 | Normal veg growth, ready to work with |

**Total time from harvest to usable veg plant: 5–7 weeks.**

## Common mistakes

1. **Cutting the entire plant and leaving just a stump** — a
   bare stump with no leaves has no photosynthetic surface.
   It CAN reveg but takes much longer and has a higher failure
   rate. Leave leaves and small bud sites
2. **Full-strength bloom nutrients after switching to 18/6** —
   the plant has minimal leaf area and is in transition. Start
   with half-strength veg nutrients and work up
3. **Expecting fast results** — reveg takes 3–6 weeks to see
   normal growth. The ugly phase is discouraging but normal.
   Don't give up at week 2 when the plant looks dead
4. **Not taking clones once revegged** — if you revegged to save
   genetics, take clones as soon as the plant has vigorous
   growth. Don't risk losing the genetics again to a second
   harvest disaster
5. **Revegging a plant that wasn't special** — reveg is a
   rescue technique for irreplaceable genetics. If the plant
   was mediocre, start fresh with new seeds. The time and
   electricity spent revegging a mediocre plant is better
   invested in a new grow
