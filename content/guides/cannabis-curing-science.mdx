Most growers understand that curing improves cannabis. Fewer
understand WHY. The standard advice — "put it in jars and burp
them" — describes the procedure without explaining the
biochemistry. When you understand what's actually happening
inside the jar, you make better decisions about humidity,
temperature, duration, and troubleshooting.

Curing is not slow drying. It's a controlled environment where
specific biochemical processes transform rough, hay-smelling
dried flower into smooth, aromatic, properly burning cannabis.

## What happens during curing

### Chlorophyll degradation

Fresh-dried cannabis smells like hay or grass. That smell is
chlorophyll — the green pigment that powers photosynthesis.
Chlorophyll has a harsh, vegetal taste when combusted.

During curing, chlorophyll breaks down through enzymatic
degradation:
- Chlorophyllase enzymes (still active in dried tissue at
  moderate moisture levels) cleave the phytol tail from the
  chlorophyll molecule
- The remaining chlorophyllide continues degrading into
  colorless pheophytins
- The process requires moisture (which is why jar curing works
  — the sealed environment maintains humidity) and time

**Timeline:** major chlorophyll breakdown occurs in the first
2–3 weeks of curing. This is why the hay smell fades and the
strain's true terpene aroma emerges during this period.

**Too dry = no cure.** If you over-dried the flower (below 55%
RH in the jar), the enzymes denature and chlorophyll
degradation stops. The hay taste persists permanently. This is
the most common curing failure.

### Starch and sugar conversion

Living cannabis stores energy as starch in the buds and leaves.
During curing, residual amylase enzymes convert starch into
simple sugars, which further break down:

- Starch → maltose → glucose → consumed by aerobic bacteria
  in the jar
- The removal of starches and sugars produces a smoother,
  cleaner smoke
- This is part of why cured cannabis burns more evenly (less
  unburned carbon residue from starches)

### Terpene preservation and transformation

Terpenes are volatile — they evaporate. The curing environment
(sealed jar, 58–62% RH, 60–68°F) slows terpene evaporation
while allowing enzymatic terpene modification:

- Some terpene precursors continue converting to active
  terpenes during curing
- The sealed jar prevents the rapid terpene loss that occurs
  in open-air drying
- Cool temperatures slow evaporation (terpenes evaporate
  faster at higher temperatures)

**This is why cured cannabis smells stronger than fresh-dried:**
the chlorophyll smell fades (unmasking the terpenes) while the
sealed environment retains them.

### Minor cannabinoid maturation

A small amount of THC-to-CBN conversion occurs during curing
(the same process that happens on the plant during late
flower). The effect is minor — perhaps a 1–3% shift — but
contributes to the "smoother" effect profile that well-cured
cannabis is known for.

THCA also continues decarboxylating very slowly at room
temperature, though the rate is negligible compared to heat
decarboxylation.

## The curing protocol

### Prerequisites

**The flower must be properly dried first.** Curing starts
AFTER drying, not instead of it:

- Dried to 60–65% moisture content
- Small stems snap (don't bend) when flexed
- The outside of buds feels dry to the touch but isn't crispy
- This typically takes 7–14 days of hanging in 60°F / 60% RH

See our [drying guide](/guides/drying-room-setup).

### Week-by-week

**Week 1: Active burping**

1. Place dried buds loosely in mason jars (fill 75% — leave
   airspace for gas exchange)
2. Open (burp) each jar 2–3 times daily for 10–15 minutes
3. If the inside of the jar smells like ammonia or the buds
   feel moist, they're too wet — take buds out and dry for
   2–4 hours, then re-jar
4. Humidity inside the jar should stabilize at 58–65% RH
   (use a small hygrometer in the jar)
5. If humidity reads above 70%: buds are too wet. Remove and
   dry further. If below 55%: buds are too dry. Add a humidity
   pack (Boveda 62%) or a small piece of fresh fan leaf for
   24 hours to rehydrate slightly

**Week 2: Reduced burping**

- Burp once daily for 5–10 minutes
- The hay smell should be fading
- The strain's natural terpene aroma begins emerging
- Humidity should be stable at 58–62% RH

**Weeks 3–4: Maintenance**

- Burp every 2–3 days for 5 minutes
- The aroma should be developing fully
- Smoke quality improves noticeably between weeks 2 and 4
- Some growers consider the cure "done" at 4 weeks

**Weeks 4–8+: Extended cure (optional)**

- Burp once per week (or seal with a Boveda pack and don't
  open)
- Flavor and smoothness continue improving through week 8
- Diminishing returns after 8 weeks for most strains
- Some strains (heavy indicas, high-terpene varieties) benefit
  from 3–6 month cures

### Optimal conditions

| Parameter | Target | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Temperature | 60–68°F | Preserves terpenes, prevents mold |
| Humidity (in jar) | 58–62% RH | Maintains enzymatic activity without mold risk |
| Light | Complete darkness | Light degrades THC and terpenes |
| Container | Glass mason jars | Inert, airtight, doesn't impart flavors |

### Humidity control

**Boveda or Integra Boost packs** (62% RH) are the simplest
way to maintain perfect humidity:

- Place one pack per quart jar (or one per ounce of flower)
- The pack absorbs or releases moisture to maintain 62% RH
- Eliminates the need for careful burping after week 2
- Replace when the pack becomes rigid (no longer pliable)

**The debate:** some growers believe humidity packs strip
terpenes. The research is mixed — Boveda claims their packs
don't absorb terpenes, and most side-by-side tests show
negligible difference. The convenience benefit outweighs any
theoretical terpene risk.

## Curing containers

### Glass mason jars (recommended)

- Inert (doesn't react with terpenes or cannabinoids)
- Airtight seal
- Transparent (store in dark location or use dark jars)
- Available in quart (1 oz capacity) and half-gallon (2 oz)
- Affordable and reusable indefinitely

### Stainless steel containers

- Inert and lightproof
- More expensive
- Work well but no advantage over glass in a dark location

### Plastic containers

**Not recommended.** Plastic can:
- Absorb terpenes (reducing aroma)
- Outgas plasticizers that impart off-flavors
- Build static charge that strips trichomes from buds
- Not seal as tightly as glass

### Vacuum-sealed bags

Useful for long-term storage (3+ months) after initial jar
curing is complete. Not suitable for active curing — the vacuum
crushes buds and doesn't allow burping.

### Turkey bags / oven bags

Used by commercial operations for large quantities. The nylon
is relatively inert and doesn't strip terpenes significantly.
Acceptable for bulk curing but harder to control humidity than
jars.

## Troubleshooting

### Ammonia smell in the jar

**Cause:** buds are too wet. Anaerobic bacteria are
decomposing plant material. This is the beginning of mold.

**Fix:** immediately remove buds from jars. Spread on a drying
rack in a well-ventilated space for 12–24 hours. Re-jar when
buds feel dry on the outside. Resume burping 3x daily.

### Hay smell won't go away

**Cause:** either the buds were dried too fast (high
temperature, low humidity drying) or they're too dry for
enzymatic chlorophyll breakdown.

**Fix:** if buds are below 55% RH in the jar, add a 62%
Boveda pack to slowly rehydrate them. Some chlorophyll
breakdown may resume. If the buds were dried at high
temperature (above 75°F), the enzymes may be denatured and the
hay taste is permanent — a drying error, not a curing one.

### Buds are too dry (crispy)

**Cause:** over-dried before jarring, or too much burping.

**Fix:** add a 62% Boveda pack and seal the jar for 48 hours
without opening. The pack will slowly rehydrate the buds.
Don't add wet material (orange peels, lettuce, wet paper
towel) — these introduce mold risk and inconsistent moisture.

### Mold in the jar

**Cause:** buds were too wet when jarred (above 65% RH),
inadequate burping, or ambient contamination.

**Fix:** if caught early (small white fuzzy spots on one bud),
remove the affected bud and all buds touching it. Dry the
remaining buds for 6–12 hours and re-jar. If extensive (entire
jar has visible mold), discard the contents. Do NOT smoke
moldy cannabis.

**Prevention:** always dry to the point where small stems snap
before jarring. Use a hygrometer in the jar. Never jar above
65% RH.

## How long to cure

| Duration | Quality level | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| 0 weeks (uncured) | Harsh, hay smell, burns unevenly | Smokeable but unpleasant |
| 2 weeks | Noticeable improvement, reduced hay | Minimum acceptable cure |
| 4 weeks | Good — smooth smoke, emerging terpenes | Standard recommendation |
| 8 weeks | Excellent — full terpene expression, very smooth | Optimal for most strains |
| 3–6 months | Premium — complex, refined, wine-like aging | Diminishing returns but some strains benefit |
| 6+ months | Risk of terpene degradation | Only for long-term storage with Boveda packs in dark, cool conditions |

**The minimum viable cure is 2 weeks.** Below that, the
chlorophyll is still dominant and the smoke is harsh. Most
noticeable improvement happens between weeks 2 and 4.

## Common mistakes

1. **Jarring too wet** — the #1 curing mistake. If stems bend
   instead of snapping, the buds aren't dry enough to jar.
   Wet buds in sealed jars grow mold. Dry properly before
   jarring
2. **Drying too fast** — rapid drying at high temperature
   denatures the enzymes that break down chlorophyll.
   The result is permanently harsh, hay-tasting flower that
   no amount of curing will fix. Dry slowly at 60°F / 60% RH
3. **Not burping** — the jar builds up moisture and gases
   from biological activity. Without regular air exchange,
   humidity spikes above 70% and mold develops. Burp daily
   for the first week, then gradually reduce
4. **Storing in light** — UV and visible light degrade THC
   (converting it to CBN) and break down terpenes. Store jars
   in a dark cabinet, closet, or box. If using clear jars,
   keep them in a dark location
5. **Skipping the cure entirely** — "it's dry, let's smoke it"
   wastes weeks of growing effort. A 2-week cure dramatically
   improves the product you spent 4 months growing. The
   patience pays off in every bowl
