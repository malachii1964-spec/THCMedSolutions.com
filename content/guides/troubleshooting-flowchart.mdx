When something goes wrong with your plant, the worst thing you
can do is guess and throw solutions at it. Most cannabis problems
have a specific cause, and misdiagnosing leads to making the
problem worse. This guide is a systematic flowchart: start with
what you see, follow the decision tree, arrive at the most likely
cause, and get the specific fix.

**Before you start:** check these three things first. They cause
the majority of cannabis problems and should be verified before
investigating anything else:

1. **pH:** is your input water between 6.0–6.8 (soil) or 5.5–6.5
   (coco/hydro)? If not, fix this first — most nutrient problems
   are actually pH problems. See our
   [pH guide](/guides/ph-for-beginners)
2. **Watering:** is the medium wet or dry? Does the pot feel heavy
   (overwatered) or light (underwatered)?
3. **Light distance:** has anything changed with your light height
   or intensity recently?

If all three check out, proceed to the symptom below.

## Symptom 1: Yellow leaves

### Where are the yellow leaves?

**Bottom leaves only → Nitrogen deficiency** (most likely)
- Nitrogen is mobile — the plant pulls it from old leaves to feed
  new growth
- Older fan leaves turn uniformly pale green, then yellow, then
  drop
- New growth stays green
- **In late flower:** this is normal. The plant naturally
  cannibalizes lower leaves during senescence. No action needed
  unless it's aggressive
- **In veg or early flower:** increase nitrogen in your feed.
  Check pH first — nitrogen lockout occurs below pH 5.5 and above
  pH 7.5
- See our [deficiency chart](/guides/nutrient-deficiency-chart)

**Top/new growth → Iron deficiency or light burn**
- **If the yellowing is between veins (interveinal chlorosis) on
  new leaves:** iron deficiency. Common at high pH (above 6.8 in
  soil). Fix pH, add chelated iron
- **If the yellowing is bleaching/whitening of leaves closest to
  the light:** light burn. Raise the light 6–12 inches or reduce
  intensity. See our
  [light burn guide](/guides/light-burn-light-stress)

**All leaves, uniformly → pH lockout**
- When pH is outside the acceptable range, multiple nutrients
  become unavailable simultaneously
- The plant yellows everywhere, doesn't respond to additional
  nutrients
- Flush the medium with correctly pH'd water, then resume feeding
  at correct pH
- See our [pH lockout guide](/guides/ph-lockout-and-nutrient-problems)

**Yellowing between veins (lower leaves) → Magnesium deficiency**
- Interveinal chlorosis on lower/middle leaves — the tissue
  between veins turns yellow while veins stay green
- Common in coco grows (coco binds magnesium) and with RO water
  (no mineral content)
- Fix: cal-mag supplement. See our
  [cal-mag guide](/guides/cal-mag-complete-guide)

**Yellowing between veins (upper leaves) → Iron deficiency**
- Same interveinal pattern but on NEW growth (iron is immobile)
- Almost always a pH issue — iron locks out above pH 6.8 in soil
- Fix: lower pH. Chelated iron as a temporary supplement

## Symptom 2: Brown or burnt leaf tips

### How much of the tip is burnt?

**Just the very tips (1–3mm) → Mild nutrient burn**
- The plant is getting slightly more nutrients than it can use
- Reduce feed strength by 10–20%
- Monitor: if it doesn't progress, you're close to the plant's
  tolerance. Some tip burn is normal in aggressive feeding regimens

**Tips + edges browning progressively → Moderate nutrient burn**
- Reduce feed strength by 30–40%
- Check EC/PPM of your feed AND runoff. If runoff EC is
  significantly higher than input, salts have accumulated — flush
  with plain pH'd water, then resume at lower concentration
- See our [EC guide](/guides/ec-ppm-measuring)

**Brown crispy edges (not tips) → Potassium deficiency or heat
stress**
- **If edges brown and curl upward:** heat stress. Check
  temperature at canopy level — should be 72–82°F. Raise the
  light, improve airflow, or reduce ambient temperature
- **If edges brown from the outside in, lower leaves first:**
  potassium deficiency. Check pH (K locks out below 5.5), increase
  K in feed

## Symptom 3: Spots on leaves

### What do the spots look like?

**Small brown spots (rust-colored) → Calcium deficiency**
- Small, irregularly shaped brown spots scattered across leaves
- Often accompanied by curling leaf edges
- Common in coco (coco binds calcium) and with RO or soft water
- Fix: cal-mag supplement, check pH (Ca locks out below 6.2 in
  soil)
- See our [cal-mag guide](/guides/cal-mag-complete-guide)

**Small yellow spots (speckles) → Spider mites**
- Tiny yellow dots (stippling) on the top of leaves
- Check the underside of leaves with a loupe — mites are tiny
  (0.5mm), round, and may have webbing
- See our [pest guide](/guides/pests-mites-gnats-mildew)

**White powdery spots → Powdery mildew**
- White, powdery coating on leaf surfaces
- Starts as small circular patches, spreads rapidly
- Caused by poor airflow + high humidity
- See our [powdery mildew guide](/guides/powdery-mildew-guide)

**Large irregular brown patches → Leaf septoria or nutrient splash**
- **If patches have a yellow halo:** leaf septoria (fungal). Remove
  affected leaves, improve airflow, apply neem or copper fungicide
- **If patches are random and don't spread:** nutrient solution
  splashed on the leaf and burned it under light. Not a disease —
  avoid getting nutrient water on leaves

## Symptom 4: Wilting / drooping

### Is the medium wet or dry?

**Medium is wet (heavy pot) → Overwatering**
- Leaves droop uniformly, including the petioles (stems)
- The entire plant looks limp and heavy
- Let the medium dry out completely before watering again
- Check drainage — ensure the pot has holes and water isn't
  pooling
- See our
  [overwatering guide](/guides/overwatering-underwatering)

**Medium is dry (light pot) → Underwatering**
- Leaves droop but look thin and papery (not plump)
- Petioles may point downward while leaf blades curl slightly
  upward
- Water thoroughly until 10–20% runoff
- If severely wilted, water in stages — a small amount, wait 15
  minutes, then water fully

**Medium is wet AND leaves are wilting after days → Root rot**
- The plant wilts despite wet medium because damaged roots can't
  absorb water
- Check roots: they should be white and firm. Brown, slimy,
  foul-smelling roots = root rot
- See our [root rot guide](/guides/root-rot-pythium)

**Wilting only during lights on, recovers during lights off →
Heat or light stress**
- The plant can't transpire fast enough under intense light/heat
- Check canopy temperature — hold your hand at canopy height for
  30 seconds. If it's uncomfortably warm, it's too hot for the
  plant
- Raise the light, improve ventilation, or add an oscillating fan

## Symptom 5: Curling leaves

### Which direction?

**Curling upward (taco-ing) → Heat stress or light stress**
- Leaves fold upward along the midrib, like a taco
- The plant is trying to reduce light-exposed surface area
- Fix: raise light, lower temperature, improve airflow
- See our [light burn guide](/guides/light-burn-light-stress)

**Curling downward (clawing) → Nitrogen toxicity or overwatering**
- **If leaves are dark green AND clawing:** nitrogen toxicity.
  The plant has excess nitrogen — leaves turn very dark green and
  curl downward. Reduce nitrogen in feed. See our
  [nitrogen toxicity guide](/guides/nitrogen-toxicity-excess)
- **If leaves are normal green AND drooping/clawing:** overwatering.
  Let the medium dry out

**Edges curling upward (canoeing) → Heat or low humidity**
- Leaf edges curl up while the center stays flat
- Usually a VPD problem — temperature too high relative to
  humidity
- See our [VPD guide](/guides/temperature-humidity-vpd)

**Twisting or irregular curling → pH swings or root problems**
- Leaves that twist or curl asymmetrically usually indicate root
  zone issues
- Check pH of input AND runoff
- Inspect roots if possible

## Symptom 6: Slow or stunted growth

### When did growth slow?

**Sudden stop after a change → Stress response**
- After transplanting: transplant shock. Normal — wait 3–7 days
  for recovery. See our
  [transplant guide](/guides/transplant-shock-recovery)
- After topping or heavy training: recovery period. Normal — wait
  3–5 days
- After changing nutrients or pH: check input pH and EC. Sudden
  changes stress roots

**Gradual slowdown → Root-bound, pH creep, or environmental issue**
- **Root-bound:** roots circling the bottom of the pot, visible
  through drainage holes. Transplant to a larger container
- **pH creep:** pH of the medium has drifted over time. Check
  runoff pH — if it's significantly different from input pH, flush
  and recalibrate
- **Low light:** during winter or with aging LEDs, light output
  decreases. Check PPFD at canopy level
- **Temperature:** growth slows below 65°F and above 85°F

**Growth slow from the start → Bad genetics, poor seedling
conditions, or contaminated medium**
- Some seeds produce weak plants. If all other conditions are
  correct, it may be genetics
- Check for fungus gnats in the medium — larvae feed on root
  hairs and slow growth in seedlings. See our
  [pest guide](/guides/pests-mites-gnats-mildew)

## Symptom 7: Discoloration other than yellow

### What color?

**Dark green (almost black) → Nitrogen toxicity**
- Leaves are unnaturally dark green, often with a waxy sheen
- Usually accompanied by downward clawing
- Reduce nitrogen immediately
- See our
  [nitrogen toxicity guide](/guides/nitrogen-toxicity-excess)

**Purple stems or petioles → Genetic or phosphorus deficiency**
- **If the plant is otherwise healthy and growing well:** genetic.
  Many strains have naturally purple stems
- **If accompanied by slow growth, dark leaves, or poor flower
  development:** phosphorus deficiency. Check pH (P locks out
  below 5.5 and above 7.0)
- See our
  [color expression guide](/guides/cannabis-color-expression)

**Red or pink stems/leaves → Phosphorus deficiency or cold stress**
- Check root zone temperature — below 60°F impairs phosphorus
  uptake
- Check pH — phosphorus availability drops sharply below 6.0 in
  soil

**Brown patches on buds → Bud rot (Botrytis)**
- Gray-brown spots on buds, often starting inside dense colas
- Mushy texture, gray fuzz visible under magnification
- Remove affected buds immediately — cut 1 inch below the
  infected area
- See our [bud rot guide](/guides/bud-rot-botrytis)

## The 5-step diagnostic process

When you encounter a problem:

1. **Check pH** — test input water pH, test runoff pH. If either
   is out of range, fix it and wait 48 hours before doing
   anything else
2. **Check watering** — lift the pot. Is it too heavy (wet) or
   too light (dry)? Adjust watering before adding nutrients
3. **Check environment** — temperature, humidity, light distance.
   Use a thermometer at canopy level, not room level
4. **Identify the symptom pattern** — WHERE on the plant (top,
   bottom, everywhere), WHAT it looks like (yellowing, spots,
   curling, wilting), and HOW FAST it's progressing
5. **Make ONE change** — fix the most likely cause and wait 48–72
   hours. Multiple simultaneous changes make diagnosis impossible
   if the problem persists
