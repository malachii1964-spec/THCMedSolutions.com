You've spent 3–4 months growing this plant. The difference between
a good harvest and a great one comes down to 7–10 days — the harvest
window. Cut too early and the buds are underdeveloped, airy, and
lacking potency. Cut too late and the THC degrades, the high becomes
sleepy, and the flower loses its edge.

Trichomes — the tiny, mushroom-shaped resin glands covering the
buds — are your precision tool for timing. They change color as
they mature, and the color tells you exactly where you are in the
harvest window.

## What trichomes are

Trichomes are the resin glands that produce cannabinoids (THC, CBD),
terpenes (smell and flavor), and flavonoids. They appear as a
frosty, crystalline coating on buds and sugar leaves.

There are three types:
- **Capitate-stalked trichomes:** the largest, most visible.
  Mushroom-shaped with a visible stalk and bulbous head. These are
  what you're reading for harvest timing — they contain the most
  cannabinoids
- **Capitate-sessile trichomes:** smaller, no visible stalk. Cover
  the bud surface between the larger trichomes
- **Bulbous trichomes:** microscopic, on leaf and stem surfaces.
  Not relevant for harvest timing

## Equipment: what you need to see them

Trichomes are too small to read with the naked eye. You need
magnification:

| Tool | Magnification | Usability | Cost |
|------|--------------|-----------|------|
| Jeweler's loupe | 30x–60x | Requires steady hand, direct inspection | $5–15 |
| USB microscope | 50x–250x | Shows on phone/computer screen, easier to focus | $15–40 |
| Phone macro lens | 10x–30x | Quick check, may not show clear color detail | $10–20 |
| Phone camera + digital zoom | 2x–5x | Not enough magnification for accurate reading | Free |

**Recommendation:** a USB digital microscope ($20–30 on Amazon) is
the best tool for most growers. You can photograph the trichomes,
zoom in on-screen, and compare over days. A 60x jeweler's loupe
works but requires practice to hold steady while focusing on a
living bud.

## Trichome stages

### Stage 1: Clear (translucent)

**What it looks like:** trichome heads are glassy and see-through,
like tiny glass mushrooms. If you hold a loupe to the bud, the
heads look like clear water droplets on stalks.

**What it means:** the trichomes are still developing. THC
production is ramping up but hasn't peaked. Harvesting now produces
weak, underdeveloped flower.

**Action:** wait. The plant needs more time. Typically 1–3 weeks
before the harvest window opens.

### Stage 2: Milky/cloudy (opaque)

**What it looks like:** trichome heads turn from clear to milky
white or cloudy — like frosted glass instead of clear glass. The
stalks may still be clear, but the heads are distinctly opaque.

**What it means:** THC concentration is at or near its peak. The
trichome head is fully loaded with cannabinoids. This is the harvest
zone.

**Action:** the harvest window is open. How long you wait within
this window depends on the effect you want (see below).

### Stage 3: Amber (degraded)

**What it looks like:** trichome heads shift from milky to amber,
gold, or dark orange. The color change is gradual — individual
trichomes transition at different rates, so you'll see a mix.

**What it means:** THC is degrading into CBN (cannabinol). CBN is
less psychoactive and more sedating than THC. The more amber
trichomes, the heavier and more body-focused the effect.

**Action:** the harvest window is closing. Harvesting with a high
percentage of amber trichomes produces a sedative, couch-lock
effect.

## The harvest window

The harvest window is the period when trichomes are a mix of milky
and amber. Where you cut within this window changes the character
of the flower:

| Trichome ratio | Effect profile | Best for |
|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Mostly clear, some milky | Too early — low potency | Don't harvest yet |
| 80–90% milky, 0–10% amber | Energetic, cerebral, clear-headed | Sativa-leaning effect, daytime use |
| 70% milky, 20–30% amber | Balanced — head and body | Most growers' sweet spot |
| 50% milky, 50% amber | Heavy, sedating, strong body effect | Indica effect, pain relief, sleep |
| Mostly amber | Degraded — sleepy, flat high | Past peak; harvest immediately |

### The popular choice

**70% milky, 20–30% amber** is the most commonly recommended harvest
point. It balances peak THC (from milky trichomes) with enough CBN
(from amber trichomes) to round out the effect without making it
one-dimensional.

### Strain-specific timing

- **Sativa-dominant strains** (energetic effects desired): harvest at
  80–90% milky, minimal amber
- **Indica-dominant strains** (body/sedation desired): let more
  trichomes go amber — 30–50% amber
- **CBD strains:** trichome color matters less; focus on the breeder's
  recommended harvest time, as CBD doesn't degrade like THC

## Where to check trichomes

### Check the buds, not the sugar leaves

Sugar leaves (the small, trichome-covered leaves protruding from
buds) mature faster than the calyx trichomes. If you read sugar
leaf trichomes, you'll think the plant is riper than it actually
is.

**Always read trichomes on the calyx surface** (the actual bud
tissue, not the leaves). Focus on:
- The top 1/3 of the main colas (gets the most light, ripens first)
- Multiple bud sites (top, middle, lower) to assess overall
  maturity
- The same area each time you check, for consistent comparison

### Lower buds ripen later

Lower and interior buds receive less light and ripen 1–2 weeks
behind the top colas. You can:
- **Harvest the top colas first** and let the lowers continue for
  another week (staged harvest)
- **Harvest all at once** based on the average maturity across the
  plant (simpler)

## Other harvest indicators

Trichomes are the most precise indicator, but other signs confirm
you're in the window:

### Pistil color

- **90%+ of pistils have darkened** (turned from white to orange,
  red, or brown) and curled inward
- White pistils = the bud is still developing. Don't harvest a bud
  with mostly white pistils, even if a few trichomes look milky

### Fan leaf yellowing

- **Natural fan leaf yellowing** (the "fade") in weeks 7–9 is the
  plant redirecting nutrients to the buds. This is normal and
  desirable — it means the plant is senescing (shutting down)
- The fade does NOT mean the plant is deficient — don't feed more
  nitrogen to "fix" it

### Bud structure

- Calyxes should be swollen and plump
- The bud should feel dense and firm when gently squeezed
- Foxtailing (new calyx towers growing from the top of the bud) can
  indicate the harvest window is closing

## Checking schedule

Once you suspect the plant is approaching harvest:

| Time before expected harvest | Check frequency |
|-----------------------------|----------------|
| 3+ weeks | Weekly visual inspection |
| 2 weeks | Every 3–4 days with loupe/microscope |
| 1 week | Daily trichome checks |
| In the window | Daily — the transition from milky to amber accelerates |

## Common mistakes

1. **Relying on pistil color alone** — pistils darken for many
   reasons (wind, touch, age). They're a rough indicator, not a
   precise one. Trichomes are the real tool
2. **Checking sugar leaves instead of calyxes** — sugar leaf
   trichomes ripen 1–2 weeks ahead of calyx trichomes. You'll
   harvest early if you read the wrong surface
3. **Harvesting at the first sign of amber** — a few amber trichomes
   on a field of milky is barely into the harvest window. Wait for
   20–30% amber for a balanced effect
4. **Not using magnification** — trichomes appear milky to the naked
   eye long before they actually are. A 30x loupe minimum is
   necessary for accurate reading
5. **Applying the same timing to every strain** — a 9-week strain and
   a 12-week strain have different harvest windows. Check the
   breeder's estimated flower time and start trichome inspections
   1–2 weeks before that date
