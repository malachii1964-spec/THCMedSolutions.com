Mainlining (also called manifolding) is a high-stress training
technique that creates a single hub from which all branches
radiate equally. Instead of one dominant cola with diminishing
side branches, a mainlined plant produces 8, 16, or 32 colas
of nearly identical size — because every branch receives the
same amount of energy from the root system through a
symmetrical plumbing system.

The technique takes 2–3 extra weeks in veg compared to an
untrained plant. But the result is a flat, even canopy that
maximizes light utilization and produces remarkably consistent
bud size across every cola.

## Why mainlining works

### The apical dominance problem

Untrained cannabis directs growth hormone (auxin) to the main
apex — the top of the plant. This creates apical dominance:
the main cola grows tall and thick while side branches lag
behind. The result is a Christmas tree shape with one large
top bud and many smaller, airy lowers.

Topping removes the apex and redistributes growth hormone to
the two branches below the cut. But a single topping still
leaves the plant slightly asymmetrical — the two resulting
branches aren't perfectly equal because one was always the
slightly more vigorous of the pair.

### The manifold solution

Mainlining solves this by:
1. Topping to create two branches
2. Removing ALL other growth below the manifold point
3. Waiting for the two branches to grow equally
4. Topping each of those two branches → four branches
5. Removing all growth below → clean manifold
6. Optionally topping again → eight branches (or → sixteen)

Each topping doubles the cola count. Each cleanup forces all
energy through the manifold. The result is a hub-and-spoke
structure where every branch has the same length of stem
between it and the root system — equal plumbing, equal flow,
equal bud development.

## Step-by-step mainlining

### Prerequisites

- **Start from seed or a vigorous clone.** Mainlining requires
  a healthy plant that can handle repeated high-stress training
- **The plant should have at least 5–6 nodes** before the first
  topping (typically 3–4 weeks from seed)
- **Not recommended for autoflowers.** The extended veg time
  required for mainlining cuts into an autoflower's fixed life
  cycle. Use LST or SOG for autos instead

### Step 1: First topping (creating the Y)

**When:** plant has 5–6 nodes of growth.

**Where to top:** above the 3rd node. This leaves three pairs
of branches below the cut. You'll keep the 3rd node branches
and remove everything below.

**Process:**
1. Top the plant above node 3 with a clean blade
2. Remove ALL growth at nodes 1 and 2 (the lowest branches
   and their fan leaves)
3. Leave the two branches at node 3 and their fan leaves
4. The result is a single stem topped into a Y shape with
   two equal branches

**Why node 3?** Node 3 provides enough stem below the manifold
for the plant to develop a strong base, while keeping the
manifold low enough for easy canopy management. Some growers
prefer node 4 for slightly more height clearance.

**After the cut:**
- The plant will stall for 3–5 days as it redirects growth
  hormone
- New growth will emerge from both branches at the manifold
  point
- Wait until both branches have grown 3–4 new nodes each
  before proceeding (typically 7–14 days)

### Step 2: Training the Y flat

While the two branches grow:
- Gently bend them outward to a roughly horizontal angle
- Tie them down with soft plant ties to the pot rim or a
  training wire
- The goal is a flat, wide Y — not a vertical V
- As the branches grow, continue bending outward to keep the
  canopy flat

### Step 3: Second topping (creating 4 colas)

**When:** each of the two branches has grown 3–4 new nodes.

**Process:**
1. Top each branch above its 3rd new node (counting from the
   manifold, not from the soil)
2. Remove all growth below the topping point on each branch
   EXCEPT the top pair of branches at the cut
3. The result: 4 branches radiating from the manifold

**After the cut:**
- Another 3–5 day stall
- Wait for all 4 branches to resume vigorous growth (7–14 days)
- Continue tying branches outward for flat canopy shape

### Step 4: Third topping (creating 8 colas — optional)

**When:** each of the 4 branches has grown 2–3 new nodes.

**Process:**
1. Top each of the 4 branches above their 2nd or 3rd new node
2. Remove lower growth on each branch except the top pair
3. The result: 8 branches radiating from the manifold

For most home grows (2×2 to 4×4 tents), 8 colas is the sweet
spot. It's enough to fill a canopy without overcrowding, and
each cola receives enough light and energy to develop fully.

### Step 5 (optional): Fourth topping (creating 16 colas)

For larger spaces (4×8+) or growers who want maximum cola count.
The same process: top each of 8 branches to create 16. This
requires a longer veg period and is only practical in larger
grows with strong lighting.

### Step 6: Flip to flower

Once all branches in the manifold have recovered from the last
topping and are growing vigorously (usually 5–7 days after
the final top), flip to 12/12.

**Expected stretch:** mainlined plants stretch 50–100% of their
pre-flip height during flower (strain-dependent). Account for
this when deciding when to flip.

## Timeline

| Week | Action | Node count/cola count |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| Week 1–3 | Grow from seed/clone to 5–6 nodes | — |
| Week 3–4 | First topping at node 3, strip below | 2 branches |
| Week 4–5 | Train flat, wait for regrowth | 2 growing |
| Week 5–6 | Second topping | 4 branches |
| Week 6–7 | Train flat, wait for regrowth | 4 growing |
| Week 7–8 | Third topping (optional) | 8 branches |
| Week 8–9 | Train flat, final recovery | 8 growing |
| Week 9+ | Flip to 12/12 | 8 flowering colas |

**Total veg time:** 8–10 weeks for an 8-cola manifold (vs.
5–6 weeks for an untrained plant). The extra 2–4 weeks in
veg is the cost of mainlining.

## Mainlining vs. other training methods

| Method | Cola uniformity | Veg time | Stress level | Best for |
|--------|----------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Mainlining | Excellent (all equal) | Long (+2-4 weeks) | High (multiple toppings) | Patient growers wanting uniformity |
| Topping + LST | Good (mostly equal) | Moderate (+1-2 weeks) | Moderate | Most home growers |
| SCROG | Good (net equalizes) | Moderate to long | Low to moderate | Maximizing canopy fill |
| LST only | Fair (dominant cola still exists) | Minimal | Low | Autoflowers, quick grows |
| SOG | N/A (one cola per plant) | Minimal | None | Many small plants from clone |

### When mainlining is worth it

- You have the patience for 8–10 week veg cycles
- You want maximum uniformity (all buds the same size and
  quality)
- You're growing 1–2 plants in a tent and need to fill the
  canopy efficiently
- You're growing photoperiod strains (not autos)
- You enjoy the craftsmanship of training

### When to skip mainlining

- Autoflowers (veg time is fixed by genetics — don't waste it
  recovering from high-stress training)
- Quick turnaround grows (perpetual harvest cycles where veg
  time matters)
- SOG setups (many small plants don't need individual training)
- You want low-stress growing with minimal intervention

## Tips for success

### During topping

- Use clean, sharp scissors or a razor blade. Crush cuts heal
  slower and invite infection
- Top in the evening (or just before lights-off) so the plant
  has a dark period to start recovery
- Remove growth below the manifold completely — don't leave
  stubs that redirect energy away from the manifold branches

### During training

- Tie branches gently — don't bend past 90 degrees in one
  session. Gradual bending over 2–3 days prevents snapping
- Use soft ties (pipe cleaners, rubber-coated wire, cloth
  strips). Hard wire cuts into growing stems
- Check ties every 2–3 days and loosen if they're digging in.
  Fast-growing stems can be girdled by tight ties in days
- Keep the canopy flat by adjusting tie positions as branches
  grow. The goal is a perfectly level plane at the same height

### During flower

- Lollipop the lower 1/3 of each branch in the first two weeks
  of flower (remove small side shoots that won't receive enough
  light to produce quality buds)
- The symmetrical manifold means light reaches all colas equally
  — less need for defoliation than with untrained plants
- Support branches with stakes or a trellis net once buds gain
  weight — mainlined colas can be uniformly heavy

## Yield expectations

Mainlining doesn't necessarily increase total yield compared
to other advanced training methods (topping + LST + SCROG).
What it does is improve yield QUALITY:

- All 8 colas are top-shelf quality (not a mix of tops and
  lowers)
- Less trim waste (fewer larfy lower buds)
- More consistent dry weight per cola
- Better bag appeal (uniform bud size)

**Typical yields (4×4 tent, 300W LED, single mainlined plant):**
- 8-cola manifold: 4–8 oz total (0.5–1.0 oz per cola)
- For comparison, an untrained plant in the same space: 3–6 oz
  total but with 1 large top cola and many smaller, less dense
  lower buds

## Common mistakes

1. **Topping too early** — topping before node 5 doesn't leave
   enough stem below the manifold for structural support. Wait
   until the plant is vigorous with 5–6 nodes
2. **Not removing all growth below the manifold** — leaving
   extra branches defeats the purpose. The manifold only works
   when ALL energy flows through the hub. Strip everything below
   clean
3. **Rushing the recovery period** — each topping needs 5–7
   days of recovery before the next step. Topping a plant that
   hasn't recovered from the last topping compounds the stress
   and stunts growth
4. **Mainlining autoflowers** — autoflowers can't extend veg
   to recover from repeated toppings. By the time the manifold
   is built, the plant is flowering. Use LST on autos
5. **Uneven training** — if one branch is trained higher than
   others, it gets more light and grows faster, defeating the
   symmetry. Keep all branches at the same height by adjusting
   tie points regularly
6. **Over-topping (too many colas)** — 8 colas is ideal for
   most home grows. 16 or 32 colas splits the plant's energy
   so many ways that individual bud size suffers unless the
   plant is large and the light is powerful
