Cloning lets you copy a great plant. A mother plant lets you copy it
forever. Instead of hunting through seeds hoping to find the same
phenotype twice (which never happens — every seed is genetically
unique), you keep one exceptional plant alive in perpetual vegetative
state and take cuttings from it whenever you need a new round.

One healthy mother can supply 10–30 clones per cycle, indefinitely.
The clones are genetically identical to the mother and to each other:
same potency, same terpene profile, same growth habit, same flowering
time. No surprises.

This guide covers how to select, grow, maintain, and manage mother
plants for a home grow operation.

## Selecting the mother

The mother plant is the genetic blueprint for every clone you'll grow
from here on. This decision is worth investing time in.

### The pheno hunt

If you're starting from seed, you need to grow out multiple plants,
flower them, and evaluate the results before picking your mother. This
is called a "pheno hunt."

**The process:**

1. **Grow 4–10 seeds** of the strain you want to keep
2. **Take clones from each plant** before flipping to flower (label them
   meticulously — Plant A clone, Plant B clone, etc.). Keep these clones
   in veg while the originals flower
3. **Flower the originals** and evaluate:
   - Potency (smoke test — there's no substitute)
   - Terpene profile (smell and taste)
   - Yield (total dry weight per plant)
   - Bud structure (density, bag appeal, frost)
   - Flowering time (shorter is more efficient)
   - Pest/mold resistance (did it handle issues better than siblings?)
   - Growth habit (does it respond well to training?)
4. **Pick the winner** — the plant that scores highest across all
   criteria
5. **The clone of the winner becomes your mother.** The other clones
   can be discarded or given away

### What makes a good mother

Not every phenotype makes a good mother, even if it produces great
flower. The ideal mother also:

- **Branches vigorously** — more branching = more clone sites per
  cycle. A leggy, single-cola plant makes a poor mother because you
  can't harvest many cuttings without damaging it
- **Recovers quickly from cutting** — after you take clones, the
  mother should bounce back within a week and start filling out again.
  Some phenotypes are resilient; others stall after cutting
- **Stays healthy in veg indefinitely** — a mother will live in veg
  for months or years. She needs to tolerate the low-stress conditions
  of a maintenance veg without declining
- **Clones root easily** — some phenotypes root in 5 days; others take
  14+. Consistent, fast rooting saves time and reduces failure rates

## Setting up the mother space

### Light

- **18/6 or 20/4 light schedule** — the mother must stay in veg. Any
  interruption that gives her 12+ hours of uninterrupted darkness can
  trigger flowering
- **Low-to-moderate intensity** — the mother doesn't need to grow fast.
  200–400 PPFD is sufficient. A 50–100W LED works for 1–2 mothers.
  Running her at full blast wastes electricity and forces more frequent
  pruning
- **Spectrum** — blue-heavy or full spectrum. Blue light promotes
  compact, bushy growth, which is exactly what you want for a mother
  that stays manageable in a small space

### Container

- **5–7 gallon pot** for a long-term mother. Smaller pots (3 gallon)
  work but require more frequent feeding and become root-bound sooner
- **Fabric pots** are ideal — they air-prune roots, preventing
  circling and root-binding
- **Plan to up-pot or root-prune** every 6–12 months (see maintenance
  below)

### Medium and nutrients

- **Soil or coco** — either works. Soil is lower maintenance; coco
  offers more control
- **Nitrogen-heavy feed** — the mother is in veg indefinitely, so she
  needs a veg nutrient ratio (higher N) throughout her life. No bloom
  nutrients ever. She never flowers
- **Feed at 50–75% strength** — you don't want explosive growth. You
  want steady, manageable growth that produces healthy cutting material

### Space

A single mother takes about 2×2 feet. She'll be pruned regularly so
she never outgrows this footprint. If you're running multiple mothers
(different strains), a small shelving unit with individual LED bars
works well.

## Taking clones from the mother

### When to cut

Take clones when the mother has enough growth to donate without being
stripped bare. A healthy, well-maintained mother can provide 5–10
clones every 2–3 weeks without stress. Taking more than 30–40% of
the foliage at once stresses the plant and slows recovery.

### Where to cut

- **Take cuts from lower and middle branches** — these root faster
  than top growth because they have higher levels of natural rooting
  hormones (the top is flooded with auxins that promote upward growth,
  not rooting)
- **Each cutting should be 4–6 inches long** with 2–3 nodes
- **Cut at a 45° angle** just below a node — this maximizes the surface
  area for root development
- **Remove the lower leaves** — only the top 2–3 leaves should remain.
  The cutting can't support a full canopy without roots

### The rooting process

1. Cut the clone from the mother with a sterilized blade (razor blade
   or very sharp scissors)
2. Immediately dip the cut end in rooting hormone (Clonex gel is the
   standard — dip, don't drip)
3. Insert into your rooting medium:
   - **Rapid Rooters** in a humidity dome (easiest method)
   - **Rockwool cubes** pre-soaked at pH 5.5
   - **Aero cloner** (fastest rooting, 5–7 days)
   - **Cup of water** (works but slowest, 10–14 days)
4. Place under low light (100–200 PPFD) with high humidity (80–90% RH)
   in a humidity dome
5. Mist the leaves daily; don't water the medium until roots appear
6. Roots should show in 7–14 days depending on the method and strain

## Ongoing maintenance

### Pruning

The mother needs regular pruning to stay manageable and produce quality
clone sites. Without pruning, she becomes a tangled, woody tree that's
hard to work with and produces fewer viable cuttings.

**Pruning strategy:**
- **Top regularly** — every time the main stems reach 12–18 inches,
  top them back. This promotes branching below the cut and gives you
  more clone sites
- **Remove woody growth** — as the mother ages, the lower stems become
  thick and woody. Clones taken from woody growth root slower and are
  less vigorous. Prune old woody branches to encourage fresh, green
  growth
- **Thin the interior** — remove crossing branches and interior
  growth that doesn't get light. This improves airflow (preventing
  mold) and directs the plant's energy to the perimeter branches
  where you'll take cuttings
- **Maintain the shape** — the ideal mother is a low, wide bush with
  many branch tips at roughly the same height. Think bonsai, not
  Christmas tree

### Feeding

- **Consistent, moderate veg nutrients** — the same ratio every time.
  No bloom boosters, no transition feeds, no flowering stimulants
- **Monitor EC** — keep input EC consistent. The mother's nutrient
  demands don't change much since she's not going through growth
  stages
- **Flush monthly** — a light flush (2× pot volume with pH'd water)
  every 4–6 weeks prevents salt buildup, which is a slow-building
  problem in long-term containers

### Root pruning and transplanting

A mother in a 5-gallon pot will become root-bound within 6–12 months.
Signs: roots circling the bottom and sides, water running through
without absorbing, slowed growth despite adequate feeding.

**Option 1: Transplant to a larger pot**
- Simple but eventually you run out of space. Good for the first
  year; a 7-gallon pot gives another 6–12 months

**Option 2: Root pruning (sustainable long-term)**
1. Carefully remove the mother from her pot
2. Using a sharp knife or saw, slice off the outer 1–2 inches of the
   root ball on all sides and the bottom
3. Re-pot in the same container with fresh medium filling the gaps
4. Water thoroughly with a mild nutrient solution
5. The mother will look stressed for 3–5 days, then bounce back with
   fresh root growth into the new medium

Root pruning can be done every 6–12 months indefinitely. It's how
bonsai trees live for decades in small containers, and the same
principle applies to cannabis mothers.

### Pest and disease prevention

A mother plant is a long-term resident. If she gets spider mites or
powdery mildew, every clone she produces carries that problem into
your flower room.

- **Inspect weekly** — flip leaves, check stems, look for webbing
  or spots
- **Preventive treatment** — a biweekly spray of neem oil or
  insecticidal soap keeps pests suppressed. Since the mother never
  flowers, you can spray freely without worrying about bud
  contamination
- **Quarantine new clones** — if you bring in clones from outside,
  quarantine them for 2 weeks before placing near the mother

## When to retire a mother

Mother plants don't live forever — or rather, they can, but their
quality may decline over time.

### Signs of decline

- **Clones root slower** than they used to (14+ days instead of 7–10)
- **Clones show reduced vigor** — slower growth, weaker stems, less
  branching
- **The mother is mostly woody** with little new green growth despite
  pruning
- **Persistent pest or disease issues** that treatments can't resolve
- **Genetic drift** — rare, but after years of cloning, some growers
  report subtle changes in potency or terpene profile

### How to replace

Before retiring a mother, take a round of fresh clones from her
healthiest growth. Root them, veg one into a new mother, and let the
old mother go. This "mother from a clone of a mother" can be done
multiple times — the genetics don't degrade through clonal propagation
(despite urban myths to the contrary — cloning copies the DNA exactly).

### How long can a mother live?

With proper care (root pruning, feeding, pruning, pest prevention),
a mother plant can produce viable clones for **3–5 years** without
issues. Some growers maintain mothers for 10+ years. The plant doesn't
age the way animals do — its meristematic tissue (growth tips) stays
genetically young indefinitely.

## The practical takeaway

A mother plant is a commitment: a dedicated space, consistent light
schedule, regular pruning, and ongoing care — even when you're not
running a flower cycle. But the payoff is enormous: once you find a
phenotype you love, you never have to buy seeds for that strain again,
and every harvest is predictable.

For most home growers with 1–2 flowering plants per cycle, one mother
in a small corner of the grow space or on a shelf with a cheap LED is
all you need. The clones she provides save $10–15 per seed you don't
buy, and the consistency they deliver saves weeks of guessing.
