Organic amendments are the raw materials that build fertile
soil. Unlike bottled liquid nutrients (which feed the plant
directly), amendments feed the soil biology, which in turn
feeds the plant. The result is a living medium that buffers
pH, supplies nutrients on demand, and improves every cycle
instead of depleting.

This guide covers the amendments that actually matter for
cannabis, what each one does, and how to combine them into
blends that make bottled nutrients unnecessary.

## How organic amendments work

### The biology pipeline

When you add bone meal to soil, the phosphorus in it isn't
immediately available to the plant. Here's what happens:

1. **Soil bacteria and fungi** break down the bone meal
   particles (mineralization)
2. The breakdown releases phosphorus ions into the soil
   solution
3. **Mycorrhizal fungi** form networks that transport
   phosphorus to plant roots far more efficiently than roots
   alone
4. The plant absorbs what it needs, when it needs it

This pipeline takes time (days to weeks, not hours). You can't
add bone meal today and fix a phosphorus deficiency tomorrow.
Organic amendments are preventive — you build the soil before
planting, and the biology delivers nutrients throughout the
cycle.

### Slow vs. fast release

| Speed | Time to availability | Examples |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| Fast (days) | 3–10 days | Worm castings, fish hydrolysate, compost tea |
| Medium (weeks) | 2–6 weeks | Blood meal, bat guano, kelp meal |
| Slow (months) | 1–6 months | Bone meal, rock dust, greensand, gypsum |

**Blend slow and fast sources** to provide both immediate and
long-term nutrition. Fast sources bridge the gap while slow
sources activate.

## The essential amendments

### Nitrogen sources

**Worm castings (vermicompost)**
- NPK: ~1-0-0 (low but balanced, with micronutrients)
- Rate: 15–30% of total soil mix by volume
- Speed: fast (immediately available)
- The single most useful amendment. Provides gentle nutrition,
  beneficial microbes, humic acids, and improved soil
  structure. You cannot add too much worm castings
- See our [living soil guide](/guides/living-soil-water-only)

**Blood meal**
- NPK: ~12-0-0 (high nitrogen)
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- Strong nitrogen source for veg growth. Can burn if over-
  applied. Don't use in flower mixes (too much N)
- **Note:** attracts animals if used outdoors (it's dried
  blood). Indoor-only for cannabis

**Fish meal / fish bone meal**
- NPK: ~8-6-0 (fish meal) or ~4-12-0 (fish bone meal)
- Rate: 2–3 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- Good balanced amendment. Fish bone meal is an excellent
  phosphorus source that also provides calcium. Less smell
  than blood meal
- Can attract pests — use in sealed indoor containers

**Neem cake (neem seed meal)**
- NPK: ~6-1-2
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- Dual purpose: nitrogen source AND natural pest deterrent.
  Neem compounds in the soil repel fungus gnat larvae, root
  aphids, and some nematodes
- A foundational amendment for organic cannabis

### Phosphorus sources

**Bone meal (steamed)**
- NPK: ~3-15-0
- Rate: 2–3 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: slow (4–8 weeks to full availability)
- Primary organic phosphorus source for flowering. Add during
  soil building — it won't be available in time if added at
  flip
- Availability depends on soil pH: optimal at 6.0–7.0.
  Locked out below 5.5

**Bat guano (high-phosphorus variety)**
- NPK: ~0-10-0 (varies by source)
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- Fast-acting phosphorus for flower. Can be used as a top
  dress during flower for additional P
- **Source responsibly** — some bat guano harvesting damages
  cave ecosystems. Look for sustainably sourced products

**Rock phosphate**
- NPK: ~0-3-0 (slow release)
- Rate: 2–3 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: very slow (months)
- Long-term phosphorus bank. Best for no-till beds where the
  soil persists across multiple cycles. Not useful for single-
  cycle grows

### Potassium sources

**Kelp meal**
- NPK: ~1-0-2 (plus growth hormones and trace minerals)
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- More than just potassium — kelp meal provides cytokinins
  (natural growth hormones), over 60 trace minerals, and
  alginic acid (improves soil water retention). One of the
  most valuable amendments for overall plant health

**Langbeinite (Sul-Po-Mag / K-Mag)**
- NPK: ~0-0-22 (plus 11% Mg, 22% S)
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: medium (2–4 weeks)
- Triple-mineral source: potassium, magnesium, and sulfur.
  Excellent for flower mixes where K and Mg demand is high
- pH neutral — doesn't shift soil pH

**Wood ash**
- NPK: ~0-1-5 (plus calcium)
- Rate: 1 tablespoon per gallon of soil maximum
- Speed: fast (immediately available)
- **Use cautiously** — wood ash is alkaline (pH 10+) and can
  dramatically raise soil pH if over-applied. Only use if
  your soil tends acidic

### Calcium and magnesium

**Gypsum (calcium sulfate)**
- Provides calcium + sulfur without changing pH
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Unlike lime, gypsum is pH-neutral. Use when you need
  calcium but your pH is already correct

**Dolomite lime**
- Provides calcium + magnesium, raises pH
- Rate: 1 tablespoon per gallon of soil
- Use when soil is too acidic (below 6.0) AND you need Ca/Mg
- Don't use if pH is already 6.5+ — it will push pH too high

**Oyster shell flour**
- Provides slow-release calcium, gentle pH buffer
- Rate: 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Excellent long-term pH stabilizer for organic soils

### Micronutrient sources

**Glacial rock dust (Azomite, basalt)**
- Provides 60+ trace minerals in slow-release form
- Rate: 2–4 tablespoons per gallon of soil
- Speed: very slow (months)
- The "insurance policy" amendment. Even if you never identify
  a specific micronutrient deficiency, rock dust ensures trace
  minerals are available
- Improves soil structure and water retention

**Humic acid / leonardite**
- Not a nutrient — a chelation agent. Makes existing nutrients
  more available to roots
- Rate: per product label
- Improves nutrient uptake efficiency across the board

## Building amendment blends

### Vegetative blend (high nitrogen)

Per 5 gallons of base soil (peat/compost/perlite):

| Amendment | Amount | Provides |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Worm castings | 1 gallon (20% of mix) | N, microbes, humus |
| Blood meal | 3 tablespoons | N (fast) |
| Neem cake | 2 tablespoons | N + pest prevention |
| Kelp meal | 2 tablespoons | K, hormones, trace minerals |
| Fish bone meal | 2 tablespoons | P, Ca |
| Gypsum | 1 tablespoon | Ca, S |
| Glacial rock dust | 3 tablespoons | Trace minerals |
| Dolomite lime | 1 tablespoon | Ca, Mg, pH buffer |

### Flower blend (high phosphorus and potassium)

Per 5 gallons of base soil:

| Amendment | Amount | Provides |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Worm castings | 1 gallon | N (gentle), microbes |
| Bone meal | 3 tablespoons | P (primary) |
| Bat guano (high P) | 2 tablespoons | P (fast) |
| Kelp meal | 2 tablespoons | K, hormones |
| Langbeinite | 2 tablespoons | K, Mg, S |
| Neem cake | 1 tablespoon | Slow N + pest prevention |
| Gypsum | 1 tablespoon | Ca, S |
| Glacial rock dust | 3 tablespoons | Trace minerals |
| Oyster shell | 1 tablespoon | Ca, pH buffer |

### Cooking the soil

After mixing amendments into soil, the blend needs 2–4 weeks
to "cook" — the biology breaks down raw amendments into
plant-available forms:

1. Mix all amendments into moist (not wet) soil
2. Store in a covered container (tarp over a trash can, or
   a large sealed tub) at room temperature
3. Stir or turn the mix every 3–4 days for aeration
4. The mix may heat up during the first week (microbial
   activity) — this is normal
5. After 2–4 weeks, the soil smells earthy (not sour or
   ammonia-like) and is ready to plant in

See our [living soil guide](/guides/living-soil-water-only)
for the complete process.

## Top dressing during the grow

Top dressing is adding dry amendments to the soil surface
during an active grow:

- Sprinkle the amendment on the soil surface
- Scratch it into the top ½ inch of soil
- Water in to activate biological breakdown
- Effects begin in 1–3 weeks (depending on the amendment)

**Common top dress schedule:**

| Timing | Top dress | Purpose |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Week 3 of veg | Neem cake + worm castings | Refresh N + pest prevention |
| Transition to flower | Bone meal + bat guano + kelp meal | Load P and K for flower |
| Week 4 of flower | Langbeinite + kelp meal | K boost for bud development |

## Common mistakes

1. **Adding too much blood meal** — blood meal at 12-0-0 is
   potent. Excess causes nitrogen toxicity (dark green clawing
   leaves). Measure carefully and err on the side of less
2. **Not cooking the soil** — raw amendments can burn roots.
   The 2–4 week cook period allows biology to process the
   amendments before roots contact them. Skipping the cook
   is the #1 cause of organic grow failures
3. **Expecting immediate results from top dressing** — top
   dressed amendments take 1–3 weeks to become available.
   If the plant needs phosphorus NOW, a compost tea or
   fish hydrolysate is faster
4. **Using lime when pH is already correct** — dolomite lime
   raises pH. Adding it to soil that's already at 6.5 pushes
   pH above 7.0, locking out phosphorus and micronutrients.
   Use gypsum instead if you need calcium without pH change
5. **Over-amending** — more is not better. Excess minerals
   create imbalances and toxicities even in organic form.
   Follow the rates and let the biology regulate delivery
