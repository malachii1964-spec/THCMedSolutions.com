Seedlings are fragile. They have almost no root mass, tiny reserves of
stored energy, and zero tolerance for the mistakes that older plants
shrug off. Most first-grow failures happen in the first two weeks — and
almost all of them are preventable.

Here are the 8 problems you'll actually face and what to do about each.

## 1. Stretching (leggy seedlings)

**What it looks like:** a tall, thin stem with leaves far from the soil
surface. The seedling leans or falls over.

**The cause:** not enough light, or light too far away.

**The fix:**
- Move the light closer (for LED: 24–30 inches at 40–60% power for
  seedlings)
- Aim for 200–300 PPFD at canopy level
- If the seedling has already stretched, gently mound soil around the
  stem up to the cotyledon leaves — the buried stem will root
- Add a small fan for gentle airflow — stem movement triggers
  thigmotropism (the plant thickens its stem in response to mechanical
  stress)

**Prevention:** start seeds under the grow light from day one, not on a
windowsill.

## 2. Damping off

**What it looks like:** the stem at soil level turns brown, pinches thin,
and the seedling collapses. It happens fast — healthy at night, dead by
morning.

**The cause:** a fungal infection (Pythium, Fusarium, or Rhizoctonia)
triggered by overwatering and poor airflow in warm, humid conditions.

**The fix:** once damping off hits, the seedling is dead. There's no
saving it. Remove the dead plant and the surrounding soil to prevent
spread.

**Prevention:**
- Don't overwater. Seedlings need moist soil, not wet soil. If the
  surface stays soggy, you're watering too much
- Use a clean, well-draining medium (sterile seed-starting mix or
  coco/perlite)
- Ensure airflow around the base of the stem — a gentle fan helps
- Don't reuse soil from a damping-off event without sterilizing it
- Some growers sprinkle a thin layer of perlite or sand on the soil
  surface around seedlings — it dries fast and discourages fungus

## 3. Yellowing cotyledons

**What it looks like:** the round first leaves (cotyledons) turn yellow
and droop.

**Is it a problem?** Usually no. Cotyledons are seed food — they feed the
seedling until the first true leaves take over photosynthesis. They're
supposed to yellow and fall off eventually.

**When to worry:** if the first true leaves are also yellowing, the plant
needs food. In a hot soil (Fox Farm Ocean Forest, for example), this
shouldn't happen for 2–3 weeks. In coco or a light seed-starting mix,
start feeding at 1/4 strength once the first true leaves are fully open.

## 4. Curling or clawing leaves

**What it looks like:** leaf edges curl up (taco) or leaf tips claw down.

**Possible causes:**
- **Heat stress** (curling up) — check temperature at canopy level. If
  it's above 82°F, raise the light or increase exhaust
- **Overwatering** (clawing down) — the soil is staying soggy for days.
  Let it dry out slightly between waterings
- **Wind burn** (curling up, edges crispy) — the fan is too close or too
  strong. Seedlings need a gentle breeze, not a direct blast
- **Light stress** (curling up, bleaching) — the light is too close or
  too intense. Raise it 4–6 inches and reduce dimmer

## 5. Slow or stalled growth

**What it looks like:** the seedling is alive but hasn't visibly grown in
5+ days after the first true leaves appeared.

**Common causes and fixes:**

| Cause | How to check | Fix |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| Cold root zone | Touch the pot — if it's cold, the floor is too cold | Put a mat or foam under pots; root zone should be 68–72°F |
| Wrong pH | Test runoff — should be 6.0–6.5 in soil, 5.5–6.0 in coco | Adjust input water pH |
| Overwatering | Pot feels heavy, soil is dark and wet | Stop watering until the top inch dries |
| Root bound | Roots circling the drain holes of a tiny container | Transplant to a bigger pot |
| Bad genetics | Everything else is fine but the plant won't grow | Sometimes a seed is just a dud. Start another |

**The most common one:** cold root zone. If pots are sitting on a cold
concrete floor or tile, roots grow slowly regardless of what you do
above ground. A simple seedling heat mat ($15–20) solves this.

## 6. Purple or red stems

**What it looks like:** the stem turns purple or reddish, especially on
the side facing the light.

**Is it a problem?** Usually no. Many cannabis strains have natural
anthocyanin pigmentation that shows up in stems, especially when there's
a temperature differential between day and night. It's cosmetic.

**When to worry:** if purple stems come with yellowing leaves, slow
growth, and cold temperatures, the plant may have a phosphorus uptake
issue caused by cold roots (phosphorus becomes less available below
60°F). Warm the root zone.

## 7. Helmet head (seed shell stuck on cotyledon)

**What it looks like:** the seed shell doesn't fall off after the
seedling emerges. The cotyledons can't open and the plant can't
photosynthesize.

**The fix:**
1. Mist the seed shell with water to soften it (a few drops, don't soak)
2. Wait 12–24 hours
3. If it still hasn't fallen off, gently remove it with tweezers — hold
   the stem steady with one hand and ease the shell off with the other
4. Work slowly. If the cotyledons tear, the plant can usually recover
   from the first true leaves, but it'll be set back a week

**Prevention:** plant seeds 1/4 to 1/2 inch deep — too shallow and the
shell doesn't get scraped off by the soil as the seedling pushes through.

## 8. Fungus gnats

**What they look like:** tiny flying insects around the soil surface.
Larvae are white, worm-like, living in the top inch of soil.

**Why they're dangerous to seedlings:** adult gnats are annoying but
harmless. The larvae eat root hairs — and seedlings don't have many root
hairs to spare.

**The fix:**
- Let the top inch of soil dry between waterings (gnats need moist
  surface soil to breed)
- Yellow sticky traps catch adults and break the breeding cycle
- A 1/4-inch layer of sand or perlite on the soil surface blocks egg
  laying
- Mosquito dunk bits (BTI) in your water kills larvae without harming
  the plant

**Prevention:** don't overwater, and don't leave standing water in
saucers. Gnats are an overwatering indicator.

## The seedling environment cheat sheet

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Light (PPFD) | 200–300 |
| Light height (LED) | 24–30 inches at 40–60% power |
| Temperature | 72–78°F |
| Humidity | 65–70% RH |
| Watering | Moist, not wet — every 2–3 days |
| pH | 6.0–6.5 (soil), 5.5–6.0 (coco) |
| Nutrients | None for 1–2 weeks in hot soil; 1/4 strength in coco |
| Airflow | Gentle breeze, not direct wind |

Get these right and you'll avoid 90% of seedling problems. If something
does go wrong, check this guide first — the answer is almost always
light, water, or temperature.
