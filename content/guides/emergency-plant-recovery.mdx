Something went wrong. Your plant looks terrible — and it happened fast.
Maybe it was fine yesterday, maybe it's been slowly declining for a week,
but now it's clearly in trouble. Leaves are dying, growth has stopped,
and you're not sure what happened or what to do first.

This guide is the emergency protocol. It's designed to be followed in
order, under pressure, when you need to stop the damage before
diagnosing the cause.

## Step 1: Stop doing what you're doing

The first rule of plant emergency care: **stop all inputs.**

- Stop feeding nutrients
- Stop spraying anything
- Stop adjusting the light
- Stop moving the plant
- Stop watering (unless the plant is clearly bone-dry — see Step 2)

Most acute plant emergencies are caused by something the grower did
(or overdid) in the last 48 hours. Continuing to add inputs makes
diagnosis harder and often makes the problem worse. Stop, observe,
then act.

## Step 2: The 60-second triage

Check these five things, in this order:

### 1. Is the soil wet or dry?

Pick up the pot. Heavy = wet. Light = dry.

| Finding | Immediate action |
|---------|-----------------|
| Pot feels like a brick, very heavy | Overwatered. Do NOT water. Skip to Step 3 |
| Pot feels light as a feather, soil pulling from edges | Severely underwatered. Water slowly right now, pH'd, until 10% runoff |
| Pot feels normal weight | Moisture isn't the acute issue. Continue triage |

### 2. What temperature is the canopy?

Hold your hand at bud level for 30 seconds. If it's uncomfortable for
your hand, it's too hot for the plant.

| Finding | Immediate action |
|---------|-----------------|
| Above 90°F | Heat emergency. Raise the light, increase exhaust, open the tent. Cool the room. This is your priority |
| Below 55°F | Cold stress. Add a heater or heat mat. Root-zone cold is especially damaging |
| 65–85°F | Temperature isn't the acute issue. Continue triage |

### 3. What does the plant smell like?

Get your nose close to the soil surface and the stem base.

| Finding | Immediate action |
|---------|-----------------|
| Sour, swampy, rotting smell | Root rot. See the root rot protocol below |
| Mold/mildew smell | Check for white powder (PM) or gray fuzz (botrytis) on buds and leaves |
| Normal cannabis smell | Root zone is probably OK. Continue triage |

### 4. When did you last feed, and at what strength?

If you fed in the last 48 hours at higher than recommended strength (or
didn't measure), nutrient burn or toxicity is the most likely cause.

### 5. When did you last change anything?

New light? New nutrients? Transplanted? Defoliated? Moved the plant?
Changed light schedule? Changed watering frequency? The most recent
change is the most likely culprit.

## Step 3: The stabilization protocol

Once you've triaged, stabilize the environment. The goal is to stop the
damage from progressing while you diagnose the root cause.

### The stable environment

| Parameter | Stabilization target |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Temperature | 72–78°F |
| Humidity | 50–60% |
| Light | Dim to 50% intensity or raise 6–8 inches |
| Airflow | Gentle circulation, no direct wind on the plant |
| Water | None until you've diagnosed (unless triage identified dehydration) |
| Nutrients | Zero until you've diagnosed |

**Why dim the light?** A stressed plant can't photosynthesize at full
capacity. Intense light on a sick plant creates additional stress that
compounds the original problem. Think of it as letting a sick person
rest instead of making them run laps.

## Step 4: Diagnose

Now that the plant is stabilized, determine what happened. Work through
these in order — they're ranked by frequency (most common first).

### A. pH lockout (most common)

**Symptoms:** multiple nutrient deficiency symptoms appearing
simultaneously. Yellowing, spots, curling, and tip burn all at once.
The plant looks confused — no single deficiency pattern fits.

**Confirm it:** water with pH'd water to 10–15% runoff. Measure the
runoff pH. If it's below 5.5 or above 7.0, lockout is likely.

**Fix:** flush with 2–3× pot volume of pH'd water (6.3 for soil, 5.8
for coco). Resume feeding at 50% strength with correct pH. See our
[pH lockout guide](/guides/ph-lockout-and-nutrient-problems) for the
complete protocol.

### B. Overwatering / root suffocation

**Symptoms:** drooping leaves that feel thick and heavy (not papery).
Plant looks limp and sad. Soil is wet and stays wet. Growth has stalled.

**Confirm it:** the pot is heavy, soil is wet, and the plant has been
watered in the last 24–48 hours despite the soil not drying back.

**Fix:** stop watering completely. Increase airflow around the base.
If the pot stays wet for more than 5 days without drying, something is
wrong with drainage — check that drain holes aren't clogged, that the
pot isn't sitting in a saucer of standing water, and that the soil
isn't compacted (dense, clay-like). If using fabric pots, airflow fixes
this naturally. If in plastic, consider transplanting into a better-
draining medium once the plant stabilizes.

### C. Nutrient burn / toxicity

**Symptoms:** burnt (brown, crispy) leaf tips, dark green waxy leaves,
leaf tips curling downward ("clawing"). Usually nitrogen toxicity.

**Confirm it:** you recently increased nutrient strength, or have been
feeding without measuring EC, or the runoff EC is significantly higher
than input EC.

**Fix:** flush with half-strength nutrient solution (not plain water —
that can shock the plant). Resume feeding at 50% of what you were using.
Tip burn is permanent on affected leaves; watch new growth for
improvement.

### D. Heat / light stress

**Symptoms:** leaves curling up ("taco"), bleaching (whitening) of bud
tops closest to the light, rapid drying of the top canopy while lower
plant is fine.

**Confirm it:** canopy temperature above 85°F, or light is closer than
manufacturer specs.

**Fix:** raise the light 6–8 inches, dim to 75%, increase exhaust or
add AC. The plant will show improvement in new growth within 3–5 days.
Bleached tissue won't recover.

### E. Root rot

**Symptoms:** sour smell from the root zone, brown/mushy roots (visible
in fabric pots or when you lift the plant from the pot), plant wilting
despite wet soil, rapid leaf yellowing.

**Confirm it:** pull the plant from the pot (gently) and examine the
roots. Healthy roots are white or light tan. Rotting roots are brown,
slimy, and smell bad.

**Fix:**
1. Trim all dead (brown, mushy) roots with sterilized scissors
2. Soak remaining roots in a hydrogen peroxide solution (3% H₂O₂,
   1 tbsp per gallon of water) for 5 minutes
3. Transplant into fresh, well-draining medium with plenty of perlite
4. Water sparingly — just enough to settle the new medium
5. Add beneficial mycorrhizae (Great White, Recharge, or similar) to
   help repopulate the root zone with beneficial fungi
6. Don't feed for 5–7 days. The damaged root system can't absorb
   nutrients — feeding now just adds salt stress

### F. Pest damage

**Symptoms:** tiny dots (stippling) on leaves, webbing between
branches, small flying insects, visible insects on leaf undersides,
chewed leaf edges.

**Confirm it:** flip 5–10 leaves and examine the undersides with a
loupe. Spider mites are tiny dots; thrips are small elongated insects;
fungus gnats are small flies near the soil surface.

**Fix:** see our [pest control guide](/guides/pests-mites-gnats-mildew)
for species-specific treatment. For immediate triage: spray undersides
of leaves with neem oil (veg only — never in flower) or Safer Brand
insecticidal soap (both stages).

### G. Chemical damage

**Symptoms:** sudden widespread leaf burn, spots, or curling within
hours of applying a foliar spray, pesticide, or homemade remedy.

**Confirm it:** you sprayed something in the last 24 hours, and the
damage pattern matches where the spray landed.

**Fix:** rinse leaves gently with plain water (a shower head works).
Remove the most damaged leaves. Stop spraying. The plant will recover
through new growth if the damage isn't too severe. Chemical damage to
existing leaves is permanent.

## Step 5: The recovery timeline

After diagnosing and treating, here's what to expect:

| Time after fix | What you'll see |
|---------------|----------------|
| Day 1–2 | No visible improvement (the plant is recovering internally) |
| Day 3–5 | Damaged leaves stop getting worse. New growth may appear slightly healthier |
| Day 5–10 | New growth is visibly healthy — correct color, shape, and posture |
| Day 10–14 | Plant resumes normal growth rate. Damaged leaves remain damaged (don't remove them yet — the plant still uses them for photosynthesis) |
| Day 14–21 | Full recovery. Damaged leaves can now be removed if they're more than 50% dead |

**Critical:** do NOT remove damaged leaves during the first 2 weeks of
recovery. Even a 70% damaged leaf is still photosynthesizing with its
remaining tissue. The plant needs every bit of energy production it can
get right now.

## Step 6: Prevent the next emergency

After recovery, implement these to prevent recurrence:

1. **pH every watering** — no exceptions. The 5-minute pH check prevents
   80% of cannabis growing emergencies
2. **Measure nutrients by EC** — never eyeball. A $20 EC pen pays for
   itself the first time it prevents a nutrient burn
3. **Track your inputs** — keep a simple log: date, water volume, pH in,
   EC in, pH out, EC out. When something goes wrong, the log tells you
   exactly what changed
4. **Daily 60-second inspection** — see our
   [plant reading guide](/guides/reading-your-plant) for the daily check
   routine. Catching a problem on day 1 vs. day 5 is the difference
   between a minor setback and a dead plant
5. **Change one thing at a time** — the most common pattern in plant
   emergencies is: "I changed my nutrient strength AND my light height
   AND my watering schedule, and now the plant is dying." When you change
   three things, you can't tell which one caused the problem

## When to give up

Sometimes a plant can't be saved. Signs it's past recovery:

- **Main stem is soft and mushy** — structural death. The vascular
  system is compromised
- **All roots are brown and slimy** — there's nothing left to recover.
  A plant needs live roots to rebuild
- **Complete defoliation** — if every leaf is dead or dying and there's
  no new growth at the tips, the plant has no way to photosynthesize
  and rebuild
- **Seedling collapsed at soil line** — damping off. The stem is dead
  at the base. It's over for this seed; start another

If the plant is genuinely dead, analyze what happened, adjust your
process, and start the next one. Every dead plant teaches you something
that makes the next grow better. The growers with the best gardens have
killed the most plants on their way there.
