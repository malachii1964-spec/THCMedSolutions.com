Compost tea is liquid biology. You take high-quality compost,
brew it in aerated water with food sources to multiply the
beneficial microorganisms, and apply the resulting liquid to
your soil or leaves. The goal is to inoculate your growing
medium with diverse populations of bacteria, fungi, protozoa,
and other microbes that form the soil food web.

It's one of the most talked-about techniques in organic
cannabis growing. It's also one of the most over-marketed.
This guide covers how to do it right AND what the science
actually supports.

## What compost tea is (and isn't)

### What it IS

- A liquid extract of compost, produced by steeping compost
  in aerated water for 24–48 hours
- A delivery vehicle for beneficial microorganisms (bacteria,
  fungi, protozoa)
- A way to rapidly increase microbial populations in soil
- A complement to a good soil-building program

### What it ISN'T

- A replacement for properly amended soil
- A significant source of plant nutrients (NPK content is
  negligible)
- A guaranteed solution for soil problems
- A standalone fertilizer

**The honest assessment:** compost tea adds biology. If your
soil already has good biology (well-amended, not sterilized,
not overwatered), the marginal benefit of compost tea is
modest. If your soil biology is depleted (reused,
chloraminated water, synthetic-only feeding), compost tea
provides real, measurable improvement.

## Types of compost tea

### Actively Aerated Compost Tea (AACT)

This is what cannabis growers mean by "compost tea":
- Compost is steeped in water with continuous aeration (air
  pump + air stone)
- Food sources (molasses, kelp, fish hydrolysate) are added
  to fuel microbial reproduction
- Brewing time: 24–36 hours
- The result is aerobic — dominated by beneficial aerobic
  bacteria and fungi

**This is the only type recommended for cannabis.** Aerobic
conditions prevent pathogenic anaerobic bacteria from
multiplying.

### Non-aerated compost extracts

Soaking compost in still water without aeration. This goes
anaerobic quickly and can harbor plant pathogens (Pythium,
E. coli). NOT recommended.

### Fermented plant extracts (not technically compost tea)

Korean Natural Farming (KNF) ferments like FPJ (fermented
plant juice) and FFJ (fermented fruit juice) are sometimes
called "teas" but are a different process entirely — they're
lacto-fermented plant material. Effective but distinct from
compost tea.

## How to brew AACT

### Equipment

| Item | Purpose | Cost |
|------|---------|------|
| 5-gallon bucket | Brewing vessel | $5 |
| Aquarium air pump (>1000cc/min) | Aeration | $15–30 |
| Air stone or air diffuser | Distributes air through the liquid | $5 |
| Mesh bag or old stocking | Holds compost (keeps chunks out of solution) | $2 |
| Thermometer | Monitors brew temperature | $10 |

**Total cost: ~$40** for the complete setup. Reusable
indefinitely (clean between brews).

### Ingredients

**Base compost (choose one or combine):**
- High-quality earthworm castings (most common, most
  reliable) — ½ to 1 cup per 5 gallons
- Well-aged garden compost — 1 cup per 5 gallons
- Forest humus (leaf mold from a deciduous forest floor) —
  ½ cup per 5 gallons (excellent fungal inoculant)

**Microbial food sources (choose 2–3):**
- Unsulfured blackstrap molasses — 1 tablespoon per 5 gallons
  (feeds bacteria)
- Liquid kelp extract — 1 tablespoon per 5 gallons (feeds
  fungi, provides trace minerals)
- Fish hydrolysate — 1 teaspoon per 5 gallons (feeds both
  bacteria and fungi)
- Humic acid — 1 teaspoon per 5 gallons (fungal food,
  chelation agent)

**Do NOT use:** sulfured molasses (sulfur kills microbes),
honey (antimicrobial), granulated sugar (feeds bacteria at
the expense of fungi), anything with preservatives.

### The brewing process

1. **Fill the bucket with water.** If using chlorinated/
   chloraminated tap water, treat first — chloramine kills
   the microbes you're trying to grow. Use RO water, or treat
   with a Campden tablet 30 minutes before brewing
2. **Add the compost** in a mesh bag. Suspend it in the water
   so it's fully submerged
3. **Add the food sources** (molasses, kelp, fish) directly to
   the water. Stir to dissolve
4. **Turn on the air pump.** The air stone should produce a
   continuous rolling boil of bubbles. Vigorous aeration is
   essential — it keeps the brew aerobic
5. **Brew for 24–36 hours.** Stir occasionally. The liquid
   should smell earthy and slightly sweet — like forest soil
6. **Check the smell.** If the brew smells bad (rotten, sour,
   sulfurous), it has gone anaerobic. Dump it and start over.
   Good tea smells earthy and alive, not foul
7. **Use immediately.** Compost tea has a short shelf life —
   the microbes need oxygen and food. Apply within 4–6 hours
   of turning off the pump. After 8 hours, microbial
   populations decline rapidly

### Temperature

- **Ideal brew temperature:** 65–80°F
- **Below 60°F:** microbial reproduction slows significantly.
  Brew longer (36–48 hours) or warm the water
- **Above 85°F:** some beneficial organisms die. Aerobic
  bacteria produce less diverse populations at high
  temperatures

**WNY winter note:** brewing in a 55°F basement in January
produces a weak tea. Move the bucket to a warmer room or use
an aquarium heater set to 72°F in the brew bucket.

## Application

### Soil drench (primary method)

- Dilute the finished tea 1:1 with dechlorinated water (or
  apply undiluted for maximum biology)
- Water the soil as you normally would, using the tea as your
  watering solution
- Apply during lights-off or low light — UV from grow lights
  kills microbes on the soil surface
- Water normally afterward — the microbes establish in the
  root zone

### Foliar spray (secondary method)

- Strain the tea through a fine mesh to remove particles
  (they clog spray nozzles)
- Spray all leaf surfaces (top and bottom) during lights-off
- Foliar application inoculates the leaf surface with
  beneficial bacteria that can outcompete pathogens (powdery
  mildew, Botrytis)
- **Stop foliar tea applications by week 2 of flower** — same
  rule as all foliar sprays

### Application frequency

- **Soil drench:** every 2–4 weeks during veg and early flower
- **Foliar:** every 2 weeks during veg only
- **After transplanting:** one application to inoculate the
  new root zone
- **Don't over-apply** — more frequent isn't better. The
  microbes need time to establish and find equilibrium

## What the science says

### Supported by evidence

- Compost tea introduces diverse microbial populations to the
  root zone
- The bacteria and fungi in tea CAN establish in soil and
  contribute to nutrient cycling
- Foliar applications of compost tea show disease suppression
  in some studies (particularly against powdery mildew)
- Tea-treated soils show increased microbial biomass

### Partially supported

- Claims of significant nutrient delivery — tea provides
  minimal NPK. The biology it introduces helps make EXISTING
  soil nutrients more available, but tea itself is not a
  meaningful fertilizer
- Claims of pest suppression — some evidence in controlled
  studies, but results are inconsistent

### Not supported

- "Compost tea replaces fertilizer" — it doesn't. Tea
  supplements biology; the soil still needs amendment-based
  nutrition
- "More tea = better results" — frequency studies show
  diminishing returns beyond monthly application
- "You can fix any soil problem with tea" — dead, depleted,
  or contaminated soil needs physical amendment, not just a
  microbial spray

## Common mistakes

1. **Brewing without aeration** — still water goes anaerobic
   within hours. Anaerobic tea can harbor plant pathogens.
   The air pump is non-negotiable
2. **Using chloraminated water** — chloramine kills the
   microbes you're trying to multiply. Treat your water
   before brewing. See our
   [water quality guide](/guides/cannabis-water-quality)
3. **Storing tea for later** — compost tea has a 4–6 hour
   window after brewing. You can't bottle it and use it
   next week. Brew fresh each time
4. **Over-feeding the brew** — too much molasses causes a
   bacterial explosion that consumes all the oxygen, killing
   the aerobic organisms and crashing the brew. Use the
   recommended amounts
5. **Expecting tea to replace soil building** — compost tea
   adds biology. It doesn't add structure, minerals, or
   organic matter in meaningful amounts. Build your soil
   properly first, then use tea to enhance the biology
