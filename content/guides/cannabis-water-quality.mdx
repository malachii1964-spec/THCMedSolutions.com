Most new growers obsess over nutrients and ignore the water
carrying them. That's backwards. Nutrients are 0.1–0.3% of the
solution — the other 99.7% is water. If your water is loaded
with calcium, your cal-mag supplement is double-dosing one
element. If it's chloraminated, it's slowly killing your soil
biology. If it's 400 PPM from the tap, your "half-strength feed"
is actually full-strength plus whatever the city put in there.

The water you start with determines what you need to add, what
you need to remove, and how your plants will respond to feeding.

## What's in your tap water

### Reading your municipal water report

Every US water utility publishes an annual Consumer Confidence
Report (CCR). Find yours at the EPA's website or Google
"[your city] water quality report." The numbers that matter for
cannabis:

| Parameter | What it means | Target for cannabis |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Total Dissolved Solids (TDS/PPM) | Everything dissolved in the water | < 200 PPM ideal, < 400 PPM workable |
| pH | Acidity/alkalinity | 6.0–8.0 typical tap (you'll adjust) |
| Calcium (Ca) | Hard water mineral | < 80 PPM (affects cal-mag dosing) |
| Magnesium (Mg) | Hard water mineral | < 30 PPM |
| Sodium (Na) | Salt — accumulates in soil | < 50 PPM (high sodium = problems) |
| Chlorine (Cl₂) | Disinfectant | Any amount — remove before use |
| Chloramine (NH₂Cl) | Persistent disinfectant | Any amount — harder to remove than chlorine |
| Alkalinity (CaCO₃) | Buffering capacity | < 150 PPM (high = pH hard to lower) |
| Iron (Fe) | Can stain and accumulate | < 0.3 PPM |

### WNY water profiles

Western New York water varies significantly by source:

**City of Buffalo (Lake Erie source):**
- TDS: 150–250 PPM
- Moderately hard (calcium 40–80 PPM)
- Chloraminated (not just chlorine)
- pH: 7.2–7.8
- Workable for cannabis with chloramine treatment

**Suburban well water (varies widely):**
- TDS: 200–600+ PPM
- Often very hard (calcium 100–200+ PPM)
- No chlorine/chloramine (untreated)
- pH: 7.0–8.5
- May need RO or softening for cannabis

**Rural spring/well:**
- Test it. Well water varies from pristine to unusable within
  miles. A $30 water test kit or a lab analysis is essential
  before building a grow around untested well water

## Chlorine vs. chloramine

This distinction matters more than most growers realize:

### Chlorine (Cl₂)

**Easy to remove:**
- Let water sit in an open container for 24 hours — chlorine
  off-gasses naturally
- Aerate with an air stone for 2–4 hours
- A basic carbon filter removes it on contact

Most US cities have switched from chlorine to chloramine.
Check your water report — if it says "chloramine" or
"monochloramine," the sit-and-wait method doesn't work.

### Chloramine (NH₂Cl)

**Harder to remove:**
- Does NOT off-gas. Leaving water out overnight does nothing
- Does NOT break down with aeration
- Requires a catalytic carbon filter (not standard activated
  carbon) or chemical treatment
- Campden tablets (potassium metabisulfite): 1/4 tablet per
  5 gallons, dissolve and stir. Neutralizes chloramine in
  minutes. $5 for 100 tablets at any homebrew store

**Why it matters for growing:**
- Chloramine kills beneficial microbes in soil — devastating
  for living soil and organic growing
- Even in synthetic grows, chloramine at high concentrations
  can stress root cells
- If you're running a soil food web or compost tea program,
  chloramine removal is non-negotiable

### Buffalo-specific note

Buffalo Water Authority uses chloramine year-round. If you're
growing in the city, treat your water. Campden tablets are the
cheapest, simplest solution for small grows (1–4 plants).

## Water source options

### Tap water (treated)

**When to use it:** TDS below 200 PPM, no problematic minerals,
after chlorine/chloramine removal.

**Advantages:**
- Free and convenient
- Provides baseline calcium and magnesium (reduces cal-mag
  needed)
- Consistent supply

**Process:**
1. Fill container and treat for chloramine (Campden tablet or
   catalytic carbon filter)
2. Check pH (adjust to 6.0–6.5 for soil, 5.5–6.0 for hydro)
3. Add nutrients to treated water
4. Check final PPM/EC and pH

### Reverse osmosis (RO) water

**When to use it:** TDS above 300 PPM, high sodium, very hard
water, or when you want complete control over the mineral
profile.

**Advantages:**
- Starts at 0–20 PPM — you control everything
- No chlorine, chloramine, sodium, or heavy metals
- Perfect for hydro (coco, DWC, NFT)
- Eliminates the "what's in my tap" variable

**Disadvantages:**
- RO systems cost $100–300
- Wastes 2–4 gallons of water per gallon of RO produced
- Removes ALL minerals — you must add cal-mag to every
  feeding
- Slightly acidic (pH 6.0–6.5) — less buffering capacity

**RO system sizing:**
- A 4-stage, 100 GPD (gallons per day) RO system is sufficient
  for 1–4 plants
- Store RO water in food-grade containers — it absorbs CO₂
  from air and drops in pH over time (use within a few days
  of production)

### Distilled water

Similar to RO — near-zero TDS. Buying it by the gallon at the
store costs $1–2/gallon, which adds up fast. For more than a
plant or two, an RO system is more cost-effective.

### Well water

**Test before using.** Well water can be excellent (low TDS,
naturally filtered through rock) or terrible (high iron,
sulfur, heavy metals, extreme hardness). A lab water test costs
$30–50 and tells you exactly what you're working with.

**Common well water problems for cannabis:**
- Iron above 0.3 PPM: stains roots and medium, can accumulate
  to toxic levels
- Hardness above 250 PPM: excessive calcium overwhelms the
  nutrient profile
- pH above 8.0: constant battle to lower pH, especially with
  high alkalinity
- Sulfur (rotten egg smell): not directly harmful but indicates
  anaerobic bacterial activity in the well

## Hard water vs. soft water

### What is water hardness?

Hardness measures dissolved calcium and magnesium:
- **Soft:** 0–60 PPM CaCO₃
- **Moderately hard:** 61–120 PPM
- **Hard:** 121–180 PPM
- **Very hard:** 180+ PPM

### Hard water and cannabis

**Moderate hardness (60–120 PPM) is actually ideal.** It
provides calcium and magnesium that you'd otherwise need to add.
Many "hard water" nutrient lines (like General Hydroponics Flora
series "hard water Micro") are formulated to account for this
baseline.

**Very hard water (180+ PPM) causes problems:**
- The calcium and magnesium count toward your total PPM budget.
  If your tap starts at 300 PPM, adding nutrients to 800 PPM
  target means you only have 500 PPM of nutrient room
- Excess calcium can lock out other elements (potassium,
  magnesium, iron)
- Scale buildup in drip systems, reservoirs, and pumps
- pH tends to be high and resistant to adjustment (high
  alkalinity)

**Water softeners are NOT the solution.** Salt-based water
softeners replace calcium with sodium. Sodium is far worse for
cannabis — it accumulates in soil and causes osmotic stress.
Never use softened water for growing. If your water is very
hard, use an RO system instead.

## PPM/EC and what the numbers mean

### The basics

- **PPM (Parts Per Million):** how many dissolved particles per
  million water molecules. Higher = more stuff dissolved
- **EC (Electrical Conductivity):** measures the water's ability
  to conduct electricity (dissolved minerals conduct current).
  Higher EC = more dissolved minerals
- **Conversion:** 1.0 EC ≈ 500 PPM (500 scale) or 700 PPM
  (700 scale). Know which scale your meter uses

### Starting PPM matters

Your nutrient calculator assumes you're starting from zero.
If your tap water is 200 PPM, you're starting 200 PPM ahead:

| Tap PPM | Target feed PPM | PPM to add | Effective dilution |
|---------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| 0 (RO) | 800 | 800 | Full strength |
| 100 | 800 | 700 | 87.5% strength |
| 200 | 800 | 600 | 75% strength |
| 300 | 800 | 500 | 62.5% strength |
| 400 | 800 | 400 | 50% strength |

**The problem:** those 200–400 PPM from tap water aren't the
minerals your plant needs in the ratios it needs them. They're
whatever the city put in there — often heavy on calcium and
bicarbonates. High starting PPM forces you to under-dose
nutrients to stay within total PPM range.

## Building a water protocol

### For soil growers

1. **Test your tap water** — buy a TDS meter ($15) and pH meter
   ($30) and test your tap. Record the numbers
2. **If TDS < 200 PPM and no sodium issues:**
   - Treat for chloramine (Campden tablets or filter)
   - Use tap as-is
   - Reduce cal-mag by the calcium already in your tap
   - pH to 6.2–6.5 after adding nutrients
3. **If TDS 200–400 PPM:**
   - Consider a carbon block filter to remove chloramine and
     some dissolved solids
   - Use a "hard water" nutrient formula
   - Watch for calcium buildup (white crust on soil surface)
   - Flush more frequently to prevent salt accumulation
4. **If TDS > 400 PPM or high sodium:**
   - Use RO water
   - Add cal-mag to every feeding (RO removes everything)
   - Mix RO with tap at a ratio that gives you 100–150 PPM
     starting base (best of both worlds)

### For hydro/coco growers

RO water is strongly recommended for hydroponic systems:
- Complete control over the nutrient profile
- No mystery minerals competing with your feed
- No scale buildup in pumps, drippers, and reservoirs
- Consistent results batch to batch

If you must use tap water in hydro, keep TDS below 150 PPM
and treat for chloramine. Higher starting PPM makes reservoir
management unpredictable.

[coco guide](/guides/coco-coir-complete-guide).

## Water temperature

Often overlooked, water temperature affects nutrient uptake
and root health:

| Temperature | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Below 55°F | Root shock, slowed growth, poor nutrient uptake |
| 60–68°F | Ideal for most systems. Dissolved oxygen is highest |
| 68–72°F | Acceptable. Monitor dissolved oxygen in hydro |
| Above 75°F | Danger zone for hydro. Pythium and root rot risk increases sharply |
| Above 80°F | Root rot is almost guaranteed in recirculating hydro |

**Soil growers:** room-temperature water (65–72°F) is fine.
Don't water with ice-cold tap water in winter — let it warm to
room temperature first.

**Hydro growers:** water temperature is critical. A reservoir
chiller ($80–150) is not optional in warm climates or rooms
above 75°F. See our
[root rot guide](/guides/root-rot-pythium) for temperature
management in hydro.

## Common mistakes

1. **Not testing tap water before starting** — a $15 TDS meter
   and 5 minutes tell you whether your water is a problem.
   Guessing leads to months of confusing nutrient issues
2. **Assuming "letting water sit out" removes chloramine** — it
   doesn't. Only chlorine off-gasses. If your city uses
   chloramine (most do now), you need Campden tablets or a
   catalytic carbon filter
3. **Using softened water** — water softeners add sodium.
   Sodium accumulates in soil and damages roots. Use RO
   instead of a softener for growing
4. **Ignoring starting PPM when feeding** — if your tap is 300
   PPM and you add nutrients to 800 PPM total, you're only
   adding 500 PPM of nutrients. The plant is getting less food
   than you think, plus minerals it may not need
5. **Using cold water in winter** — tap water in WNY winters
   can be 40–50°F. This shocks roots and slows growth. Let it
   warm to 65°F+ before watering
6. **Not treating water for living soil** — chloramine kills the
   beneficial fungi and bacteria that make living soil work.
   If you're running organic no-till, water treatment is
   mandatory, not optional
