Outdoor cannabis growing is the oldest and simplest method —
a plant, sunlight, water, soil. But simplicity doesn't mean
easy. Outdoor grows face challenges that indoor growers never
encounter: weather, pests, theft, animals, light pollution,
and the fundamental constraint that you can't control the
environment.

This guide covers three outdoor approaches — guerrilla
(remote, hidden plots), backyard (private property), and
balcony (urban apartment) — because the strategies for each
are meaningfully different.

## The outdoor advantage

Before diving into methods, here's why outdoor growing is
worth considering:

- **Free light.** The sun delivers 1500+ PPFD at peak — more
  intensity than any grow light, with a perfect spectrum
  including UV-B that promotes trichome production
- **Free ventilation.** Wind provides constant air exchange
  and stem strengthening
- **Massive plants possible.** No height limits, no tent walls.
  An outdoor photoperiod plant can reach 6–10 feet and yield
  1–5 pounds
- **Lower cost.** No electricity bill, no equipment (or
  minimal). Soil + seeds + water = a harvest
- **Terpene expression.** Many growers report superior terpene
  profiles from outdoor-grown cannabis, potentially due to
  the full UV spectrum from natural sunlight

## Method 1: Backyard growing

### The basics

Growing in your own yard, raised bed, or garden — the most
straightforward outdoor method in legal states.

### Site selection

- **Full sun:** minimum 6 hours of direct sunlight, ideally
  8–10+ hours. South-facing exposure is best in the Northern
  Hemisphere
- **Wind protection:** gentle breeze is good. High winds
  damage branches and stress plants. A fence, wall, or hedge
  on the windward side helps
- **Privacy:** even in legal states, visible cannabis plants
  attract unwanted attention. Position away from fence lines,
  public paths, and neighbor sight lines
- **Drainage:** cannabis roots don't tolerate standing water.
  Raised beds or sloped ground prevent waterlogging

### Containers vs. ground

**In-ground:**
- Unlimited root space = bigger plants
- Better drought resilience (deep roots access moisture)
- Requires amending native soil (most yard soil needs
  compost, perlite, and amendments)
- Harder to move if needed

**Containers (raised beds, fabric pots):**
- Control over the growing medium
- Portable — can move to chase sunlight or bring indoors
  during early/late season
- Limits final plant size (20-gallon minimum for full-sized
  outdoor plants; 30–50 gallon is ideal)
- Requires more frequent watering

### WNY outdoor timeline

| Month | Activity |
|-------|---------|
| April | Start seeds indoors under lights |
| May (after last frost, ~May 15–20) | Harden off and transplant outdoors |
| June–July | Vegetative growth, training, pest prevention |
| August | Photoperiod triggers flower (days shorten below ~14 hours around Aug 1) |
| September–October | Flowering, bud development |
| October (before first frost, ~Oct 15–20) | Harvest |

**The WNY challenge:** the growing season is short. First
frost around October 15–20 means you need strains that finish
flowering in 8 weeks or less to harvest before frost. Late-
finishing sativas (10+ week flower) may not mature in WNY.

## Method 2: Guerrilla growing

### What it is

Growing in remote, hidden locations on public or unused private
land. Guerrilla growing exists because not everyone has a
backyard, and indoor growing isn't possible for everyone.

**Important:** in states where growing is legal (like New York),
guerrilla growing on land you don't own is still potentially
trespassing. Know your local laws.

### Site selection

The ideal guerrilla site has:

- **Access to water:** within reasonable carrying distance of
  a creek, pond, or water source. Carrying water long distances
  is the hardest part of guerrilla growing
- **Southern exposure:** sunlight is non-negotiable. Dense tree
  canopy blocks too much light
- **Not visible from trails, roads, or aerial views:** choose
  clearings within woods, edges of overgrown fields, or areas
  with natural visual barriers
- **Good soil or space for containers:** native forest soil is
  often acidic and low-nutrient. Many guerrilla growers dig
  holes and fill with prepared soil mix
- **Drainage:** hillsides and slopes drain naturally. Valley
  bottoms and flat areas can flood

### Security considerations

- **Visit infrequently.** Every visit creates trails that
  others may follow. Once established, visit only for watering
  and maintenance — not to "check on things"
- **Multiple sites.** Don't put all plants in one location.
  Split across 2–3 sites so that discovery of one doesn't
  mean total loss
- **Vary your route.** Don't walk the same path every time.
  Repeated foot traffic creates visible trails
- **No scent trail.** Don't handle plants and then walk through
  public areas. Change clothes if needed

### Watering logistics

The biggest operational challenge in guerrilla growing:

- **Self-watering systems:** bury a 5-gallon bucket with small
  holes next to the plant. Fill on visits and it slow-releases
  water
- **Water gel crystals:** mixed into the soil, these absorb
  and slowly release water. Extends time between watering
  visits
- **Rainfed only:** in climates with regular summer rain (like
  WNY), established plants in good soil may survive on
  rainfall alone — but yields suffer during dry spells
- **Creek/pond proximity:** if the site is near water, a
  portable camp pump and hose simplifies watering

## Method 3: Balcony growing

### The basics

Growing 1–3 plants on an apartment or condo balcony. The most
space-constrained outdoor method.

### What you need

- **Direct sunlight:** the balcony needs 5+ hours of direct
  sun. North-facing balconies usually don't work
- **Containers:** 5–10 gallon fabric pots. Limited by balcony
  weight capacity and floor space
- **Autoflowers recommended:** they stay small, don't require
  light schedule control, and finish fast (seed to harvest in
  70–90 days)
- **Wind protection:** high-floor balconies get strong winds.
  Position pots against the building wall and secure tall
  plants

### Balcony-specific challenges

- **Light pollution:** street lights, building lights, and
  neighboring apartments can disrupt photoperiod flowering.
  Autoflowers avoid this problem entirely
- **Smell:** a flowering cannabis plant on a balcony is
  detectable by neighbors above, below, and adjacent. Choose
  low-odor strains or accept the social implications
- **Visibility:** balcony plants are visible from outside. In
  legal states this is a personal choice. In others, it's a
  risk
- **Watering:** container plants on hot balconies may need
  watering twice daily in summer. Consider self-watering pots
  or drip irrigation on a timer

## Outdoor pest management

Outdoor cannabis faces pest pressures that indoor growers never
encounter:

### Common outdoor pests

| Pest | Damage | Prevention |
|------|--------|------------|
| Caterpillars / budworms | Bore into buds, cause rot | BT spray (Bacillus thuringiensis) every 7–14 days |
| Aphids | Suck sap, spread disease | Neem oil in veg, ladybugs as biocontrol |
| Spider mites | Webbing, stippled leaves | Less common outdoors (wind + predators), neem if found |
| Grasshoppers | Eat leaves | Physical barriers (row cover), hand picking |
| Deer / rabbits | Eat entire plants | Fencing, raised beds, scent deterrents |
| Slugs / snails | Eat seedlings and lower leaves | Diatomaceous earth around base, copper tape |

### The budworm problem

Caterpillars (especially corn earworm / budworm) are the #1
pest threat to outdoor cannabis. They bore into buds and their
frass (droppings) inside the bud causes Botrytis (bud rot).

**Prevention:** spray BT (Bacillus thuringiensis) every 7–14
days from early flower through harvest. BT is organic,
non-toxic to humans, and breaks down in sunlight within days.
It specifically kills caterpillar larvae without harming
beneficial insects.

**Inspection:** check inside the largest buds by gently
squeezing — healthy buds are uniformly firm. Buds with
caterpillar damage have soft spots, brown areas, or visible
frass. Pull apart and inspect any suspicious buds.

## Outdoor training

Outdoor plants have unlimited space but benefit from training
for yield and stealth:

- **Topping:** top once above node 5–6 to create multiple
  main colas. This also reduces height (important for
  stealth)
- **LST:** bend branches outward and stake to create a wide,
  low canopy. An outdoor plant trained flat can be 6 feet wide
  and only 3 feet tall
- **Caging:** a tomato cage provides structure for heavy
  branches and keeps the plant from falling over in wind
- **SCROG (outdoor):** a horizontal net supported by stakes
  creates a flat outdoor canopy. Less common but effective for
  maximizing light exposure

## Harvesting outdoor plants

### When to harvest

Same trichome reading as indoor — check with a loupe for your
target ratio of milky to amber. See our
[harvest timing guide](/guides/cannabis-harvest-timing-by-effect).

### Weather considerations

**Harvest before:**
- First frost (freezing kills plant tissue and degrades
  cannabinoids)
- Extended rain during late flower (moisture in dense buds
  causes Botrytis)
- Severe storms that can damage mature plants

**The rain dilemma:** if heavy rain is forecast during the
last week before harvest, you may need to harvest early to
avoid bud rot. A tarp or temporary shelter over the plant can
protect it for a few days but isn't a long-term solution.

## Common mistakes

1. **Starting too late in cold climates** — in WNY, seeds need
   to start indoors by April. Planting outdoors in June means
   the plant doesn't have enough veg time to produce
   meaningful yield before the October frost deadline
2. **Choosing slow-finishing strains** — a 12-week sativa in
   WNY starts flowering in August and needs until November
   to finish. Frost kills it in October. Choose 7–9 week
   flower strains for northern climates
3. **Not protecting against caterpillars** — budworms are
   inevitable in most outdoor environments. BT spray every
   7–14 days during flower is non-negotiable for outdoor grows
4. **Underwatering container plants** — a 10-gallon fabric pot
   in full sun and 85°F air can dry out in a single day.
   Outdoor containers need more frequent watering than
   in-ground plants
5. **Ignoring light pollution** — a street light shining on
   your plant during the dark period prevents flowering or
   causes revegging/hermaphrodites. Scout the location at
   night before committing
