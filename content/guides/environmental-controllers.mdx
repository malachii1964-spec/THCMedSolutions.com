A grow light on a timer is automation level one. Most growers stop
there and spend the rest of the cycle babysitting temperature and
humidity manually — turning the exhaust fan up when it's hot, plugging
in the humidifier when it's dry, unplugging the humidifier when the
tent fogs up. That's not growing — that's being a thermostat with
legs.

Environmental controllers replace the babysitting. They read
temperature, humidity, and CO₂ in real time and activate equipment
automatically to hold your targets. The difference between a manually
managed tent and a controller-managed tent is the difference between
a car with cruise control and one where you hold the gas pedal with
your hand.

## What controllers actually control

### The four variables

| Variable | Equipment that adjusts it | Target range (flower) |
|----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Temperature | Exhaust fan, AC, heater | 75–82°F lights on, 65–72°F lights off |
| Humidity | Humidifier, dehumidifier, exhaust fan | 40–50% RH in flower |
| CO₂ | CO₂ tank + regulator, exhaust fan timing | 800–1200 PPM (supplemented) or ambient 400 PPM |
| Airflow | Oscillating fans, intake fan | Constant — not typically controller-managed |

A basic controller manages temperature and humidity. An advanced
controller adds CO₂ management, light integration, and data logging.

### The control loop

Every controller runs the same logic:

1. **Read** the sensor (temp, RH, CO₂ PPM)
2. **Compare** the reading to your set point
3. **Activate** equipment if the reading is outside the dead band
4. **Wait** (the cycle delay — typically 30–120 seconds)
5. **Repeat**

The dead band is the range around your set point where the controller
does nothing. If your target temperature is 78°F with a 2°F dead band,
the controller ignores readings between 76°F and 80°F. Without a dead
band, equipment cycles on and off constantly (short cycling), which
wears out compressors and relays.

## Controller tiers

### Tier 1: Smart outlet controllers ($30–80)

**What they are:** a single device with a built-in sensor and one or
two controllable outlets. You plug equipment into the outlet, set a
target, and the controller switches the outlet on/off based on the
sensor reading.

**Examples:**
- **Inkbird ITC-308** — temperature only. Two outlets: one for
  heating, one for cooling. Plug a heater into the heating outlet and
  an exhaust fan (or AC) into the cooling outlet. Set high and low
  limits. The Inkbird turns on the appropriate device when the temp
  drifts outside range
- **Inkbird IHC-200** — humidity only. Same concept: one outlet for
  humidifier, one for dehumidifier

**Limitations:** each unit controls one variable. You need two units
(one for temp, one for humidity) to manage both. No coordination
between them — the temp controller doesn't know the humidity
controller just turned on the humidifier, which affects temperature.
No data logging.

**Best for:** growers on a budget who want basic automation without
babysitting.

### Tier 2: Integrated fan controllers ($150–250)

**What they are:** controllers built into (or designed for) inline
exhaust fans. They read temperature and humidity and adjust fan speed
dynamically.

**Examples:**
- **AC Infinity Controller 69 / Controller 80** — the brain behind
  the Cloudline T-series fans. Built-in temp/RH sensor with
  programmable triggers: "if temp exceeds 82°F, run fan at speed 8."
  "If humidity exceeds 55%, run fan at speed 6." Multiple trigger
  programs can stack. Bluetooth app for monitoring and adjustment
- **TerraBloom ECMF series** — similar concept, EC motor fan with a
  speed controller that responds to temperature

**Limitations:** they only control the exhaust fan — they can't
trigger a heater, humidifier, dehumidifier, or CO₂ system. They
manage temperature and humidity indirectly by pulling more or less
air through the tent.

**Best for:** most home growers running a single tent. The AC Infinity
controller alone handles 80% of environmental management if your
ambient room conditions are reasonable.

### Tier 3: Multi-zone smart controllers ($250–600)

**What they are:** dedicated environmental controllers with multiple
sensor inputs and multiple controllable outlets. They manage
temperature, humidity, and optionally CO₂ as a coordinated system.

**Examples:**
- **TrolMaster Hydro-X (HCS-2)** — the commercial standard. Main
  controller + expansion modules for temperature (heating/cooling),
  humidity (humidifier/dehumidifier), CO₂, light control, and
  irrigation. Each module adds 1–2 controllable outlets. Touchscreen +
  app + data logging
- **Pulse Hub** — sensor hub with cloud monitoring and basic relay
  control. Excellent data logging and alerts; fewer direct control
  outlets than TrolMaster
- **Niwa Grow Hub** — similar to Pulse, with app-based monitoring
  and control

**Capabilities:**
- Coordinate multiple pieces of equipment — the controller knows
  that running the dehumidifier raises temperature, so it can
  preemptively adjust the exhaust fan
- Day/night programs — different targets for lights-on vs. lights-off
- CO₂ management — inject CO₂ when the exhaust fan is off, disable
  injection when the fan runs (CO₂ is wasted if the fan blows it
  out immediately)
- Data logging — historical graphs of every variable, so you can
  see trends and diagnose problems

**Best for:** serious home growers, multi-tent operations, anyone
supplementing CO₂ (CO₂ without a controller is money in the exhaust
duct).

## Wiring a basic two-controller setup

The most cost-effective automated setup for a single tent:

### Equipment list

| Item | Cost | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| AC Infinity Cloudline T6 with Controller 69 | $160 | Exhaust + temp/RH triggered fan speed |
| Inkbird ITC-308 | $35 | Heating control (winter) |
| Small ceramic heater (1500W) | $25 | Heat source plugged into Inkbird |
| Inkbird IHC-200 | $35 | Humidity control |
| Small humidifier or dehumidifier | $40–80 | Humidity adjustment plugged into Inkbird |

### Wiring logic

**AC Infinity fan controller (primary):**
- Trigger 1: temp above 82°F → fan speed 8
- Trigger 2: temp above 78°F → fan speed 5
- Trigger 3: humidity above 55% → fan speed 7
- Base speed: 3 (always running for air exchange)

**Inkbird ITC-308 (heater backup):**
- Heating outlet: ceramic heater
- Set point: 68°F
- Dead band: 2°F
- The heater turns on only when the tent drops below 66°F (lights
  off in winter)

**Inkbird IHC-200 (humidity fine-tuning):**
- Humidifier outlet: small cool-mist humidifier
- Dehumidifier outlet: small dehumidifier
- Set point: 50% RH
- Dead band: 5% RH

**How they interact:** the AC Infinity handles most temp/humidity
swings by adjusting fan speed. The Inkbirds catch extremes the fan
can't fix — a cold night that needs heat, or a humid day that needs
active dehumidification.

## CO₂ integration

CO₂ supplementation only works in a sealed (or semi-sealed)
environment. If the exhaust fan runs continuously, it exhausts your
CO₂ as fast as you inject it.

### The controller's role

A CO₂ controller (or the CO₂ module on a TrolMaster) does three things:

1. **Reads CO₂ PPM** from a sensor inside the tent
2. **Opens the solenoid valve** on the CO₂ tank when PPM drops below
   the set point (e.g., 1000 PPM)
3. **Closes the solenoid** when PPM reaches the target
4. **Coordinates with the exhaust fan** — disables CO₂ injection
   while the fan is running (no point injecting if it's being
   exhausted immediately)

### The exhaust timing problem

In a CO₂-supplemented room, the exhaust fan can't run continuously.
Instead, the controller runs the fan on timed intervals — 15 minutes
on, 45 minutes off — and only injects CO₂ during the off periods.
This creates a tension between CO₂ retention and heat/humidity
management.

**The solution:** most serious CO₂ setups use a mini-split AC and a
dehumidifier instead of exhaust ventilation for temperature and
humidity control. The room stays sealed, CO₂ stays in, and the AC
handles heat. This is expensive ($500+ for the AC alone) but it's
the only way to efficiently run CO₂ above 1000 PPM.

See our [CO₂ guide](/guides/co2-supplementation) for dosing and
safety details.

## Data logging and remote monitoring

### Why logging matters

A temperature spike at 3 AM that lasts 20 minutes won't show up
on your next visit to the tent — the reading will be normal by
morning. But it happened, and it stressed the plants. Without data
logging, you'd never know why the top colas are foxtailing.

**What to log:**
- Temperature (1-minute intervals minimum)
- Humidity (1-minute intervals)
- CO₂ PPM (if supplementing)
- VPD (calculated from temp + RH)
- Equipment run time (how often is the heater/dehumidifier cycling?)

### Monitoring options

| Solution | Cost | Features |
|----------|------|----------|
| AC Infinity app | Free (with Cloudline fan) | Temp/RH history, alerts, remote fan control |
| Pulse Pro | $250+ | Best-in-class sensors, cloud logging, VPD, dew point, alerts |
| SensorPush | $50/sensor | Temp/RH logging, phone alerts, no equipment control |
| TrolMaster app | Free (with HCS-2) | Full data logging + equipment control |
| DIY (ESP32 + sensors) | $15–30 | Custom logging to any dashboard. Requires programming |

### Alert thresholds

Set alerts for conditions that need immediate attention:

| Condition | Alert threshold | Risk |
|-----------|----------------|------|
| High temperature | Above 88°F | Heat stress, foxtailing, terpene evaporation |
| Low temperature | Below 60°F | Slowed growth, purple stems (not genetics) |
| High humidity | Above 65% RH in flower | Bud rot, powdery mildew |
| Low humidity | Below 30% RH | Leaf curl, stunted growth |
| CO₂ too high | Above 1500 PPM | Wasted CO₂, possible plant stress |
| Equipment failure | No fan speed change in 2+ hours | Fan died, controller lost power |

## Common mistakes

1. **No dead band** — setting exact targets with zero tolerance causes
   equipment to short-cycle (on/off every 30 seconds). Always set a
   dead band of at least 2°F / 5% RH
2. **Conflicting controllers** — an Inkbird turning on a heater while
   the AC Infinity cranks the exhaust fan to cool down. Map out your
   control logic before plugging in — each piece of equipment should
   be controlled by ONE controller
3. **Sensor placement** — hanging the sensor next to the light reads
   radiant heat, not air temperature. Hang sensors at canopy height,
   shielded from direct light, with airflow across the sensor
4. **Ignoring lights-off** — most environmental problems happen during
   lights-off: temperature drops, humidity spikes, condensation forms.
   Set separate night programs or add a heater on an Inkbird
5. **Over-investing for a single tent** — a $500 TrolMaster system
   makes sense for a multi-room operation. For a single 4×4 tent, an
   AC Infinity controller + one Inkbird handles 90% of cases
