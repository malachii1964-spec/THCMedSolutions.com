Most growers buy an LED, hang it at the manufacturer's suggested height,
and never touch it again. That gets decent results. This guide is about
getting exceptional results — understanding the light well enough to
push your plants to their photosynthetic ceiling without burning them.

## PPFD: the number that actually matters

Lumens, lux, and watts are marketing numbers. **PPFD** (Photosynthetic
Photon Flux Density, measured in µmol/m²/s) is the only number that
tells you how much usable light your plants are actually receiving.

- **Lumens/lux** measure brightness as the human eye perceives it —
  weighted toward green light, which plants use least efficiently
- **PPFD** measures photons in the 400–700nm range (PAR), weighted
  equally across the spectrum — what the plant actually uses for
  photosynthesis

A 100W light and a 300W light can deliver the same PPFD at the canopy
if the 100W has better optics and efficiency. Wattage tells you about
your electric bill, not your plant's food supply.

### PPFD targets by growth stage

| Stage | PPFD target (µmol/m²/s) | DLI (mol/m²/day) | Notes |
|-------|------------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Seedling | 100–300 | 5–15 | More causes light stress on fragile leaves |
| Early veg | 300–600 | 19–39 | Ramp up as the plant develops |
| Late veg | 600–900 | 39–58 | Push toward 900 only with strong genetics and good VPD |
| Flower (no CO₂) | 600–900 | 26–39 | At 12/12, DLI drops — compensate with higher PPFD |
| Flower (with CO₂) | 900–1500 | 39–65 | CO₂ above 800ppm raises the light saturation point |

**The key insight:** in flower, you switch from 18/6 to 12/12. That's a
33% reduction in light hours. If you keep the same PPFD, your DLI drops
by a third. Either increase PPFD at the flip or accept lower total light
delivery.

## Reading a PPFD map

Every serious LED manufacturer publishes a PPFD map: a grid showing
light intensity at different points beneath the fixture at a specific
hanging height.

### What to look for

**Uniformity** matters more than peak center PPFD. A light that hits
1200 in the center and 400 at the edges creates hotspots and shaded
zones. A light that delivers 800 uniformly across the entire canopy
will outperform it in actual yield.

**The edge drop-off** is where cheap lights fail. Budget panels use
fewer, wider-angle diodes to cut costs. The center looks fine, but PPFD
drops 50–70% at the edges. Premium panels (Samsung LM301B/H diodes,
high-quality drivers) maintain 80%+ uniformity across the footprint.

**Hanging height changes everything.** PPFD maps are measured at one
specific height — usually 12", 18", or 24". Moving the light 6 inches
changes the distribution dramatically. Closer = higher center PPFD but
worse uniformity. Further = lower peak but more even spread.

### How to use the map

1. Find the PPFD map for your light at your planned hanging height
2. Look at the numbers at the edges of your grow space, not just the center
3. If edge PPFD is below 60% of center, either add supplemental side
   lighting or accept that edge plants will produce less
4. Use the average PPFD (not the peak) for DLI calculations

## DLI: the daily light budget

DLI (Daily Light Integral) is the total amount of light your plant
receives in a 24-hour period. Think of PPFD as rainfall intensity (inches
per hour) and DLI as total rainfall (inches per day).

### The formula

```
DLI = PPFD × hours of light × 0.0036
```

Example: 800 PPFD × 18 hours × 0.0036 = **51.8 mol/m²/day**

### Why DLI matters more than PPFD alone

Two setups can deliver the same DLI with very different PPFD:
- 600 PPFD × 18h = 38.9 DLI
- 900 PPFD × 12h = 38.9 DLI

The plants get the same total light. But the 12/12 setup runs at higher
intensity, which can cause stress if VPD and temperature aren't dialed in.
The 18/6 setup delivers the same food more gently.

**Optimal DLI targets:**
- Seedlings: 8–12 mol/m²/day
- Veg: 35–45 mol/m²/day
- Flower: 38–45 mol/m²/day (without CO₂)
- Flower with CO₂: 50–65 mol/m²/day

### The DLI ceiling without CO₂

Cannabis reaches light saturation around 800–900 PPFD without
supplemental CO₂. Beyond that point, the plant can't photosynthesize
faster because CO₂ (at ambient ~420ppm) becomes the bottleneck.
Pushing past 900 PPFD without CO₂ doesn't grow faster — it just causes
light stress and wastes electricity.

With CO₂ at 1200–1500ppm, the saturation point climbs to 1200–1500 PPFD.
This is where commercial operations extract maximum yield per square foot.

## Spectrum: what the colors do

Modern full-spectrum LEDs are marketed as "white light," but the spectrum
composition matters for each growth stage.

### The key wavelengths

| Wavelength | Color | Role |
|-----------|-------|------|
| 440–460nm | Blue | Compact growth, thick stems, leaf development |
| 620–660nm | Red | Photosynthesis driver, flowering trigger, bud development |
| 730nm | Far-red | Stretch response, flower initiation, Emerson enhancement |
| 500–600nm | Green | Penetrates canopy, reaches lower leaves, underrated |

### Stage-specific spectrum strategy

**Seedling/early veg:** if your light has a dimmer or spectrum control,
lean toward blue-heavy spectrum (or simply run at 40–50% power). Blue
light promotes compact, sturdy growth and prevents stretching. Seedlings
under red-heavy light stretch toward it and develop thin, weak stems.

**Late veg:** full spectrum at increasing intensity. The plant needs both
red and blue for robust vegetative development. This is where "white"
LEDs (3000K–5000K mix) excel — they provide a balanced spectrum that
drives both leaf expansion and stem thickness.

**Flower:** red-heavy spectrum if adjustable. Red light (620–660nm) is
the primary driver of photosynthesis and is critical during flower
development. Many growers running adjustable-spectrum bars (like the
Growers Choice ROI-E series or similar) shift to a warmer, red-heavy
spectrum at the flip.

**Far-red and the Emerson effect:** far-red photons (700–750nm) aren't
counted in PAR, but they boost photosynthetic efficiency when delivered
alongside red light. This is the "Emerson enhancement effect." Lights
with far-red diodes (like the HLG Scorpion Diablo or Spider Farmer
SE series) can deliver 5–10% more photosynthetic output than their
PAR rating suggests.

### The sunset/sunrise technique

Some growers run far-red diodes for 15 minutes after lights-off to
trigger a faster phytochrome conversion (Pfr → Pr), potentially
allowing the plant to enter its "night" cycle sooner. This could
enable running slightly longer light periods (13/11 instead of 12/12)
without revegging, squeezing more DLI into each day.

This is advanced and strain-dependent. If your light doesn't have
independent far-red control, skip this — 12/12 works fine.

## Hanging height and the inverse square law

Light intensity follows the inverse square law: double the distance,
quarter the intensity. This has practical implications:

| Height change | PPFD change |
|--------------|------------|
| 12" → 18" | ~56% of original |
| 12" → 24" | ~25% of original |
| 18" → 24" | ~56% of original |
| 24" → 36" | ~44% of original |

**In practice**, this means small height changes have big effects.
Moving your light from 18" to 14" above the canopy can increase PPFD
by 40% — which might push you from adequate to optimal, or from
optimal to light-stressed.

### The dimmer approach (better than height)

Modern LEDs with dimmers give you finer control than height adjustment:

1. **Hang at the manufacturer's recommended height** for your space size
   (this optimizes uniformity)
2. **Use the dimmer** to dial intensity up or down
3. **Measure PPFD at canopy level** with a PAR meter or phone app
   (Photone is decent for spot-checking, though less accurate than a
   dedicated meter)

This keeps uniformity constant while adjusting intensity — height changes
sacrifice uniformity for intensity (or vice versa).

## Light stress: when you've gone too far

### Symptoms

- **Bleaching:** bud tops turn white or pale yellow directly under the
  light. The trichomes and chlorophyll are being destroyed. This is
  permanent damage
- **Taco leaves:** leaf edges curl upward (see our
  [plant reading guide](/guides/reading-your-plant)). Often combined with
  heat stress since LEDs at high power raise canopy temperature
- **Fox-tailing:** buds grow new calyxes on top of existing ones in
  towers instead of filling out. Some fox-tailing is genetic, but
  light-induced fox-tailing is stress and reduces final quality
- **Reduced trichome production:** paradoxically, too much light can
  reduce resin production. Trichomes protect the plant from UV; extreme
  light overwhelms this defense

### How to fix it

1. Dim the light 20–30% immediately
2. Raise it 4–6 inches if dimming isn't possible
3. Check temperature at canopy level — light stress and heat stress
   compound each other
4. Recovery takes 5–7 days in new growth; bleached/damaged tissue won't
   recover

## Measuring light without a PAR meter

A dedicated quantum PAR meter (Apogee MQ-500) costs $400+. For home
growers, cheaper options:

1. **Phone apps** — Photone (iOS/Android) uses the phone's light sensor.
   Accuracy is ±15–20% compared to a lab meter, but relative measurements
   (comparing one spot to another) are reliable
2. **Lux meter + conversion** — a $20 lux meter can be converted to
   approximate PPFD using a multiplier for your light's spectrum. For
   white LEDs: PPFD ≈ lux × 0.015. For blurple LEDs, the conversion
   is unreliable
3. **Manufacturer data** — use the published PPFD map at your hanging
   height. Not personalized, but better than guessing

## Light schedules beyond 18/6 and 12/12

| Schedule | Stage | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| 24/0 | Seedling/veg | Maximum growth, but plants may benefit from a dark period for root development |
| 20/4 | Veg | Popular compromise — near-maximum DLI with a rest period |
| 18/6 | Veg (standard) | The safe default. Proven, efficient, allows some root-zone recovery |
| 12/12 | Flower (standard) | Triggers and maintains flowering in photoperiod strains |
| 11/13 | Late flower | Some growers reduce to 11/13 in the last 2 weeks to signal "winter" and push resin |
| 6/2 × 3 | Gas lantern (veg) | Three 8-hour cycles per day; controversial, some growers report faster veg |
| 20/4 → 12/12 | Autoflower | Many auto growers run 20/4 from seed to harvest for maximum DLI |

For autoflowers, light schedule doesn't trigger flowering (genetics
handle that), so you can optimize purely for DLI. Most auto growers
run 18/6 or 20/4 throughout.

## The practical checklist

Before your next grow, verify:

- [ ] You know your light's actual PPFD at your hanging height (measured
  or from manufacturer map)
- [ ] You've calculated your DLI for each stage
- [ ] PPFD is uniform across the canopy (within 80% center-to-edge)
- [ ] You have a dimmer or height adjustment plan for transitioning
  between stages
- [ ] You're not exceeding 900 PPFD without supplemental CO₂
- [ ] Your VPD accounts for the additional leaf temperature from
  high-intensity LEDs (leaf surface runs 2–5°F above ambient under
  strong light)
