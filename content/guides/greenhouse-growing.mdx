A greenhouse is the compromise that eliminates most of the
disadvantages of both indoor and outdoor growing. You get free
sunlight (the most powerful and natural light source), plus the
environmental control of a structure — rain protection, wind
shelter, temperature management, and the ability to control
photoperiod with light deprivation.

For growers in climates like Western New York and the Great Lakes
region, a greenhouse extends the season by 4–8 weeks on each end,
making strains with longer flower times viable that would never
finish outdoors.

## Why greenhouse

| Factor | Indoor | Outdoor | Greenhouse |
|--------|--------|---------|-----------|
| Light source | Electric (costly) | Sun (free) | Sun (free) + optional supplement |
| Light control | Full (timers) | None | Light deprivation (manual or auto) |
| Weather protection | Full | None | Full |
| Temperature control | Full (HVAC) | None | Partial (venting + heating) |
| Pest exposure | Low | High | Moderate |
| Electricity cost | High | Zero | Low to moderate |
| Yield potential | High | Highest | Very high |
| Startup cost | Moderate-high | Low | Low-moderate |

### The greenhouse advantage for WNY growers

Western New York's outdoor season runs roughly May through October,
with frost risk at both ends. A greenhouse:

- **Starts earlier:** plants can go in by mid-April (4 weeks earlier
  than outdoor) because the greenhouse traps heat during cool nights
- **Finishes later:** the structure protects from early frosts in
  October, allowing 10–12 week strains to finish that would be
  killed outdoors
- **Manages humidity:** lake-effect humidity in September-October
  causes bud rot in outdoor plants. A greenhouse with ventilation
  controls humidity around the buds
- **Light deprivation:** WNY's long summer days (15+ hours) keep
  photoperiod plants in veg. Light dep forces 12/12 whenever you
  choose, allowing summer flowering

## Greenhouse types for cannabis

### Hoop house (cheapest)

A semicircular frame of bent PVC or metal hoops covered with
greenhouse poly film. The standard entry-level greenhouse.

- **Cost:** $200–500 for a 10×20 foot structure
- **Pros:** cheap, easy to build, sufficient for most home grows
- **Cons:** no permanent heating, limited headroom at the sides,
  poly film lasts 1–4 years before replacement
- **Best for:** seasonal extension grows, light dep operations on
  a budget

### Polycarbonate panel greenhouse

A rigid structure with translucent polycarbonate panels instead of
flexible film. More permanent than a hoop house.

- **Cost:** $500–2000 for a 8×12 foot structure (kits available from
  Palram, Rion, Outsunny)
- **Pros:** 10+ year lifespan, better insulation, more structural
  rigidity, looks better in a yard
- **Cons:** more expensive, harder to build, polycarbonate reduces
  light transmission slightly (85–90% vs. 92%+ for poly film)
- **Best for:** year-round use, colder climates, permanent
  installations

### High tunnel / commercial greenhouse

A commercial-scale structure with high ceilings (12+ feet), roll-up
sides, and infrastructure for heating, cooling, and automated light
dep.

- **Cost:** $5,000–50,000+ depending on size
- **Best for:** commercial operations, not typical home grows

## Light deprivation (light dep)

### What it is

Light deprivation is the practice of covering the greenhouse with
opaque material to artificially shorten the day length to 12 hours,
forcing photoperiod cannabis to flower on your schedule — not
nature's.

Without light dep, cannabis in a greenhouse in the Northern Hemisphere
won't start flowering until mid-August (when natural day length
drops below ~14 hours). That's too late for 10-week strains to finish
before frost.

With light dep, you can trigger flowering in June or July, harvesting
in August or September — peak warm weather, minimal mold risk.

### How to do it

**Manual light dep:**
1. Cover the greenhouse with blackout tarps at the same time every
   evening (e.g., 7 PM)
2. Remove the tarps at the same time every morning (e.g., 7 AM)
3. This gives the plants 12 hours of darkness, triggering flower
4. You must do this EVERY DAY without exception. One missed day
   (or one light leak) can revert the plants to veg or cause
   hermaphroditism
5. Duration: the entire flowering cycle (8–10 weeks)

**Automated light dep:**
- Motorized blackout curtains on timers ($500–2000+ for a home
  greenhouse)
- The curtains deploy and retract automatically at set times
- Eliminates human error, which is the #1 failure point in light
  dep

### Light leak prevention

Any light that reaches the plants during the 12-hour dark period
can disrupt flowering:

- Seal every gap in the blackout material (duct tape, clips,
  weighted edges)
- Check for light leaks at night — go inside the covered greenhouse
  and look for pinpoints of light from street lamps, porch lights,
  or moon light
- Even a small LED indicator on a fan or controller can cause
  problems. Cover or disconnect any light sources inside the
  greenhouse

## Ventilation in a greenhouse

### The heat problem

A greenhouse traps solar energy — that's its purpose. But cannabis
doesn't tolerate temperatures above 85°F well, and an unventilated
greenhouse in summer can reach 120°F+.

**Cooling strategies:**

| Method | Effect | Cost |
|--------|--------|------|
| Roll-up sides | Maximum airflow, fastest cooling | $50–200 (hardware) |
| Exhaust fans | Active air exchange | $100–300 |
| Shade cloth (30–50%) | Reduces solar gain while allowing light | $30–100 |
| Evaporative cooling | Drops temp 10–15°F | $200–500 |
| Opening end walls | Cross-ventilation | Minimal |

**Roll-up sides** are the most effective ventilation for greenhouse
cannabis. When the sides are rolled up, the structure becomes an
open-sided shelter — rain protection and structural support without
heat trapping. Roll them down at night or during cold snaps.

### The humidity problem

Cannabis transpires heavily. In a closed greenhouse during flower,
relative humidity can spike to 80%+ in hours. Bud rot (Botrytis)
is the primary risk.

**Humidity management:**
- Ventilate aggressively during flower — continuous air exchange
  is more important than temperature when fighting bud rot
- Use oscillating fans inside the greenhouse for air movement
  around the plants
- Defoliate strategically to open up airflow through the canopy
- Monitor RH with a hygrometer — if it exceeds 60% during flower,
  increase ventilation immediately

## Supplemental lighting

### Why supplement?

Natural sunlight provides massive DLI during summer (50–60
mol/m²/day in peak summer), more than enough for cannabis. But:

- **Spring and fall:** shorter days and lower sun angle reduce DLI
  below optimal levels. Supplemental lighting extends the effective
  season
- **Cloudy days:** WNY gets many overcast days. On a heavy overcast
  day, DLI can drop to 10–15 mol/m²/day — below the optimal range
  for flowering
- **Extended veg photoperiod:** running a grow light during evening
  hours keeps plants in veg (prevents premature flowering in spring
  when days are still short)

### What to use

- **LED bars** (Spider Farmer, Mars Hydro, etc.) mounted on the
  greenhouse structure — supplement during morning and evening to
  extend the day
- **Run lights for 2–4 hours** in the early morning or late evening
  to maintain 18-hour days during veg
- **In flower (with light dep):** supplemental lights during the
  12-hour light period on cloudy days boost DLI

## Seasonal greenhouse schedule (WNY)

| Month | Activity | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| March | Start seeds/clones indoors | Too cold for greenhouse |
| April | Move plants to greenhouse (mid-April) | Heat the greenhouse at night if below 45°F |
| May | Full veg in greenhouse | Supplemental light to maintain 18/6 if needed |
| June | Begin light dep for first flower round | Or continue veg for a late-summer harvest |
| July | First round in full flower | Light dep daily, ventilate for heat |
| August | Harvest first round; second round starts veg | Natural day length begins shortening |
| September | Second round in flower (natural photoperiod) | Watch humidity — lake-effect season |
| October | Harvest second round | Protect from frost with greenhouse heating |
| November | Season end | Clean greenhouse, store equipment |

### Two-harvest strategy

With light dep, WNY growers can pull two harvests per season:
1. **Round 1:** start light dep in June, harvest in August
2. **Round 2:** veg new plants during round 1, flower naturally
   starting late August, harvest in October

This maximizes the greenhouse's productive window.

## Common mistakes

1. **No ventilation plan** — a sealed greenhouse becomes an oven by
   mid-morning in summer. Roll-up sides or exhaust fans are
   mandatory, not optional
2. **Inconsistent light dep** — missing one day or leaving a light
   leak can cause hermaphroditism. If you do light dep manually, set
   a non-negotiable alarm
3. **Ignoring humidity in fall** — September-October greenhouse
   humidity + dense flower = bud rot. Ventilate aggressively,
   defoliate, and consider a dehumidifier
4. **Starting too early without heating** — a greenhouse in March in
   WNY freezes at night. Wait until April or add a space heater for
   night temperatures
5. **No pest screen on vents** — open vents without insect screen
   invite every pest in the area directly onto your plants
