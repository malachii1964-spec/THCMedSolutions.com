Vegetative feeding is simple: give the plant nitrogen and it grows
leaves. Flowering feeding is where most growers lose yield. The plant's
nutritional needs change dramatically and rapidly across 8–10 weeks of
flower — from nitrogen-hungry stretch to phosphorus-demanding bud
development to the final fade where you're managing decline, not
growth.

Get it right and you harvest dense, frosty, fully-developed buds.
Get it wrong and you get airy popcorn, burnt tips, or locked-out
plants that limp to the finish line.

## The three phases of flower feeding

### Phase 1: The stretch (weeks 1–3)

The plant isn't really "flowering" yet — it's growing aggressively,
stretching vertically, and building the framework that will hold buds.
Nutritional needs during the stretch are closer to late veg than to
flower.

**What the plant needs:**
- **Nitrogen:** still moderate-high. The plant is building new stems,
  branches, and leaves at the fastest rate of the entire cycle. Cutting
  nitrogen too early stunts the stretch and reduces the number of bud
  sites
- **Phosphorus:** beginning to increase. Flower sites are forming at
  every node
- **Potassium:** moderate. Potassium demand ramps up later
- **Calcium/Magnesium:** steady. The rapid growth demands consistent
  cal-mag, especially in coco

**The mistake most growers make:** switching to "bloom" nutrients on
day one of 12/12. Bloom formulas are high-P, low-N — exactly wrong
for the stretch. The plant needs nitrogen NOW. It'll need phosphorus
later.

**The play:** continue your veg nutrient ratio for the first 7–10
days of flower, then begin transitioning to bloom ratios. Many
experienced growers run their veg base at 75% + bloom base at 25%
during weeks 1–2, then shift to 50/50 by week 3.

### Phase 2: Bud development (weeks 3–7)

This is the main event. Buds are forming, swelling, and packing on
weight. The plant's nutritional priorities shift hard toward
phosphorus and potassium.

**What the plant needs:**
- **Nitrogen:** reduced. The plant has built its canopy — it doesn't
  need more leaves. Excess nitrogen during bud development pushes
  leafy growth into the buds (airy, leafy buds instead of dense nugs)
  and can reduce terpene and resin production
- **Phosphorus:** high. Phosphorus drives flower formation, seed
  development (which the plant is attempting even in sensimilla), and
  energy transfer within the plant
- **Potassium:** high. Potassium regulates water transport, enzyme
  activation, and sugar production. Heavy K demand during this phase
- **Sulfur:** moderate but important. Sulfur contributes to terpene
  synthesis. Many bloom boosters include sulfur for this reason
- **Calcium/Magnesium:** steady to slightly increased

**Typical NPK ratio:** something in the range of 1-3-3 or 1-3-4
(N-P-K). The specific numbers depend on your nutrient line, but the
ratio is consistent: low N, high P, high K.

### Phase 3: Ripening (weeks 7–10)

The plant is finishing. Buds are hardening, trichomes are maturing
from clear to milky to amber, and the plant is beginning to senesce
(natural decline). Fan leaves yellow and fall — this is normal, not
a deficiency.

**What the plant needs:**
- **Nitrogen:** minimal. You WANT the leaves to yellow — it means
  the plant is pulling stored nitrogen from leaves into the buds.
  Feeding nitrogen now keeps leaves green but doesn't benefit the
  buds
- **Phosphorus:** decreasing. Bud formation is complete; the buds
  are ripening, not growing
- **Potassium:** still moderate. Potassium supports the final
  maturation processes
- **Everything:** reduced. Many growers drop to 50% strength in the
  last 2 weeks and plain water for the final 3–7 days (the "flush")

## Week-by-week feeding chart

This chart assumes a 9-week flowering strain. Adjust for shorter or
longer strains by compressing or stretching the middle phase.

### Soil (organic or amended)

| Week | Base feed | Supplements | EC target | Notes |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Veg base 100% | Cal-mag if needed | 1.2–1.4 | Still stretching — don't switch to bloom yet |
| 2 | Veg 50% + Bloom 50% | Cal-mag | 1.4–1.6 | Transition week |
| 3 | Bloom base 100% | PK booster (light) | 1.6–1.8 | Bud sites forming |
| 4 | Bloom 100% | PK booster | 1.8–2.0 | Bud swell starting |
| 5 | Bloom 100% | PK booster (peak) | 1.8–2.2 | Peak feeding — heaviest nutrient demand |
| 6 | Bloom 100% | PK booster (reduce) | 1.6–2.0 | Begin tapering |
| 7 | Bloom 75% | Reduce all supplements | 1.4–1.6 | Ripening begins |
| 8 | Bloom 50% | None | 1.0–1.2 | Pre-flush taper |
| 9 | Plain water | None | 0.0–0.4 | Flush (optional/debated — see notes) |

### Coco coir

Coco requires higher feeding frequency (daily to twice daily in
flower) and consistent cal-mag supplementation.

| Week | Base feed | Supplements | EC target | Notes |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Veg base 100% | Cal-mag 1–2 mL/L | 1.4–1.6 | Stretch — feed daily |
| 2 | Veg 50% + Bloom 50% | Cal-mag | 1.6–1.8 | Transition |
| 3 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK light | 1.8–2.0 | Feed 1–2× daily, 20% runoff |
| 4 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK | 2.0–2.2 | Monitor runoff EC — if rising, flush with half-strength |
| 5 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK peak | 2.0–2.4 | Peak — some strains tolerate 2.4; most prefer 2.0–2.2 |
| 6 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK reduce | 1.8–2.0 | Begin taper |
| 7 | Bloom 75% | Cal-mag only | 1.4–1.6 | Ripening |
| 8 | Bloom 50% | Cal-mag light | 1.0–1.4 | Pre-flush |
| 9 | Plain water | None | 0.2–0.6 | 2–3 days plain water is sufficient in coco |

### Hydro (DWC)

DWC feeding is continuous — the reservoir IS the feed. Adjust the
reservoir, don't add plain water feeds between nutrient feeds.

| Week | Base feed | Supplements | EC target | Notes |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Veg base 100% | Cal-mag, Hydroguard | 1.2–1.4 | Res temp below 68°F |
| 2 | Veg 50% + Bloom 50% | Cal-mag | 1.4–1.6 | Change res weekly |
| 3 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK light | 1.6–1.8 | pH drifts faster in flower |
| 4 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK | 1.8–2.0 | Check pH/EC daily |
| 5 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK peak | 1.8–2.2 | Don't exceed 2.2 in DWC — root uptake is direct |
| 6 | Bloom 100% | Cal-mag + PK reduce | 1.6–1.8 | Taper begins |
| 7 | Bloom 75% | Cal-mag | 1.2–1.4 | Ripening |
| 8 | Bloom 50% | None | 0.8–1.0 | Light feed |
| 9 | Plain water + Hydroguard | None | 0.0–0.4 | Flush res, pH to 5.8 |

## PK boosters: necessary or snake oil?

PK boosters (phosphorus + potassium supplements added on top of base
nutrients) are the most marketed — and most debated — products in
cannabis cultivation.

### The case for

- Cannabis demand for P and K spikes during weeks 3–6 of flower
- Most base nutrients don't provide enough P and K at the ratios the
  plant needs during peak bud development
- Controlled studies (Caplan et al., 2017) showed that increasing P
  application during flower increased cannabis yield, up to a point

### The case against

- Excess P locks out micronutrients (especially zinc and iron)
- Most commercial "bloom boosters" are just concentrated monopotassium
  phosphate (MKP) — you can buy MKP for $3/pound instead of $30/bottle
- Diminishing returns — the yield increase from proper PK
  supplementation is real but modest (10–15% in controlled studies).
  The marketing suggests 30–50%, which is fiction

### The recommendation

Use a PK booster at **half the manufacturer's recommended rate**
during weeks 3–6 only. The manufacturer's rate is designed to sell
you more product, not to optimize your plant's growth. Start at half,
watch the plant's response, and increase only if you see no tip burn
and the plant looks hungry (pale new growth, slow bud development).

**Good PK products:** Canna PK 13/14, General Hydroponics Kool Bloom
(dry), Advanced Nutrients Big Bud (at half rate)

## Reading the plant during flower

### Healthy feeding signs

- **Leaves:** dark green (not too dark — that's N excess) with no tip
  burn
- **Buds:** swelling visibly week over week
- **Trichomes:** developing on schedule (appear week 3–4, fill in
  week 5–7)
- **New growth:** healthy, lighter green (normal — it hasn't
  chlorophyll-loaded yet)

### Overfeeding signs

- **Burnt tips:** the classic sign of nutrient excess. Leaf tips turn
  brown and crispy. Reduce EC by 20% and increase runoff percentage
- **Clawing:** leaves curl downward at the tips (nitrogen toxicity
  specifically). Reduce nitrogen — switch to bloom base if you
  haven't already
- **Dark, waxy leaves:** excess nitrogen. The plant can't use what
  you're giving it

### Underfeeding signs

- **Yellowing lower leaves (before week 7):** if the bottom fan leaves
  are yellowing before the plant is naturally ripening, it's running
  out of mobile nutrients (N, P, K, Mg). Increase base feed 10–20%
- **Light green new growth:** the plant is hungry. Increase EC
- **Red/purple stems:** can indicate P deficiency (or just genetics
  or cold temps). Check P levels if other signs are present

### Normal ripening signs (not deficiency)

- **Yellowing fan leaves in weeks 8–9:** this is natural senescence.
  The plant is pulling nutrients from leaves into buds. Don't panic-
  feed
- **Fading colors:** some strains fade to yellow, purple, or even
  red as they ripen. This is genetics + reduced nitrogen, and it's
  beautiful, not broken

## The flush debate

**What flushing means:** feeding only plain water (no nutrients) for
the last 3–14 days before harvest.

**The claimed benefit:** removes excess nutrients from the plant
tissue, resulting in smoother, cleaner-tasting smoke with white ash
instead of black.

**What the research says:** a 2020 study by RX Green Technologies
found no significant difference in mineral content, taste, or
smoothness between flushed and unflushed cannabis. The study was
small and industry-funded, so take it as evidence rather than proof.

**The practical recommendation:**
- **Coco and hydro:** a 3–7 day flush does no harm and lets you stop
  buying nutrients a week early. It probably doesn't dramatically
  affect quality, but it reduces salt buildup
- **Soil:** soil has its own nutrient reservoir. A "flush" in soil
  means the plant draws down the soil's nutrients naturally as you
  withhold feeding. 7–14 days is common
- **Living soil:** never flush living soil. The soil biology IS the
  nutrient delivery system — flushing destroys the microbial ecosystem

## Common mistakes

1. **Switching to bloom too early** — the stretch needs nitrogen.
   Don't change your base feed until at least day 7 of 12/12
2. **PK booster all through flower** — PK supplementation is for
   weeks 3–6 only. Continuing into ripening causes lockout
3. **Chasing every yellow leaf** — some leaf yellowing in late flower
   is normal. Panic-feeding nitrogen in week 8 pushes N into the
   buds and delays maturation
4. **Not monitoring runoff EC** — especially in coco. If runoff EC
   is 30%+ higher than input EC, salts are building up in the medium.
   Flush with half-strength until runoff EC drops
5. **Same EC all through flower** — the plant's demand peaks in weeks
   4–6 and drops after. Your feed schedule should follow the same
   curve, not a flat line
