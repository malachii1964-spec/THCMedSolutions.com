You're feeding your plant a premium nutrient line. The labels say
everything's covered. But the leaves look sick — yellowing, spots,
purple stems, burnt tips. You add more nutrients. It gets worse.

The problem isn't what you're feeding. It's that the plant can't access
it. The pH is wrong, and no amount of extra nutrients will fix a pH
problem. This is nutrient lockout, and it's the most common invisible
killer in cannabis cultivation.

## What is nutrient lockout?

Every essential nutrient has a pH range where it's soluble and available
to roots. Outside that range, the nutrient precipitates out of solution
or binds to soil particles, becoming chemically unavailable — even though
it's technically present.

The plant shows deficiency symptoms, you think it needs more food, you
add more, the excess salts push pH further out of range, and the cycle
accelerates. This is why "more nutes" is almost never the right first
response to deficiency symptoms.

## The pH availability chart

This is the single most important chart in cannabis growing:

| Nutrient | Available in soil (pH) | Available in coco/hydro (pH) |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| Nitrogen (N) | 6.0–7.5 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Phosphorus (P) | 6.0–7.0 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Potassium (K) | 6.0–7.5 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Calcium (Ca) | 6.5–8.0 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Magnesium (Mg) | 6.0–8.0 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Sulfur (S) | 6.0–8.0 | 5.5–6.5 |
| Iron (Fe) | 4.0–6.5 | 5.0–6.5 |
| Manganese (Mn) | 5.0–6.5 | 5.0–6.0 |
| Zinc (Zn) | 5.0–7.0 | 5.0–6.5 |
| Copper (Cu) | 5.0–7.0 | 5.0–6.5 |
| Boron (B) | 5.0–7.0 | 5.0–6.5 |
| Molybdenum (Mo) | 7.0–8.5 | 5.5–7.0 |

**The sweet spot:**
- **Soil:** 6.0–6.8 (optimal: 6.3–6.5)
- **Coco coir:** 5.5–6.3 (optimal: 5.8–6.0)
- **Hydro/DWC:** 5.5–6.3 (optimal: 5.8–6.0)

At pH 6.3 in soil, essentially every nutrient is available. At pH 4.5 or
pH 8.0, half the menu is locked out.

## How to diagnose lockout

### Step 1: check your runoff

Water the plant to 10–15% runoff and measure the runoff pH and EC.

| Observation | Likely cause |
|------------|-------------|
| Runoff pH below 5.5 | Acidic lockout — calcium, magnesium, phosphorus unavailable |
| Runoff pH above 7.0 | Alkaline lockout — iron, manganese, zinc unavailable |
| Runoff EC much higher than input | Salt buildup pushing pH out of range |
| Runoff EC lower than input | Medium is buffering poorly — may need conditioning |

### Step 2: match symptoms to the locked-out nutrient

The deficiency symptoms you see tell you which nutrients are locked out,
which tells you whether pH is too high or too low:

**Low pH lockout (below 5.5) — common symptoms:**
- Calcium deficiency: brown spots on new growth, curled leaf tips
- Magnesium deficiency: interveinal yellowing on middle leaves
- Phosphorus deficiency: dark leaves with purple petioles, slow growth

**High pH lockout (above 7.0) — common symptoms:**
- Iron deficiency: bright yellow new growth with green veins
- Manganese deficiency: yellow spots between veins on new leaves
- Zinc deficiency: small, twisted new leaves, short internodes

For a complete visual reference, see our
[nutrient deficiency chart](/guides/nutrient-deficiency-chart).

### Step 3: rule out other causes

Before you blame pH, confirm:
- You're actually checking pH with a calibrated meter (cheap pH pens
  drift — calibrate weekly)
- You're not massively over- or underwatering (both cause symptoms that
  look like deficiencies)
- The root zone isn't too cold (below 60°F kills nutrient uptake
  regardless of pH)

## How to fix lockout

### The corrective flush

This is the reset button:

1. **Mix plain water** at the correct pH for your medium (6.3 for soil,
   5.8 for coco/hydro)
2. **Run it through the pot** at 2–3× the pot volume. A 5-gallon pot
   gets 10–15 gallons of water
3. **Measure the last runoff** — the pH should now be close to your
   input pH. If it's still way off, run more water through
4. **Let the medium dry back** to the appropriate level before resuming
   feeding
5. **Resume feeding** at 50% strength with correctly pH'd solution

### Recovery timeline

| Days after flush | What to expect |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Day 1–2 | No visible change (roots are recovering) |
| Day 3–5 | New growth looks healthier; damaged leaves won't recover |
| Day 5–10 | Plant rebounds with normal color and growth rate |
| Day 10+ | Full recovery if the flush was effective |

**Damaged leaves don't heal.** The existing spots, yellowing, and burns
are permanent on those leaves. Recovery shows in new growth. Don't keep
flushing because old damage is still visible — that's the plant's scar
tissue, not an ongoing problem.

## How to prevent lockout

### Always pH your water

This is non-negotiable in any medium except living soil (which
self-buffers if built correctly).

**Tools you need:**
- A digital pH pen ($15–30 for a decent one)
- pH Up (potassium hydroxide) and pH Down (phosphoric acid)
- Calibration solution (pH 7.0 and 4.0 packets)

**The routine:**
1. Mix your nutrients into the water
2. Let it sit for 5 minutes (nutrient interaction changes pH)
3. Measure the pH
4. Adjust with pH Up or pH Down as needed
5. Water the plant

### Alternate pH in the target range

Instead of hitting the exact same pH every time, alternate within the
range:
- Soil: alternate between 6.2 and 6.8
- Coco: alternate between 5.6 and 6.2

This ensures that every nutrient gets an optimal window during the grow.
If you always water at exactly 6.0, some nutrients at the edges of their
availability range get starved over time.

### Don't chase runoff pH with extreme adjustments

If your runoff pH is 5.0 and you want it at 6.3, don't water at pH 8.0
to compensate. That shock is worse than the problem. Water at 6.5 and let
the runoff gradually correct over 2–3 waterings. Soil biology needs
gradual pH shifts, not chemical shocks.

### Calibrate your pH pen regularly

A drifted pH pen is the same as having no pH pen. Calibrate at least
every 2 weeks using 7.0 and 4.0 reference solutions. If the pen won't
calibrate within 0.2 of the reference, replace it.

### Monitor EC alongside pH

EC (electrical conductivity) tells you the total dissolved salts in your
solution and runoff. High EC = high salt concentration = pH instability.
Keep input EC within the recommended range for your nutrient line and
medium.

## Medium-specific pH guidance

### Soil

Soil has natural buffering capacity from organic matter, CEC (cation
exchange capacity), and microbiology. It resists pH changes more than
coco or hydro. This is forgiving for beginners but means problems build
slowly and can be harder to detect.

**Watch out for:** peat-based mixes becoming acidic over time as the peat
decomposes. A monthly runoff check catches this before symptoms appear.

### Coco coir

Coco has its own CEC and preferentially holds calcium and magnesium on
its exchange sites. Without pre-buffering (charging with cal-mag before
use), coco steals Ca and Mg from your nutrient solution, causing lockout
symptoms even at correct pH.

**Always:** use pre-buffered coco or buffer it yourself (soak in
cal-mag + pH'd water overnight, then rinse).

### Hydro (DWC, NFT, etc.)

Hydro is the most pH-sensitive medium because there's no buffer — the
nutrient solution is the root zone. pH can swing wildly between
reservoir changes.

**Check pH daily** in hydro. Top-off water should be pH'd. Reservoir
changes every 7–10 days prevent drift and salt accumulation.

### Living soil

Living soil is the exception. A well-built living soil with active
microbiology self-regulates pH through microbial activity and organic
acid production. If you're in true living soil (no-till, worm castings,
cover crop), don't pH your water — the chlorine in pH'd tap water can
harm the biology that's doing the buffering for you.

Use filtered or dechlorinated water and let the soil do its job.

## The five-minute pH checklist

Before every watering:

- [ ] pH pen calibrated within the last 2 weeks
- [ ] Nutrients mixed into water first
- [ ] pH measured after nutrients settle (5 min)
- [ ] pH adjusted to target range for your medium
- [ ] EC checked (within range for growth stage)

That's it. This takes 5 minutes and prevents 80% of cannabis growing
problems. It's not glamorous, but it's the highest-ROI habit in
the hobby.
