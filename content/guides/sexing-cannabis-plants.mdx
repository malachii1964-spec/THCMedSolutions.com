If you're growing from regular (non-feminized) seeds, roughly half
your plants will be male. Males produce pollen, not smokable flower.
A single male plant can pollinate every female in the room, filling
your buds with seeds and dramatically reducing their quality and
potency. Identifying and removing males before they release pollen
is essential.

Even with feminized seeds, it's worth knowing what male and female
pre-flowers look like — feminized seeds are 99%+ female, but not
100%, and hermaphrodites can appear under stress.

## When sex becomes visible

Cannabis doesn't show sex during the seedling stage. Sex organs
develop during late vegetative growth or early flower:

| Growing from | Sex visible when |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Regular seeds (photoperiod) | Late veg (weeks 4–6) or within 1–2 weeks of flipping to 12/12 |
| Feminized seeds (photoperiod) | Same timing, but 99%+ will be female |
| Autoflower seeds | Weeks 3–4 (auto-flower pre-flowers appear without a light change) |

**Pre-flowers** are the first sex organs to appear. They develop at
the nodes (where branches meet the main stem), usually starting at
the 4th–6th node from the bottom. Pre-flowers are small — you may
need a 10x loupe to identify them confidently.

## Female pre-flowers

**What to look for:**

- **Calyx (bract):** a small, teardrop-shaped structure at the node
  junction. It's slightly pointed, almost pear-shaped
- **Pistils:** one or two white hairs (stigmas) emerging from the
  tip of the calyx. This is the definitive female sign — no male
  structure produces pistils
- **Stipules:** small, pointed leaf-like structures at the node. Both
  sexes have stipules, so they're not diagnostic on their own. But
  female stipules tend to be wider and more spread apart

**Confirmation:** if you see a white hair emerging from a small
teardrop at a node, the plant is female. No ambiguity.

## Male pre-flowers

**What to look for:**

- **Pollen sacs:** small, round, ball-shaped structures at the
  nodes. They hang on short stems (pedicels), often in small
  clusters — like tiny bunches of grapes
- **No pistils:** male pre-flowers are smooth and round with no
  hairs emerging
- **Shape:** round or slightly oval. The difference from female
  pre-flowers is shape: female = teardrop with a point. Male = round
  ball on a stick

**Confirmation:** round, ball-shaped structures at the nodes with
no white hairs = male. If you see multiple round balls forming a
cluster, that's definitive.

## Female vs. male: side-by-side comparison

| Feature | Female | Male |
|---------|--------|------|
| Pre-flower shape | Teardrop / pear-shaped | Round / ball-shaped |
| Pistils (white hairs) | Yes — 1 or 2 emerging from the tip | No |
| Attachment | Directly at the node | On a short stem (pedicel) |
| Clustering | Single at each node | Often clusters of 2–5+ |
| Timing | Sometimes 1–3 days later than males | Often the first to show |

### The "wait and see" rule

If you're not sure, wait 2–3 days. Pre-flowers develop rapidly once
they start. A structure that's ambiguous today will be obvious in 48
hours. It's better to wait and be certain than to pull a female you
misidentified.

## What to do when you identify males

### Regular seeds: expect males

If you planted 10 regular seeds, expect 4–6 males. This is normal.
Plan for it by starting more seeds than you intend to grow to
flower.

### Removal procedure

1. **Confirm the identification.** Check multiple nodes. If pre-
   flowers on different nodes all show the same sex, the
   identification is reliable
2. **Remove the male plant carefully.** Don't shake it — even early
   pollen sacs can release a small amount of pollen if jostled. Place
   a bag over the plant before cutting, or gently carry it out of the
   grow space
3. **Discard away from the grow.** Don't compost male plants near
   your grow — pollen can travel by air
4. **Check the remaining plants.** If one plant showed male, inspect
   all others again within the next few days

### When is it too late?

Male pollen sacs typically open 1–2 weeks after they become visible.
If you identify and remove males when pre-flowers first appear,
you're safe. If you see open pollen sacs (they split into segments
and release yellow powder), pollen has already been released and your
females may already be pollinated.

**Signs of pollination:**
- Pistils (white hairs) on female buds turn brown and shrivel early
  (before normal ripening)
- Calyxes swell noticeably — they're forming seeds inside
- Seeds become visible in mature buds (hard, dark shells)

If pollination happened, the buds will still be usable but will
contain seeds and will be less potent.

## Why males exist (and when to keep them)

Males are essential for breeding — they contribute half the genetics
to the next generation. If you're interested in creating your own
crosses:

- **Keep one male** in a completely separate space (different room,
  tent, or ideally building)
- **Collect pollen** from the male by placing a bag over a mature
  pollen sac cluster and shaking gently
- **Apply pollen selectively** to one branch of a female plant using
  a paintbrush
- See our [breeding guide](/guides/breeding-at-home) for the full
  process

For all other purposes, males are removed as soon as identified.

## Feminized seeds

Feminized seeds are produced by treating a female plant with
colloidal silver or silver thiosulfate, forcing it to produce pollen
sacs with female (XX) genetics. The resulting seeds are almost
always female because both parents are genetically female.

**Reliability:** 99%+ female. Reputable seed banks producing
feminized seeds have very low male/hermaphrodite rates. It's still
worth checking pre-flowers, but you'll almost never find a male from
quality feminized seeds.

**Hermaphrodite risk:** feminized seeds can hermaphrodite under
stress (light leaks, heat, physical damage). This is different from
a true male — see our
[hermaphrodite guide](/guides/hermaphrodite-prevention).

## Common mistakes

1. **Pulling plants before pre-flowers are confirmed** — swollen
   stipules (the pointed leaf-like structures at nodes) can look like
   pollen sacs to new growers. Wait for definitive pre-flowers. If
   it has a white hair, it's female. If it's a smooth round ball, it's
   male. If it's neither yet, wait
2. **Assuming feminized seeds are guaranteed female** — they're 99%+,
   not 100%. Always check
3. **Leaving males "to see what happens"** — one male pollinates
   every female in the room. A single open pollen sac releases
   thousands of grains. Remove males immediately
4. **Not checking all nodes** — pre-flowers can develop at different
   rates on different nodes. Check at least 3–4 nodes before
   deciding
5. **Growing regular seeds without extras** — if you need 4 females
   for your tent, start 8–10 regular seeds. Budget for a 50% male
   rate
