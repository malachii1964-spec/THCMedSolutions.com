Roots are the primary nutrient delivery system for cannabis.
But leaves can absorb nutrients too — through their stomata
(pores) and, to a lesser extent, through the cuticle (waxy
surface). Foliar feeding exploits this by spraying dilute
nutrient solutions directly onto leaves, bypassing the root
system entirely.

Foliar feeding isn't a replacement for root feeding. It's a
targeted intervention for specific situations where getting
nutrients into the plant fast matters more than getting them in
the normal way.

## When foliar feeding makes sense

### Deficiency rescue

A calcium or magnesium deficiency in mid-veg shows up as brown
spots or interveinal yellowing. Root-applied cal-mag takes 5–7
days to show improvement. A foliar spray of cal-mag solution
reaches the leaf cells in hours — you see improvement within
24–48 hours.

**Use foliar for:** rapid correction of mobile-nutrient
deficiencies (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium) and
immobile-nutrient deficiencies (calcium, iron, manganese) where
speed matters.

### Root zone problems

If the root system is compromised — overwatered, root rot,
pH lockout, salt buildup — foliar feeding keeps the plant alive
while you fix the root problem. The plant can absorb nutrients
through its leaves even when its roots can't.

### Clone and seedling support

Fresh clones with minimal root systems benefit from light
foliar feeding. The leaves can absorb nutrients that the tiny
root system can't yet deliver. This bridges the gap until roots
establish.

### Micronutrient supplementation

Iron, zinc, manganese, and boron deficiencies respond faster
to foliar application than root application. These
micronutrients can be locked out in the root zone by pH or
competing minerals but absorbed readily through the leaf
surface.

## What to spray

### Nutrient solutions

| Nutrient | Foliar concentration | When to use |
|----------|---------------------|------------|
| Cal-Mag | 25–50% of root-feed strength | Calcium or magnesium deficiency |
| Epsom salt (MgSO₄) | 1 tsp per gallon | Magnesium deficiency specifically |
| Chelated iron | Per label at 25% strength | Iron chlorosis (yellow leaves, green veins) |
| Fish hydrolysate | 5ml per gallon | General nitrogen boost, microbe food |
| Seaweed/kelp extract | Per label at 50% strength | Growth stimulant, micronutrients, stress tolerance |
| Silica (potassium silicate) | 1–2 ml per gallon | Stem strength, pest resistance, heat tolerance |
| Full NPK nutrient | 25% of root-feed strength | Emergency feeding during root problems |

### The concentration rule

**Foliar solutions must be MUCH more dilute than root feeds.**
Leaves are not designed for high-concentration salt solutions.
Spraying full-strength nutrients on leaves burns them.

**General rule:** mix foliar sprays at 25–50% of the strength
you would use for root feeding. If your normal feed is 2 ml/L,
your foliar spray is 0.5–1.0 ml/L.

### pH for foliar sprays

Foliar absorption is pH-dependent. Optimal pH for foliar
uptake is slightly acidic:

- **Ideal foliar pH: 5.8–6.2** (slightly lower than soil
  feeding pH)
- Most nutrients absorb best through leaves in this range
- Calcium absorbs better at 6.0–6.5
- Use pH-down sparingly — citric acid-based pH adjusters are
  gentler on leaf surfaces than phosphoric acid

### Surfactant (wetting agent)

Leaf surfaces are waxy and repel water. Adding a surfactant
(wetting agent) helps the spray stick and spread:

- **Yucca extract:** 1–2 ml per gallon. Natural surfactant,
  also provides saponins. Most common in organic growing
- **Insecticidal soap (unscented):** a few drops per gallon.
  Acts as both surfactant and mild pest deterrent
- **Don't use dish soap.** Commercial dish detergent contains
  dyes, fragrances, and degreasers that damage leaf cells

## How to spray

### Technique

1. **Mist, don't drench.** The goal is a fine, even coating on
   all leaf surfaces — not runoff. Use a pump sprayer set to
   fine mist, not a stream
2. **Spray the undersides of leaves.** Stomata are concentrated
   on the leaf underside (abaxial surface). Spraying only the
   tops wastes solution. Angle the sprayer upward to hit the
   undersides
3. **Spray until leaves glisten, then stop.** Droplets should
   coat the surface without dripping off. If solution is
   running down the leaf and dripping, you're applying too much

### Timing

**When in the day:**
- **Best:** during the last hour of the light period, or just
  after lights off. Stomata are still open, and the cooler
  dark period prevents the spray from evaporating too fast
  (leaving concentrated salt residue) or causing light-burn
  through water-droplet lensing
- **Acceptable:** first hour of the light period (lights on low
  or just turned on)
- **Never:** mid-day when lights are at full intensity. Water
  droplets on leaves under intense light can focus light like a
  lens and burn spots into the leaf

**How often:**
- For deficiency correction: spray every 3–4 days until
  symptoms improve (usually 2–3 applications)
- For general supplementation (kelp, silica): once per week
  during veg
- Don't spray daily — the plant needs dry periods to transpire
  normally

### The flower cutoff

**STOP all foliar spraying by week 2 of flower.** After this
point:

- Buds are developing and absorb anything sprayed on them
- Residues (nutrient salts, oils, surfactants) affect taste
  and combustion quality
- Moisture trapped in dense buds promotes mold (Botrytis)
- Everything you spray on the plant from week 3 onward, you
  will eventually smoke

**There are no exceptions to this rule.** If you have a
deficiency in late flower, address it through root feeding or
accept the yield impact. Do not spray buds.

## What NOT to spray

- **Neem oil on buds** — neem residue is harsh to inhale and
  affects taste. Stop neem sprays by week 2 of flower at the
  absolute latest
- **Full-strength nutrients** — burns leaves. Always dilute to
  25–50%
- **Any spray with a strong surfactant** — some commercial
  surfactants strip the leaf cuticle, leaving it vulnerable to
  disease and evaporative stress
- **Hydrogen peroxide at high concentration** — H₂O₂ above
  0.5% can bleach and damage leaf tissue
- **Any product not labeled for foliar use** — systemic
  pesticides, herbicides, and many fungicides are designed for
  root zone application and can cause phytotoxicity when
  applied to leaves

## Foliar feeding in specific situations

### Clones with weak roots

Spray with kelp extract (25% strength) + a few drops of yucca
extract every 3–4 days until roots are established. This
provides micronutrients and growth stimulants through the
leaves while the root system develops.

### pH lockout recovery

While you flush and reset the root zone pH, foliar feeding
keeps the plant from starving:

1. Flush the root zone with pH'd water
2. Spray leaves with a complete nutrient solution at 25%
   strength
3. Continue foliar feeding every 3 days until root feeding
   resumes

See our [lockout recovery guide](/guides/cannabis-nutrient-lockout-recovery).

### Heat stress support

During heat waves, spray with kelp extract + silica (potassium
silicate). Silica strengthens cell walls against heat damage,
and kelp provides cytokinins that help the plant manage stress.
Spray in the evening after the heat has broken.

## Common mistakes

1. **Spraying too concentrated** — foliar sprays must be 25–50%
   of root-feed strength. Full-strength nutrients on leaves
   burn the tissue. When in doubt, dilute more
2. **Spraying under strong light** — water droplets under
   intense LED or HPS light burn spots into leaves. Spray
   during the last hour of light or just after lights off
3. **Spraying buds in flower** — everything you spray on buds,
   you smoke. Stop all foliar applications by week 2 of flower.
   No exceptions
4. **Replacing root feeding with foliar** — foliar is a
   supplement, not a primary feeding method. Plants need root-
   delivered nutrients for the bulk of their nutritional needs.
   Foliar is for emergencies and targeted supplementation
5. **Not spraying leaf undersides** — stomata are on the bottom
   of the leaf. Spraying only the top surface misses the main
   absorption pathway. Angle your sprayer upward
