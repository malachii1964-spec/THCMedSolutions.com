Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is the professional approach to pest
control: prevention first, biological controls second, chemical
intervention only as a last resort. It's how commercial farms grow clean
cannabis, and it works just as well for home growers.

## The IPM pyramid

Think of pest management as a pyramid — work from the bottom up:

```
        ╱ Chemical ╲       ← Last resort
       ╱ Biological ╲      ← Beneficial insects
      ╱  Mechanical   ╲    ← Physical barriers
     ╱   Cultural       ╲  ← Prevention
    ╱____________________╲
```

Most home growers skip straight to spraying. Professional growers spend
90% of their effort on the bottom two tiers and rarely need the top.

## Tier 1: Cultural controls (prevention)

These are the habits that prevent problems before they start.

### Cleanliness

- **Clean your grow space** between cycles — wipe surfaces with
  hydrogen peroxide or isopropyl alcohol
- **Change clothes** before entering the grow — pests hitch rides on
  fabric
- **Quarantine new plants** for 7–14 days before introducing them to
  your garden
- **Remove dead plant matter** immediately — decaying leaves are pest
  nurseries

### Environment

- **Keep humidity in range** — spider mites thrive below 40% RH;
  powdery mildew thrives above 60% RH in flower
- **Good airflow** — oscillating fans and intake/exhaust prevent stagnant
  microclimates where pests and mold establish
- **Proper temperature** — most pests reproduce faster in heat;
  keeping temps below 80°F slows their lifecycle

### Plant health

- **Healthy plants resist pests.** A well-fed, properly watered plant
  produces its own defense compounds (terpenes, alkaloids). Stressed
  plants are pest magnets
- **Don't overwater** — wet topsoil breeds fungus gnats
- **Don't overfeed** — excess nitrogen makes leaves soft and
  attractive to aphids and mites

## Tier 2: Mechanical controls

Physical barriers and manual removal.

### Sticky traps

Yellow sticky traps catch fungus gnats, whiteflies, and thrips. Place
them at canopy height and near the soil surface. Check them weekly — a
sudden increase in catches is an early warning.

### Screens and filters

- **Intake filters** on your grow tent (fine mesh over intake ports)
  prevent flying insects from entering
- **Bug netting** over outdoor plants stops moths and caterpillars
- **Diatomaceous earth** on the soil surface kills soft-bodied pests
  (fungus gnat larvae, root aphids) mechanically

### Manual removal

For small infestations, physically removing pests works:

- **Spray with water** — a strong jet of water knocks aphids and mites
  off leaves (outdoor grows)
- **Wipe leaves** with a damp cloth for indoor plants with visible pests
- **Prune infested leaves** — if the infestation is localized, removing
  those leaves stops the spread

## Tier 3: Biological controls

Using living organisms to control pests. This is the heart of
professional IPM.

### Beneficial insects

| Beneficial | Targets | How to use |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Ladybugs | Aphids, mites, whiteflies | Release 1,500 per 1,500 sq ft, evening, on wet foliage |
| Lacewing larvae | Aphids, thrips, mites | Release eggs or larvae on affected plants |
| Predatory mites (*Phytoseiulus persimilis*) | Spider mites specifically | Release at first sign of mites; they eat 5–7 mites/day |
| Hypoaspis miles | Fungus gnat larvae, root aphids | Mix into top 1" of soil |
| Parasitic wasps (*Encarsia formosa*) | Whiteflies | Hang cards with pupae near infested plants |
| Nematodes (*Steinernema feltiae*) | Fungus gnat larvae, root aphids | Drench soil with solution |

**When to use beneficials:**

- Introduce them **before** you see pests (preventive) or at the
  **first** sign of a problem
- Don't use pesticides alongside beneficials — even organic sprays kill
  beneficial insects
- Indoor grows need repeated introductions since beneficials can't
  sustain naturally in a closed environment

### Beneficial microbes

- **Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)** — a bacteria that kills caterpillars
  and larvae when ingested. Apply as a foliar spray. Safe on cannabis
  up to harvest
- **Beauveria bassiana** — a fungus that infects and kills soft-bodied
  insects (aphids, whiteflies, thrips). Apply as a foliar spray
- **Trichoderma** — beneficial fungi added to soil that outcompete
  pathogenic fungi (Fusarium, Pythium)

## Tier 4: Sprays (the last resort)

When prevention and biologicals aren't enough, targeted sprays are the
final tool. Even here, choose the least toxic effective option.

### Safe for cannabis (up to early flower)

| Product | Targets | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Neem oil | Broad-spectrum (mites, aphids, whiteflies, gnats) | Don't use in flower — affects flavor. Apply at lights-off |
| Insecticidal soap (potassium salts of fatty acids) | Soft-bodied insects | Must contact the pest directly. Rinse after 4 hours |
| Spinosad | Thrips, caterpillars, spider mites | Organic-certified. Don't apply in direct light |
| Pyrethrin (organic) | Broad-spectrum knockdown | Breaks down in 24 hours in UV. Use as a last resort |

### Safe through flower

| Product | Targets | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Dr. Zymes | Broad-spectrum | Citric acid based. Safe until harvest |
| Lost Coast Plant Therapy | Mites, mildew | Soybean oil based. Light residue |
| Potassium bicarbonate | Powdery mildew | Mix 1 tbsp per gallon + a drop of soap. Spray on dry days |

### Never use on cannabis

- **Systemic pesticides** (imidacloprid, myclobutanil) — they absorb
  into plant tissue and are inhaled when smoked
- **Eagle 20** — contains myclobutanil, which produces hydrogen cyanide
  when combusted
- **Avid** (abamectin) — effective but toxic; not safe for inhalation

## Building your IPM schedule

A sample weekly IPM routine:

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Monday | Scout all plants — check undersides of leaves, new growth tips |
| Tuesday | Check sticky traps, replace if full |
| Wednesday | Foliar spray (if in veg and needed — neem or insecticidal soap) |
| Thursday | Scout again — look for new damage or pest activity |
| Friday | Environmental check — humidity, temp, airflow, dead leaves |
| Weekend | Beneficial release (if using), soil drench (if gnats present) |

## The cardinal rules

1. **Prevention beats treatment.** Every hour spent on cleanliness and
   environment saves ten hours of pest battle
2. **Scout weekly.** Look at the undersides of leaves with a jeweler's
   loupe. Catching an infestation at 10 mites is trivial; catching it
   at 10,000 is a war
3. **ID before you spray.** Different pests need different treatments.
   Spraying neem for a problem that neem doesn't solve just stresses
   the plant
4. **Stop spraying in late flower.** Nothing should go on buds after
   week 3–4 of flower. If you have pest pressure that late, it's a
   prevention failure — fix it next cycle
5. **Rotate products.** Pests develop resistance. Don't use the same
   spray more than 3 applications in a row — switch active ingredients

Clean cannabis starts with IPM. Build the pyramid from the bottom, scout
religiously, and you'll rarely need to spray anything at all.
