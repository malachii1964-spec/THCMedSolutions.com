Defoliation is removing fan leaves to increase light penetration and airflow to bud sites. It's the most debated technique in growing — some growers strip aggressively, others never touch a leaf. The truth is in the middle.

## Why leaves matter

Fan leaves are **solar panels.** They capture light and convert it to energy (sugars) that the plant uses to grow buds. Every leaf you remove reduces the plant's total energy production.

But fan leaves also **block light and trap moisture.** In a dense canopy, lower bud sites get zero direct light and humid air creates mold risk.

Defoliation is about the **trade-off:** losing some energy production in exchange for better light distribution and airflow.

## When to defoliate (the three windows)

### 1. Late veg — light cleanup
**When:** 1–2 weeks before flipping to 12/12.
**What to remove:**
- Large fan leaves that shade lower bud sites.
- Yellowing or damaged leaves.
- Inward-facing leaves that create humidity pockets.

**How much:** 10–20% of total leaf mass. Gentle — the plant needs to enter flower strong.

### 2. Day 21 of flower — the "lollipop" window
**When:** Around day 20–21 of 12/12, just as stretch ends and bud sites are set.
**What to remove:**
- Fan leaves blocking direct light to upper bud sites.
- All growth below the canopy line (small branches and leaves that won't receive light — "larf").
- Interior fan leaves creating humidity pockets.

**How much:** Up to 30–40% of fan leaf mass for experienced growers. This is the most aggressive window.

> **This is the most impactful defoliation.** The plant has committed its bud sites, stretch is ending, and you're redirecting energy to the sites that will actually produce.

### 3. Day 42–49 — final cleanup (optional)
**When:** Week 6–7 of flower, only if the canopy is dense and humidity is a concern.
**What to remove:** Only fan leaves directly blocking bud sites or creating trapped moisture against buds.
**How much:** Minimal — 10% max. The plant is building buds and needs every leaf it can keep.

## When NOT to defoliate

- **Autoflowers** (unless very experienced) — the timeline is too short to recover lost leaf mass.
- **Stressed or unhealthy plants** — a plant fighting deficiency, pests, or environmental stress needs its leaves for recovery.
- **Outdoor plants** — leaves protect buds from rain, sun, wind, and temperature swings. Outdoor canopies need more leaf coverage than indoor.
- **Seedlings and early veg** — the plant is building its engine. Let it grow.

## Lollipopping

Lollipopping = removing all growth below a certain point on the plant, leaving the lower stem bare ("like a lollipop"). This concentrates energy into the top canopy.

**When:** Same window as defoliation — day 21 of flower.
**Where to cut:** Everything below the top 50–60% of the plant. Lower branches that won't reach the canopy, "sucker" branches, and lower fan leaves.

> Lollipopping is less controversial than defoliation because those lower branches would produce airy, worthless "popcorn buds" anyway. You're removing energy drains, not energy sources.

## The schwazzing debate

"Schwazzing" is an extreme defoliation technique where nearly ALL fan leaves are removed at the start of flower and again at day 21. It was popularized by Three a Light and involves stripping the plant almost bare.

**The case for it:** In high-light, perfectly-dialed, commercial-scale rooms, schwazzing can increase yields by forcing the plant to regrow fan leaves closer to bud sites.

**The case against it:** It's extremely stressful, unforgiving of errors, and risky for home growers. If your environment isn't perfect, the plant can stall, herm, or lose significant yield.

**Our take:** Don't schwazz unless you've grown the same strain multiple times and have your environment dialed. Standard targeted defoliation gives 80% of the benefit with 20% of the risk.

## How to cut

- **Use clean scissors** (Fiskars micro-tip or similar). Sterilize between plants.
- **Cut the leaf stem (petiole) close to the main branch**, but don't tear or leave a long stub.
- **Do it at lights-on**, when the plant is actively photosynthesizing and can heal fastest.
- **Don't remove more than 30–40% at once.** If you want to take more, spread it over 2–3 sessions a few days apart.

## Signs you went too far

- **Leaf tips clawing** — the remaining leaves are overwhelmed.
- **Growth stalls** — the plant is recovering instead of growing.
- **Yellowing** — energy reserves are depleted.
- **Stretched, airy buds** — not enough leaf surface to power dense bud growth.

If you see these signs after defoliation, **stop removing leaves** and let the plant recover. Feed normally, maintain environment, and give it time.
