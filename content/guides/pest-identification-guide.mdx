Pests are an inevitability in cannabis growing. Whether you're
indoors or outdoors, organic or synthetic, something will eventually
find your plants and try to eat them. The key is identification —
knowing what you're fighting determines the treatment. The wrong
treatment wastes time while the pest population explodes.

This guide covers the most common cannabis pests in order of
frequency and threat level. For each pest: what it looks like, the
damage signature, and the recommended treatment.

## Tier 1: The big three (most common)

### Spider mites

**Threat level: Critical.** Spider mites are the most destructive
indoor cannabis pest. They reproduce explosively — a population can
double every 3 days in warm conditions — and they're difficult to
eradicate completely.

**What they look like:**
- Tiny (0.5 mm) dots on the underside of leaves — tan, red, or
  green depending on species
- Nearly invisible to the naked eye. Use a 30x loupe or phone
  camera macro
- Two-spotted spider mites (the most common on cannabis) have two
  dark spots on their back

**Damage signature:**
- Tiny white or yellow speckles on the top of leaves (stippling) —
  each speckle is where a mite punctured the leaf to feed
- Leaves look dusty, faded, or bronzed from hundreds of feeding sites
- **Webbing** on the underside of leaves and between branches
  (advanced infestation). If you see webs, the population is massive
- Leaves dry out, curl, and drop

**Where to look:** underside of leaves, especially along the midrib
(the main vein). Start with the lowest, oldest leaves — mites
colonize from the bottom up.

**Treatment:**
1. **Early (no webs):** neem oil spray (underside of ALL leaves),
   every 3 days for 3 applications. Or insecticidal soap
2. **Moderate (some webs):** alternate neem oil and insecticidal soap
   every 2 days. Add predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis) as
   biological control
3. **Severe (webs everywhere):** pyrethrin-based spray (kills on
   contact), followed by predatory mites. Consider harvesting early
   if in flower — pyrethrin residue on buds is undesirable
4. **In flower:** avoid spraying anything on buds after week 3. Use
   predatory mites only, or harvest early

**Prevention:** inspect the underside of leaves weekly with a loupe.
Keep humidity above 50% during veg (mites prefer dry conditions).
Never bring outdoor plants or clones from unknown sources directly
into your grow without quarantine.

### Fungus gnats

**Threat level: Moderate.** Fungus gnats are annoying and weaken
plants but rarely kill them unless the infestation is extreme.

**What they look like:**
- Small (2–3 mm) dark flies that hover around the soil surface and
  fly erratically when disturbed
- Look like tiny fruit flies but with longer legs and a more
  mosquito-like shape
- Larvae are translucent white, 5 mm long, found in the top inch of
  soil

**Damage signature:**
- The adults don't damage the plant directly — it's the larvae
  that feed on roots and organic matter in the soil
- Slow growth, wilting, or yellowing that doesn't respond to nutrient
  changes
- In severe infestations: visible root damage when you unpot the
  plant
- The most obvious sign is the gnats themselves — you'll see them
  before you see plant damage

**Where to look:** soil surface, around drainage holes, flying near
the base of the plant. Adults are attracted to light and often
visible flying into the grow light.

**Treatment:**
1. **Let the soil dry** between waterings. Gnat larvae need moist
   conditions in the top inch of soil. A dry surface kills larvae
2. **Yellow sticky traps** catch flying adults and break the breeding
   cycle. Place them at soil level
3. **Mosquito dunks** (BTI — Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis) in
   the water. BTI is a biological pesticide that kills larvae
   specifically without affecting the plant. Crumble 1/4 of a dunk
   into your watering can
4. **Top-dress with sand or perlite** — a 1/2-inch layer of coarse
   sand or perlite on the soil surface prevents adults from laying
   eggs
5. **Hydrogen peroxide drench** — 1 part 3% H₂O₂ to 4 parts water.
   Water the soil to kill larvae. Won't harm roots at this
   concentration

**Prevention:** avoid overwatering (the #1 cause), use quality soil
without excessive organic matter, and keep yellow sticky traps in
the tent as early detection.

### Thrips

**Threat level: Moderate to High.** Thrips damage leaves and, in
flower, can damage buds directly. They're harder to eliminate than
fungus gnats but less devastating than spider mites.

**What they look like:**
- Tiny (1–2 mm) slender insects, usually pale yellow, tan, or dark
  brown
- Move in a distinctive sliding or crawling motion on the leaf
  surface
- May jump or fly short distances when disturbed

**Damage signature:**
- **Silver or bronze streaks** on the top of leaves — the signature
  sign. Thrips scrape the leaf surface and suck the cell contents,
  leaving shiny, metallic-looking trails
- Small black dots (frass/excrement) on leaves near the streaks
- Leaf distortion — edges curl and wrinkle
- In flower: buds develop brown or damaged calyxes where thrips feed

**Where to look:** top surface of leaves (unlike mites which are on
the bottom), new growth, and inside buds during flower.

**Treatment:**
1. **Spinosad** (Captain Jack's Dead Bug Brew) — the most effective
   organic treatment. Spray every 5 days for 3 applications
2. **Neem oil** — less effective than spinosad but works for light
   infestations
3. **Predatory mites** (Amblyseius cucumeris) — biological control
   that feeds on thrips larvae
4. **Blue sticky traps** — thrips are more attracted to blue than
   yellow (unlike gnats)

**Prevention:** inspect new clones or plants before introducing them
to your grow. Thrips often ride in on new plant material.

## Tier 2: Common but manageable

### Aphids

**Threat level: Moderate.** Aphids are easy to spot and easy to
treat, but they reproduce fast and secrete honeydew that promotes
sooty mold.

**What they look like:**
- Small (2–3 mm) soft-bodied insects, usually green, but can be
  black, white, or pink
- Cluster in groups on stems, new growth, and the underside of leaves
- Often found in dense colonies — where there's one, there are dozens

**Damage signature:**
- Curled, distorted new growth
- Sticky residue (honeydew) on leaves below the colony
- Black sooty mold growing on the honeydew
- Yellowing and wilting of infested shoots
- White shed skins on leaves (aphids molt repeatedly)

**Treatment:**
1. **Blast with water** — a strong spray from a hose knocks aphids
   off (outdoor) or a spray bottle (indoor). They can't fly back
   easily
2. **Insecticidal soap** every 3 days for 2 weeks
3. **Ladybugs** — the classic biological control. Release 100–200
   in the tent; they'll eat hundreds of aphids each
4. **Neem oil** as a preventive spray

### Broad mites / Russet mites

**Threat level: High.** These microscopic mites (invisible without a
30x+ loupe) are often misdiagnosed as a nutrient problem because the
damage doesn't look like typical pest damage.

**What they look like:**
- Invisible to the naked eye — you need a 60x+ microscope
- Broad mites are 0.2 mm, oval, translucent
- Russet mites are even smaller, elongated, tan-colored

**Damage signature:**
- **New growth is distorted** — twisted, glossy, and "wet looking"
  even when dry
- Leaves curl and wrinkle at the edges without typical pest marks
- Growth slows dramatically
- Often mistaken for pH problems, herbicide damage, or genetics
- By the time you diagnose correctly, the population is large

**Treatment:**
1. **Abamectin** (miticide) — the most effective treatment
2. **Sulfur spray** — works but requires multiple applications
3. **Predatory mites** (Amblyseius andersoni or Neoseiulus californicus)
4. **Heat treatment** — some growers report success raising canopy
   temperature to 115°F for 15 minutes with lights off. Risky —
   monitor carefully

**Prevention:** quarantine all new clones for 2 weeks before
introducing to the main grow. Broad/russet mites most commonly enter
on infested clones.

### Caterpillars (budworms)

**Threat level: High in flower (outdoor).** The corn earworm
(Helicoverpa zea) and cabbage looper burrow into cannabis buds and
eat them from the inside, creating ideal conditions for bud rot.

**What they look like:**
- Green, brown, or striped caterpillars, 10–30 mm long
- Often hidden inside the bud — you find the damage before you find
  the caterpillar

**Damage signature:**
- Small dark pellets (frass/droppings) on and around buds
- Brown, dead sections in otherwise healthy buds (the caterpillar
  ate the interior)
- Bud rot developing from caterpillar damage — the wounds provide
  Botrytis entry points
- Holes in leaves

**Treatment:**
1. **BTK spray** (Bacillus thuringiensis kurstaki) — a biological
   pesticide that specifically kills caterpillars without affecting
   other organisms. Safe on buds up to 2 weeks before harvest
2. **Hand-pick** caterpillars — inspect buds daily and remove by hand
3. **Spinosad** — effective but avoid in late flower if possible

**Prevention:** outdoor growers should apply BTK preventively every
7–10 days during flower, especially after moth activity (moths lay
the eggs that become caterpillars). See our
[bud rot guide](/guides/bud-rot-botrytis) — caterpillar damage and
Botrytis are often found together.

### Whiteflies

**Threat level: Low to Moderate.** Whiteflies are common in
greenhouses and can spread viruses, but they're easy to manage.

**What they look like:**
- Tiny (2 mm) white-winged insects that fly up in a cloud when you
  shake the plant
- Rest on the underside of leaves
- Nymphs are translucent and scale-like, stuck to the leaf underside

**Damage signature:**
- Yellow spotting on leaves (similar to but coarser than spider mite
  stippling)
- Sticky honeydew on lower leaves
- Sooty mold on honeydew
- Plant weakness and reduced vigor

**Treatment:**
1. **Yellow sticky traps** — very effective for catching flying adults
2. **Insecticidal soap** on the underside of leaves (where nymphs
   live)
3. **Neem oil** as a preventive
4. **Encarsia formosa** — parasitic wasps that attack whitefly nymphs
   (biological control)

## Quick identification table

| Sign | Most likely pest |
|------|-----------------|
| White speckles on leaf tops | Spider mites |
| Silver/bronze streaks on leaves | Thrips |
| Small dark flies near soil | Fungus gnats |
| White flies that scatter when disturbed | Whiteflies |
| Clusters of soft bugs on stems | Aphids |
| Twisted, glossy new growth | Broad/russet mites |
| Frass (dark pellets) on buds | Caterpillars/budworms |
| Webbing under leaves and on branches | Spider mites (advanced) |
| Sticky residue + black mold | Aphids or whiteflies (honeydew) |

## When not to spray

**In flower, after week 3–4:** anything you spray ends up on the
buds that will be smoked. Most sprays are safe on leaves during veg,
but residue on buds is unacceptable for consumption. After early
flower, rely on:
- Biological controls (predatory insects)
- Manual removal
- Environmental management (humidity, temperature)
- Harvesting early if the infestation threatens the crop

**Exception:** BTK (for caterpillars) is considered safe for
food/flower crops up to day of harvest and is approved for organic
agriculture.

## Common mistakes

1. **Spraying the top of leaves only** — most pests live on the
   UNDERSIDE. If you don't spray the underside, you're missing the
   target
2. **Spraying once and calling it done** — pests lay eggs that
   survive treatments. You need 2–3 applications spaced 3–5 days
   apart to break the reproductive cycle
3. **Not quarantining new clones** — the #1 way pests enter a clean
   grow is on infested plant material. Quarantine new clones for 2
   weeks in a separate space before adding them to the main grow
4. **Confusing pest damage with nutrient issues** — broad mites look
   like pH problems. Spider mite stippling looks like calcium
   deficiency. Always inspect with a loupe before treating with
   nutrients
5. **Ignoring the problem because "it's just a few"** — pest
   populations grow exponentially. 10 spider mites become 80 in a
   week. Act immediately at the first sign
