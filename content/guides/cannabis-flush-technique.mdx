Flushing means running a large volume of plain, pH'd water
through the growing medium to dissolve and remove accumulated
mineral salts. It's one of the most frequently discussed
techniques in cannabis growing — and one of the most
misunderstood. Some growers flush religiously. Others never
flush at all. The truth is between: flushing is a tool with
specific use cases, not a blanket practice.

## Two types of flushing

### Maintenance flush (problem-solving)

Running plain water through the medium to correct a nutrient
problem:
- **Salt buildup** causing nutrient lockout
- **Nutrient toxicity** from over-feeding
- **pH drift** in the root zone
- **Medium reset** before switching nutrient formulas

This is a corrective action, not a scheduled event. You flush
when there's a problem, not on a calendar.

### Pre-harvest flush (controversial)

Running plain water for the final 1–2 weeks before harvest,
supposedly to force the plant to "use up stored nutrients" for
a cleaner-burning, better-tasting final product.

For the science and debate around pre-harvest flushing, see
our dedicated [flush debate guide](/guides/flush-vs-no-flush).
This guide focuses on the maintenance flush technique itself.

## When to maintenance flush

### Signs you need a flush

**Nutrient lockout symptoms:**
- Multiple deficiency symptoms appearing simultaneously
- Brown, crispy leaf tips (nutrient burn) combined with
  deficiency signs on other parts of the plant
- Runoff EC/PPM much higher than input EC/PPM (salts are
  accumulating, not being used)

**How to confirm:** check runoff PPM. If your input is 800 PPM
and your runoff is 2000+ PPM, salts are building up in the
medium and a flush is warranted.

**pH drift:**
- You're feeding at pH 6.3 but runoff pH reads 5.2 (or 7.5)
- Accumulated salts shift the root zone pH out of the
  acceptable range
- Flushing removes the salts and allows pH to reset to your
  input level

**Nutrient toxicity:**
- Dark green, waxy leaves with downward curling (nitrogen
  toxicity)
- Severe leaf tip burn (general over-feeding)
- The plant is clearly receiving too much of something and
  you need to clear the medium

### When NOT to flush

- **Healthy, normally growing plants** — if there's no
  problem, there's nothing to fix
- **During the first 2 weeks after transplant** — the root
  system is establishing. Flushing stresses it unnecessarily
- **Immediately before or after another stressor** (topping,
  major defoliation, transplant) — don't stack stress events

## How to flush correctly

### The process

1. **Use plain water, pH'd to your target range:**
   - Soil: pH 6.2–6.5
   - Coco: pH 5.8–6.0
   - Hydro: pH 5.5–5.8

2. **Volume:** run 2–3× the volume of the container through
   the medium:
   - 3-gallon pot = 6–9 gallons of water
   - 5-gallon pot = 10–15 gallons of water
   - 7-gallon pot = 14–21 gallons of water

3. **Speed:** water slowly and continuously. Let the water
   percolate through the medium, dissolving salts on the way
   through. Don't dump 10 gallons at once — the water finds
   the path of least resistance and channels through without
   contacting all the soil

4. **Monitor runoff PPM:** start by measuring the first runoff.
   It will be high (the salts being flushed out). Continue
   flushing until runoff PPM drops to within 50–100 PPM of
   your input water's PPM. This confirms the salts are gone

5. **Let the medium drain completely.** Remove all standing
   water from saucers. The medium should be thoroughly wet
   but not sitting in a pool

### After the flush

- **Wait before feeding.** Let the plant use the water in the
  medium for 1–2 days (until the pot feels lighter / top inch
  of soil is dry)
- **Resume feeding at 50% strength.** The medium is now clean.
  A full-strength feeding immediately after a flush can shock
  the root zone. Ramp back up over the next 2–3 waterings
- **Check pH of the first post-flush runoff.** This is your
  new baseline. If it's in range, you're reset. If it's still
  off, there may be a deeper medium problem

## Medium-specific considerations

### Soil

Soil has natural buffering capacity (cation exchange, organic
matter). It accumulates salts more slowly but also releases
them more slowly during a flush:

- Flush volume: 3× pot volume
- Flush speed: slow — soil drains slowly, and fast flooding
  causes anaerobic conditions
- Recovery: soil microbes may be disrupted by the flush. A
  light compost tea after the flush helps re-establish biology
- Don't flush living soil / no-till beds — the biology IS the
  nutrient delivery system. Flushing destroys it. Address
  problems in living soil through amendments and top dressing,
  not flushing

### Coco coir

Coco has high cation exchange capacity — it holds onto calcium
and magnesium and releases sodium and potassium. This means
coco accumulates specific salts differently than soil:

- Flush volume: 2–3× pot volume (coco drains faster than soil)
- Always flush with cal-mag water (not plain water) — flushing
  with zero-cal-mag water strips calcium from coco's CEC
  sites and triggers calcium deficiency
- Resume feeding sooner (within 24 hours — coco doesn't hold
  nutrients like soil)
- Flush more frequently than soil (every 2–4 weeks as
  preventive maintenance)

### Hydro (DWC, NFT, recirculating)

"Flushing" in hydro means draining and replacing the reservoir:

- Drain the old nutrient solution completely
- Refill with fresh, pH'd water
- Run for 24–48 hours
- Drain again and refill with fresh nutrients at target
  strength
- Clean reservoir walls and air stones during the flush

In recirculating systems, salt buildup is continuous. Regular
reservoir changes (every 7–14 days) prevent the need for
emergency flushing.

## Measuring the flush

### PPM/EC monitoring

The most objective way to know if a flush is working:

| Measurement | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| Input PPM | What you're putting in (plain water = 0–50 PPM) |
| First runoff PPM | How much salt is in the medium (high = buildup) |
| Final runoff PPM | When this approaches input PPM, the flush is done |

**Example:** input water is 30 PPM. First runoff reads 2400
PPM (heavy salt buildup). After flushing 3× volume, runoff
reads 80 PPM. The medium is clean.

### pH monitoring

| Measurement | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| Input pH | What you're putting in (target range) |
| Runoff pH | What the root zone pH actually is |
| Post-flush pH | Should be close to input pH if flush was successful |

## Common mistakes

1. **Flushing when there's no problem** — flushing a healthy
   plant removes the nutrients it's currently using. Only
   flush to solve a diagnosed issue (high runoff PPM, lockout
   symptoms, toxicity)
2. **Flushing too fast** — dumping the full volume at once
   creates channels in the soil where water flows through
   without contacting the salt deposits. Flow slowly and
   evenly over 30–60 minutes
3. **Not measuring runoff** — flushing without checking runoff
   PPM is guessing. You don't know if the flush worked unless
   you measure
4. **Resuming full-strength feeding immediately** — the clean
   medium absorbs nutrients differently than salt-loaded
   medium. Start at 50% and ramp up to prevent shock
5. **Flushing coco with plain water** — coco's cation exchange
   sites release calcium when flushed with calcium-free water,
   creating a calcium deficiency. Always include cal-mag
   (150–200 PPM) in coco flush water
