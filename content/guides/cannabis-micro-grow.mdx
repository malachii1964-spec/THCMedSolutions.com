Not everyone has room for a 4×4 tent. Apartment growers,
closet growers, and anyone with limited space can still produce
excellent cannabis in spaces as small as a 2×2 tent or even a
5-gallon bucket. Micro growing isn't about compromising — it's
about optimizing every cubic inch of a small space for maximum
quality output.

The key insight: small spaces actually have advantages. They're
easier to control environmentally, cheaper to equip, faster to
fill with canopy, and easier to keep stealthy. The limiting
factor is height, not floor space — and that's solvable with
the right training.

## Defining micro grow spaces

| Space | Footprint | Typical height | Max plants | Expected yield |
|-------|-----------|---------------|------------|----------------|
| Space bucket | 12" diameter | 24–36" | 1 | 0.5–2 oz |
| PC case / mini cabinet | 16×8" | 18–24" | 1 | 0.5–1.5 oz |
| 2×2 tent | 24×24" | 48–60" | 1–2 | 1–4 oz |
| Closet (3×2) | 36×24" | 72–84" | 2–3 | 2–6 oz |
| 2×4 tent | 48×24" | 60" | 2–3 | 3–8 oz |

For this guide, "micro grow" means anything 2×2 or smaller.
2×4 is borderline micro; 4×4 is a standard grow.

## Equipment for micro spaces

### Lighting

| Space | Light recommendation | Wattage | PPFD target |
|-------|---------------------|---------|-------------|
| Space bucket | LED strip or CFL spiral | 30–60W | 300–500 |
| 2×2 tent | Small quantum board | 100–150W | 600–800 |
| Closet | Quantum board | 150–200W | 600–900 |

**In micro grows, light efficiency matters more than raw power.**
A 150W quantum board in a 2×2 provides the same PPFD as a 300W
board in a 4×4. You need LESS total wattage to achieve the same
intensity per square foot.

**Height matters:** choose the thinnest light available. A slim
quantum board adds 1–2 inches to the ceiling. A COB or panel
with a large heatsink adds 4–6 inches. In a 48-inch tent,
those 4 inches are the difference between a comfortable grow
and a fight with the ceiling.

### Ventilation

- A 4-inch inline fan + carbon filter is sufficient for any
  space up to 2×4
- Mount the fan and filter at the top of the tent (hot air
  rises)
- In very small spaces (space bucket, PC case), a 120mm PC
  fan with a small carbon filter works
- Negative pressure is even more important in small spaces —
  one gap in a 2×2 tent releases concentrated odor into a
  small room

### Containers

| Space | Container size | Why |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| Space bucket | 1–3 gallon | Matches the footprint |
| 2×2 tent | 3–5 gallon | Balances root space with canopy room |
| Closet | 3–5 gallon | 7-gallon is too wide for tight spaces |

**In micro grows, plant the right size.** A 7-gallon pot in a
2×2 tent leaves no room for drainage, access, or airflow. A
3-gallon fabric pot gives sufficient root space for a well-
trained plant yielding 2–3 oz.

## Strain selection for micro grows

### Best traits for small spaces

- **Short stature:** indica-dominant strains or compact hybrids
- **Low stretch:** plants that stay compact when flipped
- **Fast flower:** 7–8 week strains mean less time fighting
  height
- **Autoflowers:** fixed life cycle, typically short, ideal for
  minimal-intervention micro grows

### Recommended strains

**Photoperiod (height-manageable):**
- Northern Lights — naturally short, dense, forgiving
- White Widow — moderate height, excellent yield-to-size ratio
- Critical Mass — stays compact, heavy producer
- Hindu Kush — very short indica, ideal for space buckets

**Autoflower:**
- Most autoflowers stay under 3 feet, making them inherently
  suited to micro grows
- Look for "dwarf" or "micro" labeled autos if available —
  some breeders specifically breed for sub-2-foot plants

"compact" or "short" growing patterns.

## Training for micro grows

Training is non-negotiable in micro grows. An untrained plant
in a 2×2 hits the ceiling before flower finishes. Every plant
must be actively shaped to fit the space.

### LST (most important)

Low Stress Training is the foundation of micro growing:
- Begin as soon as the plant has 4–5 nodes
- Bend the main stem parallel to the pot rim
- Continue bending new growth outward as it develops
- The goal: a flat, wide canopy that fills the floor space
  without growing tall

### Topping

Top the plant once above the 3rd or 4th node to create
multiple colas at the same height:
- 2 tops (1 topping) is minimum for micro grows
- 4 tops (2 toppings) is ideal for 2×2 tents
- More than 4 tops in a tiny space can overcrowd

### SCROG

A Screen of Green net at 8–12 inches above the pot rim in a
2×2 tent creates a perfectly flat canopy:
- Weave branches through the net as they grow
- Every square of the net should be filled before flipping
  to 12/12
- The net keeps height under control and maximizes light
  coverage

### What NOT to do

- **Don't mainline in micro grows** — mainlining requires
  extra weeks of veg that micro growers can't afford (height
  budget, time budget)
- **Don't run multiple untrained plants** — one well-trained
  plant fills a 2×2 better than two untrained plants fighting
  for space

## The one-plant vs. two-plant question

### One well-trained plant

**Advantages:**
- More root space per plant (bigger pot)
- Easier to train into a perfectly flat canopy
- More uniform bud quality
- Less canopy management

**Yield:** 2–4 oz from a 2×2 with proper training

### Two smaller plants

**Advantages:**
- Two strains = variety
- If one dies, you still have a grow
- Faster canopy fill with two plants

**Yield:** 2–4 oz total from a 2×2 (similar to one plant —
the space is the limiting factor, not the plant count)

**Our recommendation for beginners:** one plant, well-trained.
Simpler to manage, easier to learn on, and the yield is the
same because the limiting factor is light and space, not plant
count.

## Height management timeline

In a 48-inch tent with light + filter + pot eating 18–20
inches, you have about 28–30 inches of usable growing height.

| Week | Target canopy height | Action |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| Seed to week 3 | 4–8 inches | Grow normally, begin LST at node 4–5 |
| Week 3–5 | 8–12 inches | LST aggressively, top once, SCROG if using |
| Flip to 12/12 | 12–15 inches | Flip when canopy is at 50% of usable height |
| Stretch (weeks 1–3 flower) | 15–24 inches | Continue LST, supercrop if needed |
| Week 4+ flower | 20–28 inches | Height locked, buds filling in |

**The critical number:** flip to 12/12 when the canopy reaches
50% of your usable height. Indica-dominant strains stretch
25–50%. Sativa-dominant strains stretch 50–100%. In a micro
grow, sativa stretch can be unmanageable — choose low-stretch
strains.

## Environmental advantages of micro spaces

Small spaces are actually easier to control:

- **Temperature:** a 150W LED produces minimal heat. A single
  4-inch fan easily keeps a 2×2 at 75°F
- **Humidity:** a small humidifier or a wet towel in the tent
  raises humidity quickly. A tiny dehumidifier or increased
  ventilation lowers it
- **CO₂:** fresh air through the intake keeps ambient CO₂
  levels adequate. Small spaces don't deplete CO₂ as quickly
  as large rooms
- **Odor:** a small carbon filter handles the output of 1–2
  plants easily

## Drying in micro spaces

Drying is the biggest logistical challenge for micro growers:
the tent is occupied by the growing plant, and there's nowhere
else to dry.

**Options:**
- **Dry in the tent** after harvest: turn off the light, leave
  the fan on low, hang buds from the SCROG net or a line
  across the tent. This works perfectly — the tent is already
  set up for odor control
- **Cardboard box:** a cardboard box with small holes for
  airflow, a computer fan, and a carbon filter works for
  small harvests (under 2 oz). Keep in a dark, 60–65°F room
- **Drying net/rack:** hang a mesh drying rack in a closet
  with a small fan

See our [drying guide](/guides/drying-room-setup).

## Common mistakes

1. **Not training** — the #1 micro grow mistake. An untrained
   plant reaches the ceiling by week 4 of flower. LST is
   mandatory in small spaces
2. **Too many plants** — two untrained plants in a 2×2 compete
   for light and space. One well-trained plant fills the canopy
   better than two crowded plants
3. **Choosing sativa-dominant strains** — a sativa that doubles
   in height during the stretch is unmanageable in a 2×2.
   Choose indica-dominant or autoflower strains for micro grows
4. **Pot too big** — a 7-gallon pot in a 2×2 leaves no room
   for anything else. Use 3-gallon (5-gallon max) in tight
   spaces
5. **Flipping too late** — in a micro space, flip to 12/12
   when the canopy reaches half your available height. Waiting
   until the plant "looks ready" usually means it's already
   too tall for the stretch
