Walk into a dispensary and smell two different strains. One hits you
with fuel and skunk. The other smells like blueberries and lavender.
That's not marketing — that's chemistry. The molecules responsible for
those smells are terpenes, and they're the biggest factor in why
different strains feel different, even at the same THC percentage.

## What terpenes actually are

Terpenes are aromatic compounds produced by the trichome glands on
cannabis flowers (the same glands that produce cannabinoids like THC
and CBD). Cannabis produces over 200 identified terpenes, though only
10–20 appear in significant concentrations.

Every plant produces terpenes — they're the reason lemons smell like
lemons and pine trees smell like pine. In nature, terpenes serve as
pest repellents, UV protectants, and pollinators. In cannabis, they
do all of that plus something else: they modulate how cannabinoids
affect your brain and body.

### The entourage effect

THC alone feels different from THC + terpenes. This is the entourage
effect: terpenes interact with cannabinoids (and with each other) to
shape the overall experience. It's why a 20% THC strain rich in
myrcene feels sedating and heavy, while a 20% THC strain rich in
limonene feels uplifting and energetic — same THC, completely different
experience.

This is also why THC percentage alone is a terrible predictor of how
a strain will feel. The terpene profile is a much better indicator.

## The major terpenes

### Myrcene

**Smell:** earthy, musky, herbal. Think mangoes, hops, and thyme.
Myrcene is the most abundant terpene in most cannabis strains.

**Effects:** sedating, relaxing, body-heavy. Myrcene is the primary
driver of the "indica effect" — couch lock, sleepiness, pain relief.
Strains with more than 0.5% myrcene are generally classified as
"indica-feeling" regardless of their actual indica/sativa genetics.

**Also found in:** mangoes, hops, lemongrass, thyme, bay leaves

**Strains known for it:** OG Kush, Granddaddy Purple, Blue Dream,
Northern Lights

**Growing tip:** myrcene levels increase with longer flowering times.
Harvesting a few days later (more amber trichomes) tends to produce
higher myrcene content.

### Limonene

**Smell:** citrus — lemon, orange, grapefruit. Bright and sharp. If
a strain smells like citrus peel, limonene is the dominant terpene.

**Effects:** uplifting, mood-enhancing, anti-anxiety. Limonene is
associated with the "sativa effect" — energy, creativity, focus.
It also has documented anti-anxiety and antidepressant properties in
isolation.

**Also found in:** citrus fruit rinds, juniper, rosemary, peppermint

**Strains known for it:** Super Lemon Haze, Tangie, Lemon Skunk,
Do-Si-Dos, Wedding Cake

**Growing tip:** limonene is highly volatile — it evaporates easily at
high temperatures. Low-temp drying (60–65°F) preserves limonene better
than fast drying at 75°F+.

### Caryophyllene (beta-caryophyllene)

**Smell:** spicy, peppery, woody. Think cracked black pepper and
cloves. Caryophyllene is the reason some strains make you sneeze.

**Effects:** anti-inflammatory, pain-relieving, stress-reducing.
Unique among terpenes because it binds directly to CB2 receptors in
the endocannabinoid system — technically making it both a terpene and
a cannabinoid. It doesn't get you "high" but contributes to body
relief and anti-inflammatory effects.

**Also found in:** black pepper, cloves, cinnamon, oregano, hops

**Strains known for it:** GSC (Girl Scout Cookies), Bubba Kush,
Chemdawg, Sour Diesel, Original Glue

**Growing tip:** caryophyllene is relatively heat-stable compared to
other terpenes, so it survives drying and curing well. It's one of the
few terpenes you can still smell strongly in cured flower.

### Linalool

**Smell:** floral, lavender, slightly spicy. If a strain smells like a
spa, that's linalool.

**Effects:** calming, sedating, anti-anxiety. Linalool is the active
compound in lavender essential oil and has documented anxiolytic
(anti-anxiety) and analgesic (pain-relieving) properties. Strains high
in linalool tend toward relaxation without heavy sedation.

**Also found in:** lavender, coriander, cinnamon, birch

**Strains known for it:** Lavender, Do-Si-Dos, LA Confidential, Amnesia
Haze, Zkittlez

**Growing tip:** linalool levels can increase with mild stress in late
flower — light temperature drops (night temps in the low 60s°F) may
boost production. Don't overdo it.

### Pinene (alpha-pinene)

**Smell:** sharp pine, like walking through a conifer forest. Fresh,
clean, and instantly recognizable.

**Effects:** alert, focused, memory-enhancing. Pinene is a
bronchodilator (opens airways) and counteracts some of THC's short-term
memory impairment. Strains high in pinene tend to feel clear-headed and
functional rather than foggy.

**Also found in:** pine needles, rosemary, basil, dill, parsley

**Strains known for it:** Jack Herer, Blue Dream, OG Kush, Dutch Treat,
Trainwreck

**Growing tip:** pinene is the most common terpene in the natural world
but not always dominant in cannabis. Strains with significant pinene
often have piney, "forest" aromas that are unmistakable.

### Humulene

**Smell:** earthy, woody, spicy. The "hoppy" aroma in craft beer comes
from humulene (hops and cannabis are botanical cousins).

**Effects:** appetite-suppressing (unique among cannabis compounds —
most promote appetite), anti-inflammatory. Humulene contributes to the
"functional" feeling of some strains that don't trigger munchies.

**Also found in:** hops, coriander, cloves, basil, ginger

**Strains known for it:** White Widow, Headband, GSC, Sour Diesel,
Pink Kush

### Terpinolene

**Smell:** complex and hard to pin down — floral, herbal, slightly
piney, with hints of citrus. Some describe it as "fresh paint" or
"woody sweet." It's the least common of the major terpenes.

**Effects:** mildly sedating at higher concentrations, uplifting at
lower concentrations. Strains dominant in terpinolene are relatively
rare and often described as having unique, hard-to-categorize effects.

**Also found in:** nutmeg, cumin, apples, lilacs, tea tree

**Strains known for it:** Jack Herer, Dutch Treat, Golden Pineapple,
Orange Cookies, Ghost Train Haze

## How terpene profiles create distinct effects

Individual terpenes are interesting, but in practice, cannabis always
delivers a blend. The ratio of terpenes — not just their presence —
determines the experience.

### Common terpene profiles and what they feel like

| Profile type | Dominant terpenes | Experience |
|-------------|------------------|-----------|
| "Couch lock" | Myrcene + linalool + caryophyllene | Heavy body relaxation, sleepy, pain relief |
| "Energetic sativa" | Limonene + pinene + terpinolene | Uplifting, focused, creative, cerebral |
| "Balanced hybrid" | Caryophyllene + limonene + myrcene | Body + mind, social, versatile |
| "Daytime functional" | Pinene + humulene + caryophyllene | Clear-headed, alert, not impaired |
| "Dessert/fruit" | Limonene + linalool + caryophyllene | Sweet-smelling, mildly relaxing, mood-boost |

### Reading a lab test's terpene profile

If you have access to COAs (certificates of analysis) from dispensaries
or testing labs, the terpene section tells you more about how the
strain will feel than the THC percentage does.

Look for:
- **Total terpene percentage** — above 2% is terpy; above 3% is
  exceptional. Below 1% means the terpenes were lost to poor drying,
  old storage, or low-quality genetics
- **Which terpenes dominate** — the top 2–3 terpenes by concentration
  define the strain's character
- **The myrcene ratio** — if myrcene is the dominant terpene and
  comprises more than 40% of the total terpene content, expect a
  sedating experience regardless of the strain's name or marketing

## Growing for maximum terpene expression

Genetics set the ceiling, but growing conditions determine how close
you get to it. Two clones from the same mother can produce dramatically
different terpene profiles depending on how they're grown.

### Temperature

- **Cool nighttime temps (62–68°F) in the last 2–3 weeks of flower**
  preserve volatile terpenes that would evaporate at higher temps
- **Excessive heat (above 80°F) destroys terpenes** — they're volatile
  organic compounds that evaporate at relatively low temperatures
- **The day/night differential matters** — a 10°F drop at night slows
  terpene evaporation during the dark period

### Light

- **Moderate to high PPFD** (700–900 µmol in flower) drives terpene
  production as a UV/light stress response
- **UV-B supplementation** (in the final 2–3 weeks) can boost terpene
  and trichome production. The plant produces more resin as a sunscreen.
  Use carefully — 1–2 hours per day, not continuous
- **Too much light** can degrade surface terpenes on the top colas. If
  the top buds smell less than the middle buds, the light may be too
  intense

### Nutrients

- **Don't overfeed in late flower** — excess nitrogen in flower
  suppresses terpene production. The plant focuses on growth instead of
  resin
- **Sulfur supplementation** — some growers add sulfur (in the form of
  Epsom salt or specialized supplements) in mid-flower to boost pungent
  terpenes like myrcene and caryophyllene. The evidence is anecdotal
  but the practice is widespread among connoisseur growers

### Drying and curing

**This is where most terpenes are lost.** A rough, fast dry at 75–80°F
can evaporate 50%+ of the volatile terpene content. The cure develops
remaining terpenes but can't replace what was lost in drying.

- **Dry at 60–65°F and 55–62% RH for 10–14 days**
- **Cure in jars for 4+ weeks** — terpene complexity continues to
  develop during the cure
- **Store in cool, dark conditions** — terpenes degrade with heat,
  light, and air exposure

## Using terpenes to choose strains

Next time you're selecting a strain, look beyond the THC number:

1. **What do you want to feel?** Match the desired effect to the
   terpene profile (see the table above)
2. **What do you want to smell?** Terpene profiles map directly to
   aroma — citrus = limonene, pine = pinene, diesel = caryophyllene +
   myrcene, berry = myrcene + linalool
3. **Check breeder descriptions** for terpene information — serious
   breeders now list dominant terpenes alongside THC percentages
