Every grower wants bigger yields. The problem is that "yield" is
poorly defined in most growing communities — "I got 500 grams!"
means nothing without knowing how much light, space, and time
produced it. A grower running a 600W light for 16 weeks who harvests
500 grams is doing worse than a grower with a 300W light for 12
weeks who harvests 300 grams.

The only meaningful yield metric is **grams per watt (g/W)** — how
much dried flower you produced per watt of light input. This
normalizes for equipment differences and lets you compare grows
objectively.

## How to measure yield

### Grams per watt (g/W)

**Formula:** total dried weight (grams) ÷ light wattage (actual
wall draw)

| g/W | Rating | What it means |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| Below 0.5 | Below average | Significant room for improvement |
| 0.5–0.75 | Average | Typical for beginners and intermediate growers |
| 0.75–1.0 | Good | Competent grower, optimized environment |
| 1.0–1.5 | Excellent | Well-tuned system, experienced grower |
| 1.5+ | Elite | Commercial-level optimization, everything dialed |

**Example:** 300g from a 300W LED = 1.0 g/W (good). 300g from a
480W LED = 0.63 g/W (average — room for improvement despite the
absolute yield being the same).

### Other useful metrics

- **Grams per square foot:** total dried weight ÷ canopy area.
  Typical: 1–2 oz/sq ft
- **Grams per day of flower:** total dried weight ÷ days in flower.
  Normalizes for strain differences in flower time
- **Yield per plant** is the least useful metric — a single plant
  can yield 1 oz or 12 oz depending on training, veg time, and
  container size

## The yield hierarchy

The factors that affect yield, ranked by impact:

### 1. Light (the ceiling)

Light is the single biggest determinant of yield. Everything else
on this list is about getting closer to the ceiling that light sets.

- **More photons = more photosynthesis = more biomass.** This is the
  fundamental equation
- **A 480W LED at 100% produces roughly 60% more yield potential
  than a 300W LED** (all else equal)
- **Diminishing returns above 1000 PPFD** (without supplemental
  CO₂). Beyond this, additional photons are wasted

**Optimization:**
- Run your light at maximum usable intensity during flower (measured
  by PPFD at canopy level, not label wattage)
- Target 800–1000 PPFD at the canopy during peak flower
- Use a PAR meter or phone app (Photone) to verify actual delivery
- See our [lighting guide](/guides/led-grow-light-mastery)

### 2. Canopy management (the multiplier)

A flat, even canopy with maximum light coverage is worth more than
a bigger light with uneven coverage.

**The problem:** untrained cannabis grows as a Christmas tree — one
dominant cola at the top receiving most of the light, with
progressively smaller buds below. The top cola maxes out, and 60% of
the plant is in shadow.

**The solution:** training techniques that spread the canopy
horizontally:
- **LST (Low Stress Training):** bend and tie branches to create
  multiple even tops
- **Topping/FIMing:** remove the apical dominant tip to create 2–4
  main colas
- **SCROG (Screen of Green):** weave branches through a horizontal
  screen to create a perfectly flat canopy
- **Mainlining/manifolding:** create a symmetrical plant with 8–16
  equal colas

**Impact:** proper canopy management can increase yield by 30–50%
over an untrained plant under the same light. The difference is
light distribution — every bud site receives similar PPFD instead
of just the top.

See our [training comparison guide](/guides/training-techniques-compared).

### 3. Environment (VPD, temperature)

Optimal VPD during flower allows maximum transpiration, which drives
nutrient uptake, which builds bud mass.

- **Target VPD:** 1.0–1.4 kPa during flower
- **Temperature:** 75–80°F days, 68–72°F nights during peak flower
- **Impact:** dialing in VPD from "roughly OK" to optimal can add
  10–20% yield by increasing the plant's metabolic rate

See our [VPD guide](/guides/temperature-humidity-vpd).

### 4. Nutrition

After light, canopy, and environment, nutrition is the next lever.
The plant needs adequate building blocks to fill the buds that
the canopy and light can support.

- **Adequate P and K during flower:** underfed plants produce smaller
  buds. But overfed plants don't produce bigger ones — they get
  lockout and toxicity
- **Sweet spot:** feed at 75–100% of optimal, with EC monitoring.
  Target EC 1.4–2.0 during peak flower (strain-dependent)
- **PK boosters:** optional 5–15% yield improvement when timed
  correctly (weeks 3–6 of flower). Easy to overdose. See our
  [PK booster guide](/guides/pk-boosters-bloom-supplements)
- **Impact:** going from underfed to properly fed adds 15–25%.
  Going from properly fed to "optimized" adds 5–10%

### 5. Genetics

Genetics set the ceiling for bud density, trichome coverage, and
growth rate. A high-yielding strain (White Widow, Big Bud, Critical
Mass) grown badly will outyield a low-yielding strain grown
perfectly.

- **High-yielding strains** tend to be indica-dominant with dense
  bud structure
- **Low-yielding strains** tend to be sativa-dominant with airy buds
  (but compensate with longer colas and different growth patterns)
- **Impact:** strain choice alone can account for 30–50% yield
  difference, all else equal

### 6. CO₂ supplementation

Adding CO₂ raises the photosynthetic ceiling from ~1000 PPFD to
~1500 PPFD, allowing the plant to use more light.

- **Ambient CO₂:** ~420 PPM (normal room air)
- **Supplemented:** 1200–1500 PPM during lights on
- **Impact:** 20–30% yield increase, but ONLY if light intensity
  is also high enough to use the extra CO₂
- **Cost:** CO₂ setups cost $200–500+. Only worth it if you've
  already maxed out everything else on this list

See our [CO₂ guide](/guides/co2-supplementation).

## The 80/20 of yield improvement

For growers looking to improve yield, here are the changes ranked
by impact-per-effort:

### High impact, low effort

1. **Increase light intensity** during flower (just turn up the
   dimmer if the plant can handle it)
2. **Top the plant** once during veg to create 2+ main colas
3. **Defoliate** strategically at day 1 and day 21 of flower to
   expose bud sites
4. **Check and fix VPD** — a $15 hygrometer tells you if your
   environment is leaving yield on the table

### High impact, moderate effort

5. **SCROG or mainline** — structured canopy training maximizes
   light coverage
6. **Dial in nutrition with EC monitoring** — prevents under and
   overfeeding
7. **Upgrade the light** — a better light with higher efficiency
   (µmol/J) delivers more photons per watt

### Moderate impact, high effort

8. **CO₂ supplementation** — only worth it if everything else is
   already optimized
9. **Perpetual harvest setup** — harvest every 4–6 weeks instead of
   every 12–16 weeks (more cycles per year, not more yield per
   cycle)
10. **Genetic hunting** — running multiple seeds of the same strain
    and selecting the best phenotype for future cloning

## Common yield mistakes

1. **Chasing grams per plant instead of grams per watt** — veg a
   plant for 12 weeks to get a huge plant, but the extra veg time
   means fewer cycles per year. A grower running 4 smaller plants
   vegged for 4 weeks and flipping often outyields 1 huge plant in
   the same time period
2. **Buying supplements before fixing fundamentals** — a $50 PK
   booster adds 5–15% yield. A $30 oscillating fan improving airflow
   and VPD can add 10–20%. Fix the cheap stuff first
3. **Harvesting too early** — impatience costs yield. The last 2
   weeks of flower can add 20–30% bud mass as calyxes swell. Check
   trichomes and wait for the proper window. See our
   [trichome guide](/guides/harvest-window-trichomes)
4. **Not defoliating in flower** — leaves that block light to bud
   sites directly reduce yield from those sites. Strategic
   defoliation redirects the plant's energy to the buds that receive
   light
5. **Over-defoliating** — the flip side. Leaves are the plant's
   solar panels. Removing too many reduces total photosynthesis.
   Remove the ones blocking bud sites; keep the rest
