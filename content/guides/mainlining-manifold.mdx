Mainlining — also called manifolding or fluxing — is the most
methodical training technique in cannabis. Where LST bends branches
into position and SCROG uses a net to enforce evenness, mainlining
builds symmetry into the plant from the third node. Every branch is
the same length, the same thickness, and carries the same amount of
nutrients. The result: a plant that looks like a candelabra, with 8
or 16 colas of identical size.

It takes longer than any other training method — typically 2–4 extra
weeks of veg. But for growers who want perfectly uniform buds from
every harvest, there's no substitute.

## How mainlining works

### The principle

Every cannabis plant has a main stem that delivers nutrients from the
roots upward. When you top the plant, you split that delivery into
two branches. Top each of those, and you have four. Top again: eight.
Each split is symmetrical — the same node position, the same angle,
the same distance from the root crown.

This matters because of apical dominance. In an untopped plant, the
main stem gets priority nutrient delivery (auxin gradient). Side
branches are subordinate. In a mainlined plant, every branch IS a
main stem — they all originate from the same split points and receive
equal nutrient flow. No branch is dominant. No branch is subordinate.

### Why not just top normally?

Regular topping creates two branches, but they're not truly equal.
The node position, branch angle, and distance from the root vary.
Over time, one branch dominates and the other lags. You can correct
this with LST, but you're fighting the plant's natural tendency.

Mainlining eliminates the fight by removing everything except the
symmetrical branches. There's nothing to compete with — every branch
is structurally identical.

## The build: step by step

### Step 1: Let the plant grow to node 5–6

Don't touch the plant until it has 5–6 nodes (pairs of leaves). This
takes about 3 weeks from sprout. You need this height because you're
about to remove most of it.

**Why node 5–6?** The stem at this height is woody and strong enough
to handle the stress of topping and the weight of two trained
branches. Topping at node 3 (as some guides suggest) works but
produces thinner branches that are easier to snap.

### Step 2: Top at node 3

Cut the main stem cleanly above the third node. Remove everything
below node 3 — all growth, all fan leaves, everything. You're left
with a bare stem with two branches at node 3. It looks brutal. This
is normal.

**What you're removing:**
- The entire top of the plant above node 3
- Nodes 1 and 2 (all growth below node 3)
- Fan leaves on nodes 1 and 2

**What remains:**
- The main stem up to node 3
- Two branches at node 3 (the pair of growth tips that were at that
  node)
- Fan leaves on node 3 (leave these — they're the plant's only energy
  source now)

### Step 3: Train the two branches flat

Once the two remaining branches grow 4–6 inches, bend them outward
and down so they're horizontal — running parallel to the soil surface
in opposite directions. Secure them with soft ties to the pot rim or
stakes.

**The angle:** 90° from the stem, pointing straight outward. Not
upward at 45° — truly horizontal. The new growth tips will turn
upward within 24 hours.

**Wait for recovery:** 5–7 days. The plant needs time to heal from
the topping and adjust to the new branch angles. Growth will slow
for a few days, then resume vigorously.

### Step 4: Top each branch at its node 3

Once each of the two branches has grown 3+ new nodes past where they
were tied down, top each branch above its third node. Again, remove
all growth below the cut point on each branch.

**Now you have 4 branches.** Each one originates from a symmetrical
split. Let them grow a few inches, then bend them outward and secure
them — creating a + or × pattern when viewed from above.

### Step 5: Top for 8 (optional: 16)

Repeat step 4 on each of the 4 branches. Now you have 8 branches
in a radial pattern. For most indoor grows, 8 colas is the sweet
spot.

**Going to 16:** you can top each of the 8 branches one more time.
This produces 16 colas but requires a significantly longer veg
period (2–3 extra weeks) and a larger pot (5+ gallons). Each cola
will be smaller individually but the total yield is similar or
slightly higher.

### Step 6: Defoliate and clean up

Once all branches are established and growing vertically:
- Remove any growth on the undersides of the branches (these will
  never reach the canopy)
- Remove fan leaves that shade bud sites on neighboring branches
- Keep the interior clean — the manifold structure should be visible
  as a neat hub of branches radiating from a central point

## Timeline

| Phase | Duration | What happens |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Seedling to node 5–6 | 3 weeks | Let the plant grow untouched |
| First top + recovery | 1 week | Top at node 3, strip below, wait for recovery |
| Train branches flat | 1 week | Bend horizontal, secure, let tips turn upward |
| Second top + recovery | 1 week | Top each branch, strip, recovery |
| Train 4 branches | 1 week | Bend, secure, fill the space |
| Third top (for 8) + recovery | 1 week | Top each of 4 branches, strip |
| Fill and flip | 1–2 weeks | Let 8 branches fill the canopy, then flip to 12/12 |
| **Total veg time** | **8–10 weeks** | vs. 4–6 weeks for LST |

That extra 3–4 weeks of veg is the main cost of mainlining. For
impatient growers, it's a deal-breaker. For growers who prioritize
consistency, it's the price of perfection.

## Mainlining vs. other techniques

| Factor | Mainlining | LST | SCROG | Topping only |
|--------|-----------|-----|-------|-------------|
| Canopy evenness | Best | Good (with effort) | Very good | Moderate |
| Veg time required | Longest | Moderate | Moderate | Shortest |
| Complexity | Moderate | Low | Low-moderate | Low |
| Cola uniformity | Best — identical | Good — some variation | Good | Poor — one dominant |
| Yield | High | High | Highest (fills space) | Moderate |
| Best for | Quality-focused, few plants | Beginners, flexibility | Max yield, trellis growers | Quick turnaround |

## Keys to success

### Use the right strain

Not every strain mainlines well:

**Good candidates:**
- Strong stems that handle stress (OG Kush, GG4, GSC)
- Indica-dominant hybrids with sturdy branches
- Known vigorous vegetative growth

**Poor candidates:**
- Autoflowers — absolutely not. Mainlining requires 8+ weeks of veg;
  autos don't wait
- Weak-stemmed sativas that snap during training
- Slow-growing strains that take forever to recover from topping

### Pot size matters

The root mass needs to support 8–16 colas. Use at least:
- **8 colas:** 5-gallon pot
- **16 colas:** 7–10 gallon pot
- Fabric pots preferred (air pruning keeps roots healthy through
  the long veg)

### The hub must be strong

The central "manifold" — the point where all branches originate —
takes enormous mechanical stress in flower when buds are heavy. If
the hub is weak:
- The weight of multiple colas can crack the manifold
- Use plant stakes or a support ring in flower
- Some growers wrap the hub with plant tape during veg to thicken it

### Keep it symmetrical

The entire point of mainlining is symmetry. If one branch is growing
faster than its partner:
- Supercrop (pinch and bend) the fast one to slow it down
- Remove a fan leaf from the fast branch to reduce its photosynthetic
  advantage
- Never top one branch without topping its symmetrical partner

## Combining mainlining with SCROG

Mainlining builds the manifold; a SCROG net enforces the final canopy
evenness. They're complementary, not redundant:

1. Build the 8-branch manifold during veg
2. Place the SCROG net at canopy height
3. Tuck branches under the net as they grow through, filling empty
   squares
4. Flip to 12/12 when 70%+ of the net is filled
5. Continue tucking during the stretch (weeks 1–2 of flower)

This combination — mainlining for structural symmetry + SCROG for
spatial coverage — produces the most uniform canopies in indoor
growing.

## Common mistakes

1. **Topping too early** — topping at node 2 or 3 when the plant is
   only 2 weeks old. The stem is too thin and the plant is too small
   to recover quickly. Wait until node 5–6
2. **Not stripping below the cut** — leaving growth below the
   manifold creates subordinate branches that steal energy from the
   main colas. Clean it off completely
3. **Rushing veg** — flipping to flower before the 8 branches have
   filled the canopy. The whole point of the extra veg time is
   to build a full canopy
4. **Mainlining autoflowers** — autoflowers can't wait 8 weeks in
   veg. Use LST or SOG for autos
5. **Ignoring symmetry** — letting one branch dominate defeats the
   purpose. Check branch lengths weekly and correct imbalances
   immediately
