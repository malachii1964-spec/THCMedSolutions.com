If you learn one number as a cannabis grower, make it pH. More
plant problems are caused by incorrect pH than by incorrect
nutrients, light, temperature, or watering combined. And the
cruelest part: a pH problem looks like a nutrient deficiency, so
growers add more nutrients (making it worse) instead of fixing the
pH (the actual problem).

## What pH is

pH measures how acidic or alkaline (basic) a solution is, on a
scale from 0 to 14:
- **pH 0–6.9:** acidic (lower = more acidic)
- **pH 7.0:** neutral (pure water)
- **pH 7.1–14:** alkaline / basic (higher = more alkaline)

**Cannabis likes slightly acidic conditions.** The optimal range
depends on your growing medium:

| Medium | Optimal pH range |
|--------|-----------------|
| Soil | 6.0–6.8 (sweet spot: 6.3–6.5) |
| Coco coir | 5.5–6.5 (sweet spot: 5.8–6.2) |
| Hydro (DWC) | 5.5–6.0 (sweet spot: 5.8) |

## Why pH matters

Nutrients dissolve in water as ions. The availability of each ion
depends on pH — at certain pH levels, some nutrients become
chemically "locked out," meaning they're present in the solution
but the roots can't absorb them.

- **Below pH 5.5:** calcium, magnesium, and phosphorus lock out
- **Above pH 7.0:** iron, manganese, zinc, and boron lock out
- **At pH 6.0–6.5 (soil):** all essential nutrients are available
  simultaneously

When pH is out of range, the plant shows deficiency symptoms even
though the nutrients are there. Adding more nutrients doesn't fix
it — it just increases salt concentration while the lockout
persists. The ONLY fix is correcting the pH.

See our
[pH lockout guide](/guides/ph-lockout-and-nutrient-problems) for
the detailed science.

## How to measure pH

### pH pen (digital meter)

The most practical tool for cannabis growers. Dip the probe in your
nutrient solution, read the number on the screen.

**Good pens:**
- **Apera PH20** (~$45) — reliable, accurate, long-lasting probe
- **Bluelab pH Pen** (~$80) — professional quality, replaceable
  probe
- **Milwaukee pH600** (~$30) — budget, works fine if calibrated
  regularly

**Calibration:** calibrate your pH pen every 2–4 weeks using
calibration solution (pH 4.0 and pH 7.0 buffer solutions). An
uncalibrated pen drifts over time and gives false readings — the
worst kind of wrong, because you trust it.

### pH drops (liquid test kit)

A plastic vial with drops that change color based on pH. Cheap ($5),
no calibration needed, but less precise (accuracy ±0.5 pH vs. ±0.1
for a good pen). Fine for beginners.

### pH test strips

Paper strips you dip in the solution. Cheap but imprecise (color
changes are hard to read accurately). Not recommended — spend the
$30 on a digital pen.

## What to measure

### 1. Feed pH (input)

After mixing your nutrient solution, measure the pH before watering
the plant. Adjust up or down to hit the target range for your
medium.

### 2. Runoff pH (output)

After watering, collect the runoff (water that drains from the
bottom of the pot) and measure its pH. Runoff pH tells you what's
happening inside the root zone.

| Runoff vs. feed | What it means |
|----------------|---------------|
| Runoff pH ≈ feed pH | Root zone is stable |
| Runoff pH lower than feed | Root zone is acidifying (common as nutrients break down) |
| Runoff pH higher than feed | Root zone is alkalinizing (common in some soils with lime) |
| Runoff pH below 5.5 | Lockout zone — flush with pH 6.5 water |
| Runoff pH above 7.0 | Lockout zone — flush with pH 6.0 water |

## How to adjust pH

### pH down (too high → lower it)

**Products:**
- **General Hydroponics pH Down** — phosphoric acid. The standard.
  A few drops per gallon is usually enough
- **Citric acid** — organic option. Works but degrades faster in the
  solution

**Method:**
1. Mix your nutrients in the water first
2. Measure pH
3. Add 1–2 drops of pH Down per gallon, stir
4. Measure again
5. Repeat until in range

### pH up (too low → raise it)

**Products:**
- **General Hydroponics pH Up** — potassium hydroxide. The standard
- **Baking soda** — sodium bicarbonate. Works in a pinch but adds
  sodium (not ideal long-term)

**Method:** same as above — add tiny amounts, stir, re-measure.

### How much to add

pH adjusters are concentrated. Start with 1 drop (from a dropper)
per gallon and work up. It's easy to overshoot — going from pH 7.0
to pH 5.0 with too much pH Down is worse than not adjusting at all.

### Mixing order matters

Always add nutrients to water FIRST, then adjust pH LAST. Nutrients
change the pH of the solution — if you pH first and then add
nutrients, the pH will shift and you'll need to adjust again.

Correct order:
1. Water (at room temperature)
2. Silica supplement (if using — highly alkaline, add first)
3. Base nutrients
4. Cal-mag and other supplements
5. **pH adjustment (last)**

## Common pH problems

### pH drift in reservoirs (hydro)

In DWC and recirculating hydro, pH naturally drifts upward over time
as the plant absorbs acidic ions. Check reservoir pH daily and
adjust as needed.

### Soil buffering

Soil has buffering capacity — it resists pH changes. This is
usually good (it protects against small errors) but means that if
pH gets out of range in soil, it's harder to correct. You may need
to water with adjusted pH water several times before runoff pH
reaches the target.

### Coco has no buffer

Coco coir has almost no buffering capacity. pH swings happen fast
and hit the plant immediately. Always pH every feeding in coco —
skipping once can cause problems.

### Tap water pH

Tap water pH varies by location (6.5–8.5+). Always measure your
tap water pH before adding nutrients, and measure again after
mixing. Your tap water's pH has no direct bearing on the feed pH —
the nutrients change it significantly.

## The daily routine

For consistent pH management:

1. **Mix nutrients in water**
2. **Measure pH**
3. **Adjust to target range** (6.3–6.5 for soil, 5.8–6.0 for
   coco/hydro)
4. **Water the plant**
5. **Check runoff pH** once per week (or more in coco)

Total time: 2 minutes. This single habit prevents more problems
than any other practice in cannabis growing.

## Common mistakes

1. **Not checking pH at all** — "I just use tap water and nutrients,
   it's probably fine." It's probably not. Tap water ranges from
   pH 6.5–8.5+, and nutrients shift it further
2. **Checking pH before adding nutrients** — nutrients change the
   pH of the solution. Always add nutrients first, pH adjust last
3. **Chasing exact numbers** — pH 6.3 and pH 6.5 produce the same
   results. Don't over-adjust. Anywhere in the target range is fine
4. **Using litmus strips or color drops for precision** — these
   tools give a rough estimate (±0.5). A digital pen gives ±0.1.
   The difference matters when the target range is only 0.8 units
   wide
5. **Not calibrating the pH pen** — an uncalibrated pen can read
   0.5+ off. Calibrate monthly with pH 4.0 and 7.0 buffer solutions.
   A wrong reading is worse than no reading — it sends you in the
   wrong direction
