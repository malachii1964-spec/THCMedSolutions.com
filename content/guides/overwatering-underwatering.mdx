Overwatering kills more cannabis plants than any other single
mistake. It's also the most misunderstood — overwatering doesn't
mean giving too much water at once. It means watering too frequently,
keeping the root zone constantly wet and depriving roots of the
oxygen they need to function.

Underwatering is less common (most beginners err on the wet side)
but just as stressful. Both conditions present as drooping, and
telling them apart is a critical skill.

## Overwatering vs. underwatering: how to tell the difference

### Overwatering

**The look:**
- Leaves droop downward, but they're **thick, heavy, and firm** —
  not limp. The leaves feel swollen, almost fat
- The droop affects the entire plant uniformly — all leaves hang at
  the same angle
- Stems may curve downward under the weight of waterlogged leaves
- Leaf color is often darker than normal (dark green, not yellowing)
- In advanced cases, lower leaves turn yellow and fall off

**The feel:**
- Soil is wet or muddy. If you push your finger 2 inches into the
  medium, it's moist
- The pot feels heavy when you lift it
- The surface may have green algae or a white crust (salt deposits)

**The timeline:**
- Develops slowly over days of consistently wet soil
- The plant was watered yesterday (or even today) and is still
  drooping

### Underwatering

**The look:**
- Leaves droop, but they're **thin, papery, and limp** — wilted like
  a neglected houseplant. The leaves feel fragile
- The droop may be more severe — leaves hang almost straight down,
  folding at the petiole (stem)
- Leaf edges may curl inward (dry edges)
- Color is lighter than usual, sometimes with yellowing

**The feel:**
- Soil is dry. The surface is cracked or pulled away from the pot
  edges
- The pot feels very light when you lift it — noticeably lighter
  than when watered
- The medium may be hydrophobic — water runs through immediately
  without soaking in

**The timeline:**
- The plant was last watered 3+ days ago
- The droop appeared relatively quickly (within hours) after the
  soil dried completely

### The quick test

**Lift the pot.** This is the single most reliable test:
- **Heavy pot = overwatered** (too much water in the medium)
- **Light pot = underwatered** (medium is dry)
- **Learn the weight difference** by lifting the pot right after
  watering (heavy) and just before the next watering (light). After
  a few cycles, you'll know the right weight by feel

## Overwatering: causes and fixes

### Why overwatering happens

1. **Watering on a schedule** instead of by plant need — "I water
   every 2 days" doesn't work because the plant's water usage changes
   with temperature, humidity, growth stage, and pot size
2. **Not letting the medium dry between waterings** — roots need a
   wet-dry cycle. Constantly wet roots can't absorb oxygen, leading
   to root suffocation and eventually root rot
3. **Too-small drainage holes** or no drainage at all — water pools
   at the bottom of the pot
4. **Medium too dense** — heavy soil without enough perlite holds too
   much water and doesn't drain
5. **Pot too large for the plant** — a seedling in a 5-gallon pot
   can't drink the water fast enough, so the medium stays wet for
   days

### How to fix overwatering

**Immediate:**
1. Stop watering. Don't water again until the pot feels significantly
   lighter and the top inch of soil is dry
2. Increase airflow around the base of the plant (fan pointed at
   soil surface) to speed evaporation
3. If the plant is in a saucer, empty the saucer — don't let the
   pot sit in standing water

**If root rot has developed** (mushy brown roots, foul smell from
the soil):
1. Unpot the plant carefully
2. Remove dead, brown, mushy root material
3. Repot in fresh, well-draining medium (add 25–30% perlite)
4. Water lightly and don't water again until the medium is mostly dry
5. See our [root health guide](/guides/root-health-guide)

**Prevention:**
- **Add perlite:** mix 25–30% perlite into your soil for better
  drainage and aeration
- **Use fabric pots:** they drain from the sides and prevent water
  from pooling (see our
  [fabric pots guide](/guides/fabric-pots-vs-plastic))
- **Lift the pot** before watering — only water when it feels light
- **Stick your finger in the soil** — if the top 1–2 inches are
  dry, water. If moist, wait

## Underwatering: causes and fixes

### Why underwatering happens

1. **Forgot to water** — life happens
2. **Medium is hydrophobic** — very dry peat-based soil repels water.
   The water runs down the sides of the pot and out the bottom
   without actually soaking the root zone
3. **Pot too small** — a large plant in a small pot drinks the water
   in hours. In hot tents, a rootbound plant in a 1-gallon pot may
   need water twice daily
4. **High VPD / low humidity** — the plant transpires faster in dry,
   warm conditions

### How to fix underwatering

**Immediate:**
1. Water slowly and thoroughly until runoff appears from the bottom
2. If the soil is hydrophobic (water runs through instantly without
   being absorbed), water in stages: add a small amount, wait 5
   minutes for absorption, add more. Repeat until the medium is
   evenly moist
3. Adding a drop of unscented dish soap to the watering can helps
   break surface tension in hydrophobic soil (a single application)

**The plant should recover in 1–4 hours.** Underwatering recovery
is fast — the leaves perk up visibly within an hour of proper
watering. If the plant doesn't recover within 4 hours, the problem
isn't underwatering.

**Prevention:**
- **Check the pot weight daily** — daily lift-and-feel becomes
  automatic
- **Set a phone reminder** if you're forgetful
- **Automated watering** (drip irrigation on a timer, Blumat stakes,
  or similar) eliminates the problem entirely
- **Mulch the surface** — a layer of straw or cover crop on the soil
  surface reduces evaporation by 30–50%

## The wet-dry cycle

Healthy roots need alternating wet and dry conditions. The cycle:

1. **Water thoroughly** — until you see 10–20% runoff from the
   bottom (in coco or soil). This ensures the entire root zone is
   saturated and flushes accumulated salts
2. **Wait** — let the medium dry. The top inch should feel dry to
   the touch, and the pot should feel significantly lighter than when
   watered
3. **Water again** — when the dry criteria are met

### How long should the dry period last?

| Situation | Typical dry period |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Seedling in a big pot | 4–7 days |
| Veg plant in 3-gallon | 2–4 days |
| Flowering plant in 5-gallon | 1–3 days |
| Large plant in small pot | 12–24 hours |
| Coco coir | 1–2 days (coco shouldn't fully dry) |
| DWC/hydro | N/A — roots are always submerged (but aerated) |

**Coco is different:** coco coir should not fully dry out. It's
designed for frequent, small waterings (many growers fertigate 2–4
times daily in coco). The wet-dry cycle in coco is more of a
"moist-to-slightly-drier" cycle. See our
[coco growing guide](/guides/coco-coir-growing).

## Watering technique

### How much water per feeding?

Water until you see 10–20% runoff. If you put in 1 gallon and get
back about 1–2 cups of runoff, that's about right. Runoff serves
two purposes:
- Ensures the entire medium is saturated (not just the top layer)
- Flushes accumulated salts from the root zone

### Watering patterns

- **Water slowly and evenly** — don't dump water in one spot and
  call it done. Circle the pot, wetting the entire surface
- **Water the edges too** — roots grow outward. Watering only the
  center keeps roots concentrated and the outer medium salty
- **Don't water the stem directly** — keep the stem base dry to
  prevent stem rot (Fusarium)

## Common mistakes

1. **Watering on a fixed schedule** — water when the plant needs it,
   not when the calendar says. Lift the pot
2. **Using a pot without drainage holes** — this is a death sentence.
   Standing water at the bottom rots roots within days
3. **Misting leaves as a substitute for watering** — misting raises
   humidity but doesn't hydrate the roots. Water the soil
4. **Watering a tiny seedling in a huge pot** — the seedling uses
   0.5% of the water, and the rest sits stagnant. Start in a smaller
   container or water only a small circle around the seedling until
   it grows
5. **Confusing overwatering droop with underwatering droop** — the
   pot-lift test takes 2 seconds and gives the right answer every
   time. Heavy pot = too wet. Light pot = too dry
