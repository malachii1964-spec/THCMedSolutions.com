A healthy cannabis plant in veg should show visible daily growth —
new leaves unfolding, stem lengthening, the canopy expanding
noticeably from day to day. If your plant looks the same today as it
did three days ago, something is wrong. It's alive, but it's not
thriving.

Slow growth is frustrating because the symptoms are vague — the plant
doesn't look sick, it just isn't growing. The cause is usually one
of a small number of common problems, all fixable.

## The diagnostic checklist

Work through these in order. The most common cause is listed first.

### 1. Overwatering

**The #1 cause of slow growth in soil and coco.**

When the root zone stays wet for too long, roots can't access oxygen.
Without oxygen, root function slows or stops. Without root function,
the plant can't absorb nutrients or water efficiently, even though
both are present. Growth stalls.

**How to check:**
- Lift the pot. Is it heavy? The medium is still wet
- Stick your finger 2 inches into the soil. Is it moist? Don't water
  yet
- Has the surface been wet for more than 3 days? You're overwatering

**The fix:** water less frequently. Let the medium dry until the pot
feels light and the top inch of soil is dry before watering again.
In veg, most plants in soil need water every 2–4 days. If you're
watering daily in soil, that's almost certainly too often (coco is
different — daily watering is normal in coco).

### 2. Root problems

**Directly related to overwatering, but also includes other root
issues.**

- **Root rot:** brown, slimy, smelly roots. Growth stalls
  completely, leaves droop even when the soil is wet
- **Rootbound:** roots circling the bottom of the pot. The plant
  has outgrown its container. Growth slows as root mass can no longer
  expand
- **Cold roots:** the pot is sitting on a cold floor (concrete,
  tile). Root-zone temperature below 60°F dramatically slows nutrient
  uptake

**How to check:**
- Gently remove the plant from its pot and inspect the roots (if
  in soil or coco). Healthy roots = white, firm. Problem roots =
  brown, mushy, or wrapped in circles at the bottom
- Check the floor temperature where the pot sits. If it's below
  60°F, elevate the pot on a plant riser

**The fix:**
- Root rot: let soil dry, add hydrogen peroxide (1 mL/L) to next
  watering, improve drainage, add beneficial microbes
- Rootbound: transplant to a larger container
- Cold roots: elevate pot, add a heat mat under the pot (set to
  72°F), or move to a warmer spot

### 3. Insufficient light

**The plant can only grow as fast as its energy supply allows.**

Low light levels limit photosynthesis, which limits growth. A plant
under a single 23W CFL grows, but slowly. A plant under a 300W LED
at appropriate height grows much faster because it's producing more
energy per hour.

**How to check:**
- Is the light on 18/6 (or 20/4 for autos)? A shorter light
  schedule during veg reduces daily light integral (DLI), which
  slows growth
- Is the light far enough away? Too far = not enough PPFD at the
  canopy. Too close = light stress (different symptom — see below)
- What is the light wattage per square foot? Below 30W/sq ft of
  actual draw, growth will be slow. Target 40–50W/sq ft for good
  veg growth

**The fix:**
- Lower the light (if no signs of light stress) or increase
  intensity (if dimmable)
- Run 18/6 during veg (not 12/12 or shorter)
- If using CFL or weak LED, upgrade to a proper grow light

### 4. pH problems

**The most common "hidden" cause of slow growth.**

When the root-zone pH is outside the optimal range, the plant can't
absorb certain nutrients — even though those nutrients are present in
the soil. Growth slows because the plant is functionally nutrient-
starved despite being fed.

**Optimal pH ranges:**

| Medium | Optimal pH |
|--------|-----------|
| Soil | 6.3–6.5 |
| Coco | 5.8–6.0 |
| Hydro | 5.8 |

**How to check:**
- Test the pH of your feed water. If you're not pH-ing your water,
  this is very likely the problem
- Test runoff pH (water that drains out the bottom of the pot after
  watering). If runoff pH is below 5.5 or above 7.0, the root zone
  is out of range

**The fix:** pH every watering to the optimal range for your medium.
If the root zone has drifted far, flush with correctly pH'd water at
1.5× pot volume, then resume normal feeding.

See our [pH guide](/guides/ph-lockout-and-nutrient-problems) for the
full diagnostic.

### 5. Temperature too low

**Cannabis growth rate is directly temperature-dependent.**

| Temp range | Growth rate |
|-----------|-------------|
| Below 60°F | Very slow — plant is essentially dormant |
| 60–68°F | Slow — metabolic rate is reduced |
| 68–78°F | Optimal — fastest vegetative growth |
| 78–85°F | Still good (with adequate CO₂ and humidity) |
| Above 85°F | Heat stress — growth slows, leaves curl |

**How to check:** measure the temperature AT canopy level (not at the
ceiling or floor of the tent). Night-time temperatures during lights-
off are often 10–15°F lower than daytime — if daytime is 72°F and
nighttime is 58°F, the plant is spending 6 hours per day in slow-
growth conditions.

**The fix:** add a small heater during lights-off (on an Inkbird
controller set to 65°F minimum). Insulate the tent floor if it's on
concrete.

### 6. Nutrient deficiency or lockout

**The plant needs building materials to grow. If they're not
available, it stops building.**

Slow growth without visible deficiency symptoms (yellowing, spotting)
suggests early-stage nutrient shortage or lockout. The plant hasn't
progressed to visual symptoms yet but has already slowed growth to
conserve what it has.

**Common early-stage deficiencies that cause slow growth:**
- **Nitrogen** — the primary growth nutrient. Insufficient N =
  slow vegetative growth before the classic yellowing appears
- **Phosphorus** — especially in cold root zones. Causes dark,
  stunted growth
- **Calcium** — slow new growth, eventual brown spots on new leaves

**How to check:**
- Are you feeding nutrients at all? In soil, the medium provides
  nutrients for 3–4 weeks. After that, you need to feed
- Is the EC/PPM of your nutrient solution appropriate for the
  growth stage? Seedlings: 0.4–0.8 EC. Veg: 1.0–1.6 EC
- Check pH (above) — lockout looks like deficiency

**The fix:** ensure proper pH first (lockout mimics deficiency).
Then check that your nutrient solution provides appropriate NPK
for the growth stage.

### 7. Genetics

**Some plants are just slow.**

Before panicking:
- Indica-dominant strains often grow slower in early veg than
  sativas, then catch up
- Some seeds are simply weaker performers — this is normal genetic
  variation
- Autoflowers can have a slow start (first 2 weeks) before
  explosive growth

**How to check:** if all environmental factors are correct and the
plant still seems slow, compare it to its siblings (if growing
multiple plants). If one is slow and the others aren't, it's likely
genetics.

**The fix:** give it time. If the plant is healthy (green leaves,
no symptoms), it may just be a slower phenotype. If it's
significantly behind its siblings after 3–4 weeks with no
explanation, consider culling it and replacing with a stronger
plant.

## Quick diagnostic flow

Ask these questions in order:

1. **Am I watering too often?** → Let soil dry before watering
2. **What do the roots look like?** → Check for rot, binding, cold
3. **Is the light adequate?** → 30–50W/sq ft, 18/6, proper height
4. **Am I pH-ing my water?** → Target 6.3–6.5 (soil) or 5.8–6.0
   (coco/hydro)
5. **Is the temperature right?** → 68–78°F at canopy, min 65°F at
   night
6. **Am I feeding?** → After week 3–4 in pre-amended soil, you need
   to start feeding
7. **Is it just genetics?** → Compare to siblings, give it time

## Common mistakes

1. **Adding more nutrients when the plant is slow** — the instinct
   is to feed more, but slow growth is usually NOT about
   insufficient feeding. It's overwatering, pH, or environment.
   Adding more nutrients to a locked-out or overwatered plant makes
   it worse
2. **Reducing light "to help the plant recover"** — unless there's
   light stress (bleaching, leaf curl), reducing light reduces the
   plant's energy supply and further slows growth. The plant usually
   needs MORE light, not less
3. **Transplanting a stressed plant** — if the plant is already
   struggling, transplant shock makes it worse. Fix the underlying
   problem first, transplant after recovery
4. **Panic-changing multiple things at once** — adjust one variable,
   wait 3–5 days to see the response, then adjust the next. Changing
   light + nutrients + watering simultaneously makes it impossible
   to identify which change helped
