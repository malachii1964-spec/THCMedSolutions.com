Regular cannabis seeds produce roughly 50% male and 50%
female plants. Only females produce smokeable flower, so half
your seeds are destined for the trash bin (or breeding
projects). Feminized seeds solve this — they produce female
plants 99%+ of the time.

The technique: chemically force a female plant to produce
male pollen sacs. That pollen contains only X chromosomes
(because the donor was female: XX). When it pollinates
another female (also XX), the resulting seeds are XX —
female.

This isn't genetic modification. It's hormonal manipulation
that tricks the plant into expressing male flowering
structures. The genetics are unchanged — the offspring are
genetically female with a female plant's traits.

## The chemistry: ethylene and sex expression

Cannabis sex expression is influenced by the plant hormone
ethylene. Female flowers require ethylene signaling. When
you block ethylene production or reception, the plant
defaults to male flower development even if it's genetically
female.

**Two chemicals block ethylene in cannabis:**

### Silver thiosulfate (STS)

STS is a solution of silver nitrate and sodium thiosulfate
that blocks ethylene receptors at the cellular level. When
sprayed on female cannabis branches, it prevents those
branches from developing female flowers — they produce male
pollen sacs instead.

**STS is the industry standard** for feminized seed
production. It's reliable, fast-acting, and produces
consistent results.

### Colloidal silver (CS)

Colloidal silver is a suspension of silver nanoparticles in
water. Silver ions interfere with ethylene production
(rather than blocking the receptor like STS). When sprayed
repeatedly on female cannabis, it induces male flower
development.

**CS is the home-grower method** because it's available
commercially and doesn't require mixing chemicals. However,
it requires more applications, takes longer, and is less
reliable than STS.

### Comparison

| Factor | STS | Colloidal silver |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| Mechanism | Blocks ethylene receptor | Inhibits ethylene production |
| Applications needed | 2–3 sprays | Daily sprays for 10–14 days |
| Reliability | 95–99% reversal | 70–90% reversal |
| Availability | Must mix from components | Buy premade (30+ PPM) |
| Shelf life | Mix fresh each use (hours) | Months (store dark) |
| Cost | ~$20 for chemicals | $15–30 for premade bottle |

## Making STS solution

### Components

- **Silver nitrate (AgNO3):** available from chemical
  suppliers or photography supply stores. Technical grade
  is sufficient
- **Sodium thiosulfate (Na2S2O3):** available as
  "hypo" from photography suppliers or aquarium
  dechlorinator crystals

### Mixing procedure

**Solution A (0.1M silver nitrate):**
- Dissolve 1.7g silver nitrate in 100mL distilled water

**Solution B (0.1M sodium thiosulfate):**
- Dissolve 2.5g sodium thiosulfate pentahydrate in 100mL
  distilled water

**Working STS solution:**
- Mix Solution A into Solution B at a 1:4 ratio (20mL A
  into 80mL B)
- Always add silver nitrate INTO thiosulfate, not the
  reverse — the thiosulfate complexes the silver ions
  immediately, forming the stable silver thiosulfate complex
- The working solution should be slightly yellow and clear.
  If it turns brown or cloudy, the ratio is off — discard
  and remix
- **Use within 24 hours.** STS degrades rapidly. Mix only
  what you need for each application

### Safety

- **Silver nitrate stains skin, clothing, and surfaces
  permanently brown/black.** Wear nitrile gloves and
  eye protection. Work over a disposable surface
- **Store solutions A and B separately** — they're stable
  individually for months (dark bottles, room temperature)
- **Dispose of unused STS responsibly.** Don't pour silver
  solutions down the drain — silver is toxic to aquatic
  organisms. Absorb with paper towels, bag, and trash

## The reversal process

### Step 1: Select the donor plant

Choose the female plant whose genetics you want in the pollen.
This is the plant that will be reversed — it will produce
male pollen sacs instead of female flowers on the treated
branches.

**Selection criteria:**
- Healthy, vigorous female in veg or early pre-flower
- Proven genetics (a clone from a plant you've already
  flowered and verified is female)
- Traits you want to preserve: terpene profile, potency,
  structure, yield

**Critical:** do NOT reverse a plant that has shown
hermaphroditic tendency. Herming under stress is genetic —
reversing a stress-hermie produces feminized seeds that are
also prone to herming.

### Step 2: Spray the donor

**STS method:**
1. Start spraying 1–3 days BEFORE flipping to 12/12
2. Spray the branches you want to reverse — thoroughly wet
   all leaf and stem surfaces until dripping
3. Spray again 10–14 days later (when pollen sacs should
   be visibly forming)
4. Some growers do a third spray at day 21 for insurance

**Colloidal silver method:**
1. Use 30+ PPM colloidal silver (higher is better — 40–60
   PPM is common for cannabis reversal)
2. Spray target branches daily starting 1–3 days before
   the flip to 12/12
3. Continue daily spraying for 10–21 days after the flip,
   until you see pollen sacs forming
4. Drench the tissue — light misting isn't enough. The
   silver ions need to penetrate the tissue

**Spray coverage:** you can reverse the entire plant or
select branches. Reversing select branches (while the rest
of the plant flowers normally as female) is possible but
increases the risk of self-pollination — the female flowers
on the same plant will get pollinated by the reversed
branches.

### Step 3: Pollen sac development

After treatment, male pollen sacs develop on the reversed
branches:

| Timeline | What you see |
|----------|-------------|
| Day 7–10 | Swelling at nodes — round clusters instead of pistils |
| Day 14–21 | Recognizable pollen sacs (look identical to male plant sacs) |
| Day 21–28 | Sacs yellow and swell — approaching maturity |
| Day 28–35 | Sacs crack open and release pollen |

**The pollen from reversed females is viable and genetically
XX.** It functions identically to male pollen — it just
carries only female chromosomes.

### Step 4: Collect the pollen

When sacs begin to yellow and crack:

1. **Cut the branch** with mature sacs
2. **Place it in a paper bag** or over a sheet of glass/
   parchment paper
3. **Let it dry for 24–48 hours** in a dark, dry space.
   The sacs will open and release pollen
4. **Collect the pollen.** Tap the branch to release pollen
   onto the paper. Use a small brush to gather it into a
   sealed container

**Pollen storage:**
- Fresh pollen: viable for 2–3 days at room temperature
- Refrigerated (sealed container with desiccant): viable
  for 1–2 weeks
- Frozen (sealed, desiccant, deep freeze): viable for
  months to years (some viability loss over time)
- **Moisture kills pollen.** Always store with a small
  desiccant packet. Never introduce moisture into the
  storage container

### Step 5: Pollinate the recipient

The recipient is a separate, untreated female plant in
flower. She will produce the feminized seeds.

**Timing:** pollinate at day 14–21 of flower. Earlier and the
flowers aren't receptive enough; later and the seeds won't
have time to mature before harvest.

**Method:**
1. Turn off all fans in the grow space (prevents pollen
   from spreading to unintended plants)
2. Isolate the recipient from other flowering females if
   possible (separate room or tent)
3. Use a small paintbrush to apply pollen directly to
   the pistils (white hairs) of the buds you want to seed
4. Alternatively, put pollen in a plastic bag, place the
   bag over a branch, and shake gently — the pollen coats
   the flowers inside the bag
5. **Wait 2–4 hours** for pollination to occur
6. Mist the plant with plain water after pollination to
   neutralize any remaining airborne pollen (water kills
   pollen instantly)
7. Resume normal growing operations

### Step 6: Seed development

After pollination:
- **Day 1–7:** calyxes (seed pods) begin swelling
- **Day 7–14:** seeds forming inside, visible bulging
- **Day 14–28:** seeds hardening, developing tiger-stripe
  pattern
- **Day 28–35+:** seeds mature — hard shell, dark
  coloration, visible pattern

**Seeds need 4–6 weeks to mature after pollination.** This
means pollinating at day 14 of an 8-week strain gives you
6 weeks of seed development — just enough. Pollinating later
than day 21 risks immature seeds at harvest.

### Step 7: Harvest seeds

At harvest, the seeded buds will contain mature seeds among
the flower material:

1. Dry the seeded buds normally (hang dry 7–10 days)
2. Break apart the dried buds gently — seeds fall out or
   are easily picked out
3. Mature seeds are hard, dark (gray/brown/tiger-striped),
   and resist pressure when squeezed between fingers
4. Immature seeds are pale, soft, and crush easily —
   discard these
5. Store mature seeds in a cool, dark, dry place. Viable
   for 3–5+ years

## Safety of reversed plant material

**Never smoke, vaporize, or consume any plant material that
was sprayed with STS or colloidal silver.** Silver compounds
are toxic. The reversed branches and any flower they produce
are waste — not consumable product.

- Dispose of all reversed plant material
- The POLLEN is safe (it's plant reproductive material, not
  tissue that absorbed silver)
- The SEEDS produced from that pollen are safe (they grew
  on the untreated recipient plant)
- The RECIPIENT plant's flower (the unseeded portions) is
  safe to consume — it was never sprayed

## Whole-plant vs. branch reversal

**Whole-plant reversal:** spray the entire plant. It produces
only pollen sacs, no female flower. Maximizes pollen
production but sacrifices the entire plant.

**Branch reversal:** spray specific branches while the rest
flowers normally. Produces pollen from the reversed branches
and female flower from the untreated branches. Risk: the
reversed branches may pollinate the female branches on the
same plant, seeding your harvest unintentionally.

**Separate-plant method (recommended):** reverse one plant
entirely. Grow the recipient in a separate tent or space.
Bring pollen to the recipient with a brush. Zero risk of
accidental pollination on other plants.

## Common mistakes

1. **Using weak colloidal silver** — 10–20 PPM CS is too
   dilute for reliable reversal. Use 30 PPM minimum, 40–60
   PPM preferred. Low-concentration CS produces partial
   reversals (some sacs, some pistils, unreliable pollen)
2. **Reversing hermie-prone genetics** — a plant that hermied
   under stress carries that tendency genetically. Feminized
   seeds from reversed hermies produce hermie-prone offspring.
   Only reverse stable female plants with no history of stress
   herming
3. **Pollinating too late** — seeds need 4–6 weeks to mature.
   Pollinating at week 5 of an 8-week strain gives only 3
   weeks — not enough for mature seeds. Pollinate by day 21
   of flower
4. **No fan shutdown during pollination** — pollen travels
   on air currents. If your fans are running and you have
   other flowering plants nearby, congratulations: you just
   seeded your entire grow. Shut off fans, isolate the
   recipient, and mist with water afterward
5. **Consuming reversed plant material** — STS-sprayed
   branches contain silver compounds. They are not safe to
   smoke or eat. Dispose of all treated plant material.
   Only the pollen and resulting seeds (on the untreated
   recipient) are safe
