You germinated your seed. The taproot is showing. Now what? The medium
you put that seed into for its first 5–10 days matters more than most
growers realize. The wrong starter medium causes damping off, drowning,
drying out, or a root system that never gets established.

This guide compares every common seed starting option, tells you which
one to pick for your setup, and walks through the gotchas that ruin
seedlings.

## The comparison

| Medium | Ease of use | Root visibility | pH control needed | Best for | Cost |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-------------------|----------|------|
| Rapid Rooters | Very easy | Yes (plugs are translucent) | No (pre-buffered) | Soil, coco, hydro — any setup | ~$0.30/plug |
| Jiffy pellets | Easy | Partial (peat mesh) | Minimal | Soil grows, beginners | ~$0.10/pellet |
| Rockwool cubes | Moderate | Yes (inert, clear view) | Yes (must pre-soak at 5.5) | Hydro, DWC, NFT, ebb-flow | ~$0.15/cube |
| Straight into soil | Easy | No | No (soil buffers) | Soil growers who don't want to transplant | Free |
| Coco plugs | Easy | Partial | Minimal | Coco growers | ~$0.25/plug |
| Perlite/vermiculite | Moderate | No | No | Budget option, bulk starts | Very cheap |

## Rapid Rooters (recommended for most growers)

Rapid Rooters are pre-formed sponge plugs made from composted organic
materials. They hold the perfect ratio of moisture to air for root
development and work in every growing medium.

### How to use them

1. Soak the Rapid Rooter in pH'd water (6.0–6.5) for 5 minutes
2. Squeeze out excess water — the plug should be moist but not dripping.
   If you squeeze it and water streams out, it's too wet
3. Drop the germinated seed (taproot down) into the pre-made hole, about
   ¼ inch deep
4. Pinch the top of the hole closed gently — don't pack it tight, just
   barely cover the seed
5. Place in a seedling tray with a humidity dome
6. Keep the dome on until the seedling has its first set of true leaves
   (usually 3–5 days after emergence)

### Why they work

- **Pre-buffered pH** — no conditioning required
- **Perfect moisture retention** — holds water without waterlogging
- **Air channels** — roots get oxygen even when the plug is moist
- **Universal compatibility** — transplant directly into soil, coco,
  hydro net pots, or any medium

### The one mistake

Over-watering. The plug holds moisture for 2–3 days. Growers who mist
the plug every few hours drown the seed. After the initial soak, don't
add water until the plug starts to feel lighter and the surface begins
to dry. For most environments, that's 2–3 days.

## Jiffy pellets (peat or coco)

Jiffy pellets are compressed peat (or coconut coir) discs that expand
when soaked in water. They're cheap, widely available, and work well for
soil grows.

### How to use them

1. Drop pellets into warm water — they expand in 5–10 minutes from a
   flat disc to a cylindrical plug
2. Squeeze out excess water gently
3. Make a small hole (¼ inch deep) in the top with a pencil or finger
4. Drop in the germinated seed, taproot down
5. Pinch the top closed
6. Place in a tray with humidity dome

### Pros and cons

**Good:**
- Cheapest option ($5 for 50+)
- Available at every garden center
- The mesh netting holds the pellet together during transplant —
  just drop the whole thing into the next pot
- Roots grow through the mesh into the surrounding medium

**Watch out for:**
- Peat pellets can be acidic (pH 5.0–5.5 before buffering). If your
  tap water is also acidic, the combined pH can lock out calcium and
  magnesium early
- The mesh netting can restrict root growth in fast-growing strains.
  Some growers peel the mesh off at transplant to avoid this — just
  be gentle with the root ball
- Peat dries out from the top while staying wet inside. Check moisture
  by weight (lift the pellet), not by touching the surface

### Coco Jiffy pellets

Coco-based Jiffy pellets (Jiffy-7C) avoid the pH issues of peat and
work better for coco grows. Same process, slightly more forgiving on
moisture management.

## Rockwool cubes

Rockwool is spun basite fiber — essentially stone wool. It's the
standard in commercial hydroponic operations because it's inert,
sterile, and consistent.

### How to use them

**The critical step: pre-soaking**

Rockwool cubes have a natural pH of 7.5–8.0. If you plant a seed
directly into a dry or water-soaked rockwool cube, the high pH will
lock out nutrients immediately. You must pre-soak.

1. Fill a container with pH'd water at **5.5** (yes, lower than your
   target — the rockwool needs to absorb acid to bring itself down)
2. Submerge the cubes for **at least 1 hour** (overnight is fine)
3. Do NOT squeeze the cubes after soaking — this destroys the internal
   fiber structure. Instead, lift the cubes out and let them drain
   naturally for 30 seconds
4. The cube should be evenly moist. Drop in a germinated seed (taproot
   down, ¼ inch deep), cover lightly with a torn-off piece of rockwool
   or a small piece of the same cube
5. Place in a tray, humidity dome on

### Why some growers swear by rockwool

- **Completely inert** — no nutrients, no pH buffering, no organic
  material. You control everything
- **Sterile** — no pathogens, no fungi, no damping-off risk from the
  medium itself
- **Consistent** — every cube is identical. No batch variation
- **Transparent** — roots grow visibly through the sides and bottom,
  so you can see exactly when they're ready to transplant

### Why other growers avoid it

- **pH conditioning is mandatory** — skip it and you'll kill seedlings
- **Not organic** — it's essentially glass fiber. Not compatible with
  living soil philosophy
- **Disposal** — rockwool doesn't decompose. It sits in landfills
  forever. This matters to growers who care about environmental impact
- **Overwatering risk** — rockwool holds a LOT of water. In a high-
  humidity environment with low airflow, it stays wet too long and roots
  suffocate

### Best for

Hydroponic systems where the cube drops into a net pot with hydroton.
The rockwool-to-net-pot transition is seamless and avoids any transplant
shock. For DWC, NFT, and ebb-and-flow systems, rockwool is the standard
for good reason.

## Straight into soil

The simplest method: put the seed directly in its final (or
intermediate) pot of soil.

### How to do it

1. Fill a small pot (solo cup or 1-gallon) with moist (not wet) soil
2. Make a hole ½ inch deep
3. Drop the germinated seed in, taproot down
4. Cover lightly with loose soil — don't pack it down
5. Mist the surface lightly. Don't water heavily — the soil around
   the seed should be damp, not saturated
6. Cover with a humidity dome (a clear plastic cup works perfectly for
   a solo cup grow)

### Why it works

No transplant shock. The seed germinates, establishes roots, and grows
in the same medium from day one. For growers who want simplicity and
aren't running hydro, this is the lowest-risk approach.

### Why it can fail

- **Overwatering** — the most common killer. A small seedling in a big
  pot can't drink fast enough to dry back the soil. The root zone stays
  wet for days, promoting damping off and root rot
- **Soil too hot** — some nutrient-rich soils (like Fox Farms Ocean
  Forest) can burn seedlings in the first 1–2 weeks. Use a milder
  seedling mix (Happy Frog, Light Warrior, or cut OF 50/50 with perlite)
  for the top 2 inches where the seed goes
- **No visibility** — you can't see the roots. You won't know if
  they're healthy, struggling, or rotting until the plant shows symptoms
  above ground

### The solo cup method

A classic for good reason:
1. Poke 4–6 drainage holes in the bottom of a solo cup
2. Fill with seedling-appropriate soil
3. Plant seed ½ inch deep
4. Water sparingly — only when the cup feels light when lifted
5. Transplant to a larger pot when roots emerge from the drainage holes
   (usually 10–14 days)

The small container forces faster dry-backs, which encourages root
growth and prevents overwatering.

## Choosing your medium: the decision tree

**Are you growing in hydroponics (DWC, NFT, ebb-flow)?**
→ Use rockwool cubes. Pre-soak at pH 5.5. Transplant cube directly
into net pot with hydroton.

**Are you growing in coco coir?**
→ Use Rapid Rooters or coco Jiffy pellets. Both transplant cleanly
into coco. Rockwool also works but adds an inert-medium step into an
organic-ish system.

**Are you growing in soil?**
→ Three good options:
- **Rapid Rooters** if you want to germinate separately and transplant
  (most flexibility, best visibility)
- **Jiffy pellets** if you want cheap and easy (watch pH with peat
  versions)
- **Straight into soil** if you want zero transplanting (use a solo
  cup, not a 5-gallon pot)

**Are you a first-time grower?**
→ Use Rapid Rooters or straight-into-soil in a solo cup. Both are
very forgiving. Avoid rockwool (pH conditioning is easy to mess up)
and avoid starting seeds in large pots (overwatering is almost
guaranteed).

## The universal seedling environment

Regardless of which starting medium you choose, the environment should
be:

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Temperature | 75–80°F (24–27°C) |
| Humidity | 70–80% RH (under a dome) |
| Light | Low — 100–200 PPFD, 18/6 schedule |
| Airflow | Minimal — dome cracked slightly for gas exchange |
| Watering | Mist only; medium should be moist, never wet |

Remove the humidity dome when the first true leaves appear (the serrated
ones, not the round cotyledons). Leaving it on too long promotes
stretching and increases damping-off risk.
