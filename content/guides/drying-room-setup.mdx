You spent months dialing in your grow. The buds are dense, frosty, and
smell incredible on the plant. Now comes the part most growers get wrong:
drying.

Bad drying is the #1 way to ruin a great harvest. Too fast and you get
hay-smelling, harsh flower that makes people cough. Too slow and you get
mold. This guide covers how to set up and manage a drying environment
that preserves everything you worked for.

## Why drying matters more than most growers think

When you chop a cannabis plant, the buds are 75–80% water. During drying,
you reduce that to 10–12%. How you remove that water determines:

- **Terpene preservation** — volatile terpenes evaporate at low
  temperatures. Fast drying at high temps = terpene loss = no smell
- **Chlorophyll breakdown** — chlorophyll breaks down slowly during a
  proper dry. Rush it, and the remaining chlorophyll makes the smoke
  harsh and green-tasting
- **Cannabinoid stability** — THCA and other cannabinoids are more
  stable in slow, controlled drying conditions
- **Mold risk** — too slow or too humid, and Botrytis or Aspergillus
  colonize the buds from the inside out

The ideal dry is a **slow, controlled evaporation over 7–14 days** in
a dark room with stable temperature and humidity.

## The environment targets

| Parameter | Target | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Temperature | 60–65°F (15–18°C) | Preserves terpenes, slows chlorophyll breakdown |
| Humidity | 55–62% RH | Slow enough for quality, fast enough to prevent mold |
| Airflow | Gentle, indirect | Moves moisture out without creating dry spots |
| Light | Complete darkness | Light degrades THC and terpenes |
| Duration | 7–14 days | Minimum 7, ideally 10–14 for peak flavor |

**The #1 rule:** 60/60 — aim for 60°F and 60% RH. It's the simplest
target to remember and produces excellent results. Anything in the
58–65°F and 55–62% range works.

### What happens at wrong temperatures

| Temp | Result |
|------|--------|
| Below 55°F | Drying stalls, mold risk increases |
| 60–65°F | Optimal — slow evaporation, terpene preservation |
| 70–75°F | Acceptable but faster — less terpene preservation |
| Above 80°F | Hay dry — terpenes evaporate, chlorophyll trapped |

### What happens at wrong humidity

| RH | Result |
|----|--------|
| Below 45% | Buds dry too fast — crispy outside, wet inside |
| 45–55% | Faster dry, acceptable quality for large harvests |
| 55–62% | Optimal — slow, even moisture removal |
| Above 65% | Mold risk — especially inside dense colas |

## Equipment you need

### The minimum setup (tent dry)

You can dry in your grow tent after harvest:

1. **Turn off the grow light** — complete darkness
2. **Run the exhaust fan on low** — just enough to cycle air gently
3. **Add a hygrometer** — monitor temp and RH constantly
4. **Hang branches** from the tent's horizontal bars or a drying line

This works for small harvests but has limitations — you can't grow your
next round while drying, and controlling temp/RH in a tent is harder than
a dedicated space.

### The dedicated drying room

For serious growers, a dedicated drying space:

- **Small room, closet, or cabinet** — a 4×4 closet works perfectly for
  a home grow harvest
- **Box fan or small oscillating fan** — aimed at the wall, not at the
  buds. You want air circulation, not a breeze on the flower
- **Dehumidifier or humidifier** — depending on your climate. Dry
  climates need a humidifier; humid climates need a dehumidifier
- **Small AC unit or heater** — to hold 60–65°F. In summer you may need
  cooling; in winter you may need a bit of heat
- **Data-logging hygrometer** — Govee WiFi sensors are cheap and let you
  monitor from your phone. Two sensors: one at bud level, one at floor
  level (humidity stratifies)

### Drying line or rack

**Hanging (recommended for most growers):**
- Hang whole branches or partial branches from a line, wire, or hanger
- Buds dry more evenly because the stem feeds moisture outward gradually
- Takes more space but produces better results

**Drying rack (for space-constrained):**
- Mesh drying racks (like the stackable round ones)
- Buds dry faster because they're trimmed from the stem
- Rotate buds daily to prevent flat spots and uneven drying
- Higher risk of over-drying — check buds twice daily

## The drying process

### Step 1: Harvest and trim (or not)

Two approaches:

**Wet trim:** trim fan leaves and sugar leaves off the buds immediately
after cutting, then hang the trimmed buds.
- Pros: faster, buds look cleaner sooner
- Cons: buds dry faster (less leaf protection), less terpene preservation
- Best for: dry climates, when you need faster drying

**Dry trim (recommended):** cut whole branches, remove only the large fan
leaves, and hang with sugar leaves still attached. Trim the sugar leaves
after drying.
- Pros: slower dry, better terpene preservation, leaves protect buds
- Cons: slower, more trimming work at the end
- Best for: most situations, especially humid climates

### Step 2: Hang the branches

- Leave 6–8 inches between branches for airflow
- Don't let buds touch each other or the wall
- Hang from sturdy line or wire (not string, which can cut into stems)
- Larger branches hang by the stem; smaller ones can be clipped to a
  hanger or drying line

### Step 3: Monitor and adjust

**Day 1–3:** humidity will spike as the buds release moisture. You may
need to increase exhaust or run a dehumidifier to stay in the 55–62%
range.

**Day 3–5:** the outer buds start feeling dry to the touch. Small buds
may be approaching done. Check stems — they should still bend, not snap.

**Day 5–7:** stems of smaller branches start to snap. Buds feel dry on
the outside but still have some give when squeezed.

**Day 7–14:** the "snap test" — small stems snap cleanly when bent.
Larger stems may still bend (that's fine — they take longer). Buds feel
dry on the outside with slight resistance when squeezed.

### Step 4: The snap test

The primary indicator that drying is complete:

- **Small stems snap** when bent (they don't fold or bend)
- **Larger stems crack** but may not snap completely
- **Buds feel dry** on the outside but not crispy
- **The bud springs back** slightly when squeezed (it's not completely
  dessicated)

If stems still bend without snapping, the buds aren't dry enough for
jarring. If buds crumble when touched, you've over-dried (you can still
recover somewhat during the cure, but some damage is done).

## Common mistakes

### 1. Drying too fast

The most common mistake. Signs: buds smell like hay or grass 3 days in,
outer surface is crispy while inside is still wet.

**Fix:** lower the temperature, reduce airflow, add a humidifier if RH
is below 50%.

### 2. Fan blowing directly on buds

Moving air is important. Wind on buds is not. Direct airflow creates
dry spots on the surface while the interior stays wet, and it strips
terpenes off the surface.

**Fix:** point fans at walls or the ceiling. The air should circulate
the room, not blow on the flower.

### 3. Not checking for mold

Dense colas can harbor mold inside even when the outside looks fine.
During days 3–7, gently squeeze the largest colas and check for any
mushy, dark, or gray spots inside.

**Fix:** if you find mold, remove the affected bud immediately and
increase airflow/reduce humidity. Inspect nearby buds — mold spreads.

### 4. Drying in light

Light (especially UV) degrades THC into CBN and breaks down terpenes.
The drying room should be dark. If you need to check buds, use a dim
green light briefly.

### 5. Ignoring temperature

Growers focus on humidity but forget temperature. A room at 78°F and
60% RH will produce worse buds than 62°F and 60% RH, even though the
humidity is identical. Temperature controls the speed and quality of
chlorophyll breakdown and terpene preservation.

## Budget vs. premium drying setup

| Item | Budget | Premium |
|------|--------|---------|
| Drying space | Grow tent (reused) | Dedicated closet/room |
| Climate control | Exhaust fan only | AC + dehumidifier |
| Monitoring | $10 thermo-hygrometer | Govee WiFi sensor × 2 |
| Hanging | String/wire | Drying line with clips |
| Trimming | Hand scissors | Trimming machine + detail scissors |
| **Total** | **$0–20** | **$150–400** |

You don't need premium equipment to dry well. A dark closet with a small
fan and a hygrometer produces excellent results. The expensive gear is
for consistency at scale — not for quality on a single harvest.
