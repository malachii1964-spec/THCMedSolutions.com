You buy a 10-pack of Blue Dream seeds. You pop all 10. You give them
identical soil, identical light, identical nutrients. By week 6,
you have 10 noticeably different plants — different heights, different
leaf shapes, different smells, different growth rates.

This isn't a quality control problem. It's genetics working as
intended. Each seed carries a unique combination of its parents'
traits, and that combination determines the phenotype — the
observable characteristics of the plant. Understanding this is the
difference between growing whatever the seed gives you and
intentionally selecting the best expression of a strain.

## Genotype vs. phenotype

### Genotype

The genotype is the plant's DNA — the complete set of genetic
instructions it inherited from its parents. You can't see the
genotype. It's the blueprint.

Every cannabis seed (from sexual reproduction) gets half its DNA
from the mother and half from the father. Because each parent has
their own mix of dominant and recessive alleles, every seed gets a
different combination. Even siblings — seeds from the same cross —
have different genotypes.

### Phenotype

The phenotype is what you can see, smell, and measure: height, leaf
shape, flower structure, terpene profile, potency, color, growth
speed, resistance to pests and disease.

**Phenotype = genotype + environment.**

The genotype sets the range of possibilities. The environment
determines where within that range the plant actually lands. A
genotype with the potential for purple coloration will only express
purple if nighttime temperatures drop below 60°F during flower. The
genetics are there, but the environment triggers the expression.

## Why seeds from the same pack differ

### F1 hybrids

Most commercial cannabis seeds are F1 hybrids — the first generation
cross between two different parent strains. F1 hybrids exhibit
"hybrid vigor" (faster growth, higher yield), but they're genetically
heterozygous — each seed carries different allele combinations from
the two parents.

Think of it like siblings in a human family. Same parents, but each
child gets a different shuffle of the deck. One might be tall with
dark hair, another average height with light hair. Same genetics
pool, different results.

### F2 and beyond

If you breed two F1 siblings, their offspring (F2) show even MORE
variation — the genetic diversity fans out further. This is why
"breeding" at home produces wildly inconsistent results unless you
understand selection and stabilization.

### IBL (Inbred Lines)

An IBL is a strain that has been selfed or sibling-crossed for many
generations (usually 6+). The genetic diversity narrows with each
generation until the plants are nearly identical. Seeds from a true
IBL produce consistent phenotypes.

**IBL examples:** Northern Lights, Skunk #1, Afghani — the old-school
"landrace-derived" strains that breeders stabilized over decades.

**Why most modern strains aren't IBLs:** the market rewards novelty.
Breeders cross trendy strains (Gelato × Wedding Cake, OG Kush ×
Cookies) to create exciting new combinations. These F1 crosses are
often sold as seeds after minimal testing, meaning high variation
between seeds.

### Feminized seeds

Feminization (using colloidal silver or STS to force a female plant
to produce pollen) doesn't eliminate phenotype variation. It
eliminates males, but the female-to-female cross still produces
genetically diverse offspring unless the parents were highly
stabilized.

### Clones: zero variation

The only way to guarantee identical phenotype is cloning. Every
clone is a genetic duplicate of the mother. Same genotype →
functionally same phenotype (environment still matters, but the
genetic component is locked in).

## Pheno hunting: finding the best plant

### What pheno hunting is

Pheno hunting is the process of growing multiple seeds of the same
strain, evaluating each plant, and selecting the best one as a
"keeper" — a mother plant that you'll clone for future grows.

Professional breeders grow hundreds or thousands of seeds to find
exceptional phenotypes. Home growers typically work with 4–10 seeds,
which is enough to find meaningful variation.

### The process

**Step 1: Germinate and grow all seeds**

Pop as many seeds as you can manage — 6–10 is a good number for a
home pheno hunt. Give them identical conditions: same medium, same
light, same nutrients. You want the ONLY variable to be genetics.

**Step 2: Observe during veg**

Note differences in:
- Growth rate (some will be noticeably faster)
- Stem thickness and structure
- Leaf shape (wide indica leaves vs. narrow sativa leaves)
- Node spacing (tighter spacing = more potential bud sites)
- Smell (rub the stem — some plants express terpenes even in veg)
- Response to training (some strains bend easier, some break)

**Step 3: Take clones before flipping to flower**

This is critical. Take 2–3 clones from EVERY plant before you flip
to 12/12. Label them carefully. These clones are your insurance — if
plant #7 turns out to be the best phenotype, you need its clones
alive and rooted.

If you don't take clones and the best plant is the one that gets
chopped at harvest — that phenotype is gone forever (unless you
re-veg, which is slower and less reliable).

**Step 4: Evaluate during flower**

This is where the important differences appear:

| Trait | What to look for |
|-------|-----------------|
| Flower structure | Dense, tight buds vs. airy, loose buds |
| Trichome coverage | Frosty, resin-coated buds are higher potency |
| Terpene profile | Smell the buds in week 5–7. Which plant smells best to YOU? |
| Yield | Count bud sites, estimate final weight |
| Stretch | How much height increase after flip? Extreme stretch can be a problem |
| Pest/disease resistance | Which plants handled the same environment with fewer problems? |
| Maturation speed | Some phenos finish a week earlier or later. Faster can be better |

**Step 5: Harvest and test**

After harvest, dry, and cure:
- Weigh each plant separately
- Smoke each one separately and note effects
- Assess bag appeal (appearance, density, trim quality)
- Compare terpene profiles (smell and taste)

**Step 6: Select your keeper**

The keeper is the phenotype that best matches YOUR priorities:
- Maximum yield? Keep the biggest producer
- Best flavor? Keep the best-smelling pheno
- Easiest to grow? Keep the one that handled problems best
- Best high? Keep the one with your preferred effect

There's no universal "best" — it depends on what you value.

### Tips for effective pheno hunting

- **Label everything.** Use numbered plant tags from day one. When
  you're evaluating 8 plants at week 7 of flower, you need to know
  exactly which one is which
- **Take notes.** A grow journal is essential for pheno hunting.
  Record observations weekly for each numbered plant
- **Don't judge too early.** A plant that's ugly in veg can produce
  the best buds. A plant that looks great at week 3 of flower can
  underperform at harvest. Wait for the full cycle
- **Keep the clones alive.** If you lose the clones, you lose the
  ability to reproduce the keeper. Maintain them under low light in
  small containers until you've selected

## Selecting for specific traits

### For yield

- Choose plants with tight node spacing and many bud sites
- Plants that respond well to topping (producing equal-sized branches)
  tend to yield more than plants with strong apical dominance
- Thick stems indicate strong nutrient transport, which supports
  larger buds

### For potency

- Trichome density is the primary visual indicator. Frosty plants
  with trichomes extending onto stems and fan leaves are typically
  more potent
- Large trichome heads (visible with a jeweler's loupe) indicate
  more cannabinoid production per trichome

### For terpenes

- Smell during late veg and early flower. Plants that express strong
  terpenes early tend to have the most complex profiles at harvest
- The terpene profile you smell during growth is what you'll taste
  when smoking — select for the aroma you enjoy

### For ease of growing

- Mold and pest resistance are largely genetic. If one plant
  develops powdery mildew while its siblings don't (same
  environment), the clean plants have better resistance genetics
- Plants that maintain an even canopy without aggressive training
  are easier to manage in future grows

## Common mistakes

1. **Not taking clones before flower** — you can't go back. If the
   best pheno gets harvested without clones, it's gone. Always clone
   before flipping
2. **Judging by veg appearance only** — veg and flower performance
   don't always correlate. The prettiest veg plant can produce
   mediocre buds
3. **Too few seeds** — popping 2 seeds isn't a pheno hunt. You need
   at least 4–6 to see meaningful variation. 10+ is better
4. **Different growing conditions** — if plant #3 gets more light
   than plant #7, you can't compare them fairly. Keep conditions
   identical
5. **Selecting by only one trait** — the highest-yielding pheno
   might taste terrible. Evaluate multiple traits and prioritize
   based on your goals
