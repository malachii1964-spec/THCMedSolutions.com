Many growers start because they want relief — from pain that
prescriptions don't touch, from insomnia that sleep hygiene can't fix,
or from anxiety that they'd rather not manage with benzodiazepines.
Cannabis can help with all of these. But "smoke weed" is not a medical
protocol. What you consume, how you consume it, and how much matters
enormously.

This guide covers the practical intersection of cannabis and wellness:
which compounds address which symptoms, what to grow for specific
goals, dosing strategy, and what the research actually supports vs.
what's bro science.

**Important:** this is educational information, not medical advice.
Cannabis affects everyone differently. If you're managing a medical
condition, work with a healthcare provider who understands cannabis.
For medical card information in New York State, see our
[medical card page](/medical-card).

## How cannabis affects the body

Cannabis compounds interact with the **endocannabinoid system (ECS)**
— a network of receptors (CB1 and CB2) found throughout the brain,
nervous system, immune system, and organs. The ECS regulates pain
perception, mood, sleep, appetite, inflammation, and immune response.

- **CB1 receptors** — concentrated in the brain and central nervous
  system. THC binds here. This is where the psychoactive effects
  (euphoria, relaxation, altered perception) originate, plus effects
  on pain, sleep, and appetite
- **CB2 receptors** — concentrated in the immune system and peripheral
  tissues. CBD interacts here (indirectly). This is where
  anti-inflammatory and immune-modulating effects originate

### The key compounds

| Compound | Type | Primary effects |
|----------|------|----------------|
| THC (Δ9-THC) | Cannabinoid | Psychoactive, pain relief, appetite stimulation, antiemetic, sleep aid (dose-dependent) |
| CBD | Cannabinoid | Anti-anxiety, anti-inflammatory, anticonvulsant, muscle relaxant. NOT psychoactive |
| CBN | Cannabinoid | Mildly sedating, produced when THC degrades (aged cannabis, amber trichomes) |
| CBG | Cannabinoid | Anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, antibacterial. Emerging research |
| Myrcene | Terpene | Sedating, muscle relaxant, enhances THC absorption |
| Linalool | Terpene | Anxiolytic (anti-anxiety), analgesic (pain relief) |
| Caryophyllene | Terpene | Anti-inflammatory (binds CB2 directly), pain relief |
| Limonene | Terpene | Mood-elevating, anti-anxiety, antidepressant |

## Cannabis for sleep

### What works

**High-THC + high-myrcene strains** are the most consistently effective
for sleep. Myrcene is the dominant terpene in most indica-leaning
strains and has documented sedating properties. Combined with THC
(which reduces time to fall asleep), this is the classic "indica
knockout" formula.

**CBN (cannabinol)** is THC that has degraded through age, heat, or
light. It's mildly sedating on its own and synergizes with THC for
sleep. You can increase CBN content by:
- Harvesting later (more amber trichomes = more CBN)
- Allowing cured flower to age longer (THC converts to CBN over months)

**Linalool-dominant strains** add calming, anxiolytic effects that
help with the "can't turn off my brain" component of insomnia.

### What to grow for sleep

Look for indica or indica-dominant hybrids with these characteristics:
- High myrcene content (earthy, musky, herbal aroma)
- THC in the 18–25% range (more isn't necessarily better for sleep —
  very high THC can cause anxiety that counteracts the sedation)
- Linalool or caryophyllene as secondary terpenes

**Strain suggestions:** Granddaddy Purple, Northern Lights, Purple
Kush, Hindu Kush, 9 Pound Hammer, OG Kush (the right pheno)

### Dosing for sleep

- **Start low:** 2.5–5mg THC (a single small hit from a pipe or
  vaporizer, or a carefully dosed edible)
- **Take 30–60 minutes before bed** (inhaled: 10–15 minutes before;
  edibles: 60–90 minutes before)
- **Too much is counterproductive** — high doses of THC can cause
  racing thoughts, anxiety, and restlessness. The dose that helps you
  sleep is lower than the dose that gets you very high
- **Tolerance builds:** THC for sleep loses effectiveness over time.
  Consider cycling (5 days on, 2 days off) or using CBD-dominant
  products during off-days

### What the research says

A 2022 systematic review in the Journal of Clinical Medicine found
that cannabis reduces self-reported sleep latency (time to fall asleep)
and increases self-reported sleep quality, particularly with THC-
dominant products. However, long-term daily use may suppress REM sleep,
and withdrawal after heavy use can temporarily worsen insomnia.

## Cannabis for chronic pain

### What works

Pain management with cannabis is the best-supported medical application,
with the most research backing.

**THC** — acts on CB1 receptors in the central nervous system to reduce
pain signal transmission. Effective for neuropathic pain (nerve damage),
inflammatory pain (arthritis, autoimmune), and cancer-related pain.

**CBD** — anti-inflammatory via multiple pathways (not just ECS).
Effective for inflammatory pain conditions. Does not provide the acute
pain relief that THC does but reduces the underlying inflammation that
causes pain.

**Caryophyllene** — the only terpene that directly binds to CB2
receptors, producing anti-inflammatory effects similar to CBD.
Strains high in caryophyllene (spicy, peppery aroma) are particularly
suited for pain management.

**The combination** — THC + CBD + caryophyllene is the most effective
profile for pain. The entourage effect means the combination works
better than any component alone.

### What to grow for pain

Ideal pain-management strains have:
- Moderate THC (15–22%) — enough for analgesic effect without
  overwhelming psychoactivity
- Meaningful CBD (1–5% or higher for CBD-dominant strains)
- High caryophyllene and/or myrcene

**Strain suggestions:**
- **Balanced (THC:CBD roughly 1:1):** Harlequin, Cannatonic, ACDC,
  Pennywise
- **THC-dominant with caryophyllene:** OG Kush, GSC, Bubba Kush,
  Original Glue
- **CBD-dominant (minimal high):** Charlotte's Web, Ringo's Gift,
  Harle-Tsu

### Dosing for pain

- **Inhaled (fastest onset, 1–3 hours duration):** good for
  breakthrough pain. Start with one hit, wait 10 minutes, add more
  if needed
- **Edibles (slower onset, 4–8 hours duration):** better for sustained
  pain management. Start at 2.5–5mg THC, increase by 2.5mg increments
  on subsequent sessions until you find effective dose
- **Topicals (localized, no psychoactive effect):** apply directly to
  the painful area for localized relief. THC and CBD topicals don't
  enter the bloodstream in significant amounts, so no high — useful
  for joint pain, muscle soreness, arthritis
- **Tinctures (under the tongue, 15–45 min onset):** good balance of
  speed and duration. Sublingual absorption bypasses the liver, giving
  more predictable dosing than edibles

### What the research says

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine concluded
in their 2017 report that there is "substantial evidence" that cannabis
is effective for chronic pain in adults. Multiple meta-analyses since
have confirmed efficacy, particularly for neuropathic pain. Cannabis
is not a replacement for all pain medication, but it can reduce opioid
dependence when used as an adjunct.

## Cannabis for anxiety

### The paradox

Cannabis and anxiety have a complex relationship. At low doses, THC
can be profoundly anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing). At high doses, THC
can cause or worsen anxiety and panic. This dose-dependent effect is
the most important thing to understand.

**CBD is consistently anxiolytic** across all doses studied. It does
not cause anxiety at any dose, and it counteracts THC-induced anxiety.
For anxiety-prone individuals, CBD-dominant products are the safest
starting point.

### What to grow for anxiety

**CBD-dominant strains (safest starting point):**
- Minimal THC, maximum CBD
- No risk of THC-induced anxiety
- Grow: ACDC, Charlotte's Web, Harle-Tsu, Cannatonic (high-CBD pheno)

**Balanced THC:CBD strains (moderate approach):**
- Equal parts THC and CBD
- CBD buffers the anxiety-inducing potential of THC
- Grow: Harlequin, Pennywise, Critical Mass CBD

**THC-dominant with limonene/linalool (experienced users):**
- Limonene and linalool are the most anxiolytic terpenes
- Strains smelling of citrus and lavender tend to be mood-elevating
  without anxiety
- Grow: Strawberry Cough (famous for social anxiety relief),
  Tangie, Lavender

### Dosing for anxiety

- **Microdosing is key** — 1–2.5mg THC is the therapeutic range for
  anxiety in many people. This is much less than a typical recreational
  dose
- **CBD can be dosed higher** — 10–25mg CBD is a common therapeutic
  dose for anxiety with no psychoactive effect
- **Avoid high-THC edibles** — edibles metabolize through the liver
  into 11-hydroxy-THC, which is more potent and more anxiety-inducing
  than inhaled THC. Start at 2.5mg or less
- **Strain matters more for anxiety than for any other condition** —
  the wrong terpene profile (high myrcene + very high THC) can
  exacerbate anxiety even at moderate doses

### What the research says

A 2019 study in the Brazilian Journal of Psychiatry found that 300mg
CBD significantly reduced anxiety in a simulated public speaking test
compared to placebo. Multiple studies confirm CBD's anxiolytic effects
through 5-HT1A serotonin receptor activation. For THC, the evidence
is biphasic: low doses reduce anxiety, high doses increase it.

## Making your own medicine

If you're growing for wellness, you control the entire chain from
genetics to final product. This lets you:

1. **Choose the right genetics** — select strains with the cannabinoid
   and terpene profile that matches your condition
2. **Control the harvest timing** — harvest earlier (more THC, more
   cerebral) or later (more CBN, more sedating) based on your goal
3. **Process appropriately** — make tinctures for precise dosing,
   edibles for sustained effect, or simply vaporize flower for fast
   onset
4. **Dose accurately** — homegrown with known genetics lets you build
   a consistent, repeatable dosing protocol

## The bottom line

Cannabis for wellness works best when you treat it like any other
tool: understand what it does, start conservatively, track your
results, and adjust based on data rather than guessing. A grow journal
(see our [journal guide](/guides/grow-journal)) is just as useful for
tracking your consumption and response as it is for tracking your
plant's growth.
