Defoliation — removing fan leaves from a cannabis plant — is
one of the most debated techniques in growing. Proponents claim
it increases light penetration, airflow, and bud development.
Critics say the plant needs those leaves for photosynthesis.
Both are right, depending entirely on WHEN you do it.

The technique itself is simple: remove leaves. The strategy
is knowing which leaves, how many, and when. Get the timing
wrong and you slow the plant at the worst possible moment.

## The three defoliation windows

### Window 1: Late veg (days 1–7 before flip)

**Purpose:** open up the canopy before flower to establish
bud site access to light.

**How much:** remove 20–30% of fan leaves. Focus on:
- Large fan leaves in the center of the canopy that are
  shading bud sites directly below them
- Overlapping leaves where two or more fans are stacked
- Any leaf that's blocking light from reaching a developing
  branch tip

**What to leave:**
- All sugar leaves (the small leaves growing from bud sites)
- Fan leaves on the outer edges of the canopy (they're not
  blocking anything)
- The top fan leaf on each branch (it's the primary solar
  panel for that branch)

**Why this window works:** the plant has 1–2 weeks before
flower demands peak energy. It recovers from the defoliation
during the stretch phase while simultaneously developing new
bud sites. Removing shading leaves now means those bud sites
are exposed to light from day one of flower.

### Window 2: Day 21 of flower (the "schwazze" point)

**Purpose:** the most impactful defoliation. By day 21, the
stretch is over, bud sites are established, and the plant is
transitioning from vegetative growth to pure flower production.
Removing fan leaves now ensures maximum light reaches every
bud for the remaining 5–6 weeks of flower.

**How much:** this is the aggressive window. Remove 50–70%
of fan leaves:
- All fan leaves in the interior of the canopy
- All fan leaves shading bud sites
- Leaves below the top 2/3 of the plant (these bud sites
  won't develop into quality flower anyway)

**What to leave:**
- Sugar leaves (always)
- The top 1–2 fan leaves on each cola (energy supply)
- Any fan leaf that isn't blocking a bud site (no reason to
  remove it)

**Why this window works:** the stretch is done — the plant
won't produce new vertical growth. Bud site positions are
locked. Every photon that hits a bud site instead of a fan
leaf contributes directly to flower weight. The next 4–5 weeks
are the bulking phase, and light penetration is everything.

**The recovery:** the plant redirects energy from leaf
production to flower production. New fan leaf growth is minimal
after week 3 of flower in most strains. The leaves you remove
won't be replaced, so every removal is permanent canopy
modification.

### Window 3: Lollipopping (days 14–21 of flower)

**Purpose:** remove ALL growth (branches and leaves) from the
bottom 1/3 of the plant. This isn't selective defoliation —
it's removing entire lower branches that won't produce quality
flower.

**How much:** everything below the canopy line:
- Small branches that are shaded by the main canopy
- "Sucker" branches that grew after topping
- All leaves and bud sites below the light penetration zone

**Why this window works:** the plant has established its
flowering structure during the stretch. By day 14–21, you can
see which branches made it to the light and which are hopelessly
shaded. Removing the lowers redirects energy to the tops.

**What lollipoping produces:** fewer, larger, denser top buds
instead of many top buds plus a collection of small, airy
lowers (larf). The total dry weight is often similar, but the
quality composition shifts from 60% tops + 40% larf to 90%
tops + 10% larf.

## When NOT to defoliate

### Seedling stage (never)

Seedlings have minimal leaf area. Every leaf is critical for
photosynthesis and growth. Removing even one fan leaf from a
seedling slows growth measurably.

### Early veg (weeks 1–3)

The plant is building its energy capture system. Removing
leaves during rapid vegetative development slows canopy
expansion and delays everything downstream.

### Weeks 4–6 of flower (the bulking window)

**This is the most expensive time to defoliate.** The plant is
converting light energy into flower mass at its maximum rate.
Every fan leaf removed reduces the plant's photosynthetic
capacity during the period when photosynthesis matters most
for yield.

Removing leaves during the bulking window directly reduces
the energy available for bud development. Any yield that those
leaves would have powered is lost permanently.

### Late flower (weeks 7–8+)

The damage from defoliation at this stage is less severe
(the plant is finishing, not building), but there's no benefit
either. The bud sites are already formed and sized. Removing
leaves now doesn't increase light to buds in a meaningful way
because the growth phase is ending.

### When the plant is stressed

Defoliation is a stress event. Combining it with other stresses
(transplant shock, nutrient lockout, pest damage, extreme heat)
compounds the stress and can stall growth for a week or more.
Address the existing stress first, then defoliate during a
recovery period.

## How much is too much

### The energy balance

Every fan leaf is a solar panel. Remove too many and the
plant's energy income drops below what's needed to fill the
buds. The goal is to remove JUST enough leaves to open
light paths to shaded bud sites — not to strip the plant bare.

### Guidelines by method

| Stage | Approach | Max removal |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Pre-flip (late veg) | Selective thinning | 20–30% of fan leaves |
| Day 21 of flower | Heavy defoliation | 50–70% of fan leaves |
| Lollipopping | Complete lower removal | Bottom 1/3 of plant |
| During bulking (wk 4–6) | Leave it alone | 0% |

### The strain factor

Some strains handle heavy defoliation better than others:

- **Heavy defoliators (leafy indicas):** dense-canopy strains
  with broad, overlapping fan leaves benefit most from
  defoliation. Without it, interior bud sites receive almost
  no light
- **Light defoliators (open sativas):** naturally airy canopy
  structure. Less shading means less need for removal. Heavy
  defoliation on sativas can slow growth without proportional
  benefit
- **Autoflowers:** defoliate minimally or not at all. Autos
  don't have time to recover from heavy stress. Stick to
  removing only leaves directly blocking bud sites

## The alternative: leaf tucking

Instead of removing a leaf, tuck it behind a branch or under
the canopy. This exposes the bud site to light without
removing the leaf's photosynthetic capacity.

**When to tuck instead of cut:**
- During veg (preserve all possible leaf area)
- When the leaf isn't large enough to shade significantly
- When you've already removed your target percentage and
  another bud site is still shaded
- On autoflowers (low-stress alternative to removal)

## Common mistakes

1. **Defoliating during the bulking window (weeks 4–6)** —
   this is when the plant needs maximum energy for bud
   development. Removing fan leaves during bulking directly
   reduces yield. Save defoliation for the pre-flip and day
   21 windows
2. **Stripping the plant bare** — total defoliation (removing
   ALL fan leaves) removes the plant's entire energy income.
   Some experienced growers use this technique (schwazzing),
   but it requires strain-specific knowledge and perfect
   environmental control. For most growers, removing 50–70%
   at the right time is safer and nearly as effective
3. **Defoliating stressed plants** — if the plant is already
   fighting nutrient lockout, heat stress, or pest damage,
   defoliation adds more stress. Fix the problem first
4. **Removing sugar leaves** — sugar leaves (the small leaves
   growing from bud sites) are photosynthesizing directly
   into the bud. Never remove them during the grow. Trim them
   at harvest
5. **Defoliating autoflowers heavily** — autos can't extend
   their vegetative period to recover. Heavy defoliation
   reduces their limited leaf area during a fixed life cycle.
   Tuck leaves or remove only the most obstructive fans
