Every new grower asks the same question: "How much should I feed?" The
honest answer is less than you think. Overfeeding causes more problems
than underfeeding, and the best feeding schedule is one that responds to
what the plant tells you — not what the bottle says.

## The universal feeding principle

**Start at 50% of the manufacturer's recommendation.** Then adjust based
on what the plant shows you. If tips stay green and growth is vigorous,
you can creep up. If leaf tips burn, back off. This applies to every
nutrient line — General Hydroponics, Jack's, Canna, Advanced Nutrients,
Fox Farm, Athena, all of them.

Nutrient companies want you to use more product. Their feed charts are
marketing, not agronomy.

## Understanding N-P-K

Every nutrient label shows three numbers: N-P-K (Nitrogen-Phosphorus-
Potassium). These are the macronutrients — the big three.

| Nutrient | Primary role | When the plant wants most |
|----------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Nitrogen (N) | Leaf and stem growth | Veg through early flower |
| Phosphorus (P) | Root development, flower formation | Mid-to-late flower |
| Potassium (K) | Water regulation, enzyme activation | All stages, peak in flower |

**Veg** = higher N, moderate P and K
**Flower** = lower N, higher P and K

That's the entire concept. Everything else is details.

## The feed-water-feed cycle

For soil and coco growers, alternating between feed and plain water
prevents salt buildup:

- **Feed** (nutrients at your target strength)
- **Water** (plain pH'd water)
- **Feed** (nutrients)
- **Water** (plain pH'd water)
- Repeat

In living soil, you don't feed at all — the biology does it. In hydro,
the reservoir is always nutrient-charged, so this cycle doesn't apply.

## Stage-by-stage feeding guide

### Seedling (Week 1–2)

**Don't feed.** Seedlings get everything they need from the seed and
the starting medium. If you're in hot soil (like Fox Farm Ocean Forest),
don't add anything for 3–4 weeks. In coco or inert media, start at
1/4 strength in the second week.

### Early veg (Week 3–4)

- 25–50% of recommended dose
- Higher N formula (veg nutrient)
- EC/PPM target: 400–600 PPM / 0.8–1.2 EC
- pH: 6.0–6.5 (soil) / 5.5–6.0 (coco/hydro)

### Late veg (Week 5–6)

- 50–75% of recommended dose
- Same veg formula, can add cal-mag if in coco or RO water
- EC/PPM target: 600–900 PPM / 1.2–1.8 EC
- Watch for tip burn — the first sign of overfeeding

### Transition / Early flower (Week 7–8)

- Switch to bloom formula (higher P-K, lower N)
- 50–75% of recommended dose
- EC/PPM target: 800–1100 PPM / 1.6–2.2 EC
- Many growers make the mistake of dropping N entirely — plants still
  need nitrogen in early flower for stretch growth

### Peak flower (Week 9–12)

- Full bloom formula at 75–100% recommended dose
- This is when the plant can handle the most nutrition
- EC/PPM target: 1000–1400 PPM / 2.0–2.8 EC
- Add PK booster (if using one) during weeks 3–5 of flower only
- Monitor runoff — if output EC is much higher than input, back off

### Late flower / Ripening (Week 13–15)

- Taper back to 50–75%
- Some growers drop to plain water (see our
  [flushing guide](/guides/flushing-facts-and-myths) for the science)
- Reduce nitrogen further — yellowing fan leaves in late flower is
  natural senescence, not a deficiency

## Measuring what matters: EC and pH

If you only buy two tools, buy a pH pen and an EC/TDS meter.

### pH

| Medium | Target range |
|--------|-------------|
| Soil | 6.0–6.8 (sweet spot: 6.3–6.5) |
| Coco coir | 5.5–6.5 (sweet spot: 5.8–6.0) |
| Hydro (DWC, NFT) | 5.5–6.0 (sweet spot: 5.8) |

Outside these ranges, nutrients lock out even if they're present in the
solution. pH problems look like deficiencies but adding more nutrients
makes them worse.

### EC (Electrical Conductivity)

EC measures total dissolved salts. Higher EC = stronger feed. Use it to:

1. **Mix consistently** — same EC every time, regardless of water source
2. **Monitor runoff** — if runoff EC is 50%+ higher than input EC, salts
   are building up
3. **Catch overfeeding early** — before tip burn appears

## Common nutrient mistakes

### 1. Following the bottle chart exactly

Manufacturer charts assume perfect conditions — strong light, perfect
temperature, CO2 supplementation. In a typical home grow, 50–75% is right.

### 2. Stacking too many products

Base nutrients (A+B or 3-part) cover 90% of what the plant needs. Cal-mag
in coco is the one necessary addition. Everything else — bloom boosters,
root stimulators, sugars, enzymes — is marginal at best.

**The diminishing returns stack:**

1. Base nutrients (essential)
2. Cal-mag in coco/RO water (essential for those media)
3. Silica (genuinely useful for stem strength)
4. Everything else (nice to have, not necessary)

### 3. Feeding the same strength throughout

Plants have different appetites at different stages. A seedling fed at
full veg strength will burn. A plant in peak flower fed at seedling
strength will starve.

### 4. Ignoring runoff

If you never check what comes out the bottom of the pot, you're flying
blind. A 30-second runoff EC check after watering tells you whether salts
are accumulating.

### 5. Chasing deficiencies with more nutrients

When you see yellow leaves or spots, the instinct is to add more
nutrients. But the most common cause is pH lockout, not deficiency. Check
pH first, always.

## Budget feeding: Jack's 321

If you want the simplest, cheapest effective feeding program:

- **Jack's 321:** 3.6g Jack's Part A + 2.4g calcium nitrate + 1.2g
  Epsom salt per gallon. That's it — one formula, seed to harvest.
  Adjust strength by stage (50% veg, 75% flower, taper late).

Cost: roughly $0.03 per gallon. Compare that to $1–2 per gallon for
bottled nutrients.

## The bottom line

1. Start at 50% of the bottle chart
2. Alternate feed and water in soil/coco
3. Monitor pH religiously — it's the #1 cause of nutrient problems
4. Check runoff EC to catch salt buildup
5. Less is more — a slightly underfed plant recovers in days; a burned
   plant needs weeks

The best growers aren't the ones who feed the most. They're the ones who
feed just enough.
