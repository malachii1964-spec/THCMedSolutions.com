Low-stress training is the highest-return technique in growing: zero cost, no recovery time, and yield gains of 40 %+ over an untrained plant under the same light. You're not cutting anything — you're bending the plant flat so every bud site becomes a top.

## Why it works: apical dominance

Cannabis pours energy into whatever tip sits highest, hormonally suppressing everything below it. Bend that main tip horizontal and the suppression signal collapses — every side branch responds as if it were the main cola. One plant becomes a dozen tops of equal rank.

## What you need

- Soft plant ties or garden wire (never bare string — it cuts stems as they thicken)
- Drilled holes around the pot rim, binder clips, or ground staples as anchors
- 10 minutes, twice a week

## The technique

1. **Start early** — around node 4–6, while stems still bend like rubber. Woody stems snap.
2. **Bend the main stem horizontal** and anchor it near the pot rim. Gentle arc, not a kink.
3. **As side branches rise to become the new "top," bend them outward too.** Think spokes of a wheel radiating from the stem.
4. **Re-tie every few days.** The plant fights back within 24–48 hours, curling tips skyward — that upward curl tells you it's healthy and responding.
5. **Stop adding new bends about a week into flower.** Stems harden fast after the flip; from then on only maintain existing ties.

## If a stem snaps

Don't panic, and don't cut. Splint it: straighten the break, wrap with tape, and support it against a stake. Cannabis heals stem breaks in about a week and the knuckle that forms is stronger than the original stem.

## LST + topping: the standard indoor recipe

| Week | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Veg wk 2–3 | Top above node 4–5 |
| Veg wk 3 | Begin LST on the two resulting mains |
| Veg wk 4–6 | Spread all secondaries outward weekly; canopy fills the pot footprint |
| Flip week | Final adjustment, then hands off |

The result is a flat table of 8–16 evenly-lit tops — the shape every efficient indoor grow converges on.

## Common mistakes

- **Tying too tight.** Leave a finger's width of slack; stems double in girth by harvest.
- **Bending dry plants.** Water the day before training — hydrated stems flex, dry ones snap.
- **Chasing perfection in flower.** After the stretch ends, the shape is what it is. Late aggressive bending stresses the plant when it can least afford it.
