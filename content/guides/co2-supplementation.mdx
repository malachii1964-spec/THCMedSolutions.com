CO₂ is the most overhyped and misunderstood input in cannabis
growing. Yes, increasing CO₂ from ambient (420 PPM) to 1200–1500
PPM can increase yields 20–30%. But that number comes with a
massive asterisk: CO₂ supplementation only delivers that return
when EVERY other variable — light intensity, temperature, VPD,
nutrients, and genetics — is already optimized. Adding CO₂ to a
setup that's limited by light is like putting racing fuel in a
car with flat tires.

This guide covers the real science, the real math, and the honest
assessment of whether CO₂ makes sense for your grow.

## The photosynthesis bottleneck

### How CO₂ drives growth

Photosynthesis uses CO₂ + water + light energy to build
sugars (plant biomass). The simplified equation:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

CO₂ is one of the raw inputs. More CO₂ = more raw material =
faster photosynthesis — up to a point.

### The saturation curve

Photosynthesis rate vs. CO₂ concentration follows a saturation
curve:

| CO₂ level (PPM) | Photosynthesis rate | Notes |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| 200 | ~60% of max | Below ambient — CO₂-starved |
| 420 (ambient) | ~75% of max | Normal outdoor/indoor level |
| 800 | ~90% of max | Moderate supplementation |
| 1200 | ~95% of max | Optimal supplementation for most cultivars |
| 1500 | ~98% of max | Diminishing returns begin |
| 2000+ | ~99% | No practical benefit, higher cost, safety risk |

**The critical insight:** going from 420 to 1200 PPM gives
you roughly a 20% increase in photosynthetic rate. Going from
1200 to 1500 gives only 3% more. And going above 1500 gives
almost nothing while increasing cost and danger.

### The prerequisite chain

CO₂ is only the bottleneck when everything else is sufficient.
The chain of requirements, in order:

1. **Light > 800 PPFD.** Below 800 PPFD, the plant can't use
   additional CO₂ — light is the bottleneck, not CO₂. Most home
   grows run 600–800 PPFD. At that intensity, CO₂
   supplementation provides minimal benefit
2. **Temperature 80–85°F.** CO₂-enriched plants photosynthesize
   faster, which generates more heat internally. They PREFER
   slightly higher temperatures. Running CO₂ at 75°F wastes
   most of the potential
3. **VPD 1.2–1.6 kPa.** Higher temperature + CO₂ requires
   adjusted VPD. If your VPD is wrong, stomata close and CO₂
   can't enter the leaf regardless of ambient concentration
4. **Nutrients at full strength.** Faster growth needs more
   nutrients. Under-feeding a CO₂-enriched plant creates
   deficiencies faster than normal
5. **Strong genetics.** High CO₂ amplifies what the plant can
   do. A low-yielding phenotype at 420 PPM is still a
   low-yielding phenotype at 1500 PPM. It just gets there
   faster

If any link in this chain is weak, CO₂ supplementation returns
minimal benefit for its cost.

## CO₂ delivery methods

### Compressed CO₂ tank + regulator

**How it works:** a steel cylinder of compressed CO₂ (the same
type used for draft beer or paintball) feeds through a pressure
regulator and flow meter, dispensing a controlled amount of CO₂
into the grow space.

**Components:**
- 20 lb or 50 lb CO₂ tank ($80–150 for the tank, $15–30 to
  refill)
- CO₂ regulator with flow meter ($40–100)
- CO₂ monitor/controller with PPM sensor ($100–300)
- Solenoid valve + timer (included in most controllers)

**Advantages:**
- Precise control — set it to exactly 1200 PPM
- No heat or moisture added to the room
- No combustion byproducts
- Reliable and predictable

**Disadvantages:**
- Heavy tanks need regular refilling (every 2–4 weeks for a
  4×4 space at 1200 PPM)
- Initial equipment cost: $200–400 total
- Requires a sealed room (CO₂ leaks out through ventilation)

**Best for:** dedicated grow rooms that can be sealed. The most
common setup for serious home growers.

### CO₂ burners (natural gas or propane)

**How it works:** burns natural gas or propane to produce CO₂
(and water vapor and heat).

**Advantages:**
- Simple operation — burn fuel, get CO₂
- Lower per-PPM cost than tank refills for large spaces

**Disadvantages:**
- Produces significant heat (a burner in a 4×4 tent is
  unmanageable)
- Produces water vapor (increases humidity)
- Open flame in a grow space (fire risk)
- Combustion byproducts (carbon monoxide, ethylene)
- Requires ventilation for safety — but ventilation removes CO₂

**Best for:** large commercial grows (10×10+) with industrial
ventilation and climate control. NOT recommended for home grows.

### CO₂ bags (mycelium fermentation)

**How it works:** bags of mycelium slowly produce CO₂ through
biological fermentation. Hang the bag near the canopy and it
passively releases CO₂.

**Advantages:**
- Cheap ($20–30 per bag)
- No equipment, no electricity, no maintenance
- Safe — no compressed gas, no combustion

**Disadvantages:**
- Minimal CO₂ output. A single bag produces roughly 500–700 ml
  of CO₂ per day — not enough to meaningfully raise PPM in
  any space larger than a 2×2 closet
- Uncontrollable — you can't set a target PPM
- Attracts fungus gnats (the mycelium is a food source)

**The honest assessment:** CO₂ bags don't produce enough CO₂
to make a measurable difference in most grow spaces. The
marketing claims are based on best-case sealed-closet scenarios
that don't apply to tents with active ventilation. Save the $30.

### DIY sugar + yeast fermentation

Similar to CO₂ bags but homemade: sugar water with yeast in
a sealed container, releasing CO₂ through a tube.

**The honest assessment:** produces even less CO₂ than
commercial bags. The setup frequently fails (yeast dies, mold
grows, leaks develop). Not worth the effort.

## Sealed room vs. ventilated tent

This is the fundamental decision for CO₂ supplementation:

### Why CO₂ requires a sealed room

In a ventilated tent, the exhaust fan replaces all air every
1–3 minutes. Any CO₂ you add is exhausted before it can
accumulate. Running CO₂ in a ventilated tent is literally
pumping gas out the exhaust.

**To maintain elevated CO₂, you need:**
- The room sealed — no active exhaust during CO₂ delivery
- Climate control that doesn't rely on ventilation (AC for
  cooling, dehumidifier for humidity)
- A CO₂ controller that cycles between dosing (exhaust off)
  and ventilation (exhaust on, CO₂ off) based on PPM readings

### The tent grower's dilemma

Most home growers use tents with inline fans for cooling and
humidity control. Sealing the tent for CO₂ means:
- You need an AC unit (can't rely on exhaust cooling)
- You need a standalone dehumidifier (can't rely on exhaust
  for humidity removal)
- The added equipment cost often exceeds the value of the
  CO₂ yield increase

**The math:** a mini-split AC ($300–600) + dehumidifier ($150)
+ CO₂ tank + regulator + controller = $600–1200 in equipment.
A 20% yield increase on a 4×4 tent (adding maybe 2 oz to a
10 oz harvest) doesn't justify that investment unless you're
running continuous cycles.

## Target PPM by stage

If you do supplement CO₂:

| Stage | Target PPM | Light intensity needed |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Seedling/clone | 420 (ambient — don't supplement) | 200–400 PPFD |
| Vegetative | 800–1000 | 600–800 PPFD |
| Early flower (weeks 1–3) | 1000–1200 | 800–1000 PPFD |
| Mid flower (weeks 4–6) | 1200–1500 | 1000–1200 PPFD |
| Late flower (weeks 7+) | 800–1000 (taper down) | 800–1000 PPFD |

**CO₂ only during lights on.** Plants don't photosynthesize in
darkness — CO₂ delivered during the dark period is wasted. Run
CO₂ on a timer or controller that stops dosing when lights turn
off.

## Temperature adjustment with CO₂

CO₂-enriched plants tolerate and prefer higher temperatures:

| CO₂ level | Optimal temperature |
|-----------|-------------------|
| 420 PPM (ambient) | 75–80°F |
| 800–1000 PPM | 78–82°F |
| 1200–1500 PPM | 80–85°F |

If you supplement CO₂ but keep temperatures at 75°F, you're
getting maybe 50% of the potential benefit. The higher
temperature is part of the package.

## Safety

CO₂ is an asphyxiant at high concentrations:

- **1500 PPM:** target grow room level. Safe for brief
  human exposure but above outdoor norms
- **5000 PPM:** 8-hour occupational exposure limit (OSHA).
  Extended time in a room at this level causes headaches and
  drowsiness
- **40,000+ PPM:** immediately dangerous. Loss of
  consciousness within minutes
- **A leaking 50 lb CO₂ tank in a sealed bedroom can reach
  dangerous levels.** Never store CO₂ tanks in small sealed
  rooms. Ensure the space is ventilated when you're inside

**CO₂ monitor with alarm:** if you're running CO₂
supplementation, a monitor that alarms above 5000 PPM in the
workspace is a safety essential.

## The honest assessment for home growers

### CO₂ probably doesn't make sense if:

- Your light is below 800 PPFD (most sub-$300 LEDs)
- You grow in a ventilated tent (the CO₂ leaves through the
  exhaust)
- You're growing 1–4 plants (the yield increase doesn't
  justify the equipment cost)
- You haven't optimized VPD, feeding, and training first
  (CO₂ is the last optimization, not the first)

### CO₂ might make sense if:

- You have a dedicated sealed grow room (not a tent)
- Your light exceeds 800 PPFD across the canopy
- You already have AC and dehumidification
- VPD, feeding, and training are dialed in
- You're running continuous cycles and want to maximize
  return per square foot

### The "poor man's CO₂"

Fresh air exchange IS CO₂ supplementation. Outdoor air
contains 420 PPM of CO₂. A sealed tent with plants actively
consuming CO₂ drops below ambient — sometimes to 200–300 PPM.
At 200 PPM, you're losing 20% of photosynthetic potential.

**Running your exhaust fan brings fresh 420 PPM air into the
tent.** This is free CO₂ supplementation from ambient to
ambient — and for most home growers, maintaining ambient (not
depleting it) provides more benefit than trying to go above
ambient.

## Common mistakes

1. **Adding CO₂ before maxing out light** — if your PPFD is
   below 800, the plant can't use more CO₂. Add more light
   first. CO₂ without sufficient light is wasted
2. **Running CO₂ bags in a ventilated tent** — the bag
   produces trivial CO₂, and the fan exhausts it in seconds.
   Double waste of money
3. **Running CO₂ during lights off** — no photosynthesis
   happens in darkness. CO₂ during dark period is wasted gas
4. **Not adjusting temperature for CO₂** — CO₂-enriched plants
   need 80–85°F to fully benefit. At 75°F, you're getting half
   the return
5. **Forgetting that CO₂ requires a sealed room** — any
   ventilation during dosing period removes the CO₂ you just
   paid for. You can't supplement CO₂ and run an exhaust fan
   simultaneously
