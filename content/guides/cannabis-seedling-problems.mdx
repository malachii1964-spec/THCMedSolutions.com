Seedlings are the most fragile stage of a cannabis plant's
life. A mature plant can shrug off a missed watering, a pH
swing, or a temperature spike. A seedling can die from any of
them. But the good news: most seedling problems are caused by
the same 3–4 mistakes, and nearly all are fixable if you catch
them early.

## Problem 1: Stretching (tall, thin, falling over)

**What it looks like:** the seedling grows a long, thin stem
(2+ inches) before producing its first real leaves. The stem
is too weak to support the plant and it falls over.

**Cause:** insufficient light or light too far from the
canopy. The seedling is stretching toward the light source.

**Fix:**
- Lower the light or increase intensity. Seedlings need
  200–400 PPFD at canopy level
- If the stem is already stretched, gently mound soil around
  the stem up to the cotyledons (first round leaves) to
  provide support. Cannabis stems buried in soil will produce
  adventitious roots
- Add a small fan for gentle airflow — stem movement
  strengthens the stem over time

**Prevention:** set your light at the correct height before
germination. For LED quantum boards, start at 24–30 inches
above the canopy at 40–50% power.

## Problem 2: Damping off (seedling collapses at soil line)

**What it looks like:** the stem constricts and turns brown/
mushy at the soil surface. The seedling topples over. The
constriction looks like someone pinched the stem at the base.

**Cause:** a fungal infection (Pythium, Fusarium, or
Rhizoctonia) that attacks the soft stem tissue at the soil
line. Triggered by:
- Overwatering (constantly wet surface)
- Poor air circulation
- Cool temperatures with high humidity
- Contaminated soil or reused medium

**Fix:** once a seedling has damped off, it's almost always
too late — the stem tissue is destroyed. Focus on prevention
for the next seed.

**Prevention:**
- Don't overwater — the soil surface should dry between
  waterings. Water around the seedling, not directly on the
  stem base
- Ensure air circulation with a gentle fan
- Use clean, quality seed-starting medium (not garden soil)
- Maintain 75–80°F soil temperature
- A light dusting of cinnamon on the soil surface has mild
  antifungal properties

## Problem 3: Yellowing cotyledons (first round leaves)

**What it looks like:** the cotyledons (the first small, round
leaves) turn yellow and may drop off.

**When it's normal:** cotyledons naturally yellow and drop
after the first 2–3 sets of true leaves develop. The plant has
used the stored energy in the cotyledons and no longer needs
them. This is expected and not a problem.

**When it's a problem:** if cotyledons yellow BEFORE the first
true leaves develop (days 5–10), something is wrong:
- **Overwatering:** roots can't breathe, plant can't absorb
  nutrients. Let soil dry slightly between waterings
- **No nutrients in the medium:** if growing in an inert
  medium (perlite, coco without amendments), the seedling has
  no nutrient source after cotyledon reserves are spent. Begin
  feeding at 25% strength
- **pH too far off:** if soil pH is below 5.5 or above 7.5,
  nutrient uptake is impaired even if nutrients are present.
  Check and adjust pH of water to 6.2–6.5

## Problem 4: Helmet head (seed shell stuck on cotyledons)

**What it looks like:** the seedling emerges but the seed
shell is stuck on the cotyledons, preventing them from opening.
The cotyledons are trapped inside the shell.

**Cause:** seed planted too shallow (not enough soil friction
to strip the shell during emergence), dry air causing the
shell to harden, or a tough seed coat.

**Fix:**
1. Mist the stuck shell with water to soften it
2. Wait 12–24 hours — often the shell falls off on its own
   once softened
3. If it's still stuck after 24 hours, gently hold the stem
   with one hand and use tweezers to carefully peel the shell
   off. Be EXTREMELY gentle — the cotyledons inside are
   delicate
4. If a thin membrane (seed coat) is still stuck to the
   cotyledons after removing the shell, mist it and gently
   peel with tweezers

**Prevention:** plant seeds ¼–½ inch deep (not on the surface)
and maintain humidity above 60% during germination.

## Problem 5: Curling leaves (up or down)

### Leaves curling upward (edges folding up like a taco)

**Cause:** heat stress or light stress. The leaves are folding
to reduce surface area exposed to light/heat.

**Fix:**
- Raise the light 4–6 inches
- Reduce light intensity by 10–20%
- Check temperature — if above 82°F, improve ventilation
- Ensure humidity is above 60% (low humidity + heat compounds
  the stress)

### Leaves curling downward (edges folding down, "clawing")

**Cause:** overwatering (most common in seedlings) or nitrogen
toxicity (dark green, waxy leaves with downward curl).

**Fix:**
- If overwatering: let the medium dry before watering again.
  Seedlings in large pots are especially vulnerable — the pot
  holds more water than the tiny root system can use
- If nitrogen toxicity: flush with plain pH'd water and reduce
  nitrogen in the next feeding. This is rare in seedlings
  unless you're using very hot soil

## Problem 6: Very slow growth (stunted)

**What it looks like:** the seedling is alive but barely
growing — same size for a week or more when it should be
doubling in size every few days.

**Common causes:**
- **Root problems:** overwatering, root rot, or root-bound
  conditions. Check drainage and watering frequency
- **Cold temperatures:** below 68°F, seedling growth slows
  dramatically. Use a heat mat if room temperature is low
- **pH lockout:** nutrients in the soil are unavailable because
  pH is too high or too low. Check and adjust pH
- **Compacted soil:** dense, clay-heavy soil suffocates roots.
  Use a light, airy seed-starting mix
- **Too much or too little light:** below 200 PPFD, growth is
  slow. Above 600 PPFD, seedling stress slows growth

**Fix:** identify which factor is off (usually temperature or
watering) and correct it. Seedling growth should resume within
3–5 days of fixing the issue.

## Problem 7: Purple or red stems

**What it looks like:** the stem and petioles (leaf stems) are
purple or reddish instead of green.

**When it's genetics:** some strains naturally have purple
stems. If the plant is otherwise healthy and growing normally,
purple stems are just genetics — not a problem.

**When it's a problem:**
- **Phosphorus deficiency:** cool temperatures (below 65°F)
  can cause phosphorus lockout, which shows as purple stems
  and slow growth. Warm the environment
- **Cool root zone:** even if air temperature is fine, cold
  soil (below 60°F) restricts phosphorus uptake. Use a heat
  mat under the pot
- **pH issues:** phosphorus is less available above pH 7.0.
  Check soil pH

**Fix:** if temps and pH are correct and the plant is growing
well, ignore it. If growth is slow AND stems are purple,
check soil temperature and pH first.

## Problem 8: White or bleached leaf tips

**What it looks like:** the tips (or edges) of new leaves are
white or very pale, almost bleached.

**Cause:** light stress. The seedling is too close to the
light or the light is too intense.

**Fix:**
- Raise the light 6–8 inches
- Reduce intensity to 200–300 PPFD
- New growth will come in green once the light is adjusted.
  The bleached tips won't recover but the plant will grow
  past them

## Problem 9: Leggy with wide internodes

**What it looks like:** the seedling is growing but with long
spaces between each set of leaves. The structure is open and
floppy instead of compact.

**Cause:** not enough light (stretching for light) or too much
heat (encouraging elongation).

**Fix:**
- Increase light intensity (target 300–400 PPFD for seedlings)
- Lower the light closer to the canopy
- Reduce temperature to 75–78°F
- Add gentle air movement (fan) to strengthen stems

## Problem 10: Brown or crispy leaf edges

**What it looks like:** leaf margins turn brown and dry,
starting at the tips and progressing inward.

**Cause in seedlings:**
- **Low humidity:** below 40% RH, seedlings lose water
  through leaves faster than their tiny roots can replace it
- **Nutrient burn:** if feeding too strong for the stage (above
  0.4 EC/200 PPM for seedlings in amended soil)
- **Heat damage:** combined with low humidity, heat crisps
  leaf edges

**Fix:**
- Increase humidity to 65–75% (use a humidity dome)
- If feeding: reduce to 25% strength or plain water
- Check temperature — below 80°F for seedlings

## The seedling survival checklist

Most seedling problems come from the same few environmental
errors. Before troubleshooting, verify these fundamentals:

| Factor | Target | Common mistake |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Temperature | 75–80°F | Too cold (below 68°F) slows/kills |
| Humidity | 65–75% | Too dry (below 50%) causes crispy edges |
| Light | 200–400 PPFD | Too close/bright causes bleaching; too far causes stretching |
| Watering | Moist, not soaked | Overwatering is the #1 seedling killer |
| pH | 6.2–6.5 (soil) | Too high locks out nutrients |
| Nutrients | None to 25% strength | Too strong burns seedlings |
| Airflow | Gentle, indirect | Direct wind dries seedlings; no wind weakens stems |

If all 7 parameters are in range, the seedling will almost
certainly thrive.

## Common mistakes

1. **Overwatering** — the #1 seedling killer. Seedlings in
   large pots are especially vulnerable because the soil holds
   far more water than the tiny root system needs. Water a
   small circle around the seedling, not the entire pot
2. **Too much light** — a 480W LED at full power 12 inches
   from a seedling is 3–4x the intensity it can handle. Start
   at 30–40% power, 24–30 inches height. Increase gradually
3. **Feeding too early or too strong** — seedlings in quality
   potting soil don't need nutrients for the first 2–3 weeks.
   The soil and cotyledon reserves provide enough. Start
   feeding at 25% strength after the 3rd set of true leaves
4. **Diagnosing genetics as problems** — purple stems, slightly
   twisted first leaves, and cotyledons that don't look
   identical are all within the range of normal seedling
   variation. Only investigate if the plant is visibly unhappy
   (yellowing, wilting, not growing)
