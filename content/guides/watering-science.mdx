Every watering guide says "water when the pot feels light" and
"don't overwater." That's good advice, but it doesn't explain WHY.
Understanding the physics and biology of water movement in plants
and growing media transforms watering from a guessing game into a
decision you can reason about. This guide covers the science — not
the schedule.

## How water moves through a plant

### The transpiration stream

Water moves through cannabis via a continuous column driven by
evaporation at the leaf surface:

1. **Root uptake:** water enters root hairs through osmosis (moving
   from low-concentration soil solution to higher-concentration
   root cell cytoplasm)
2. **Xylem transport:** water moves upward through xylem vessels
   (dead, hollow cells that act as pipes). The driving force is
   not the roots pushing — it's the leaves pulling
3. **Leaf transpiration:** stomata (pores on the underside of
   leaves) open, water vapor exits the leaf, and the resulting
   tension pulls the water column upward. This is the engine of
   the whole system
4. **Continuous column:** water molecules are cohesive (they stick
   to each other via hydrogen bonds). The evaporation of one
   molecule at a leaf stoma pulls the next one up, creating an
   unbroken chain from root to leaf

**Quantifying it:** a mature cannabis plant in flower can transpire
1–3 liters of water per day under high-light conditions. On a
hot, dry day with high VPD (vapor pressure deficit), transpiration
increases — the plant uses more water. On a cool, humid day (low
VPD), transpiration slows — the plant uses less.

This is why watering needs change with weather and environment,
not just plant size. See our
[VPD guide](/guides/temperature-humidity-vpd).

### What transpiration delivers

Transpiration isn't just about water — it's the delivery mechanism
for dissolved nutrients:

- **Mass flow:** nutrients dissolved in soil water enter roots and
  travel upward with the transpiration stream. This is how most
  nitrogen, calcium, magnesium, and sulfur reach the leaves
- **Active transport:** some nutrients (potassium, phosphorus) are
  actively pumped into root cells against concentration gradients
  using cellular energy (ATP). This requires oxygen — which is
  why waterlogged roots can't absorb nutrients even when they're
  present in the medium

**Calcium is a transpiration-dependent nutrient.** It moves
through the plant almost entirely via mass flow in the
transpiration stream. It cannot be redistributed once deposited
(it's "immobile"). This is why calcium deficiency shows up in new
growth first and why low-transpiration environments (high humidity,
low airflow) cause calcium deficiency even when calcium is
abundant in the medium.

## Osmosis and root pressure

### How roots absorb water

Roots absorb water through osmosis — the movement of water across
a semi-permeable membrane from lower solute concentration to
higher. Root cell cytoplasm has a higher solute concentration than
the surrounding soil solution, so water flows inward.

**Root pressure** is the slight positive pressure created by this
osmotic gradient. On cool, humid mornings (low transpiration),
root pressure can push water upward on its own. This is what
causes **guttation** — those droplets that appear on leaf tips
overnight. Guttation is a sign of healthy root pressure and good
hydration (not overwatering).

### The salt problem

When you feed salt-based nutrients (most liquid fertilizers),
you're increasing the solute concentration of the soil solution.
If the nutrient concentration gets too high, the osmotic gradient
reverses — water moves OUT of the roots and into the medium.

This is **fertilizer burn** at the root level: the roots
literally lose water to the concentrated solution around them.
Symptoms:
- Tip burn (leaf tips brown and crispy)
- Wilting despite wet medium
- Slow growth

**EC (electrical conductivity)** measures the dissolved salt
concentration. Keeping EC within range prevents osmotic stress.
See our [EC guide](/guides/ec-ppm-measuring).

## Why wet-dry cycles matter

### The oxygen requirement

Roots need oxygen. Period. Root cells perform aerobic respiration
(burning sugars for energy) to power nutrient uptake. Without
oxygen, they switch to anaerobic respiration — which produces
ethanol and damages cell membranes. Extended oxygen deprivation
kills root cells.

**Where root oxygen comes from:**
- **Air-filled pore space** in the growing medium. After watering,
  gravity pulls water down, and air fills the emptied pore space.
  This air is the primary oxygen source
- **Dissolved oxygen** in irrigation water — a minor source
  compared to pore air, but significant in hydro systems

### What happens when you overwater

Overwatering fills ALL pore space with water, displacing air.
Root oxygen drops to critical levels within hours. The cascade:

1. **Hours 1–6:** oxygen depletes in the saturated root zone.
   Aerobic respiration stops
2. **Hours 6–24:** root cells switch to anaerobic respiration.
   Ethanol and acetaldehyde accumulate, damaging cell membranes
3. **Days 1–3:** root hairs begin dying. Nutrient uptake declines.
   The plant wilts even though the medium is wet (roots can't
   absorb water without energy from respiration)
4. **Days 3–7:** dead root tissue provides a food source for
   Pythium and other root pathogens. Root rot begins
5. **Days 7+:** significant root loss. Above-ground decline
   accelerates

**This is why cannabis wilts when overwatered** — it looks
identical to underwatering because the mechanism is the same:
roots can't deliver water to the leaves. The difference is
whether the medium is wet (overwatering) or dry (underwatering).
See our
[overwatering guide](/guides/overwatering-underwatering).

### The dry-back

The "dry" part of the wet-dry cycle isn't about stressing the
plant — it's about restoring oxygen:

- As the medium dries, water drains from the largest pore spaces
  first, replaced by air
- Root respiration resumes in these newly aerated zones
- The plant resumes normal nutrient uptake
- New root growth is stimulated (roots grow toward moisture,
  expanding the root system)

**Optimal dry-back in soil:**
- Water thoroughly until 10–20% runoff
- Let the medium dry until the top 1–2 inches are dry and the pot
  feels noticeably lighter
- Water again

**Optimal dry-back in coco:**
- Coco should never dry out completely (it becomes hydrophobic)
- Target 50–70% dry-back between waterings
- In flower with mature plants, this may mean watering 1–3 times
  daily

### Substrate physics: field capacity and wilting point

**Field capacity:** the maximum amount of water a growing medium
holds against gravity after draining. When you water to runoff and
the medium stops dripping, it's at field capacity. This is the
"wet" end of the wet-dry cycle.

**Permanent wilting point:** the moisture level at which the plant
can no longer extract water from the medium. Below this point, the
suction required to pull water from tiny pore spaces exceeds the
plant's osmotic capacity. The plant wilts irreversibly.

**Available water:** the range between field capacity and wilting
point. This is the useful water. Different media have different
available water capacities:

| Medium | Field capacity | Available water | Air-filled porosity |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Peat-based soil | High | Wide range | Low to moderate |
| Coco coir | High | Moderate range | High |
| Perlite | Low | Narrow range | Very high |
| Rockwool | Very high | Wide range | Low when saturated |
| Clay pebbles (hydroton) | Very low | Very narrow | Very high |

**Practical meaning:** soil has a wide buffer between "wet enough"
and "too dry" — it's forgiving. Perlite has a very narrow buffer —
it needs frequent watering. Coco is in between. This is why soil
is recommended for beginners.

## Water quality

### pH and nutrient availability

Water pH determines which nutrients are available for root uptake.
Each nutrient has a pH range where it's soluble and absorbable:

- **pH 6.0–6.8 (soil):** optimal range where all essential
  nutrients are simultaneously available
- **Below 5.5:** iron, manganese, and zinc become overly available
  (toxicity risk), while calcium and magnesium become locked out
- **Above 7.5:** iron, manganese, zinc, and phosphorus become
  locked out

This is why pH is the single most important variable in watering.
See our [pH guide](/guides/ph-for-beginners).

### Dissolved minerals

Tap water contains dissolved minerals (calcium, magnesium,
chlorine, carbonates) that affect your nutrient balance:
- **Hard water** (high mineral content, high EC) — requires
  adjusting nutrient formulas to account for existing minerals
- **Soft water** (low mineral content) — may need cal-mag
  supplementation
- **RO water** (reverse osmosis, near-zero minerals) — requires
  adding back calcium, magnesium, and buffer before use

See our [water quality guide](/guides/water-quality-for-cannabis).

## Temperature effects on watering

### Cold water

Root metabolic rate decreases in cold water. Below 60°F (15°C),
roots absorb water and nutrients significantly more slowly. Cold
water also holds more dissolved oxygen (good) but slows biological
activity (potentially bad for living soil).

**Practical rule:** room-temperature water (65–72°F / 18–22°C) is
ideal. Avoid ice-cold water directly from outdoor taps in winter.

### Warm water

Warm water (above 75°F / 24°C) holds less dissolved oxygen and
promotes pathogenic growth. In hydro systems, warm reservoir
temperatures are the primary root rot risk factor.

## Container effects on watering

### Pot size vs. plant size

A small plant in a large pot is an overwatering risk — the plant
can't transpire enough water to dry the medium in a reasonable
time. The unused soil stays wet, becomes anaerobic, and roots rot.

**Right-sizing rule:** transplant to the next pot size when roots
have colonized 70–80% of the current container. Don't go from a
solo cup to a 15-gallon pot in one step.

### Fabric pots vs. plastic

Fabric pots allow air exchange through the walls, providing
constant passive aeration of the root zone. They also air-prune
roots (roots that reach the fabric edge are killed by air
exposure, stimulating lateral branching instead of circling).

This means fabric pots are more forgiving of overwatering —
excess moisture can evaporate through the sides, and the root zone
stays more aerobic. The trade-off: they dry out faster, requiring
more frequent watering. See our
[pot guide](/guides/fabric-pots-vs-plastic).

### Drainage

No drainage holes = guaranteed overwatering. Water pools at the
bottom, creating an anaerobic swamp where roots die. Every
container must have drainage holes, and water that runs out the
bottom should not sit in a saucer for more than 30 minutes.

## Common mistakes

1. **Watering on a schedule instead of by weight** — the plant's
   water needs change daily based on temperature, humidity, growth
   stage, and light intensity. A fixed schedule guarantees
   over- or under-watering at some point. Lift the pot
2. **Not watering to runoff** — watering a small amount on top
   means the bottom of the medium stays dry. Roots there get no
   water, and salts accumulate in the dry zone. Water thoroughly
   until 10–20% runs out the bottom
3. **Misting seedlings instead of watering the medium** — seedling
   roots need moisture in the medium, not on the leaves. Misting
   provides leaf moisture but doesn't hydrate roots. Water the
   soil around the seedling gently
4. **Ignoring environmental changes** — a heatwave that raises
   tent temperatures from 75°F to 85°F can double transpiration
   rate. The plant may need water twice as often. Watch the
   environment, not just the medium
5. **Using cold water from the tap** — in cold climates, tap water
   can be 40–50°F in winter. This shocks roots and slows
   metabolism. Let water reach room temperature before use
