You've been given a clone — a rooted cutting from a known female
cannabis plant. Unlike seeds, where you're working with unknown
genetics until the plant reveals itself, a clone is a genetic
copy of its mother. You know exactly what it will produce (same
strain, same sex, same growth pattern). But growing from a clone
is NOT the same as growing from a seed. The first two weeks are
different, the veg timeline is different, and the mistakes that
kill clones are different from the ones that kill seedlings.

## What you're working with

A clone is a section of stem with a few nodes and leaves, cut from
a mother plant and rooted. When you receive it:

- **It's already female** — no need to sex it (assuming the mother
  was female and healthy)
- **It's genetically mature** — unlike a seedling, a clone has the
  same biological age as its mother. This means it can flower
  immediately if put under 12/12 (even if it's tiny)
- **It has no taproot** — clones develop lateral root systems from
  the cut stem. No central taproot means the root structure is
  different from seed-grown plants
- **It's fragile** — a fresh clone is a wounded plant that's just
  regrown roots. It has minimal root mass and is vulnerable to
  environmental stress

## Receiving and inspecting the clone

When you first get the clone:

1. **Check the roots** — if the clone is in a small container,
   gently tilt it and peek. Healthy roots are white. Brown, slimy,
   or absent roots mean the clone isn't ready for normal growing
   conditions
2. **Check for pests** — inspect leaves top and bottom with a
   loupe. Spider mites, thrips, and broad mites transfer with
   clones. Treat before introducing to your grow space
3. **Check for yellow or drooping leaves** — some stress is normal
   from transport. Severe wilting or yellowing may indicate root
   problems
4. **Quarantine** — if you have other plants, keep the new clone
   separate for 3–7 days to watch for pests or disease before
   introducing it to your grow

## The first week (critical)

The first 7 days determine whether the clone thrives or dies.
Fresh clones need gentler conditions than established plants:

### Environment

| Parameter | Target | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Temperature | 72–78°F | Stress-free range for recovery |
| Humidity | 65–80% | Reduces transpiration demand on weak roots |
| Light intensity | Low (200–300 PPFD) | Roots can't support high transpiration yet |
| Light schedule | 18/6 | Prevents accidental flowering |
| Airflow | Gentle, indirect | Direct wind dries leaves faster than roots can replace water |

**Humidity dome (if roots are weak):** if the clone is recently
rooted and roots are sparse, place it under a humidity dome (clear
plastic cup or cut bottle) for the first 3–5 days. The dome
maintains high humidity around the leaves, reducing transpiration
stress while roots establish.

### Watering

- Water the medium lightly — keep it moist but not soaked
- Don't feed nutrients for the first 5–7 days (unless the clone
  is in an inert medium like rockwool or perlite, where a very
  dilute nutrient solution at 25% strength is needed)
- If the clone wilts despite moist soil, it's a humidity/root
  problem, not a watering problem. Dome it

### What NOT to do

- **Don't transplant immediately** — let the clone acclimate for
  at least 5–7 days before moving it to a larger container
- **Don't top or train** — the clone is recovering. Any stress
  slows root development
- **Don't blast it with strong light** — start at 200–300 PPFD and
  increase gradually
- **Don't put it directly into 12/12** — a small clone can flower
  immediately (it's genetically mature) but will produce almost
  nothing. Veg it first

## Weeks 2–3: transition to normal conditions

Once the clone shows new growth (new leaves forming at the top),
it's establishing and can handle gradually normal conditions:

**Week 2:**
- Remove the humidity dome (if used) gradually — open for a few
  hours, then overnight, then fully remove
- Increase light to 400–500 PPFD
- Begin feeding at 25–50% strength
- Monitor for signs of stress (wilting, yellowing, curling)

**Week 3:**
- Clone should be actively growing
- Increase light to normal veg levels (500–700 PPFD)
- Feed at 50–75% strength
- **Transplant to the final container** — once roots are visible
  through the drainage holes of the starter container

See our [transplanting guide](/guides/transplanting-cannabis).

## Veg timing for clones

Clones don't need as much veg time as seed-grown plants because:
- They start with a mature stem (no seedling phase)
- Multiple nodes are already established
- Root development is faster once established
- They can be trained immediately once vigorous

**Typical clone veg times:**

| Setup | Recommended veg | Expected size at flip |
|-------|----------------|----------------------|
| Space bucket | 2–3 weeks | 8–10 inches |
| 2×2 tent | 3–4 weeks | 12–18 inches |
| 4×4 tent | 4–6 weeks | 18–24 inches |
| Outdoor | Until natural photoperiod triggers (or full season) | 3–6 feet |

**The fast-track option:** clones can be flipped to 12/12 as
early as week 2–3 of veg (once showing vigorous growth) for a
"sea of green" (SOG) approach. This produces smaller plants with
one main cola each. See our [SOG guide](/guides/sea-of-green-sog).

## Training clones

Clones can be trained sooner than seed-grown plants because they
skip the seedling phase:

### Topping

Top the clone once it has established in the new container and is
actively growing — typically 7–14 days after transplant. Top above
the 3rd or 4th node (clones already have the node structure from
the mother).

### LST

Begin LST as soon as the clone is vigorous (usually 1–2 weeks
after transplant). Bend the main stem gently outward and secure
to the pot edge.

### SCROG

Clones are ideal for SCROG because you can take multiple identical
clones and fill a screen evenly — every plant grows at the same
rate with the same structure.

See our [training schedule](/guides/progressive-plant-training).

## Clone vs. seed: key differences

| Factor | Clone | Seed |
|--------|-------|------|
| Sex | Known female | Unknown (unless feminized) |
| Genetics | Exact copy of mother | Unique individual |
| Start size | Established stem + nodes | Single taproot |
| Root type | Lateral (no taproot) | Taproot + laterals |
| Time to flower-ready | 3–5 weeks | 5–8 weeks |
| Genetic age | Same as mother | Fresh/juvenile |
| Vigor | Depends on mother age | Typically high (hybrid vigor) |
| Disease carriage | Can inherit mother's pathogens | Clean (unless seed-borne) |

### The vigor difference

Clones from old mother plants (mothers that have been kept in veg
for months or years) can show reduced vigor compared to seed-grown
plants. This is normal — the clone is biologically old even though
it's a new plant. Refreshing the mother line (growing a new mother
from seed or from an early-generation clone) restores vigor.

See our [mother plant guide](/guides/mother-plant-maintenance).

### The disease risk

Clones inherit any pathogens present in the mother. The most
significant:

- **Hop Latent Viroid (HpLVd):** reduces THC by 30–50% and yield
  significantly. Symptomless in many plants — you can't tell by
  looking. The only detection is RT-qPCR testing
- **Broad mites:** microscopic pests invisible to the naked eye.
  Symptoms mimic herbicide damage (twisted, glossy new growth)
- **Powdery mildew:** dormant spores can transfer with cuttings
  and activate under the right conditions

When receiving clones from unknown sources, quarantine and inspect
carefully.

## Multiple clones from one mother

If you have access to a mother plant (or a vigorous clone you can
take cuttings from), running multiple identical clones in a grow
has advantages:

- **Identical growth rate** — every plant finishes at the same
  time. No mixed-maturity harvests
- **Consistent canopy** — uniform height and structure, perfect
  for SCROG
- **Known outcome** — you know what the flower will look, smell,
  and taste like

See our [cloning guide](/guides/cloning-and-propagation).

## Common mistakes

1. **Blasting a fresh clone with full light** — a clone with
   sparse roots can't support high transpiration. Start low
   (200–300 PPFD) and ramp up over 1–2 weeks
2. **Transplanting immediately** — let the clone acclimate for
   5–7 days first. The root system needs time to recover from
   transport stress
3. **Overwatering** — clones have small root systems. A small
   root ball in a large pot of wet soil is a root rot setup.
   Water the area around the root ball, not the entire pot
4. **Not checking for pests** — clones from dispensaries, friends,
   or other growers can carry spider mites, thrips, or broad
   mites. Inspect with a loupe before introducing to your space
5. **Flipping too early** — a 6-inch clone flipped to 12/12 will
   produce 1–3 grams of flower. Unless you're running a
   deliberate SOG, give clones 3–5 weeks of veg to build size
6. **Assuming identical treatment** — even though all clones from
   the same mother are genetically identical, environmental
   position (closer to light, corner vs. center, intake draft)
   causes variation. Rotate pots weekly for even development
