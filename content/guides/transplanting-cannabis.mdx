Every cannabis plant outgrows its first home. Transplanting at the right time
gives roots fresh space to expand, which directly translates to bigger plants
and heavier yields. Do it wrong — or too late — and you get days of stalled
growth or root-bound plants that never recover.

## Why transplant at all?

Starting seeds in small containers lets you control moisture precisely during
the fragile seedling stage. But once the root ball fills that pot, the plant
slows down. Transplanting into a larger container restarts vigorous growth.

**Benefits of potting up in stages:**

- Better moisture control early on (small pot = less overwatering risk)
- Stronger root structure (roots hit the edge, branch, then fill new soil)
- Healthier plants overall vs. starting in the final pot

**When to skip transplanting:** Autoflowers. Their short life means any
transplant shock costs proportionally more. Start autos in their final pot
(3–5 gallon).

## When to transplant — the signs

Don't go by a calendar. Go by what the plant tells you:

| Signal | What it means |
|--------|--------------|
| Roots visible at drain holes | Root ball is filling the pot |
| Plant drinks the pot dry in 1 day | Not enough soil to hold moisture |
| Leaves wider than the pot rim | Canopy outpacing the container |
| Growth has stalled | Roots are circling with nowhere to go |
| 4–5 nodes on the plant | Standard timing for first pot-up |

If you see yellowing lower leaves plus any of the above, you're already late.
Move quickly.

## Container sizing ladder

A standard photoperiod goes through 2–3 pots:

| Stage | Container size | Days in pot |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Seedling | Solo cup or 1/2 gallon | 10–14 days |
| Early veg | 1–2 gallon | 14–21 days |
| Final pot | 3–5 gal (indoor) / 7–15 gal (outdoor) | Rest of life |

**Rule of thumb:** double or triple the volume each time. Going from a solo
cup straight to 5 gallons works but makes early watering harder — the soil
stays wet too long in the unrooted zone.

## How to transplant — step by step

**Supplies:** new pot with drainage, pre-moistened soil/media, clean hands or
gloves, optional mycorrhizae inoculant.

### 1. Water the current pot

Water the plant in its current container 1–2 hours before transplanting. This
holds the root ball together and prevents it from crumbling apart. Don't
waterlog it — just moist throughout.

### 2. Prepare the new pot

Fill the new pot with pre-moistened medium. Dig or press a hole roughly the
size of the old pot. If using mycorrhizae, dust a thin layer inside the hole
where roots will contact fresh soil.

### 3. Remove the plant

Flip the old pot upside-down with your hand cradling the soil surface and
stem between two fingers. Tap the bottom and gently slide the root ball out.
If roots are circling, gently tease the outer roots loose with your fingers.

**Never pull the plant by the stem.** Always support the root ball.

### 4. Place and fill

Set the root ball into the prepared hole so the soil surface sits about
1/2 inch below the new pot's rim. Fill around the sides and gently press
down to eliminate air pockets. Don't pack it — roots need air.

### 5. Water in

Give a light watering around the edges of the root ball (not on top of it).
This encourages roots to grow outward into the new soil. A splash of
seaweed extract or humic acid in the water helps reduce shock.

### 6. Low light for 24 hours

Dim the lights slightly or move the plant out of direct sun for 24 hours.
This reduces transpiration demand while the roots settle in.

## Avoiding transplant shock

Transplant shock shows up as drooping leaves, slowed growth, or slight
yellowing for 1–3 days after the move. Here's how to minimize it:

- **Handle the root ball gently** — broken roots are the #1 cause
- **Don't transplant during peak heat or peak light** — early morning or
  lights-on is ideal
- **Match the soil/media** — switching from coco to soil mid-grow confuses
  the root zone pH
- **Don't fertilize immediately** — fresh soil has enough nutrition for 1–2
  weeks; let roots establish first
- **Mycorrhizae at transplant** — the best time to inoculate, since spores
  need root contact

## Fabric pots: a cheat code

Fabric pots (Smart Pots, Root Pouches) air-prune roots instead of letting
them circle. This means:

- Less risk of becoming root-bound
- Better air-to-root ratio
- You can delay transplanting by a few days without damage

The trade-off: fabric pots dry out faster and you can't "slide" the plant
out. For fabric-to-fabric transplanting, just cut the old pot away with
scissors.

## When it's too late

If roots are tightly circled into a dense mat, the plant is severely
root-bound. Transplant immediately, gently break up the outer root mass,
and expect 5–7 days of recovery. Feed lightly with a seaweed/kelp product
to support root regrowth. The plant will recover, but you've lost a week
of growth.

## Quick reference

1. Water the old pot 1–2 hours before
2. Prepare the new pot with pre-moistened soil
3. Slide the root ball out (never pull the stem)
4. Set it in, fill around, press gently
5. Water the edges, not the top
6. Dim lights for 24 hours

That's it. One calm, careful transplant sets up weeks of explosive growth.
