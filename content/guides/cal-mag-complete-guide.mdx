"Just add cal-mag" is the most common advice in cannabis growing
forums. Plant looks weird? Cal-mag. Spots on the leaves? Cal-mag.
Slow growth? Cal-mag. It's become the grower's aspirin — thrown at
every symptom regardless of the actual cause.

Sometimes it's the right call. Calcium and magnesium are secondary
macronutrients that cannabis uses in significant quantities, and
certain growing conditions create genuine deficiencies. But
unnecessary cal-mag supplementation can cause its own problems:
nutrient lockout, salt buildup, and masking the real issue.

## When you actually need cal-mag

### Growing in coco coir

**Coco ALWAYS needs supplemental cal-mag.** This is not optional.

Coco coir has cation exchange sites that preferentially bind calcium
and magnesium ions from the nutrient solution. The coco literally
steals Ca and Mg from your feed before the plant can absorb them.
Even if your base nutrients contain calcium and magnesium, the coco
takes its share first.

**Dose:** 1–2 mL/L of cal-mag (like General Hydroponics CaliMagic
or Botanicare Cal-Mag Plus) in every feeding throughout the entire
grow.

### Using RO or distilled water

Reverse osmosis (RO) water and distilled water contain zero minerals.
Most base nutrient formulas assume your water provides some calcium
and magnesium naturally. When you use mineral-free water, the base
nutrients alone may not supply enough.

**Dose:** 0.5–1 mL/L cal-mag to replace what the water would
normally contribute.

### Using LED grow lights

This one is debated, but there's a pattern: growers under LED lights
report more calcium and magnesium deficiency than growers under HPS.
The hypothesis is that LED's spectrum and efficiency cause faster
growth, increasing calcium demand beyond what the feed provides.
Whether it's the light spectrum or just faster growth, the practical
observation is consistent.

**Dose:** 0.5–1 mL/L if you see early cal-mag symptoms under LEDs,
even in soil.

### Growing in hydro (DWC)

DWC nutrient solutions are entirely synthetic — whatever you add is
all the plant gets. Some base nutrient lines (General Hydroponics
Flora series, for example) include calcium and magnesium in the
Micro component. Others may be borderline for cannabis's high demand.

**Dose:** 0.5–1 mL/L if using a base nutrient line that's not
cannabis-specific. Check if your base nutrients already contain Ca
and Mg before adding more.

## When you probably DON'T need cal-mag

### Growing in soil with tap water

Good soil (Fox Farms Ocean Forest, Roots Organics, living soil)
contains calcium from dolomite lime and magnesium from amendments.
Most tap water contains 30–150 PPM of calcium and some magnesium.
Together, soil + tap water usually provides adequate cal-mag without
supplementation.

**Exception:** if your tap water is very soft (below 50 PPM total),
cal-mag may be needed even in soil.

### Using a cannabis-specific nutrient line

Nutrient lines designed for cannabis (Canna, Advanced Nutrients,
General Hydroponics Flora series in the recommended ratios) include
calcium and magnesium in their formulations. Adding cal-mag on top
can push calcium too high, locking out other nutrients.

**Check the label:** look for the Guaranteed Analysis on your
nutrient bottles. If calcium is listed at 3%+ and magnesium at 1%+,
you probably don't need additional cal-mag.

### When the problem is actually pH

This is the big one. Calcium becomes unavailable below pH 6.0 in
soil. A plant at pH 5.5 shows calcium deficiency symptoms — brown
spots, stunted new growth — even if calcium is abundant in the
medium. Adding more calcium doesn't fix a pH problem. Fixing the pH
does.

**Before adding cal-mag, check your pH.** If your feed or runoff pH
is below 6.0 (soil) or below 5.5 (coco/hydro), fix the pH first.
The "deficiency" may resolve itself.

## Identifying calcium vs. magnesium deficiency

Cal-mag products contain both nutrients, but the symptoms are
different:

### Calcium deficiency

- **Where:** new growth (upper leaves). Calcium is immobile in the
  plant — it can't be moved from old leaves to new ones
- **Symptoms:** brown spots on new leaves, distorted or stunted new
  growth, curled leaf tips, hollow stems
- **Timing:** develops over days. New leaves emerge damaged

### Magnesium deficiency

- **Where:** lower/older leaves. Magnesium is mobile — the plant
  pulls it from old leaves to feed new growth
- **Symptoms:** interveinal chlorosis — the veins stay green while
  the leaf tissue between them turns yellow. Classic "striped" look
- **Timing:** lower leaves yellow first, progresses upward

### The overlap

Cal-mag products treat both simultaneously, which is fine for most
situations. If you can identify which nutrient is actually deficient,
you can supplement specifically:
- **Calcium only:** calcium chloride foliar spray (fastest fix)
- **Magnesium only:** Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate) — 1 tsp per
  gallon of water as a drench or foliar spray

## Dosing cal-mag

### By medium

| Medium | Water type | Cal-mag dose | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Coco | Any | 1–2 mL/L | Every feeding |
| Coco | RO water | 2 mL/L | Every feeding |
| Soil | Tap water | Usually none | As needed |
| Soil | RO water | 0.5–1 mL/L | Every other feeding |
| Hydro | RO water | 1 mL/L | Every reservoir change |
| Hydro | Tap water | 0.5 mL/L or none | Check base nutrient Ca/Mg content |

### By growth stage

- **Seedling:** none or very light (0.5 mL/L). Seedlings don't need
  much calcium or magnesium yet
- **Veg:** standard dose for your medium. Calcium demand is moderate
  during vegetative growth
- **Early flower (weeks 1–3):** maintain veg dose. The stretch
  increases calcium demand
- **Mid flower (weeks 4–6):** slightly reduce or maintain. Some
  growers reduce cal-mag when PK boosters are added (to avoid
  calcium-phosphorus interaction)
- **Late flower (weeks 7+):** reduce or stop. The plant's nutritional
  needs are declining

### Mixing order

If you're also using silica (potassium silicate), the mixing order
matters:

1. Water
2. Silica (first — it's highly alkaline)
3. Base nutrients
4. Cal-mag (after base nutrients)
5. Other supplements
6. pH adjustment (last)

Cal-mag added before the base nutrients can interact with phosphorus
in the solution, causing precipitate (cloudy solution with white
particles). Adding it after the base minimizes this risk.

## Common cal-mag products

| Product | NPK | Ca% | Mg% | Notes |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-------|
| General Hydroponics CaliMagic | 1-0-0 | 5% | 1.5% | Contains some nitrogen |
| Botanicare Cal-Mag Plus | 2-0-0 | 3.2% | 1.2% | Higher nitrogen, plus iron |
| Canna Calmag Agent | 0-0-0 | 4% | 2% | Zero nitrogen — good for flower |
| Mother Earth Calmag | 2-0-0 | 5% | 1.5% | Similar to CaliMagic |

**Note the nitrogen:** most cal-mag products contain nitrogen
(the first number in NPK). During flower, this extra nitrogen can
contribute to nitrogen toxicity if you're already feeding bloom
nutrients. If you need cal-mag during mid-to-late flower, choose a
zero-nitrogen product like Canna Calmag Agent.

## Common mistakes

1. **Adding cal-mag without checking pH first** — pH lockout is the
   most common cause of calcium deficiency symptoms. If pH is off,
   adding more calcium doesn't help — it makes the nutrient solution
   more concentrated while the lockout persists
2. **Using cal-mag in soil with tap water by default** — soil +
   tap water usually provides sufficient calcium and magnesium. Only
   supplement if you see symptoms
3. **Overdosing cal-mag** — excess calcium locks out magnesium,
   potassium, and iron. Excess magnesium locks out calcium. More
   isn't better — stick to recommended doses
4. **Using nitrogen-containing cal-mag in late flower** — the extra
   nitrogen causes dark, clawed leaves (N toxicity) during a stage
   when the plant needs less nitrogen, not more
5. **Diagnosing every leaf spot as cal-mag deficiency** — leaf spots
   can indicate pH problems, pest damage, nutrient burn, or several
   other issues. Verify with the symptom checklist before treating
