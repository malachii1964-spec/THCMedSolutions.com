Modern LED grow lights are powerful — a 480W LED at 12 inches from
the canopy delivers more photon energy than the plant can use,
causing cellular damage that looks nothing like a nutrient problem.
Light burn is one of the most misdiagnosed issues in cannabis
growing because the symptoms (bleaching, yellowing, curling) overlap
with several nutrient deficiencies.

The key diagnostic: **light burn symptoms appear only on the leaves
closest to the light, while the rest of the plant looks healthy.**
No nutrient deficiency is that selective about location.

## Light burn vs. heat stress

These two problems often occur together (the light produces both
photons and heat), but they have different causes and different fixes.

| Feature | Light burn (photon damage) | Heat stress |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------|
| Cause | Too many photons hitting the leaf | Air temperature too high at the canopy |
| Primary symptom | Bleaching — tops turn pale yellow or white | Taco curling — leaf edges curl upward |
| Location | Only the top 2–4 inches of the canopy | Can affect the whole plant, worst at top |
| Leaf feel | Crispy, dry, sometimes papery | Dry but may not bleach |
| LED-specific | Yes — LEDs can light-burn without excessive heat | LEDs produce less heat than HPS at equal wattage |
| HPS-specific | Less common (HPS tends to heat-burn before light-burn) | Very common — HPS produces significant radiant heat |

### Light burn symptoms

- **Bleaching:** the top leaves (closest to the light) turn pale
  yellow, then white. The bleaching starts at the leaf tips and
  progresses inward. Unlike nitrogen deficiency (which yellows from
  the bottom up), light burn yellows from the top down — only the
  leaves nearest the light
- **No green recovery:** bleached tissue is permanently damaged. The
  chloroplasts are destroyed. Unlike a nutrient deficiency that
  improves when corrected, bleached leaves don't green up
- **Foxtailing:** in flower, light burn causes buds to grow abnormal
  tower-like structures (foxtails) — new calyxes stacking on top of
  each other in thin columns instead of swelling outward. Some
  foxtailing is genetic, but widespread foxtailing on the top colas
  with none on the lower buds is light stress

### Heat stress symptoms

- **Taco curling:** leaf edges curl upward, making the leaf look
  like a taco or canoe. This is the plant's attempt to reduce the
  leaf surface area exposed to heat
- **Drooping in the heat:** leaves droop during the hottest part of
  the light cycle and recover when temperatures drop
- **Increased water consumption:** the plant transpires faster to
  cool itself
- **Fox-tailing and airy buds** in flower

## Optimal light distance

### LED grow lights

LED hanging height varies by manufacturer and wattage. Use these as
starting points, then adjust based on plant response:

| Light wattage | Seedling | Veg | Flower |
|--------------|----------|-----|--------|
| 100W | 24–30" | 18–24" | 12–18" |
| 200–300W | 30–36" | 20–28" | 14–20" |
| 400–480W | 36–42" | 24–30" | 16–24" |
| 600W+ | 42–48" | 30–36" | 20–30" |

**Use the dimmer.** Most quality LEDs have a dimmer knob. Instead
of adjusting height alone:
- **Seedlings:** 50% intensity, higher mounting
- **Early veg:** 60–75% intensity
- **Late veg:** 75–90% intensity
- **Flower:** 90–100% intensity

### Measuring with a PAR meter

If you have a PAR/PPFD meter (or a phone app like Photone):

| Stage | Target PPFD (µmol/m²/s) |
|-------|------------------------|
| Seedling | 200–400 |
| Veg | 400–600 |
| Early flower | 600–800 |
| Peak flower | 800–1000 |
| Maximum (with CO₂) | 1000–1500 |

**Above 1000 PPFD without supplemental CO₂, the plant can't
photosynthesize faster** — extra photons become damage, not growth.
CO₂ supplementation raises this ceiling to ~1500 PPFD, but only if
CO₂ levels are maintained at 1200–1500 PPM.

## How to fix light burn

### Step 1: Increase distance or reduce intensity

- **Raise the light** 4–6 inches above its current position
- **Dim the light** by 10–20%
- **Do both** if symptoms are severe

### Step 2: Wait and assess

- Watch the plant for 3–5 days after adjustment
- **Bleached leaves won't recover** — judge improvement by NEW
  growth. New leaves should emerge green and healthy
- If new growth is still showing symptoms, raise or dim further

### Step 3: Remove severely damaged tissue

- Leaves that are fully bleached (white) are no longer
  photosynthesizing and serve no purpose
- Remove them to redirect the plant's energy to healthy growth
- Don't remove green leaves that are merely at the top — only remove
  those that are permanently damaged

## Special situations

### Seedlings and young plants

Seedlings are extremely sensitive to light intensity. A light that's
perfect for a mature plant will bleach a seedling in hours. Start
seedlings at 50% dimmer and 30+ inches from the light. Gradually
reduce distance and increase intensity over the first 2 weeks.

### Post-transplant

Freshly transplanted plants are stressed. Reduce light intensity by
20% for 2–3 days after transplanting, then return to normal.

### Autoflowers

Autoflowers get 18–20 hours of light per day, meaning the daily
light integral (DLI) is higher than a 12/12 photoperiod plant at
the same PPFD. Reduce intensity slightly compared to a 12/12
flower plant to compensate for the extra hours.

### The center vs. edges

Most grow lights have a hot spot in the center of their footprint
and drop off toward the edges. The center of the canopy directly
under the light receives 20–40% more photons than the edges. If
the center plants show stress while edge plants look healthy,
raise the light or rotate plants regularly.

## Common mistakes

1. **Assuming more light is always better** — beyond the plant's
   photosynthetic capacity, additional light is pure damage. There
   is a ceiling, and exceeding it reduces quality and yield
2. **Blaming bleached tops on nitrogen deficiency** — nitrogen
   deficiency yellows from the BOTTOM up. Light burn yellows from
   the TOP down. This distinction is diagnostic
3. **Not using the dimmer** — many growers run their LED at 100%
   from day one because "I paid for all those watts." Dimming is not
   waste — it's precision. Use 100% only during peak flower when the
   plant can handle it
4. **Ignoring foxtailing** — foxtails look exotic and some growers
   think they're a good sign. Light-stress foxtails produce airy,
   loose buds with reduced potency. If only the top colas foxtail,
   the light is too close
5. **Lowering the light to "fix" a leggy plant** — stretchy growth
   is usually caused by insufficient light during veg. Suddenly
   lowering the light to compensate burns the canopy. Instead,
   gradually reduce distance over several days
