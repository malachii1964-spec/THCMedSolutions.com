Indoor gardens have no predators, stable warmth, and a buffet. When something gets in, it multiplies fast. The rule for all three of the big invaders: **treat the day you find them**, because every one of them is a week away from being ten times worse.

## Spider mites: the tent killer

**ID:** tiny specks on leaf undersides, pinprick white/yellow stippling on top of leaves. Fine webbing between buds means severe infestation. Confirm with a loupe: tiny moving dots, often two-spotted.

**Why they're the worst:** a female lays 100+ eggs, generations run 5–7 days in a warm tent, and populations develop pesticide resistance.

**Treatment:**
1. Isolate or remove badly infested plants — sentiment loses tents
2. Spray **all leaf undersides** to dripping with insecticidal soap or neem (veg only), lights off, fans off for an hour
3. Repeat **every 3 days for 3 weeks** — sprays don't kill eggs, so you're intercepting each hatch
4. In flower: no neem ever (it taints bud and won't wash off). Water spray-downs, predatory mites (*Phytoseiulus persimilis*), and lowering tent temp are your remaining tools. Badly webbed buds get cut and binned.

## Fungus gnats: annoying, then damaging

**ID:** small black flies drifting around the soil surface; larvae are tiny translucent worms in the top layer of wet medium. Adults are harmless; **larvae eat roots**, and seedlings suffer first.

**Treatment (do all four; it's cheap and total):**
1. **Let the top 3–5 cm of medium dry out** — larvae need wet soil, and chronic overwatering is almost always the root cause
2. Yellow sticky traps at soil level to crash the adult population
3. **BTI** (mosquito bits/dunks) steeped in your water — a bacterium that kills larvae, safe through flower
4. Top-dress with 1–2 cm of sand or perlite so adults can't lay

## Powdery mildew: the white dust

**ID:** flour-like white patches on leaf surfaces that **wipe off** (unlike nutrient spots). Spreads by airborne spore; loves RH above 55 %, still air, and crowded canopies.

**Treatment:**
1. Don't wipe or shake affected leaves in place — you're spreading spores. Bag leaves, then remove
2. Spray remaining foliage with **potassium bicarbonate** solution (1 tbsp/gal + drop of soap) or a 60/40 milk/water spray — both safe-ish in early flower, neither welcome on buds
3. Fix the environment or it returns: RH under 50 %, a fan moving air through (not just around) the canopy, thin the densest growth
4. Buds with mildew inside them are a loss. Don't dry them with the rest — mildew thrives in drying rooms.

## Prevention beats all of it

- **Quarantine everything living** that enters the room — clones from a friend are the #1 mite vector
- Change clothes after outdoor gardening; mites hitchhike
- Sticky traps from day one as an early-warning system, not a treatment
- Weekly 2-minute loupe patrol of leaf undersides, and RH held under 55 % in flower

| Threat | Safe in flower | Never in flower |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Spider mites | Predatory mites, water spray, temp drop | Neem, most sprays on buds |
| Fungus gnats | BTI, sand cap, dry-back, traps | Nothing needed beyond these |
| Powdery mildew | K-bicarbonate on leaves (early flower) | Any spray directly on buds |
