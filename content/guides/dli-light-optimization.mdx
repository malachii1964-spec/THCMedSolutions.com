PPFD tells you how much light is hitting your canopy at any given
moment. DLI tells you how much total light the plant receives over
the entire day — and that's the number that actually matters for
growth, yield, and quality. A plant under 400 PPFD for 18 hours
gets a very different DLI than the same light for 12 hours, and
that difference affects everything from node spacing to bud
density.

## What DLI is

**DLI (Daily Light Integral)** is the total number of
photosynthetically active photons (PAR) delivered to a square
meter of canopy per day. It's measured in **mol/m²/d** (moles of
photons per square meter per day).

Think of PPFD as the speed of a car and DLI as the total distance
traveled. PPFD is instantaneous intensity. DLI is the accumulated
total over the full photoperiod.

### The formula

```
DLI = PPFD × hours × 3600 / 1,000,000
```

Where:
- PPFD is in µmol/m²/s
- Hours is the photoperiod length
- 3600 converts hours to seconds
- 1,000,000 converts µmol to mol

**Quick shortcut:** PPFD × hours × 0.0036 = DLI

### Example calculations

| PPFD (µmol/m²/s) | Photoperiod | DLI (mol/m²/d) |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 200 | 18 hours | 12.96 |
| 400 | 18 hours | 25.92 |
| 600 | 18 hours | 38.88 |
| 800 | 18 hours | 51.84 |
| 600 | 12 hours | 25.92 |
| 800 | 12 hours | 34.56 |
| 1000 | 12 hours | 43.20 |
| 1200 | 12 hours | 51.84 |

Note that 800 PPFD × 18h and 1200 PPFD × 12h produce the same
DLI (51.84). The plant receives the same total light — but the
higher instantaneous intensity in the 12h scenario has different
implications for heat stress, water use, and CO2 demand.

## DLI targets by growth stage

| Stage | DLI target | PPFD range | Photoperiod |
|-------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Clones/seedlings | 12–18 mol/m²/d | 100–300 µmol/m²/s | 18–24h |
| Early veg | 20–30 mol/m²/d | 300–500 µmol/m²/s | 18h |
| Late veg | 30–45 mol/m²/d | 500–700 µmol/m²/s | 18h |
| Flower (no CO2) | 35–45 mol/m²/d | 600–800 µmol/m²/s | 12h |
| Flower (with CO2) | 45–65 mol/m²/d | 800–1200 µmol/m²/s | 12h |
| Autoflower | 40–55 mol/m²/d | 600–800 µmol/m²/s | 18–20h |

### Why these numbers

**Below 15 DLI:** the plant stretches (long internodes), produces
thin stems, and grows slowly. Seedlings under insufficient light
become leggy and weak. This is the "not enough light" zone.

**15–30 DLI:** adequate for vegetative growth. The plant develops
normal structure and moderate growth rate. This is the typical
range for hobby growers with budget lights.

**30–45 DLI:** the sweet spot for most cannabis grows. Tight node
spacing, thick stems, rapid growth in veg, and dense bud
production in flower. This is where well-equipped home grows
operate.

**45–65 DLI:** maximum production zone, but requires CO2
supplementation (1200–1500 ppm). Without supplemental CO2, plants
above ~45 DLI can't photosynthesize fast enough to use the light
— they hit a CO2 bottleneck. The excess light energy causes heat
stress and light burn. See our
[CO2 guide](/guides/co2-supplementation).

**Above 65 DLI:** diminishing returns even with CO2.
Photoprotective mechanisms (heat dissipation, chlorophyll
bleaching) kick in. Light burn becomes increasingly likely. Few
home growers should aim here.

## Measuring DLI

### With a PAR meter

A dedicated PAR meter (Apogee MQ-500, ~$500) or budget option
(Photone app for smartphones, free to $10) gives you PPFD
readings. Take readings at multiple points across the canopy,
average them, and apply the formula.

**Mapping your light footprint:**
1. Set up a grid across your canopy (every 6–12 inches)
2. Record PPFD at each point at canopy height
3. Note the variation — most LEDs are hottest in the center and
   drop off at the edges
4. The average PPFD across the canopy (not the peak) determines
   your effective DLI

### Without a PAR meter

If you know your light's rated PPFD at a specific height (from
the manufacturer's data sheet), you can estimate DLI directly.
Most quality LED manufacturers publish a PPFD map showing
intensity at different heights and positions.

**Warning:** manufacturer PPFD maps measure in an empty, white
reflective space with no plants. Real-world PPFD at the canopy
with plants is typically 70–85% of the rated map value due to
light absorption by leaves, less-than-perfect reflectivity, and
distance variations as plants grow.

## The flower DLI challenge

When you flip to 12/12, you lose 33% of your photoperiod compared
to 18/6. To maintain the same DLI, you need to increase PPFD by
50%:

| Veg DLI target | Veg PPFD (18h) | Same DLI in flower (12h) |
|----------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| 30 mol/m²/d | 463 PPFD | 694 PPFD needed |
| 40 mol/m²/d | 617 PPFD | 926 PPFD needed |
| 45 mol/m²/d | 694 PPFD | 1042 PPFD needed |

Many growers can't increase PPFD by 50% because their light is
already running at maximum output. Options:
- Accept a lower DLI in flower (30–35 is still productive)
- Raise light intensity gradually over the first 2 weeks of flower
  as the plant acclimates
- Add a supplemental bar light to boost edge and lower-canopy
  light
- Run autoflowers at 18–20h to maintain higher DLI without needing
  extreme PPFD

This is why autoflowers can out-produce photoperiod plants in
small grows: 18–20 hours of moderate PPFD (600) delivers more
total DLI (38.9–43.2) than 12 hours of high PPFD (800, DLI 34.6).

## DLI and CO2

Photosynthesis requires both light AND CO2. At ambient CO2 levels
(~420 ppm), the photosynthetic rate plateaus around 800 PPFD —
pushing higher doesn't help because CO2 becomes the limiting
factor.

**With CO2 supplementation:**

| CO2 level | PPFD saturation | Max practical DLI (12h) |
|-----------|----------------|------------------------|
| 420 ppm (ambient) | ~800 µmol/m²/s | ~35 mol/m²/d |
| 800 ppm | ~1000 µmol/m²/s | ~43 mol/m²/d |
| 1200 ppm | ~1200 µmol/m²/s | ~52 mol/m²/d |
| 1500 ppm | ~1500 µmol/m²/s | ~65 mol/m²/d |

**Without CO2 supplementation, don't push DLI above 40.**
The additional light energy without the CO2 to use it causes:
- Leaf bleaching (light burn) — see our
  [light burn guide](/guides/light-burn-light-stress)
- Chlorophyll degradation
- Reduced terpene production (heat damage to volatile compounds)
- Higher water and nutrient demand without proportional yield gain

## DLI uniformity

Average DLI across the canopy matters less if the distribution is
wildly uneven. A center reading of 900 PPFD dropping to 300 at
the edges gives an average of 600 — but the center is light-
burned while the edges are stretching.

### Improving uniformity

**Light height:** higher light position improves uniformity but
reduces peak intensity. Find the balance where edge PPFD is no
less than 70% of center PPFD.

**Multiple lights:** two 240W lights spread across a 4×4 tent
produce better uniformity than one 480W light centered.

**Reflective walls:** good quality mylar or white paint on tent
walls bounces edge light back toward the canopy, improving
uniformity by 10–20%.

**SCROG or LST:** training the canopy flat at a uniform height
ensures every bud site receives similar DLI. A level canopy
matters more than a level light footprint. See our
[SCROG guide](/guides/scrog-screen-of-green).

## Stage transitions

### Seedling → veg

Seedlings are sensitive to high light intensity. Start at 12–15
DLI and increase by 3–5 mol/m²/d per week as the plant develops
more leaf area. By late veg, the plant should be receiving full
intensity.

**Rapid DLI increases cause:**
- Leaf curl (taco-ing)
- Bleaching of new growth
- Stunted growth (the plant is protecting itself, not growing)

### Veg → flower

When flipping to 12/12:
1. Don't increase PPFD immediately — the sudden loss of 6 hours
   of light is already a stress signal
2. Keep PPFD the same for the first 3–5 days
3. Increase PPFD by 10–15% per week over weeks 1–3 of flower
4. Reach target flower PPFD by week 3–4, which coincides with
   the end of the stretch

### Late flower

Some growers reduce light intensity by 10–15% during the final
week before harvest. The theory: less light stress preserves
volatile terpenes and reduces the "hay" smell in dried flower.
Evidence is anecdotal but widely reported.

## DLI for autoflowers

Autoflowers don't need a 12/12 flip, which means you can optimize
DLI more freely:

| Schedule | PPFD needed for 40 DLI | Pros | Cons |
|----------|----------------------|------|------|
| 24/0 | 463 µmol/m²/s | Lowest PPFD needed | No dark period (some research suggests plants benefit from rest) |
| 20/4 | 556 µmol/m²/s | Good balance | Moderate PPFD needed |
| 18/6 | 617 µmol/m²/s | Most common | Higher PPFD needed for same DLI |

**Recommendation:** 20/4 for autoflowers delivers excellent DLI
at moderate PPFD, gives the plant a rest period, and reduces
electricity cost compared to 24/0. The 4-hour dark period also
allows for temperature and humidity cycling, which some growers
believe improves terpene development.

## Common mistakes

1. **Chasing high PPFD without considering photoperiod** — 1200
   PPFD for 12 hours is the same DLI as 800 PPFD for 18 hours,
   but the 1200 scenario is harder to manage (more heat, more
   water demand, CO2 requirement)
2. **Measuring PPFD only at the center** — your plant doesn't
   only grow at the center. Edge and lower-canopy light matters.
   Average across the growing area
3. **Ignoring the CO2 ceiling** — above ~800 PPFD at ambient CO2,
   you're wasting electricity and stressing the plant. Either add
   CO2 or accept the DLI limit
4. **Same intensity for all stages** — seedlings can't handle the
   DLI that flowering plants thrive at. Ramp up gradually
5. **Not accounting for canopy height changes** — as plants grow
   toward the light, PPFD at the canopy increases. Raise the light
   or dim it to maintain target DLI, especially during the flower
   stretch
